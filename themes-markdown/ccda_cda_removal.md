## Analysis

The theme of C-CDA / Consolidated CDA Removal in the ONC HTI-5 proposed rule has elicited a diverse range of opinions from stakeholders, reflecting the complexity of transitioning health IT standards. Many commenters emphasize the importance of the Consolidated CDA (C-CDA) as a critical document that provides a comprehensive, accessible summary of patient clinical events, which is particularly valuable for patients and caregivers. These stakeholders argue that removing the C-CDA creation certification criterion could negatively impact the clarity and usability of health information exchange, especially for non-technical users who find segmented FHIR resources difficult to navigate.

Conversely, a significant portion of commenters support deregulation and the removal of C-CDA requirements, advocating for an accelerated transition to FHIR Clinical Documents. They highlight that FHIR represents the future of health IT interoperability, offering more granular and flexible data exchange capabilities. Supporters also point to international trends, noting that several countries are migrating from CDA to FHIR standards, and urge the US to align with these global interoperability frameworks to maintain leadership and competitiveness.

Some health IT companies and consulting organizations express nuanced positions, supporting the shift toward FHIR but cautioning against premature removal of C-CDA requirements. They stress the readiness of public health agencies and the broader ecosystem, recommending a phased approach that retains existing standards like CDA and HL7 v2.5.1 until FHIR adoption is more mature. These commenters also raise concerns about potential increases in variability and burden on developers and public health entities if certification criteria are removed too quickly.

Overall, the discourse reveals a tension between innovation and stability, with stakeholders balancing the need to modernize health IT standards against the practical realities of implementation, usability, and the diverse needs of end users including patients, caregivers, and public health agencies.

## Top Arguments

### for deregulation

- FHIR Clinical Documents represent the future standard for health IT interoperability and should be prioritized.
- Accelerating the transition from CDA/CCDA to FHIR aligns the US with global interoperability trends and maintains international competitiveness.
- Providing funding, support, and a clear timeline will facilitate industry-wide adoption of FHIR and reduce implementation barriers.

### against deregulation

- Consolidated CDA documents provide a clearer, more accessible, and comprehensive summary of clinical events than segmented FHIR resources, which benefits patients and caregivers.
- Removing certification criteria prematurely increases variability and burden on health IT developers, healthcare organizations, and public health agencies not yet ready for full FHIR adoption.
- Public health agencies rely on existing standards like CDA and HL7 v2.5.1 and need continued support to maintain essential data reporting and interoperability.

## Top Recommendations

1. Reconsider and retain the Consolidated CDA creation certification criterion in §170.315(g)(6) to preserve valuable patient and caregiver information.
2. Accelerate the transition timeline from CDA/CCDA to FHIR Clinical Documents while providing funding, support, and assistance to implementers.
3. Maintain support for CDA and HL7 v2.5.1 standards until public health agencies are ready for widespread FHIR adoption.
4. Adopt higher versions of USCDI (v4 or preferably v6) in certification to ensure comprehensive patient demographic data.
5. Do not remove Insights Conditions reporting and Bulk FHIR as they provide valuable data on real-world interface deployment.

## Key Tensions

- Balancing innovation and modernization through FHIR adoption against the need for stability and usability provided by Consolidated CDA documents.
- The readiness of public health agencies and healthcare organizations to adopt FHIR versus the push to accelerate deregulation and remove legacy standards.
- Ensuring patient and caregiver access to clear, consolidated clinical information versus transitioning to more granular but potentially fragmented FHIR resources.
- Aligning US health IT certification with global interoperability trends while addressing domestic implementation challenges and stakeholder concerns.

## Dominant Position

The dominant sentiment among commenters is mixed but leans toward cautious support for deregulation and transition to FHIR, tempered by significant opposition advocating retention of the Consolidated CDA to ensure continuity, usability, and support for public health needs.
