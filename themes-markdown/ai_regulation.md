## Analysis

AI regulation and governance in the ONC HTI-5 proposed rule represent a critical juncture in health IT policy. The 112 comments on this theme reveal a deeply divided stakeholder base, with professional associations and safety-focused organizations emphasizing the urgent need for transparency, auditability, and human oversight in AI-driven clinical decision support systems, while health IT vendors and some integrators advocate for regulatory flexibility to accelerate innovation and deployment. The core tension centers on whether certification criteria designed to ensure AI transparency, explainability, and change control should be retained or removed in favor of market-based oversight mechanisms.

Proponents of deregulation argue that removing AI-specific certification criteria and transparency requirements will accelerate innovation in predictive analytics, clinical decision support, and automated workflows, enabling health IT vendors to respond more quickly to emerging market demands without navigating complex regulatory pathways. They contend that widespread AI adoption in healthcare, combined with ongoing industry investment in responsible AI practices, creates sufficient market incentives for vendors to implement safety safeguards without explicit regulatory mandates. This perspective emphasizes that rigid certification criteria may lag behind rapidly evolving AI methodologies and best practices, potentially constraining beneficial innovation.

Conversely, patient safety organizations, clinicians, and many professional associations strongly oppose deregulation of AI governance. They argue that health IT systems wielding AI-driven recommendations for diagnosis, treatment, and resource allocation represent fundamentally different risk profiles than traditional EHR systems, and that certification criteria requiring model transparency, auditability, periodic recalibration, and human override pathways are essential safeguards. They cite the documented risks of algorithmic bias, model drift over time, and failure modes that can propagate at scale once deployed in healthcare systems. Organizations like the Anesthesia Patient Safety Foundation and individual clinician groups emphasize that AI systems in healthcare should operate under the same scrutiny as pharmaceutical interventions, with evidence of safety and efficacy required before deployment.

A significant middle ground exists among commenters who support AI adoption while advocating for risk-stratified governance. They propose tiered approaches where high-risk AI applications (those affecting life-and-death decisions) maintain full transparency and auditability requirements, while lower-risk applications (workflow optimization, administrative routing) operate under lighter oversight. This perspective reflects recognition that blanket deregulation poses unacceptable risks, but that one-size-fits-all criteria may impose unnecessary burdens on lower-risk systems.

The theme also encompasses concerns about the cascading effects of removing CDS Source Attributes certification (170.315(b)(11)), which currently requires that AI model recommendations be explicitly labeled and explained. Commenters warn that removing this requirement erodes transparency and creates "black box" decision support systems where clinicians cannot assess the basis of recommendations. The debate also touches on broader questions about information blocking exceptions and automated access to patient data, with some commenters concerned that deregulating AI governance simultaneously with information blocking exceptions creates conditions for vendors to extract and use patient data in AI model training without explicit patient consent or organizational oversight.

## Top Arguments

### for deregulation

- Removing rigid certification criteria allows AI innovation to progress at industry pace without regulatory constraint.
- Widespread adoption and market pressures create sufficient incentives for responsible AI practices without explicit regulation.
- Prescriptive transparency and auditability requirements may lag behind evolving AI methodologies and best practices.
- Vendor investment in responsible AI is increasing, reducing need for regulatory mandates.

### against deregulation

- AI in healthcare poses fundamentally different risks than traditional EHR systems; requires explicit safeguards.
- Documented algorithmic bias and model drift necessitate transparency, auditability, and change control requirements.
- Removing certification criteria shifts safety verification burden from vendors to healthcare organizations and clinicians.
- Black box decision support systems erode clinician trust and transparency in care decisions.
- High-stakes AI decisions (diagnosis, treatment recommendations) warrant evidence-based safety requirements comparable to pharmaceutical interventions.

## Top Recommendations

1. Maintain minimum transparency, monitoring, auditability, and human override pathways for high-risk AI applications.
2. Implement risk-stratified governance: full requirements for high-risk predictive decision support; lighter requirements for administrative AI.
3. Require explicit labeling and source attribution for all AI-driven recommendations visible to clinicians.
4. Retain CDS Source Attributes certification criterion (170.315(b)(11)) to preserve transparency into algorithmic basis.
5. Establish periodic recalibration and drift monitoring requirements for AI models in production environments.
6. Require explicit opt-in consent mechanisms for use of patient data in AI model training and validation.
7. Create enforcement mechanism for violations of AI transparency and auditability requirements.

## Key Tensions

- Balancing acceleration of AI innovation with the need for safety safeguards in high-stakes clinical decisions.
- Market-based oversight versus regulatory certification for AI transparency and auditability.
- Risk-stratified approaches versus uniform requirements across diverse AI applications.
- Vendor autonomy in AI governance versus patient/clinician transparency and accountability.
- Cascading effects of simultaneous deregulation of AI oversight and information blocking exceptions.

## Dominant Position

Mixed with significant opposition: Professional associations and safety-focused organizations strongly oppose deregulation, while health IT vendors support flexibility. The weight of safety-critical arguments favors retention of AI transparency and auditability requirements, particularly for high-risk clinical applications.
