## Analysis

The theme of Regulatory Burden Reduction in the public comments on the ONC HTI-5 proposed rule reveals a clear divide between stakeholders prioritizing patient safety and those emphasizing the need to reduce administrative and developer burdens. Several commenters, including health care administrators, clinicians, and health systems, strongly oppose deregulation efforts that would weaken certification criteria related to usability, safety, and transparency. They argue that such deregulation risks increasing financial burdens on health institutions due to potential patient harm, clinician burnout, and safety incidents. These stakeholders emphasize that usability testing and safety-focused design are critical components of health IT certification that protect both patients and clinicians from errors and inefficiencies.

On the other hand, some health IT companies support deregulation measures that streamline reporting requirements and reduce redundant or unclear obligations, such as Real World Testing (RWT) and SVAP reporting. They advocate for clarifications and corrections to specific regulatory references to avoid unnecessary developer burden and confusion. This group focuses on improving regulatory efficiency without compromising essential protections, highlighting the need to balance burden reduction with maintaining meaningful standards.

Professional associations like the College of American Pathologists take a more nuanced stance, supporting the overall goals of burden reduction and interoperability but cautioning against removing critical transparency requirements, especially related to AI tools and the release of pathology results. They underscore the importance of clinician context in interpreting complex lab results and maintaining patient trust and safety. This reflects a tension between advancing regulatory flexibility and safeguarding clinical integrity and patient understanding.

Overall, the comments illustrate a complex landscape where the desire to reduce regulatory burden intersects with concerns about maintaining robust protections for patient safety, clinician usability, and trustworthy health IT practices. The debate centers on how to achieve meaningful deregulation without sacrificing essential safeguards that prevent harm and support effective care delivery.

## Top Arguments

### against deregulation

- Deregulation increases financial burden on health institutions and states due to potential safety incidents and errors.
- Removing usability testing and safety design criteria risks increased medication errors, clinician burnout, and patient harm.
- Maintaining AI transparency and clinician review of pathology results is essential to ensure patient safety and trust.
- Safeguards in certification criteria prevent deadly mistakes and inefficiencies in health IT use.

### for deregulation

- Reducing redundant reporting requirements (e.g., RWT Results Reports duplicating SVAP info) decreases developer burden.
- Clarifying and correcting regulatory references avoids confusion and unnecessary compliance efforts.
- Maintaining certain enforcement discretion provisions (e.g., 170.315(f)(5)(ii)) prevents reverting to outdated and burdensome standards.

## Top Recommendations

1. Preserve usability testing and safety-focused certification requirements for EHR systems.
2. Maintain AI transparency requirements and allow clinician review before releasing complex lab results to patients.
3. Clarify and streamline Real World Testing and SVAP reporting requirements to reduce redundancy and developer burden.
4. Correct inaccurate regulatory references to ambulatory and inpatient settings in certification criteria.
5. Avoid deregulation that reduces protections for patients and health care workers.
