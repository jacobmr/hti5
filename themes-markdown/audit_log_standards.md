## Analysis

The public comments on the ONC HTI-5 proposed rule regarding Audit Log Standards & Security reveal a nuanced debate balancing deregulatory ambitions with patient safety and accountability concerns. Proponents of deregulation, primarily health IT companies and advocacy groups, emphasize the need to remove outdated and low-value certification criteria that impose costly burdens without commensurate interoperability benefits. They advocate for modern, API-first integration approaches that streamline system-to-system access, reduce unintentional information blocking, and enable scalable, secure data exchange architectures. These supporters argue that reducing certification complexity allows vendors and integrators to focus on data quality, AI safety, and operational reliability, ultimately advancing health IT innovation and government program support.

Conversely, clinicians and some advocacy organizations express strong opposition or cautious skepticism toward deregulation absent robust safeguards. They highlight that current audit trail and data preservation standards are outdated and insufficient for modern clinical environments, especially given the increasing use of AI and medical devices. These commenters warn that removing certification guardrails prematurely risks undermining patient safety, legal accountability, and data integrity. They call for modernization of audit log standards to explicitly capture AI and device activities, granular metadata, and durable data retention policies before any deregulatory actions proceed. Concerns also focus on preserving transparency, contestability, and equitable patient access, particularly for vulnerable populations.

The dialogue reveals a tension between streamlining regulatory burdens to foster innovation and maintaining enforceable, testable baselines for privacy, security, accessibility, and auditability. While deregulatory supporters prioritize flexibility and modernization aligned with real-world workflows, opponents emphasize the foundational role of audit logs and security standards in ensuring trust, safety, and legal compliance. Both sides recognize the importance of balancing burden reduction with maintaining essential protections, but differ on the sequencing and sufficiency of proposed changes. The comments collectively urge ONC to carefully calibrate deregulatory actions to avoid shifting burdens onto patients or clinicians and to ensure that modernization efforts do not compromise accountability or data integrity.

## Top Arguments

### for deregulation

- Current certification requirements are costly, outdated, and do not improve interoperability effectively.
- API-first, modular integration layers better support government health programs and legacy system integration.
- Clarifying automated system-to-system access reduces unintentional information blocking and aligns policy with modern workflows.
- Reducing certification burden enables focus on data quality, AI model safety, and operational reliability.

### against deregulation

- Current audit trail standards are outdated and insufficient for modern clinical environments, risking patient harm if removed prematurely.
- Removing certification guardrails before updating data creation and preservation standards undermines legal accountability and patient trust.
- Audit trails must capture AI, medical device, and messaging activities to ensure transparency and traceability.
- Data must be preserved durably and not be subject to ephemeral deletion to maintain clinical and legal integrity.
- Removing privacy/security certification criteria without enforceable assurance floors risks weakening baseline protections.

## Top Recommendations

1. Prioritize revision and modernization of audit trail standards before deregulating certification requirements.
2. Finalize proposed certification removals with minimal re-expansion, reinforcing API-first interoperability as the primary policy objective.
3. Retain or replace WCAG conformance with a functionally equivalent, testable accessibility baseline for VDT.
4. Implement explicit, enforceable, and testable assurance floors if privacy/security certification criteria are removed.
5. Include explicit capture of AI, medical device, and messaging activities in audit trails with granular metadata collection.
6. Provide clear guidance affirming automated system-to-system access under information blocking rules.
7. Pause proposed certification changes until robust audit and preservation standards are finalized.

## Key Tensions

- Balancing the need to reduce regulatory burdens and outdated certification criteria versus maintaining robust audit log and security standards to ensure patient safety and legal accountability.
- Sequencing of modernization efforts: whether to deregulate first to enable innovation or to update foundational audit and data preservation standards prior to deregulation.
- Ensuring that deregulatory actions do not shift burdens onto vulnerable patients or clinicians, particularly regarding accessibility, transparency, and contestability.
- Reconciling the push for API-first, automated system-to-system access with the need for enforceable privacy, security, and auditability assurances.
- Determining appropriate enforceable baselines for privacy, security, and accessibility in the absence of existing certification criteria.

## Dominant Position

The theme exhibits a divided sentiment with a slight dominance of cautious opposition or conditional support. While health IT companies and some advocacy groups strongly support deregulatory actions to reduce certification burdens and modernize interoperability, a significant portion of clinicians and patient-focused advocates strongly oppose deregulation without prior modernization of audit log and security standards. Overall, the dominant position favors deregulatory streamlining only if it preserves or replaces critical safeguards for auditability, security, and patient access.
