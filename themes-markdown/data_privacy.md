## Analysis

Data privacy and security standards in the ONC HTI-5 proposed rule elicit nuanced debate reflecting deep concerns about the adequacy of current safeguards in an era of expanded data access, AI-driven analytics, and automated information exchange. The 108 comments on this theme reveal a nearly even split between deregulation advocates and safety-focused opponents, with significant middle ground among those supporting selective modernization while retaining core protections.

Opponents of deregulation emphasize that privacy and security capabilities are not ancillary features but foundational infrastructure for trusted interoperability. They argue that removing certification criteria designed to ensure authentication, access control, encryption, and intrusion detection creates a regulatory vacuum precisely when expanded access and automated use of patient data amplify breach risks. They cite the vulnerability of current health IT systems to ransomware attacks, insider threats, and data exfiltration, and contend that removing certification guardrails accelerates these vulnerabilities. Community health centers and safety-focused organizations specifically highlight that removal of certification 'floors' materially increases operational risk and financial exposure to healthcare organizations responsible for protecting patient data.

A critical concern focuses on the expansion of information blocking exceptions and definitions of 'access' and 'use' to include robotic process automation (RPA) and agentic AI. Opponents worry that codifying unlimited automated access to patient data without explicit security and privacy certification requirements creates conditions for unintended data exposure and misuse. They point to the current inability of EHR vendors to segment sensitive data (e.g., intimate partner violence survivors, substance use disorder patients) as evidence that deregulation will disproportionately harm vulnerable populations.

Proponents of selective deregulation argue that modernizing privacy and security standards to reflect cloud-based architectures, API-based data access, and AI-driven analytics is overdue. They contend that some existing certification criteria reflect outdated assumptions about network perimeters and client-server architectures, and that updated standards should emphasize outcomes (e.g., zero-trust security models, encryption in transit and at rest) rather than prescriptive mechanisms. This perspective acknowledges the importance of privacy and security while arguing that the specific criteria should evolve with technology.

A significant middle-ground position advocates for retaining privacy and security assurance floors while streamlining specific requirements. This perspective supports removing redundant or outdated criteria while maintaining enforceable baselines for authentication, access control, and breach notification. Many professional associations take this approach, supporting burden reduction in areas where standards have matured, while opposing wholesale removal of privacy and security requirements.

## Top Arguments

### for retaining standards

- Privacy and security are foundational infrastructure, not optional features; removal creates vacuum when risks are increasing.
- Current EHR systems cannot adequately segment sensitive patient data; deregulation will disproportionately harm vulnerable populations.
- Removal of certification floors eliminates accountability mechanisms for data breaches and unauthorized access.
- Expanded automated access (RPA, agentic AI) requires explicit security and privacy certification, not deregulation.

### for selective modernization

- Current certification criteria reflect outdated network perimeter assumptions; should emphasize outcomes rather than mechanisms.
- Cloud-based architectures and zero-trust security models require updated standards, not wholesale removal.
- Streamlining redundant requirements can reduce burden while retaining essential assurance floors.
- Privacy by design should be prioritized over prescriptive certifications.

## Top Recommendations

1. Retain enforceable privacy and security assurance floors, particularly for authentication, access control, and breach notification.
2. Modernize certification criteria to reflect cloud architectures, zero-trust security models, and API-based access patterns.
3. Implement explicit requirements for data segmentation to protect sensitive patient populations (IPV survivors, adolescents, pregnant/substance use disorder patients).
4. Require enhanced security and privacy certification for expanded automated access definitions (RPA, agentic AI).
5. If privacy/security certification criteria are removed, establish compensatory, explicit, and enforceable assurance mechanisms.
6. Conduct impact assessments to measure privacy and security implications of HTI-5 before finalization.
7. Retain third-party penetration testing and security audit requirements.

## Key Tensions

- Balancing innovation in data access and AI analytics with foundational privacy and security safeguards.
- Modernization of outdated certification criteria versus wholesale removal of privacy/security assurance floors.
- Vendor burden reduction versus organizational burden of implementing compensatory privacy/security controls.
- Expanded automated access to patient data versus ability to segment sensitive information.
- Deregulation of privacy/security certification simultaneous with expansion of information blocking exceptions.

## Dominant Position

Opposition to broad deregulation: While selective modernization is supported, most commenters oppose removal of privacy and security certification 'floors.' Professional associations and patient safety organizations emphasize that privacy and security are foundational to trusted interoperability, not optional features that can be deregulated.
