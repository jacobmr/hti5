## Analysis

Information blocking rules and enforcement in the ONC HTI-5 proposed rule represent an arena where commercial interests, patient care coordination needs, and regulatory clarity intersect in complex ways. The 70 comments on this theme reveal a more nuanced and commercially-oriented debate than safety-focused themes, with health IT companies and vendors arguing for clarification and narrowing of information blocking exceptions, while health systems and quality advocates express concerns about both enforcement gaps and the potential for deregulation to weaken data sharing guardrails.

Proponents of selective changes to information blocking rules argue that current exceptions (infeasibility, manner, excluded permissible uses) are too broad and are being exploited by vendors and intermediaries to block or delay patient data access in ways inconsistent with the intent of the 21st Century Cures Act. They point to specific cases where pharmacies invoke infeasibility exceptions despite demonstrated technical feasibility, and where health IT vendors use manner exceptions to justify burdensome or delayed data access workflows. This perspective supports narrowing exceptions and providing clearer regulatory guidance to reduce gaming and ensure that information blocking rules achieve their stated purpose of enabling patient access and care coordination.

Health IT companies and integrators, however, argue that current information blocking rules create regulatory uncertainty and stifle innovation in data access and exchange. They contend that clarifying the definitions of 'access,' 'use,' and 'exchange' to explicitly include automation technologies (RPA, agentic AI) and expanding safe harbors for API-based data exchange would reduce ambiguity and enable more efficient, vendor-agnostic data sharing at scale. This perspective emphasizes that modern health IT increasingly relies on automated, system-to-system data flows, and that regulatory language should evolve to accommodate these architectures.

A critical tension exists between expanding definitions of 'use' to include automated access and use (supporting vendor efficiency) and the concerns raised by patient advocates and health system operators about the unintended consequences of codifying unlimited automated access without explicit privacy, security, and governance requirements. Organizations like the American College of Obstetricians & Gynecologists and patient safety advocates worry that expanding information blocking exceptions to include unlimited automated use could enable uncontrolled extraction of sensitive patient data for AI model training, algorithm validation, or commercial purposes without patient awareness or consent.

Professional associations and health system commenters express nuanced positions. Many support narrowing infeasibility and manner exceptions while maintaining enforceable guardrails against information blocking, particularly for essential care coordination workflows. They advocate for strong enforcement mechanisms, including penalties for pharmacies and other entities that abuse exceptions, and for explicit requirements that delegated entities (e.g., pharmacy networks, HIEs) comply with information blocking rules rather than passing responsibility to smaller providers.

The enforcement landscape reveals gaps identified by commenters: pharmacy information blocking is particularly problematic, with many commenters noting that pharmacies routinely invoke infeasibility exceptions despite demonstrated technical feasibility of direct integration. Some suggest that stronger enforcement mechanisms, potentially including reduced reimbursement or removal of preferred network status, are necessary to ensure pharmacies comply with information blocking rules rather than prioritizing cost avoidance through exception claims.

## Top Arguments

### for clarification and narrowing

- Current information blocking exceptions are exploited to justify unjustified data access delays and information blocking.
- Pharmacies routinely invoke infeasibility exceptions despite demonstrated technical feasibility of direct integration.
- Clearer regulatory guidance and narrower exceptions would reduce gaming and enable genuine patient data access.
- Expanding definitions to include automation technologies would enable efficient, system-to-system data exchange at scale.

### for caution or opposition

- Expanding automated access definitions without explicit privacy and security safeguards risks uncontrolled data extraction.
- Information blocking exceptions should be narrowed, not expanded; enforcement gaps should be closed.
- Pharmacies and other delegated entities abuse exceptions; stronger enforcement is needed, not deregulation.
- Automated AI model training and validation on patient data requires explicit consent and governance, not blanket allowance.

## Top Recommendations

1. Explicitly prohibit pharmacies from invoking infeasibility and manner exceptions when technical feasibility is demonstrated.
2. Establish disincentives for Medicare Part D pharmacies including reduced dispensing fees, loss of preferred network status, and mandatory contractual disclosures of information blocking practices.
3. Provide clear regulatory guidance affirming that automated system-to-system API access is an essential, non-blocking use case under information blocking rules.
4. Finalize narrowing of information blocking exceptions with specific guidance on permissible uses and clear triggers for enforcement.
5. Require delegated entities (pharmacy networks, HIEs) to contractually obligate their members to comply with information blocking rules.
6. Implement public reporting on information blocking enforcement actions and penalties to deter future violations.
7. Establish clear definitions of 'access,' 'use,' and 'exchange' that account for modern automated data flows while preserving privacy and consent protections.

## Key Tensions

- Clarifying information blocking rules to reduce ambiguity versus maintaining guardrails against vendor misuse.
- Expanding automated access definitions to enable efficient data exchange versus preserving privacy and consent protections.
- Enforcement against pharmacy information blocking versus vendor arguments about compliance burden.
- Patient access to complete medical records versus vendor convenience in data sharing.
- Narrowing infeasibility and manner exceptions versus legitimate cases where alternative manners are technically infeasible.

## Dominant Position

Mixed but leaning toward selective clarification with enforcement emphasis: While health IT vendors support clearer definitions and expanded safe harbors for automation, health systems and patient advocates emphasize that information blocking exceptions should be narrowed, not expanded, and that enforcement gaps (particularly regarding pharmacies) should be closed. The dominant sentiment is that clarification is needed, but not in the direction of expanding vendor loopholes.
