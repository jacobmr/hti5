## Analysis

The public comments on the ONC HTI-5 proposed rule under the theme of FHIR & Interoperability Standards reveal a nuanced landscape of stakeholder perspectives balancing deregulation with the imperative to maintain robust interoperability guardrails. Many commenters recognize the value of streamlining certification requirements to reduce administrative burden and foster innovation, particularly through the adoption of FHIR-based standards. This shift is seen as critical to enabling scalable, secure, and modern health IT systems that better support patient care and government service delivery. Health IT companies, such as Trilogy Innovation, strongly support the deregulatory approach, emphasizing that removing outdated certification criteria will free resources for innovation and improve mission impact without compromising technical outcomes.

However, there is also significant caution expressed by clinicians and other stakeholders concerned about the potential weakening of interoperability standards that underpin critical programs like the Medicare Shared Savings Program (MSSP) and patient-centered care models. These commenters urge the preservation of enforceable interoperability guardrails and the narrowing of information blocking exceptions to prevent misuse that could undermine data sharing and care coordination. They highlight the risk that deregulation might shift compliance burdens from vendors to providers or accountable care organizations (ACOs), potentially eroding data integrity and trust.

A recurring theme is the call for stronger enforcement and accountability mechanisms, especially regarding pharmacies and delegated entities. For example, WENO Exchange LLC advocates for prohibiting pharmacies from abusing information blocking exceptions and mandates alternative DEA-compliant access methods to safeguard patient care. Similarly, David Rocha emphasizes the need for CMS and ONC to require delegated entities to adopt FHIR APIs and collaborate with accrediting bodies to enforce interoperability in hospitals and public health agencies. These recommendations underscore the importance of not only setting standards but also ensuring their practical adoption and enforcement across diverse health IT stakeholders.

Overall, the comments reflect a tension between advancing deregulation to promote innovation and maintaining sufficient regulatory guardrails to protect interoperability, patient care, and compliance integrity. There is broad agreement on the centrality of FHIR-based standards as the foundation for future interoperability efforts, but divergent views on how aggressively certification requirements should be relaxed and how enforcement should be structured to prevent information blocking and ensure equitable access to health information.

## Top Arguments

### for deregulation

- Removing low-value certification criteria reduces administrative burden without sacrificing technical outcomes.
- Focus on FHIR-based standards supports secure, scalable, and interoperable health IT systems.
- Reduced certification burden allows reallocation of resources toward innovation and mission impact.

### against deregulation or caution

- Reducing certification requirements too aggressively risks re-empowering vendor behaviors that hinder interoperability and patient care.
- Narrowing information blocking exceptions with clear operational guidance is essential to prevent misuse and support care coordination.
- Deregulation should not shift compliance burdens from vendors to providers or ACOs, which undermines data integrity and trust.
- Pharmacies abuse infeasibility and manner exceptions despite demonstrated technical feasibility, requiring stricter guardrails.
- Delegated entities must be held accountable to adopt FHIR APIs to enable full enforcement of CMS rules and interoperability.

## Top Recommendations

1. Explicitly prohibit pharmacies from invoking infeasibility and manner exceptions when technical feasibility is demonstrated.
2. Mandate alternative DEA-compliant retrieval methods if direct integration is lacking, denying infeasibility exceptions otherwise.
3. Update regulatory frameworks (e.g., 45 CFR Part 156) to include FHIR API compliance obligations for delegated entities.
4. Collaborate with accrediting bodies to include auditable FHIR interoperability requirements in hospital accreditation.
5. Preserve core certification criteria essential to interoperability and MSSP compliance while narrowing information blocking exceptions with practical guidance.
6. Monitor and publicly report on state and territorial public health agencies' live FHIR endpoints to encourage interoperability progress.

## Key Tensions

- Balancing deregulation to reduce certification burden with the need to maintain enforceable interoperability standards and guardrails.
- Ensuring that deregulation does not shift compliance burdens from vendors to providers or ACOs, which could undermine data integrity.
- Reconciling the push for innovation and agility in health IT with the necessity of preventing information blocking and misuse of exceptions.
- Determining the appropriate level of enforcement and accountability for pharmacies and delegated entities in adopting FHIR APIs and interoperable practices.
- Aligning modernization of interoperability regulations with updates to related legal frameworks such as HIPAA and Stark Law to support value-based care.

## Dominant Position

The dominant sentiment among commenters is cautiously supportive of deregulation efforts that streamline certification and promote FHIR-based interoperability standards, provided that strong guardrails and enforcement mechanisms remain in place to prevent information blocking and protect patient care. While health IT companies tend to strongly support deregulatory actions, clinicians and other stakeholders express mixed or neutral positions emphasizing the need for balance between reducing burden and preserving interoperability integrity.
