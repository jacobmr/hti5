# Clinical Data Completeness

**Key:** `clinical_data_completeness`
**Comments:** 73

## Analysis

Clinical data completeness in the ONC HTI-5 proposed rule addresses fundamental questions about what information clinicians need in certified health IT systems to deliver effective, coordinated, and accountable care. The 73 comments on this theme reveal strong concerns from caregiver advocates, long-term and post-acute care (LTPAC) providers, and clinicians that proposed removal of specific data elements will erode care quality for vulnerable populations and care settings.

The most substantive concern focuses on standardized caregiver data elements and dyadic care planning capabilities. Caregivers play critical roles in patient outcomes, medication adherence, and post-acute care transitions, yet healthcare IT systems have historically rendered caregiving invisible or difficult to document and coordinate. LTPAC providers, nursing facilities, home health agencies, and clinician-advocates argue that standardized caregiver identification, strain assessment, and care coordination data elements are essential infrastructure—not luxury features. They note that removing regulatory requirements for these data elements signals institutional deprioritization and creates conditions for vendors to eliminate or marginalize caregiver-focused workflows, particularly in long-term care settings where caregiver coordination is central to patient safety and quality of life.

LTPAC providers further emphasize that removing USCDI data element requirements threatens to leave behind already-disadvantaged care settings. Long-term care, skilled nursing, behavioral health, and rural providers historically lag in health IT adoption and capability. These commenters argue that uniform adoption of updated USCDI standards provides an opportunity to ensure LTPAC systems capture the data needed for coordinated transitions, medication reconciliation, functional assessment, and safety monitoring. Deregulation, they contend, would likely result in two-tiered systems: sophisticated, feature-rich EHRs in major health systems, and stripped-down systems in LTPAC and underresourced settings, perpetuating fragmentation and safety risks during care transitions.

Additionally, commenters emphasize the importance of clinical data elements that reflect contemporary understanding of what drives health outcomes and safety. These include physical activity assessment, nutritional status, comprehensive medication reconciliation at transitions of care, and detailed functional assessment. They argue that adopting updated USCDI versions (v4 or v6) represents an opportunity to modernize data standards around evidence-based clinical needs rather than regress by removing protective data elements.

Proponents of deregulation counter that prescriptive data element requirements increase vendor burden without demonstrated return on investment, and that market demand and organizational priorities will ensure important data fields are retained. However, this position is challenged by commenters who note that vendor decisions about which data elements to prioritize have historically reflected the interests of the largest customers—typically EHR-dominant hospital systems—rather than the needs of LTPAC providers, clinics serving vulnerable populations, or underresourced settings with less negotiating power.

A middle-ground position supports retention of essential clinical data requirements while acknowledging the need for streamlined, outcome-focused standards. These commenters advocate for clarity about which data elements are essential to regulatory compliance versus discretionary, and for updating outdated definitions to reflect contemporary clinical practice and safety needs.

Additionally, commenters raise concerns about specific data elements that support inclusive care and reduce clinical blind spots. Sexual orientation and gender identity (SOGI) data fields, for instance, are noted by clinicians as essential for identifying disparities in outcomes, for culturally competent care delivery, and for recognizing higher risk for substance use disorders, mental health conditions, and discriminatory treatment. While not the primary focus of comments on data completeness, SOGI and similar inclusive data elements are referenced as examples of how comprehensive, standardized data elements improve clinical decision-making and care quality.

## Top Arguments

### for maintaining or expanding standards

- Standardized caregiver data elements are essential infrastructure for coordinated, family-centered care, especially in long-term care.
- Removing regulatory requirements for data elements signals institutional deprioritization and enables vendor elimination of clinically important fields.
- LTPAC and underresourced providers will be disadvantaged if deregulation results in two-tiered EHR systems with divergent capabilities.
- Clinical data completeness supports medication safety, functional assessment, and care transition coordination in LTPAC.
- Adopting updated USCDI versions (v4 or v6) provides opportunity for modernization based on evidence; deregulation risks regression.
- Removing data element requirements undermines clinicians' ability to recognize and respond to patient needs across diverse populations.

### for deregulation or streamlining

- Prescriptive data element requirements increase vendor burden without demonstrated clinical benefit.
- Market demand and organizational priorities will ensure important data fields are retained without regulatory mandate.
- Outcome-focused standards are preferable to prescriptive data element lists.
- Vendors should have flexibility to innovate on data capture and integration methods.

## Top Recommendations

1. Maintain regulatory requirements for caregiver identification, strain assessment, and dyadic care planning in certified systems.
2. Adopt USCDI version 4 (or preferably v6) to include updated clinical data elements for physical activity, nutritional status, and comprehensive medication management.
3. Establish baseline data element sets that reflect contemporary clinical practice and evidence-based care needs across diverse care settings.
4. Ensure that data element standards support interoperability and care coordination specifically for LTPAC, behavioral health, and primary care providers.
5. Maintain regulatory clarity about essential data elements while allowing vendor flexibility in implementation and user interface design.
6. Conduct impact assessments on LTPAC and underresourced providers to ensure deregulation does not create two-tiered systems with divergent data capture capabilities.
7. Update data element definitions to reflect inclusive care principles and contemporary understanding of what clinical data supports high-quality, equitable outcomes.
8. Establish regular review cycles (3-5 years) for USCDI standards to incorporate evidence-based clinical needs and emerging data requirements.

## Key Tensions

- Vendor flexibility and burden reduction versus the need for standardized data elements that enable coordinated, equitable care.
- Two-tiered health IT systems (sophisticated in major systems, stripped-down in LTPAC) versus universal access to capabilities essential for safety and coordination.
- Prescriptive data standards versus outcome-focused flexibility in implementation.
- Market-driven priorities (largest customers) versus needs of underresourced and LTPAC providers.
- Innovation in data capture methods versus the stability of standardized, interoperable data elements.

## Dominant Position

Significant opposition to deregulation of data element standards, particularly for caregiver coordination and LTPAC-specific clinical data. LTPAC providers, caregiver advocates, and clinicians argue that standardized data element requirements ensure that underresourced care settings are not left behind, and that removing standards would create two-tiered systems with divergent capabilities. A middle-ground position supports streamlined, outcome-focused standards while maintaining essential data requirements for coordination, safety, and care quality. The dominant concern is not complexity of standards, but ensuring that modernization enhances—rather than degrades—care coordination across diverse settings and populations.
