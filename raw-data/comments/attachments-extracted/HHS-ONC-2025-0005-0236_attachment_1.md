Dear Dr. Keane,

On behalf of the AI Healthcare Coalition (AIHC), thank you for the opportunity to comment on Health
Data, Technology, and Interoperability: ASTP/ONC Deregulatory Actions To Unleash Prosperity (RIN
0955-AA09)) (“the Proposed Rule”). We appreciate the Assistant Secretary for Technology Policy
and Office of the National Coordinator for Health Information Technology’s (ASTP/ONC) efforts to
reduce regulatory burdens. Specifically, we are supportive of ASTP/ONC’s ongoing work to ensure
that its existing regulations do not impede access to U.S. Food & Drug Administration (FDA)-
authorized artificial intelligence (AI) medical services.

AIHC was founded in 2019 and convenes healthcare AI innovators to advocate for patient access to
safe, ethically developed AI medical services. Our members have created, or are in the process of
developing, AI services (or devices) that require FDA market authorization and oversight. Our
founding membership includes the first-ever autonomous AI innovator to obtain FDA authorization,
as well as creators who have obtained the first-ever Medicare reimbursement for their respective AI
medical services.

AIHC supports ASTP/ONC’s decision to rescind overly burdensome regulations for AI
innovators.

As we noted in our initial comments to the Health Data, Technology, and Interoperability:
Certification Program Updates, Algorithm Transparency, and Information Sharing Proposed Rule
(RIN 0955-AA03) (HTI-1 Rule) ASTP/ONC’s existing definition of predictive decision support
intervention (DSI) is overly broad and imposes duplicative requirements for FDA-approved medical
devices. These AI systems already undergo rigorous review by the FDA, and DSI source attribute and
intervention risk management reporting requirements did not permit reference to FDA submission
and approval in lieu of reporting or exclude FDA-approved devices as recommended by AIHC.

      I.      Predictive DSI Source Attributes

We support ASTP/ONC’s proposal to remove the requirements of 45 CFR 170.315(b)(11)(iv) and (v).
These information requirements exceed the requirements of other federal government programs
that utilize AI or Machine Learning (ML) services.

When these requirements were proposed, we noted our opposition, specifically that the
requirements are duplicative and, in general, lack the rigor of the FDA’s market authorization review
and ongoing oversight measures for AI/ML tools. The application of the requirements is inappropriate
for FDA-authorized, rigorously validated AI/ML systems, particularly those which are considered
Software as a Medical Device (SaMD). Health information technology (HIT) system users should be
able to rely on FDA authorization when utilizing an FDA-cleared AI/ML tool or system. The imposition
of additional requirements is duplicative and burdensome for HIT system users, especially clinicians
in underserved or rural areas.

    II.     Predictive DSI Interventional Risk Management

We also thank ASTP/ONC for proposing to remove the intervention risk management requirements
in 45 CFR 170.315(b)(vi). As we noted in our comments on the HTI-1 proposed rule, these standards
require certified health IT developers who enable or interface with predictive DSI’s to engage in
certain risk management practices. Specifically, such certified health IT developers are required to
conduct risk analysis of potential risks and adverse impacts associated with predictive DSI for the
following characteristics: validity, reliability, robustness, fairness, intelligibility, safety, security, and
privacy. The HTI-1 Final rule declined to mention FDA authorization in its description of safety
analysis. The HTI-1 Rule also outlined risk mitigation measures and requirements around DSI
governance. This requires HIT developers to keep detailed documentation of such risk mitigation
measures.

The FDA is best positioned to assess the safety and efficacy of AI/ML systems and risk. The FDA
employs experts in medical and clinical safety and is uniquely positioned to draw conclusions and
develop guidelines for the safe and appropriate use of AI and ML tools. Rescinding ASTP/ONC’s
requirements, as outlined in the Proposed Rule, will remove burdensome regulations that increase
costs and decrease efficiencies for patients, clinicians, and AI innovators.

We appreciate ASTP/ONC’s efforts to remove burdensome regulations and facilitate medical
innovation. If you have any questions about our comments, please contact me at
cybil.roehrenbeck@ai-coalition.org.

Kind regards,
