AdventHealth appreciates the opportunity to comment on the proposed rule entitled Health
Data, Technology, and Interoperability: ASTP/ONC Deregulatory Actions to Unleash Prosperity .
As a faith-based, nonprofit health system with more than 50 hospitals, freestanding emergency
departments, and outpatient facilities across multiple states, AdventHealth is deeply committed
to advancing secure, interoperable, and patient-centered use of health information technology.

We support the Administration’s goal of reassessing existing regulatory frameworks to reduce
unnecessary burden, improve flexibility, and foster innovation across the health IT ecosystem.
AdventHealth shares ASTP/ONC’s interest in ensuring that federal policy keeps pace with
evolving technology while continuing to promote trust, safety, and interoperability. Our
comments are offered in that spirit and are intended to provide practical, provider-informed
feedback on how these proposals may operate in real-world care environments.

Modernizing Privacy and Security Expectations Within the Certification Program

AdventHealth appreciates ASTP/ONC’s acknowledgement that enterprise-wide privacy and
security obligations cannot be fully addressed through health IT certification alone. We agree
that certification should not be viewed as a substitute for comprehensive organizational
cybersecurity governance or compliance with HIPAA and other applicable laws.

At the same time, we encourage the Agency to carefully consider the practical
implications of fully removing the privacy and security certification criteria,
including requirements related to authentication, access control, audit capabilities,
encryption, and secure connectivity. While certification does not ensure compliance, these
criteria have historically provided a consistent baseline of functionality that providers rely upon
when deploying certified health IT products across diverse care settings.

In practice, health care providers remain responsible for safeguarding electronic health
information, responding to cybersecurity incidents, and maintaining patient trust regardless of
certification changes. The underlying capabilities addressed by the existing criteria remain
operationally necessary. Removing them from certification may introduce greater variability
across products and increase the need for providers to independently evaluate, procure, and
integrate additional security solutions to meet unchanged legal and operational obligations.

This shift may be manageable for large health systems with dedicated cybersecurity
teams, but it could present meaningful challenges for smaller hospitals, rural
providers, and physician practices that rely on certification as an indicator of
baseline functionality. Increased fragmentation of security capabilities across vendors may
also complicate interoperability and incident response efforts.

AdventHealth supports ASTP/ONC’s continued exploration of more flexible, fit-for-
purpose approaches to privacy and security, particularly as data exchange
increasingly occurs through APIs and third-party applications. Preserving a limited set
of foundational, outcome-oriented expectations, such as audit and monitoring capabilities, may
help strike a balance between flexibility and the shared goal of maintaining system integrity and
patient trust as interoperability expands.

Supporting Responsible Adoption of Decision Support Interventions and Artificial
Intelligence

AdventHealth welcomes ASTP/ONC’s thoughtful engagement with the evolving role of predictive
technologies and artificial intelligence in health care. We agree that the certification program
should avoid rigid or overly prescriptive requirements that could inhibit innovation or fail to
reflect the rapid pace of technological change.

As predictive decision support tools become more integrated into clinical workflows, providers
and clinicians increasingly rely on information that enables them to understand how these tools
function, how they are intended to be used, and how they should be monitored over time.
While AdventHealth understands the Agency’s interest in reducing administrative
burden, we encourage continued attention to the importance of maintaining
appropriate transparency for tools that influence clinical decision-making but are
not currently subject to FDA oversight. Transparency may include disclosure of
training data sets, appropriate measures to mitigate risks, and instructions around
use and monitoring of providers in the loop.

From a provider perspective, access to high-level information regarding intended use, validation
approaches, and monitoring practices supports responsible adoption and clinical confidence. In
the absence of such information, clinicians may be hesitant to rely on otherwise
promising tools, particularly when vendors disclaim responsibility for clinical
outcomes. This dynamic may inadvertently slow adoption of innovative technologies, even
when they have the potential to improve care quality, efficiency, or clinician experience.

These considerations are particularly relevant for smaller hospitals, critical access facilities, and
physician practices that lack the resources to independently validate predictive tools. A flexible,
light-touch framework that preserves access to meaningful information (without imposing rigid
documentation requirements) could help ensure that innovation is both encouraged and
responsibly integrated into patient care.

AdventHealth believes there is an opportunity for ASTP/ONC to continue supporting
innovation while also reinforcing provider confidence by maintaining a baseline
expectation that predictive decision support tools are accompanied by information
sufficient to support safe and informed use.

      Advancing Patient Access While Preserving Secure Information Exchange

AdventHealth strongly supports ASTP/ONC’s efforts to promote timely patient access to
electronic health information and reduce unnecessary barriers to data sharing. We appreciate
Page 3 of 5
the Agency’s work to refine and clarify information blocking policies in ways that support these
goals.

As information blocking policy continues to evolve, we encourage the Agency to maintain clarity
around the role of reasonable security and identity-assurance practices, particularly when
patients authorize access through third-party applications which are not otherwise subject to
the HIPAA Privacy and Security Rule. In operational environments, verification and confirmation
steps are often necessary to ensure that information is disclosed accurately, securely, and to
the intended recipient.

These practices are not designed to impede access but to protect patients from misdirected
disclosures, fraud, and unauthorized use of sensitive health information. As data exchange
increasingly extends beyond traditional provider-to-provider channels, maintaining this balance
becomes even more important to sustaining patient trust.

Clear distinctions between inappropriate information blocking and legitimate safeguards will
help providers continue to support patient access while responsibly managing risk in complex
technical environments.

                                             Conclusion

AdventHealth appreciates ASTP/ONC’s commitment to modernizing health IT policy and its
openness to stakeholder input. We share the Agency’s goal of fostering interoperability,
innovation, and patient empowerment while ensuring that regulatory changes align with the
realities of delivering care across diverse clinical settings.

We look forward to continued collaboration with ASTP/ONC as these policies are refined and
implemented and would welcome further dialogue on how best to balance flexibility, security,
and responsible innovation.
