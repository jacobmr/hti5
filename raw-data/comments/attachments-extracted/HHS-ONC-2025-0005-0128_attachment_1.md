The College of Healthcare Information Management Executives (CHIME) appreciates the
opportunity to comment on the Department of Health and Human Services’ (HHS) Assistant
Secretary for Technology Policy (ASTP)/Office of the National Coordinator for Health Information
Technology (ONC) (collectively, ASTP/ONC) Health Data, Technology, and Interoperability:
ASTP/ONC Deregulatory Actions to Unleash Prosperity (HTI-5) Proposed Rule as published in
the Federal Register on December 29, 2025 (Vol. 90, No. 245).

Background

CHIME is an executive organization dedicated to serving chief information officers (CIOs), chief
medical information officers (CMIOs), chief nursing information officers (CNIOs), chief
innovation officers (CIOs), chief digital officers (CDOs) and other senior healthcare IT leaders.
With thousands of individual members, and hundreds of CHIME Foundation healthcare IT
business and professional service firm members, CHIME and its communities provide a highly
interactive, trusted environment enabling senior professional and industry leaders to collaborate,
exchange best practices, address professional development needs, and advocate for the
effective use of information management to improve the health and care in the communities
they serve.

Key Recommendations

CHIME appreciates the opportunity to offer input on the Health Data, Technology, and
Interoperability: ASTP/ONC Deregulatory Actions to Unleash Prosperity (HTI-5) Proposed Rule.
As leaders in health IT, CHIME and our members believe the following areas are
important for ASTP/ONC to consider when finalizing the HTI-5 proposed rule.

CHIME offers the following overarching recommendations:

                                                                                                1

• ASTP/ONC should ensure deregulatory actions result in measurable burden reduction
for healthcare providers.
• Implementation timelines should be no shorter than 24–36 months following publication
of a final rule.
• ASTP/ONC should retain baseline privacy and security Certification criteria under
§ 170.315(d) and the associated Privacy and Security Certification Framework under
§ 170.550(h).
• ASTP/ONC should not finalize removal of transitions of care criteria referencing patient
matching until alternative standards are adopted.
• Policymaking should account for cumulative regulatory burden on healthcare providers
across federal programs.

While CHIME appreciates ASTP/ONC’s stated deregulatory intent, we urge ASTP/ONC to
ensure that any regulatory modifications meaningfully reduce burden for hospitals and
healthcare systems without weakening essential interoperability, patient safety, or cybersecurity
safeguards. Providers remain subject to extensive statutory and regulatory obligations under the
21st Century Cures Act and its implementing regulations, the Health Insurance Portability and
Accountability Act (HIPAA), Medicare payment programs, and related authorities. Deregulation
that merely shifts compliance burdens from technology vendors to providers will not
achieve the Administration’s goals of flexibility, innovation, and patient-centered care.

Reducing regulatory burden for healthcare providers is critical to removing barriers to
data access and exchange, unlocking the full potential of recent healthcare innovations
that improve patient outcomes, and accelerating progress toward a truly patient-centered
health system. Providers should not bear disproportionate responsibility for the operational,
financial, and compliance challenges associated with advancing health IT.

General Comments & Detailed Recommendations

Standards and Implementation Specifications for Health Information Technology

ASTP/ONC is proposing – in some cases, to remove standards consistent with a certification
criterion transition period until January 1, 2027. And in “very limited circumstances”, proposing
to “retain a standard referenced in a certification criterion proposed for removal for purposes of
health IT interoperability alignment.” HHS is further proposing to remove standards that are
outdated and no longer referenced in the Certification Program.

CHIME recommends that ASTP/ONC implement reasonable timelines that take into account not
only the timelines required for both EHR vendor development and provider training and
implementation, but also one that factors in workforce shortages and other competing
mandates, including existing ones and forthcoming ones.

Removal of the criteria as proposed will require that our members develop guidelines, policies,
procedures and gather the data required to meet the requirements along with their vendor
partners – work that takes time and must be done in a manner that does not jeopardize patient
safety. Once that work is complete, the decisions need to be implemented and communicated
across their organizations. This work will take a minimum of 18 to 24 months.

CHIME recommends implementation timelines of no less than 24 to 36 months following
publication of a final rule. This timeframe better reflects vendor development cycles, provider

Certification Criteria for Health Information Technology

2. Care Coordination Certification Criteria
   A. Transitions of Care
   CHIME is a founding member of Patient ID Now, a coalition of more than 50 healthcare
   organizations representing a wide range of healthcare stakeholders, including patients,
   physicians, health information professionals, health IT companies, and public health, committed
   to advancing a nationwide strategy to address patient identification and matching. The coalition
   is pleased to have the opportunity to contribute to improving interoperability and health
   information technology within the healthcare ecosystem. The Patient ID Now coalition offers the
   following feedback in response to the request for information.

As elaborated in our comments below, the Patient ID Now coalition recommends HHS not
finalize the proposed removal of §170.315(b)(1) Transitions of Care of the Health IT Certification
Program (Certification Program) in order to avoid removing the only criteria that references
patient matching within the Certification Program until other patient matching criteria is in place.

In this section, it is proposed to revise the “transitions of care” certification criteria by “remov[ing]
the requirements in…in § 170.315(b)(1)(iii) “create”.” The “create” section states that it enables
“a user to create a transition of care/referral summary formatted in accordance with the standard
specified in § 170.205(a)(3), (4), and (5) using the Continuity of Care Document, Referral Note,
and (inpatient setting only) Discharge Summary document templates that includes, at a
minimum:…
(G) Patient matching data. First name, last name, previous name, middle name
(including middle initial), suffix, date of birth, current address, phone number, and sex.
The following constraints apply:
(1) Date of birth constraint.
(i) The year, month and day of birth must be present for a date of birth. The
technology must include a null value when the date of birth is unknown.
(ii) Optional. When the hour, minute, and second are associated with a date of
birth the technology must demonstrate that the correct time zone offset is
included.

(2) Phone number constraint. Represent phone number (home, business, cell)
in accordance with the standards adopted in § 170.207(q)(1). All phone numbers
must be included when multiple phone numbers are present.
(3) Sex Constraint: Represent sex with the standard adopted in § 170.207(n)(1)
up to and including December 31, 2025; or with the standard adopted in §
170.207(n)(2).”

The inclusion of patient matching criteria within the continuity of care document (CCD) improves
patient safety and security by reducing instances of patient misidentification. Further, it is the
only section within the certification criterion that references patient matching.

While the United States Core Data for Interoperability (USCDI) has been integral to the
standardization of available data elements within certified health IT products, many elements
within the current version of USCDI (e.g., first name, last name, and date of birth) do not have
standards that dictate how these data should be entered, causing variation across systems and
challenges in data usability and care coordination.

To address patient matching, the Patient ID Now coalition recommends adopting provisions
found in HR 2002, the Patient Matching and Transparency in Certified Health IT Act of 2025 or
the MATCH IT Act of 2025, including a provision to define and adopt a minimum data set
needed to reach a 99.9% patient match rate, and then create, update, or adopt data standards,
(including an established industry standard, if available) to ensure demographic elements are
entered in a standardized format.

However, until USCDI is updated to facilitate better patient matching, or an alternate criterion is
included around patient matching, the Patient ID Now coalition recommends not removing the
only certification criterion that specifically references patient matching.

Patient Safety and Privacy Concerns Stemming from Patient Misidentification
Without the ability of clinicians to correctly connect a patient with their medical record, lives have
been lost and medical errors have needlessly occurred. These are situations that could have
been avoided had patients been accurately identified and matched with their records. This
problem is so dire that one of the nation’s leading patient safety organizations, the ECRI
Institute, has named patient misidentification as a recurring top ten threat to patient safety.

Patient misidentification also creates financial burdens for patients, clinicians, and institutions.
The expense of repeated medical care due to duplicate records costs an average of $1,950 per
patient inpatient stay, and over $1,700 per emergency department visit. Thirty five percent of all
denied claims result from inaccurate patient identification, costing the average hospital $2.5
million and the US healthcare system over $6.7 billion annually. 1 In a survey conducted by the
Patient ID Now coalition, 72 percent of respondents agreed that there are delays in billing and
reimbursement due to inaccurate patient information, and 70 percent indicated that patients
undergo or receive duplicative or unnecessary testing or services due to difficulties in managing
patient identities. 2

Patient misidentification also contributes to patient privacy concerns. Specifically, the risk of
overlaid records can result in a patient having access to another patient’s health information,

4. Privacy and Security Certification Criteria
   ASTP/ONC is proposing to remove all the privacy and security certification criteria in
   § 170.315(d) and the associated Privacy and Security Certification Framework under
   § 170.550(h) as of the effective date of a subsequent final rule. CHIME strongly opposes this
   proposal, as eliminating these safeguards would weaken baseline security expectations
   across the healthcare ecosystem and increase operational and compliance risk for
   providers.

Healthcare delivery organizations (HDOs) operate in a threat environment that is widely
recognized as both target-rich and resource-constrained, facing persistent and increasingly
sophisticated cyber threats while simultaneously managing significant operational and financial
pressures. The existing certification criteria serves as an important standard that supports
consistent, security-informed implementation of health IT across the ecosystem. Eliminating
these criteria would remove a critical layer of assurance at a time when providers are being
asked to defend against escalating ransomware, supply chain vulnerabilities, and nation-state–
level threats.

The privacy and security certification criteria function as a baseline market assurance
mechanism. We agree with ASTP/ONC that eliminating that baseline does not relieve
providers of their independent obligations under the HIPAA Security Rule. However, it
would shift validation responsibility to hospitals and health systems without
corresponding regulatory relief and reduction in liability exposure.

Absent clear and consistent privacy and security certification expectations, hospitals and health
systems will be forced to redirect limited capital and operational resources toward additional
security validation, compensating controls, and vendor risk management activities to ensure
compliance with applicable regulatory requirements. This diversion of resources will
necessarily come at the expense of investments in innovation, digital transformation,
and advanced capabilities that support improved care delivery, patient engagement, and
health system modernization.

In effect, the proposal risks shifting costs rather than reducing burden, forcing providers
to reallocate funding from forward-looking innovation initiatives to foundational privacy
and security functions that certified technologies should – and currently do – already
support. This shift would undermine the Administration’s broader policy goals to
accelerate innovation while placing additional strain on providers operating within
already constrained margins.

From a provider perspective, the existing framework helps establish a common floor for privacy
and security functionality that supports trust, interoperability, and operational stability across a
highly interconnected ecosystem. Removing that floor would create fragmentation, increase risk
exposure, and ultimately undermine the critical work being done to strengthen the sector’s
cybersecurity posture. In an environment of escalating supply chain attacks and third-party

application integration, baseline certification standards serve as an essential risk allocation
mechanism between developers and regulated providers.

ASTP/ONC states that “there remains a disconnect between health IT certified to the privacy
and security certification criteria under the Certification Program and the implementation and
compliance responsibilities of most purchasers and users of certified health IT with the HIPAA
Privacy and Security Rule requirements. This disconnect has led to the introduction of
unnecessary regulatory burden and duplication, as well as unwarranted opportunity costs for
innovation.”

CHIME strongly disagrees with this assertion, as it does not reflect the operational
realities facing healthcare providers nor the important role that certification plays in
supporting compliance, risk management, and secure technology adoption. Rather than
creating duplication, these criteria help reduce variability, streamline due diligence, and provide
reasonable assurance that core privacy and security capabilities are present and capable of
being operationalized as part of a provider’s broad enterprise security architecture.

Removing the privacy and security certification criteria risks weakening alignment between
certified health IT capabilities and providers’ broader security and risk management obligations,
introducing unnecessary variability and uncertainty across how technologies are configured,
integrated, deployed, and maintained within provider environments. Differences in system
configuration, security settings, workflow integration, and interfaces with other technologies can
materially affect the effectiveness of security controls and overall risk posture, making consistent
certification baselines critical to providers.

The assertion that certification introduces unnecessary burden also overlooks the significant
resource constraints under which many HDOs operate. In the absence of standardized
certification criteria, providers would be required to independently assess and validate the
privacy and security functionality of each product, increasing administrative burden,
procurement complexity, and compliance risk. This shift would effectively transfer responsibility
for baseline security validation from technology developers to healthcare providers, many of
whom lack the technical and financial resources to perform such evaluations at scale. As a
result, eliminating the criteria would undoubtedly increase – rather than reduce –
regulatory and operational burden for healthcare providers that depend on their EHR
vendors, as technology suppliers, to deliver validated baseline privacy and security
assurances rather than having to independently establish them.

ASTP/ONC states that certified health IT has merely been “linked” to the Medicare Promoting
Interoperability (PI) Program and the MIPS Promoting Interoperability performance category and
that participating providers are only required to “have” certified health IT, suggesting that
providers may adopt certified capabilities they do not use because they may prefer non-certified
tools to meet their security needs. CHIME disagrees with this belief in provider preference of
“non-certified security health IT products.” As peer-reviewed research and our members note,
“Third parties are a frequent target of malicious actors because they may have more access
than needed operationally, and often have access points into many HDOs (hack one system,
breach many).” 3

Further, this characterization does not reflect how HDOs operationalize security controls in
practice, nor how certified technology functions within provider compliance frameworks. As a
practical and operational matter, hospitals and health systems overwhelmingly rely on the
privacy and security functionality embedded within their core EHR platforms – many of which
are certified – as foundational components of their enterprise security architecture.

These capabilities are not merely possessed; they are actively configured, enabled, and
operationalized to support access controls, audit logging, authentication, and other safeguards
that directly inform and support the security risk analysis required under the HIPAA Security
Rule. The suggestion that providers routinely adopt but do not use certified privacy and security
capabilities is inconsistent with both operational reality and practical risk management practices.

Capabilities such as multifactor authentication, identity management integration, and access
controls – closely tied to the technical safeguard standards at § 164.312 – do not always
seamlessly integrate into enterprise environments. This proposal effectively transfers
responsibilities that are currently shared between providers, vendors and the
Certification Program onto providers alone, without reducing the requirements and
expectations placed upon them by regulators (e.g., HHS’ Office for Civil Rights (OCR).

For example, audit logging and audit trail functionality are foundational to compliance with the
HIPAA Security Rule’s technical safeguard standards 4 and to incident response obligations.
Without reliable logs, providers cannot conduct meaningful security risk analyses, investigate
potential breaches, determine scope, or demonstrate compliance to OCR or their cyber
insurance carriers. Removing or weakening certification expectations for audit functionality
would materially increase compliance risk and liability exposure.

These functionalities are foundational to healthcare providers’ ability to manage enterprise risk
and maintain trust in digital health systems. These are not merely technical features; they are
core compliance tools that enable regulated entities to demonstrate adherence to the HIPAA
Security Rule – and crucially, to respond effectively to security and privacy incidents. From a risk
management standpoint, audit trails support continuous improvement by enabling organizations
to identify patterns of inappropriate access, misconfigurations, or workflow vulnerabilities that
may otherwise go undetected. This visibility is essential in a threat environment where
healthcare providers – and the entire sector – remain a frequent target of sophisticated cyber-
attacks.

If ASTP/ONC chooses to finalize the proposed removal of baseline privacy and security
certification criteria, CHIME requests that ASTP/ONC, in coordination with HHS’ OCR,
clarify how covered entities (CEs) are expected to demonstrate compliance with the
“reasonable and appropriate” safeguard standard 5 and related technical safeguard
requirements.4 While certification has never constituted a formal HIPAA safe harbor, the
existing criteria have functioned as a widely recognized baseline that supports procurement
diligence, risk management documentation, and demonstration of compliance during OCR
investigations and audits. In the absence of these baseline certification safeguards, it is unclear:

As the cybersecurity threat landscape facing the healthcare sector continues to intensify,
breaches impose significant financial, operational, and patient safety consequences across the
care continuum. Peer-reviewed research underscores the magnitude of these impacts and the
need for thoughtful, risk-based policy approaches that recognize both the scale and complexity
of safeguarding health data. “Breaches in healthcare are among the most expensive in any
industry, generating costs that include the conduct of forensic investigations, breach notification
of affected patients, legal fees, regulatory fines, lawsuits, and remediation efforts. The average
cost of a data breach in healthcare is US $7.42 million, the highest of any industry for the 14th
consecutive year.”3

Further, the healthcare sector has recently experienced firsthand the systemic consequences of
third-party risk, notably following the 2024 cyber incident involving Change Healthcare, which
impacted approximately 192.7 million individuals – representing an estimated 60 percent of
Americans. The cyber-attack at Change Healthcare financially affected 94 percent of hospitals,
with more than half indicating that the impact was “significant or serious.” 6 This event
underscored the degree to which vulnerabilities within the health IT ecosystem can have
cascading operational, financial, and patient care impacts across the entire healthcare
ecosystem. CHIME firmly believes that policies which prioritize investment in cybersecurity over
punitive measures are needed for providers, cyber safety is patient safety, and that
cybersecurity must be a shared responsibility in healthcare.

Audit logging and audit trail functionality are equally critical in incident response and breach
investigation. In the event of a suspected security or privacy incident, detailed logs provide the
evidentiary record necessary to determine what information was accessed, by whom, and over
what period of time. Just as importantly, they allow our members to demonstrate when data was
not accessed, which is often essential to determining the scope of an incident and fulfilling
regulatory expectations. The absence of sufficient audit capabilities can significantly delay
investigations, increase uncertainty, and expand potential liability exposure for healthcare
providers.

From a risk management standpoint, audit trails support continuous improvement by enabling
organizations to identify patterns of inappropriate access, misconfigurations, or workflow
vulnerabilities that may otherwise go undetected. This visibility is essential in a threat
environment where healthcare providers – and the entire sector – remain a frequent target of
sophisticated cyber-attacks. Audit trails also play an important role in maintaining patient
trust. The ability to verify access to sensitive health information and to demonstrate
accountability is fundamental to public confidence in digital health systems. Weakening

Furthermore, the assertion that participants in the Medicare Promoting Interoperability (PI)
Program and the Merit-based Incentive Payment System (MIPS) Promoting Interoperability
performance category are merely required to “have” certified health IT, without any meaningful
obligation to operationalize its privacy and security capabilities, materially understates both the
legal requirements of these programs and the enforcement mechanisms that attach to the
security risk analysis measure.

As of the most recent program year, the security risk analysis measure under the Medicare PI
Program, as implemented through the Inpatient Prospective Payment System (IPPS)
rulemaking, carries direct payment consequences for eligible hospitals (EHs) and critical access
hospitals (CAHs), as well as scoring implications under the Quality Payment Program (QPP) for
MIPS participants. Providers must attest not only to the completion of a security risk analysis,
but also to the identification of deficiencies and the implementation of appropriate remediation,
and failure to do so may result in downward payment adjustments or reduced performance
scores.

Taken together, these requirements make clear that Medicare programs (e.g., the PI Program)
do not merely require providers to possess certified health IT; rather, they establish a
substantive, payment-linked expectation that providers actively evaluate, integrate, and maintain
the privacy and security capabilities of their health IT environment using certified technology.

When considered alongside HIPAA’s implementation specifications, the security risk analysis
measures under the PI Program and the MIPS PI performance category demonstrate that
providers are required to operationalize certified technology within a comprehensive and
resource-intensive compliance framework. This framework places significant administrative and
operational demands on providers, particularly within an already complex regulatory regime,
underscoring the deliberate policy choice to tie effective security practices directly to program
participation, performance scoring, and reimbursement.

For these reasons, CHIME opposes this proposal, as it fails to adequately account for the
operational realities facing providers, who rely on clear, consistent certification expectations to
inform procurement, integration, and compliance strategies. Further, the increasing reliance on
third-party applications and tools – while often necessary to support innovation and functionality
– inherently introduces additional attack surface and cyber risk into provider environments.

CHIME members caution that encouraging innovation without maintaining clear
foundational privacy and security safeguards may increase the likelihood of third-party
incidents, breaches, and operational disruptions, including system outages that directly
affect patient care delivery. Maintaining clear baseline expectations serves as an
essential market signal that privacy and security are foundational requirements – not
optional features. In a sector where cybersecurity incidents can quickly become patient
safety events, maintaining robust baseline privacy and security expectations is essential
to protecting patients and ensuring the safe and reliable delivery of care.

CHIME strongly believes that maintaining robust, standardized privacy and security certification
criteria is therefore essential to mitigating supply chain risk and supporting safe deployment of
interoperable technologies. While we appreciate ASTP/ONC’s goal of reducing regulatory

Conclusion

In closing, CHIME appreciates ASTP/ONC’s continued efforts to foster a secure, interoperable
health information ecosystems that empowers patients and providers alike through patient-
centered digital tools and technologies. On behalf of our members, we offer practical, provider-
informed recommendations grounded in real-world implementation experience.

While CHIME strongly supports efforts to reduce regulatory burden and promote innovation, we
are concerned that the proposed rule does not yet provide sufficient, concrete relief for
healthcare providers. Hospitals and health systems continue to face significant operational,
financial, and workforce pressures, and new requirements without corresponding burden
reduction risk diverting resources from patient care and innovation.

CHIME believes deregulatory actions must result in meaningful burden reduction for providers
and preserve foundational privacy and security expectations. We urge ASTP/ONC to retain
baseline privacy and security certification criteria, maintain patient matching capabilities within
certification requirements, and adopt realistic implementation timelines that reflect operational
realities.

CHIME members are executives and senior healthcare IT leaders; thus, we are offering to
continue to serve as a resource to ASTP/ONC as they continue towards their goals, which
CHIME members broadly support. They remain steadfast in their commitment to using
technology to deliver high-quality care and facilitating interoperability and appropriate and
secure access to records across the care continuum.

We look forward to continuing to be a trusted stakeholder to ASTP/ONC and continuing to
deepen the long-standing relationship we have shared. Working together through the
policymaking and the notice and comment rulemaking process is crucial to accomplishing our
shared goals and make meaningful changes in healthcare.
