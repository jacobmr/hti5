The electronic health record shapes medical decisions and serves as the foundation upon which
healthcare providers base your care. If electronic health information cannot be trusted, medical
decision making and patient safety are immediately compromised.

As a patient, I depend on my medical record to be accurate, complete, and reliable. A health
informatics administrator once explained to me that the electronic medical record must be the
“source of truth” about a patient. If information can be altered or added later without clear
transparency, that record is no longer a reliable source of truth, and the risk and frequency of
preventable medical errors increases.

Professionally, as an attorney representing patients and families harmed by preventable medical
errors, I see the consequences when medical records are inaccurate, incomplete, or unclear. Poor-
quality records interfere with patient care on the front end and undermine accountability after
harm occurs.

Today’s electronic medical records are complex systems. They influence not only clinical
decision making, but also billing, quality reporting, population health measures, and legal
responsibility. For these systems to be trustworthy, audit trails must accurately reflect how,
when, and by whom, medical data is created, modified, and preserved.

Before any deregulation is considered, foundational standards must be modernized and applied
consistently. Audit trail standards must reflect how electronic records actually function today and
must keep pace with the growing use of artificial intelligence in healthcare. When data is created
or modified by software or AI, that activity must be transparent and traceable.

Deregulation without a stronger replacement framework will not solve existing problems, but it
will enable new ones. Strong, modern, and enforceable standards are necessary to protect
patients, support clinicians, and ensure that the electronic medical record remains a reliable
source of truth.
