CSTE’s General Comments on HTI-5:

1.  While CSTE appreciates ASTP/ONC’s desire to reduce regulatory burden, utilization of specific standards to
    enable interoperability between systems is necessary to maintain data integrity and quality. Public health and
    the healthcare industry have made substantial investments in the current infrastructure to receive, transform, and
    route electronic data. Retooling functional systems to accommodate less standardized data would impose new and
    significant resource demands; any perceived advances from reduced regulatory burden would not offset the
    increased resource demands. CSTE opposes ASTP/ONC’s proposal to deregulate HIT certification by removing or
    revising public health reporting criteria that requires replacing CDA standards with functional requirements alone.
    Replacement is even more problematic given there are no independent data validation processes that have been
    established to ensure data transmissions meet the receiving entity’s submission requirements. We urge ASTP/ONC
    to ensure the public health reporting certification criteria align with current or newer/more recent standards, thereby
    maintaining nationwide connectivity and sustaining progress in clinical and public health system interoperability.

            ASTP/ONC certification criteria ensure that every certified HIT product can report to public health using a predictable
            standard; as certified HIT marketplace continues to evolve and new products enter, certification criteria play a critical
            role in ensuring all certified products share a consistent baseline of public health reporting functionality. Certifying to
            a predictable standard reduces burden on all parties involved in exchanging health data:
                 • HIT developers need not independently interpret individual STLT reporting specifications or develop
                     reporting functionality to satisfy a wide array of state requirements.
                 • Providers and healthcare organizations need not evaluate and limit selection of a HIT solution that meets
                     public health reporting criteria only in one state.
                           ▪ This is especially important given patient mobility and healthcare organizations operating across
                                state lines.
                           ▪ If all HIT solutions are held to the same standard, providers can be more easily assured that they
                                are sending standardized data to STLT public health agencies, (1) in accordance with state-level
                                statutory and regulatory requirements for public health and (2) that meet high data quality
                                thresholds. This would minimize the need to respond to manual inquiries from public health when
                                information that is critical for public health action is missing on initial receipt, is uninterpretable, or is
                                sent in non-standardized reports.
                 • STLT public health agencies are able to develop and maintain systems to ingest standardized, predictable
                     data, increasing certified HIT portability across state borders. ASTP/ONC certification criteria serve as a
                     critical reference point that allows for the effectuation of consensus-based implementation guides that
                     STLTs use to align requirements. This reduces the need for resource intensive, bespoke technical
                     specifications and limits the return to non-harmonized jurisdiction-specific reporting rules (that public health
                     and health IT worked for years to harmonize). These predictable standards improve the efficiency of
                     provider onboarding into public health reporting. Predictable standards also improve data quality, which in
                     turn reduces time processing incoming data and identifying and correcting data inconsistencies by public
                     health. Ultimately, predictable standards benefit both public health agencies and providers, and create
                     efficiencies, including for those who already leverage centralized decision-making and the routing of
                     electronic data sent by health care to public health and bidirectionally back to health care.

            Certification criteria must refer to up-to-date standards to ensure high-quality data exchange that meets the
            expected public health goal. Certification requirements incentivize HIT developers to rapidly adopt public health
            reporting standards, as evidenced by the adoption of the electronic laboratory reporting (ELR) standard HL7 v2.5.1
            and syndromic surveillance standard HL7 v2.5.1, both critical components of the Meaningful Use (MU) program,
            which was established by the HITECH Act in 2009 to promote the adoption of certified Electronic Health Record
            (EHR) technology. Lack of updated standards adoption impacts HIT users, who lose functionality to efficiently and
            accurately exchange public health data and fulfill mandated reporting obligations, which ultimately stalls public
            health surveillance and response activities. The Health Data, Technology, and Interoperability: Patient Engagement,
            Information Sharing, and Public Health Interoperability (HTI-2) proposed rule made significant progress on
            advancing public health reporting between healthcare and public health by referencing updated standards. CSTE
            disagrees with ASTP/ONC’s withdrawal of HTI-2 proposed certification criteria for public health reporting
            and recommends that ASTP/ONC finalize the following f criteria proffered in the HTI-2 proposed rule (Table
            1). CSTE is proposing that ASTP/ONC adopt newer standards for ELR and electronic case reporting (eCR)
            (Table 2).

2.  While CSTE appreciates ASTP/ONC’s ambition to establish a new foundation for FHIR-based certification, the
    abandonment of the CDA standard in favor of FHIR-based exchange is premature. Abandonment of CDA at this
    stage does not account for public health agencies’ or healthcare’s currently limited capabilities and resources for
    sending and receiving FHIR. Therefore, CSTE urges ASTP/ONC to consider FHIR-based API requirements for
    public health reporting as additional or optional certification criteria, rather than deregulating CDA-based public
    health reporting standards.
     With few exceptions, STLT public health agencies are not currently prepared to receive FHIR-based data
    exchanges. Representative examples of FHIR challenges from CSTE members:
    • [STLT PHA 1]: “Our information exchange is not supporting FHIR, and it has not expressed any readiness. I
    don't have a single one of my reporting facilities in the state … who's expressed any willingness or desire to
    do anything with FHIR. I don't have any funding to support FHIR within our jurisdiction. And I don't have any
    manpower with any expertise to support FHIR.”
    • [STLT PHA 2]: “We and our reporting partners are not ready to transition from CDA to FHIR. We know how
    the data could flow in our system but still need extensive funding and development before being able to
    receive and process our first FHIR message.”

             Public health agency capability is unlikely to change in the next several years without increased and targeted
             resources to support the adoption of FHIR. Critical pandemic era funding was used to initiate the closing of
             longstanding gaps due to decades of underfunding; however, gaps still remain and pandemic era data
             modernization funding is expiring, leaving large unmet needs to bring public health agencies to current. Introducing
             new data collection and workflows on top of reductions in budget and workforce will further exacerbate longstanding
             challenges without off-setting benefits. Additionally, existing federal funding mechanisms supporting state health
             departments for public health surveillance have not prioritized FHIR capabilities, limiting STLT public health
             agencies’ ability to invest in new infrastructure, like FHIR servers. CDC and ASTP/ONC coordination with STLT
             public health agencies to plan, fund, test, and implement FHIR-capable infrastructure before deregulating
             existing standards, like CDA, is urgently needed.

             As proposed, CSTE also contends that ASTP/ONC will disrupt critical public health reporting data flows and
             surveillance activities by deregulating CDA-based standards, and that there is little benefit offset by this
             proposed deregulation. For example, ELR coverage in most STLT jurisdictions exceeds 95% using HL7 v2.5.1 and
             is meeting public health data needs. This success is due to ASTP/ONC’s early endorsement of standards in HTI-1,
             when ASTP/ONC rightly identified that functional standards alone were not sufficient for HIT developers to meet
             public health reporting needs - they needed specific standards to develop to, and extensive investment, both by
             public health agencies and by providers over the last several years, to build the infrastructure necessary to receive,
             transform, and route these data. As these investments are benefiting jurisdictions in their mission to improve the
             health of their communities (e.g., with electronic lab reporting allowing public health to do things that simply could
             not be done when dealing with paper reports2), as well to modernize their systems (e.g., through turning off manual
             reporting, which reduces STLT-level sluggish, error prone, manual processing and antiquated, manual reporting
             burden on clinical staff), it is not advised at this time to transition to a new standard, due to the resource utilization
             without added benefit. In fact, HL7’s Helios project recommends prioritizing FHIR-based exchange for use cases
             without an existing solution. The existing CDA-based standards for public health reporting are operational.

             Finally, the absence of FHIR-based implementation guides for public health reporting will create additional
             work for providers, developers, and public health agencies to develop and interpret non-harmonized
             reporting specifications. Public health reporting requires implementation guidance that describes the context,
             content, and structure for data exchange. Functional requirements for public health reporting do not provide the
             specificity needed to constrain and configure the data required for public health surveillance. That level of specificity
             is available in consensus-developed implementation guides. Without referencing implementation guides in
             certification criteria, (1) HIT developers will need to configure HIT reporting functionality to meet each jurisdiction’s
             reporting requirements, decreasing the portability of HIT products across borders, (2) providers’ HIT products may
             not adequately identify and send the appropriate data to meet legal reporting requirements in their jurisdiction, and
             (3) public health agencies will need to test and evaluate unique reporting streams from each provider to ensure they
             comply with jurisdictional requirements.

3.  Regarding the Unfunded Mandate Act of 1995: CSTE appreciates the effort of ASTP/ONC to reduce burden and
    support innovation in the HIT marketplace; however, we are concerned that some proposals within HTI-5 do
    impose unfunded mandates on STLT public health agencies and the private sector. Fewer standards and
    certification to functional requirements only can result in certified electronic health record technology (CEHRT)
    vendors using any number of formats which public health agencies would then need to support, or agencies may

 choose to adopt their own laws that further specific or limit what formats they may support. Supporting all formats
across all jurisdictions would create an unfunded cost burden both in terms of potential new development and in
operational costs for supporting multiple standards. Preserving use of CDA would also ensure no loss of investment
for STLT public health agencies and providers, who have created infrastructure to receive, transform, and route
standardized electronic health data; however, existing investments will be lost should the need arise to
accommodate less standardized data, which would require additional resources – both human and technology.
Worse yet, if public health is unable to accept the submitted data via electronic exchange, STLT public health
agencies could face additional hurdles and delays in retiring legacy reporting mechanisms or a return to provider
reporting via fax and mail. CSTE strongly recommends retaining certification criteria to support consistent
implementation, protect investments and public health capacity, and advance efficient and scalable health data
exchange nationwide.

CSTE further supports the comments offered by the American Immunization Registry Association (AIRA), the Association of
Public Health Laboratories (APHL), the Association of State and Territorial Health Officers (ASTHO), and the Joint Public
Health Informatics Taskforce (JPHIT).

Comments on Specific Sections of HTI-5:

Referenced page numbers correspond to the PDF of the proposed rules posted on the Federal Register.

    1.   RE: Proposal to remove the ‘‘transmission to cancer registries’’ certification criterion in § 170.315(f)(4) – pg.
         60992

         CSTE disagrees with removing the certification criteria for transmission to cancer registries. Removing existing
         certification requirements before replacement standards are mature, tested, and widely adopted poses a significant
         risk to cancer surveillance nationwide.

         Cancer registries currently rely on CDA and HL7 v2.5.1 based implementation guides, and the North American
         Association of Central Cancer Registries’ (NAACCR) Volume V defines the standards and guidelines to transmit
         items, data item definitions, and transmission specifications. Although HTI-5 references the HL7 FHIR Central
         Cancer Registry Reporting Implementation Guide (IG) and activities associated with the Helios FHIR Accelerator,
         these resources are not mature enough to replace existing certified standards. The FHIR IGs are newly published,
         have not been broadly tested by registries or vendors, and are lacking adoption; Helios work on cancer has focused
         on query/response use cases rather than on initial cancer case reporting.

         Eliminating these certification standards without a viable alternative would disrupt automated reporting and would
         require STLT jurisdictions to take an additional and more active role in defining new technical and operational
         requirements. This would include: (1) publishing local implementation specifications and onboarding guidance, (2)
         negotiating reporting interfaces or agreements directly with EHR vendors, (3) providing technical assistance, testing,
         and validation support, and (4) supporting temporary parallel submission processes while vendors and registries
         align. These additional steps may further compromise data quality and timeliness.

         CSTE recommends that ASTP/ONC retain the existing HL7 CDA standard and develop a transition and
         implementation plan for FHIR adoption. Removing this standard would increase variability across jurisdictions and
         place additional technical and operational burdens on state and local registries.

         CSTE further supports the comments offered on this topic by NAACCR.

    2.   RE: Proposal to revise the ‘‘transmission to public health agencies—electronic case reporting’’ (eCR)
         certification criterion in § 170.315(f)(5) – pg. 60992-60993

         CSTE disagrees with revising the eCR certification criteria to a functional requirement. While CSTE supports long-
         term modernization in the eCR space, the proposed change would undermine progress toward standardized,
         automated public health reporting and impose significant new resource and cost burdens on STLT public
         health agencies. Standards-based eCR certification has been essential to ensuring consistent data structure and
         completeness, content, security, and timeliness of data flowing to STLT public health agencies. Moving to a

functional-only requirement would introduce variability in message formats, vocabularies, and workflows. This would
require public health agencies to accept, validate, and reconcile multiple non-standard implementations or create
jurisdiction-specific standards. Standards also ensure clearly defined, minimum data elements to report; without
standards, STLT public health agencies would have limited ability to evaluate messages for validity and basic quality
measures because existing automated tools depend on standards-based messages. Non-standardized data will
require corrections or improvements when reports are incomplete, inconsistent, or unusable. STLT public health
agencies are already working to align eICR reporting requirements. CSTE convened STLTs to develop the eCR
Consensus Criteria, which specify data quality completeness thresholds and other requirements for priority data
elements from the HL7 eICR implementation guides. EHR products and healthcare organizations will be expected to
meet these requirements. If any of these requirements are not supported, some public health agencies may not
accept electronic data from sending organizations or worse yet revert to paper-based methods of reporting.

     CSTE appreciates ASTP/ONC’s desire to move towards FHIR-based standards in this space; however, many EHRs
     and public health agencies are not yet able to implement or receive FHIR at scale. On the public health side, some
     STLT public health agencies do not currently have the technical infrastructure or a workforce with expertise in
     consuming HL7 FHIR and would need additional staff, significant training and/or upskilling, and resources to
     establish FHIR capability. With anticipated level or decreasing federal funding awards, STLT public health agencies
     may have reduced ability to support and maintain existing informatics capacity, let alone support the implementation
     of a new standard. At a time when CDA-based eICR, using nationally adopted implementation guides, remains
     widely used and effective, elimination of CDA-based certification before FHIR is widely operational is problematic,
     and may lead to disruption of existing automated reporting, increase reliance on manual or fax-based reporting, and
     reverse efficiency gains already achieved.

     Therefore, CSTE recommends ASTP/ONC retain the HL7 CDA eICR [HL7 CDA® R2 Implementation Guide: Public
     Health Case Report - the Electronic Initial Case Report (eICR) Release 2, STU Release 3.1.1 - US Realm]; with a
     phased transition to HL7 FHIR-based eCR [FHIR eCR R2.1.2 (HL7 FHIR® Implementation Guide: Electronic Case
     Reporting (eCR), Release 2.1.2 - US Realm]. Supporting both standards leverages existing national infrastructure
     (like the APHL Informatics Messaging Services (AIMS) platform), minimizes burden on both EHR developers and
     STLTs, and maintains interoperability while FHIR capabilities mature. It also aligns the federal priorities of FHIR
     advancement and encourages public health agencies to eliminate manual reporting. Rolling back certification criteria
     risks signaling that eCR is no longer a federal priority, thereby weakening vendor incentives to maintain and improve
     implementations.

3.  RE: Propose to revise the ‘‘transmission to public health agencies—antimicrobial use and resistance
    reporting’’ certification criterion in § 170.315(f)(6) – pg. 60993

    CSTE conditionally supports the revision of the antimicrobial use and resistance reporting certification criteria to a
    functional requirement. This proposal would benefit from further modification to include a reference to complying with
    the most recently published CDC implementation guide.

    Antimicrobial use and resistance (AUR) data are primarily reported by hospitals to CDC through the National
    Healthcare Safety Network (NHSN), so certification should align with the standards and guidance currently used and
    recommended by CDC and NHSN. As the data are subsequently shared with STLT public health agencies and used
    for public health surveillance and response activities, changes that reduce standardization have downstream
    impacts on data quality, usability, and timeliness for public health agencies. Revision to a functional requirement,
    here, would have limited impact because there is already a process in place where NHSN staff have sufficient
    resources to perform data validation checks to ensure that submissions meet CDC’s requirements for data elements
    and formatting; if those requirements are not met, the data transmission is not possible and would not be accepted.

4.  RE: Proposal to remove the ‘‘transmission to public health agencies—health care surveys’’ certification
    criterion in § 170.315(f)(7) – pg. 60993-60994

         CSTE disagrees with removing health care survey certification criteria.

         These surveys are submitted directly to CDC, but the resulting data are widely used by state and local public health
         agencies for essential functions, including public health planning, monitoring health care access, tracking trends
         over time, and meeting federal quality and PIPs requirements. Removing certification criteria could weaken future

    participation by healthcare organizations, particularly if new CERHT products would no longer be required to support
    the submission of these data.

         Given that the current capability is well established across most certified health IT and maintaining the criterion does
         not appear to impose a significant additional burden, CSTE recommends that ASTP/ONC retain the HL7 CDA for
         healthcare surveys. This would allow for a FHIR-based standard, in addition to the current CDA-based approach, in
         order to preserve existing capabilities, encourage evolution toward newer standards, and avoid disruption to an
         established data flow. In future rules, CSTE recommends that ASTP/ONC reference adoption standards through the
         ISA.

5.  RE: Proposal to adopt USCDI v3.1 in § 170.213(a) and incorporate it by reference in § 170.299. We propose
    to remove USCDI v3 in § 170.213(b) and USCDI v1 in § 170.213(a), which expires on January 1, 2026. The
    effect of these proposals, if finalized in a subsequent final rule, would be that USCDI v3.1 would be the only
    version of USCDI in § 170.213 – pg. 60999-61000

    CSTE disagrees with adopting USCDI v3.1. CSTE proposes ASTP/ONC adopt the most recent published version of
    USCDI, USCDI v6.

    Successive USCDI versions have added data elements of significant value to public health, many of which
    are included in USCDI v6 and not in USCDI v3.1. These data elements include: facility address, unique device
    identifiers, portable medical orders, family health history, care plans, date of onset, expanded orders data classes,
    emergency department and operative notes, immunization lot numbers, medication route of administration,
    interpreter need, and provenance. Lab-related data elements in USCDI v6 are also critical for public health (e.g.,
    related to specimen (Specimen Condition Acceptability, Specimen Identifier, Specimen Source Site) and related to
    results (Result Units of Measure, Result Reference Range and Result Interpretation)). CSTE contends that adopting
    USCDI v3.1 would roll back support for key patient demographic data, which are essential for monitoring healthcare
    access, supporting patient-centered care, and public health response to outbreaks. Removing these data elements
    risks inconsistencies in reporting and an inability to properly assess public health risk and outcomes.

    Given that some larger EHR vendors have voluntarily adopted data elements included in more recent versions of
    USCDI, CSTE believes that adoption of a more current USCDI version would not necessarily impose undue burden
    and would better align certification with real-world capabilities. Therefore, CSTE encourages ASTP/ONC to adopt
    USCDI v6 to ensure certification keeps pace with current standards development and public health need.

6.  RE: Proposal to remove the following measures in § 170.407(a)(3) § 170.407(a)(3)(vi), Immunization
    administrations electronically submitted to immunization information systems through certified health IT;
    and § 170.407(a)(3)(vii), Immunization history and forecasts through certified health IT – pg. 61003-61004

    CSTE disagrees with removing these insight conditions.

    The Insight Conditions offer metrics for providers and public health to evaluate how well HIT is implementing
    required functionality and how much immunization data CEHRT vendor products are providing to immunization
    information systems. CSTE understands ASTP/ONC’s desire to shift to FHIR application usage measure offers,
    which may provide more valuable and actionable insight into interoperability; however, given there is currently no
    FHIR IG for immunization exchange, there is value in having performance measures.

7.  RE: Proposal to remove transparency and risk management requirements established in the HTI–1 Final
    Rule – pg. 60987

         CSTE disagrees with ASTP/ONC’s proposal to remove the AI transparency rules finalized as part of HTI-1.
         Responsible AI deployment typically depends on understanding model provenance, training data characteristics,
         and performance limitations, which is the information contained in the current model card format that would be
         removed by the proposed rule.

         Healthcare delivery demands high levels of provider and patient trust in the technologies used. Though individual
         providers and patients may not have the time and knowledge to effectively evaluate the AI tools integrated into
         clinical decision support workflows, AI may impact patient care. For instance, without knowing the AI decision-

    making processes, providers could inadvertently introduce bias into their decision-making, resulting in inequitable
    treatment outcomes. For public health agencies, this may result in increased exposure to AI-mediated data and
    outputs without corresponding visibility into how AI systems generate, transform, prioritize, and ultimately impact that
    information. Removing transparency requirements before alternative oversight mechanisms are established creates
    a gap in accountability precisely as AI adoption accelerates across the health system.

             The HIT market demands more, not less, AI transparency. While ASTP/ONC states there is no publicly available
             evidence that these transparency requirements have led to positive impacts on patient care, the requirements have
             only been in effect since January 2025 — this is insufficient time to evaluate the impact of AI transparency rules.
             Therefore, CSTE recommends that ASTP/ONC either retain or simplify the model card requirements or, at a
             minimum, develop voluntary guidance frameworks for AI transparency that health IT developers and healthcare
             organizations can reference.

8.  RE: Proposal to remove the requirement to submit real world testing plans for all real world testing
    certification criteria; proposal to revise the real world testing results reporting requirement – pg. 61002-
    61003

    CSTE disagrees with ASTP/ONC’s proposal to remove the requirements to submit real world testing plans for all
    real world testing certification criteria; these requirements should be retained.

    HIT developers should be testing their products to ensure that they are working correctly under real world
    constraints (e.g., reporting to public health); the reporting requirement ensures that testing is valid and performed.
    STLT public health agencies have found that, even when HIT developers attested to successful public health data
    exchange, there were issues that slow down provider onboarding which could have been fixed sooner if real world
    testing was performed.

    This aligns with recommendations previously offered by the HTI-2 Proposed Rule Taskforce 2024: Recommend that
    ASTP/ONC certify use and implementation in real world environments with thresholds for completeness and data
    quality.

9.  RE: Proposal to revise the Information Blocking Manner Exception – pg. 61011-61014

    CSTE disagrees with ASTP/ONC’s proposal “to revise § 171.301(a)(2) to add a new subparagraph (iii) to incorporate
    in regulation text and explicitly state that any contract or agreement related to the access, exchange, or use of EHI:
    (1) must be at market rate; (2) must not be a contract of adhesion; and (3) must not contain unconscionable terms.”

    This proposal is challenging as it would effectively disqualify standardized participation agreements from Manner
    Exception protection. This approach creates an unnecessary administrative burden, particularly when all data
    exchange partners receive identical services at the same rate with the same expectations. Public health participates
    in several of these standardized participation agreements. For example, many public health agencies connect to and
    use the AIMS platform as a trusted public health intermediary to exchange vital public health data; all jurisdictions
    have access to the same system and the same services, which is key to the success of this data exchange. AIMS
    has allowed for public health to access electronic data streams en masse, without requiring wholesale changes to
    existing surveillance infrastructure. Additionally, we see TEFCA as an important foundation to achieving national
    interoperability. This exception we believe helps incentivize participation in the network.

    CSTE recommends that ASTP/ONC revise this proposal so that if an agreement supports public health reporting
    and if its terms are developed or approved through a governance process that provides for meaningful input from
    current or prospective participants, then it should be exempt from ASTP/ONC's proposed contractual limitations.
