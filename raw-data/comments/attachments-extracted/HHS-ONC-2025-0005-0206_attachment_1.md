The National Association of Chain Drug Stores (NACDS) appreciates the opportunity to comment on the
recently proposed rule, Health Data, Technology, and Interoperability: ASTP/ONC Deregulatory Actions to
Unleash Prosperity (“HTI-5”). NACDS represents chain pharmacies, including traditional drug stores,
supermarkets, and mass merchants with pharmacies. Nearly 90% of Americans live within 5 miles of a
community pharmacy 1 and 85% of adults in the U.S. say pharmacists are easy to access, ranking them the
most accessible healthcare destination. 2 Pharmacies are a trusted, nationwide access point for medications,
immunizations, preventive care, patient education and counseling, and a growing range of health and wellness
services that improve health outcomes and improve healthcare efficiency.

As the healthcare system increasingly relies on community pharmacies to deliver clinical services, their
continued exclusion from national interoperability efforts and incentives has become a significant barrier to
achieving ONC/ASTP’s core mission of advancing access, quality, and equity through health information
technology (IT). A 2023 Leavitt Partners report outlines that without intentional integration of pharmacies into
modern health data exchange, nationwide interoperability will remain structurally incomplete—undermining
care coordination, patient safety, and the full return on federal health IT investments.3

NACDS supports ASTP/ONC’s efforts to modernize the Health IT Certification Program and streamline
Information Blocking regulations to promote innovation and reduce unnecessary burden. At the same time,
interoperability reforms must ensure that pharmacies can meaningfully participate in nationwide data
exchange, support bidirectional and artificial intelligence (AI)-enabled workflows, and operate within a

NACDS Recommendations

    1. Finalize the proposed regulatory clarification that the definitions of “access,” “exchange,” and “use”
       include automated and AI-enabled data flows to support scalable, workflow-integrated pharmacy
       services.
    2. Ensure FHIR-based interoperability supports structured, bidirectional exchange and aligns with real-
       world pharmacy workflows.
    3. Fully integrate pharmacies into national health information exchange frameworks, including TEFCA,
       and address historical exclusion from federal interoperability incentive programs.
    4. Narrow Information Blocking exceptions to prevent misuse of unfair contractual terms (e.g., non-
       market rate contracts, contracts of adhesion, unconscionable provisions).
    5. Remove barriers to write-back and bidirectional workflows by eliminating the “third party seeking
       modification use” condition under the Infeasibility Exception.
    6. Overhaul or eliminate the “manner exception exhausted” condition to prevent reliance on nominal
       “alternative manners” that impede meaningful interoperability.
    7. Provide guidance to protect good-faith pharmacy participation and sustain confidence in TEFCA

Discussion

I. Proposed Clarification of the Definitions of “Access,” “Exchange,” and “Use”
NACDS supports ASTP/ONC’s proposed regulatory clarification that the definitions of “access,”
“exchange,” and “use” include automated system-to-system and AI-enabled data flows. Community
pharmacy workflows increasingly rely on automated ingestion and structured exchange of clinical
information to support pharmacist-led services. Explicit recognition that automated and AI-enabled
exchange qualifies as permissible “use” will reduce ambiguity, support innovation, and help prevent
barriers to scalable, workflow-integrated interoperability.

II. FHIR-Based Interoperability Must Support Real-World Pharmacy Care
NACDS appreciates ASTP/ONC’s continued emphasis on FHIR-based Application Programming
Interfaces (APIs), recognizing that a FHIR-based architecture provides important scalability and long-
term flexibility for the health IT ecosystem. At the same time, pharmacies were not included in the
original CMS Promoting Interoperability Programs, which has contributed to ongoing connectivity gaps
even as pharmacists’ clinical responsibilities have expanded. As HTI-5 advances modern
interoperability standards, NACDS welcomes collaboration with, and coordination across HHS to
ensure that community pharmacies are fully incorporated into federal interoperability incentives and
initiatives. Integrating pharmacies into national exchange infrastructure will strengthen coordinated,
person-centered care and advance shared goals for secure, structured data exchange, consistent with

III. Information Blocking Streamlining
NACDS supports ASTP/ONC’s efforts to simplify the Information Blocking framework and remove
overly complex or duplicative conditions.

         NACDS supports proposed revisions to the “manner requested” prong of the Manner Exception that
         would limit reliance on non-market rate contracts, contracts of adhesion, or unconscionable terms to
         justify denial of access or exchange. Pharmacies must not face barriers to electronic health information
         (EHI) through restrictive or unfair contractual arrangements. Clarifying that such terms cannot be used
         to avoid interoperability obligations will strengthen patient access to data and support meaningful
         pharmacy connectivity.

         In addition, NACDS supports removal of the “third party seeking modification use” condition under the
         Infeasibility Exception. Eliminating this condition will reduce barriers to pharmacy-connected
         applications and write-back workflows, including secure bidirectional exchange. As interoperability
         capabilities mature, pharmacies should not encounter avoidable limitations when participating in two-
         way exchange.

         NACDS also supports the proposal to overhaul the “manner exception exhausted” condition under the
         Infeasibility Exception. Under the current framework, actors may deny a request by asserting that they
         could not meet the requested manner, offered limited alternative formats, and do not provide the
         same access to a “substantial number” of similarly situated entities. Tightening or eliminating this
         condition would reduce the ability to rely on nominal “alternative manners” to avoid providing
         practical, workflow-integrated exchange. For pharmacies, this reform would help ensure timely,
         structured data access that supports clinical services without unnecessary technical or contractual
         barriers.

IV. TEFCA Participation
NACDS supports the proposal to eliminate the TEFCA-specific manner exception as a means of
simplifying compliance and reinforcing consistent Information Blocking obligations across exchange
pathways. Removing this exception will help ensure that participation in TEFCA cannot be used to limit
or delay other practical and timely methods of EHI exchange. At the same time, pharmacies and their
technology partners are making investments to participate in national exchange frameworks, including
TEFCA. Accordingly, ASTP/ONC should provide clear guidance confirming that good-faith TEFCA
participation remains fully consistent with Information Blocking compliance and will not expose
participants to unintended enforcement risk. Clear expectations will sustain confidence in TEFCA
participation and advance the broader goal of nationwide, interoperable data exchange.

Conclusion
NACDS appreciates the opportunity to engage with ASTP/ONC on the HTI-5 proposed rule and shares the
agency’s goal of advancing a modern, efficient, and interoperable health IT ecosystem. Pharmacies are
essential partners in delivering coordinated, patient-centered care, and we are committed to working
collaboratively to ensure interoperability reforms strengthen real-world clinical practice. We look forward to
continued partnership with HHS and ASTP/ONC to advance practical, scalable solutions that integrate
pharmacies more fully into national exchange frameworks, support innovation, and improve health outcomes
for patients nationwide. For any questions regarding these comments, please contact Kayla McFeely, Vice
President, Health & Wellness Strategy and Policy at kmcfeely@nacds.org.
