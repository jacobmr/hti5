Public Comment on ASTP’s HTI‑5 Notice of Proposed Rulemaking (RIN 0955‑AA09)

Subject: Feedback on Interoperability Advancements and Concerns Regarding Removal of Privacy and Security
Certification Criteria

Dear ASTP/ONC:

Qualifacts appreciates the opportunity to provide feedback on the Health Data, Technology, and Interoperability:
ASTP/ONC Deregulatory Actions to Unleash Prosperity (HTI‑5) Notice of Proposed Rulemaking. We commend
the collaborative, coordinated effort among ASTP/ONC, HHS, and partner agencies to advance innovation,
strengthen interoperability, and modernize nationwide health technology infrastructure.

Qualifacts strongly supports ASTP/ONC’s focus on interoperability and data liquidity. These priorities are
essential for improving care coordination, reducing fragmentation, and enhancing the overall healthcare
experience for patients and providers. We appreciate the clear momentum toward building a more connected and
patient-centered national ecosystem.

We also appreciate the reduction of administrative burden related to Real-World Testing and Insights Condition
reporting. Narrowing these requirements to focus solely on interoperability is a meaningful improvement that
preserves accountability while reducing unnecessary operational strain on developers.

We are grateful for the collaborative work across federal agencies reflected in the HTI-5 rule. This alignment
demonstrates a shared commitment to strengthening the health tech industry, promoting innovation, and
supporting providers and patients through modernized infrastructure and thoughtful policy development.

Despite our support for many elements of HTI-5, Qualifacts respectfully submits this public comment expressing
strong concern with the Notice of Proposed Rulemaking proposals that remove or substantially scale back privacy
and security certification criteria within the ONC Health IT Certification Program. CEHRT certification plays an
essential role in enabling HIPAA Privacy and Security Rule compliance. While HIPAA places legal obligations
on covered entities and business associates, providers rely on health IT products to supply foundational features
such as access controls, audit logging, secure session management, encryption support, and protections against
client‑side data exposure. Removing these criteria undermines providers’ ability to meet regulatory obligations
and shifts disproportionate risk onto healthcare organizations and patients.

Additionally, the removal of privacy and security criteria also creates a false sense of security in the health IT
marketplace. Providers purchasing “certified” products reasonably expect that certification includes meaningful
security protections. Redefining certification in a way that deemphasizes privacy and security erodes trust and
introduces confusion at the point of procurement, precisely when clarity is most needed.

While burden reduction and innovation are important goals, the removal of privacy and security criteria presents
significant and foreseeable risk to patients, providers, and health IT developers. Over the past five years,
cybersecurity incidents in healthcare have risen dramatically in both frequency and impact. Healthcare has
consistently remained the most expensive industry sector for data breaches, with average breach costs in the

United States approaching or exceeding $9–10 million per incident. Large‑scale events such as the 2024 Change
Healthcare ransomware attack, which ultimately affected approximately 192 million individuals, demonstrate the
systemic and national consequences when health IT security fails.

The financial and operational consequences extend far beyond technology vendors. Providers experience revenue
cycle disruption, delayed care, staff furloughs, and substantial recovery costs, while patients suffer identity theft,
fraud, and long‑term harm tied to the exposure of immutable protected health information (PHI). Identity theft
victims frequently face significant out‑of‑pocket expenses and hundreds of hours resolving fraudulent medical
records and insurance claims.

Against this backdrop, the proposed removal of privacy and security criteria from Certified Electronic Health
Record Technology (CEHRT) is deeply concerning. As the saying goes, “People do what you inspect, not what
you expect.” Certification has served as a critical inspection mechanism ensuring that health IT developers
implement baseline, testable security and privacy functionality. Without inspectable requirements, expectations
alone will not prevent shortcuts in design and development, particularly under competitive pressures related to
cost, speed, and market entry.

It is also important to clarify what third‑party certifications do—and do not—address. SOC 2 Type II and
HITRUST certifications primarily assess an organization’s cybersecurity program, policies, and infrastructure
controls. They do not reliably validate application‑level functionality within the software itself. As a result, a
health IT developer may maintain strong enterprise controls while still deploying software that exposes PHI
through insecure application behavior.

One concrete example is the improper caching of PHI in web browsers. To accelerate development or improve
user performance, developers may allow sensitive data to persist in local browser storage or page history if
explicit cache‑control protections are not required and tested. This creates unnecessary risk of identity theft for
patients and providers alike, particularly in shared or compromised device scenarios. These risks are preventable
through well‑known application‑level safeguards, yet they are unlikely to be consistently implemented without
certification requirements that explicitly inspect for them.

To compound these risks, the proposed removal of the quality management system (QMS) certification
criterion in § 170.315(g)(4) further undermines confidence in the ONC Health IT Certification Program.
QMS requirements ensure that certified health IT is developed under a defined, industry-recognized
software development lifecycle (SDLC) framework, providing foundational safeguards against
uncontrolled, inconsistent, or unsafe development practices. Historically, this criterion has prevented
certification of products built without adequate design controls, testing rigor, or corrective action
processes—conditions that directly contribute to operational failures, security vulnerabilities, and patient
safety risks.

Importantly, QMS requirements do not inhibit innovation; rather, they establish minimum rigor in an
industry where the consequences of failure can be severe. For established developers, QMS maintenance is
a routine operational practice. For newer market entrants—particularly those developing AI-enabled and
highly interoperable technologies—the criterion reinforces that rapid innovation must be balanced with
disciplined risk management. Eliminating QMS certification at this stage increases the likelihood that
immature or unsafe products enter clinical and operational workflows.

In summary, reducing regulatory burden should not come at the expense of baseline patient safety and data
protection. Given the documented rise in healthcare cybersecurity incidents, the extensive financial and
operational harm experienced across the ecosystem, and the reliance of providers on CEHRT for
compliance‑enabling functionality, we urge ASTP/ONC to retain and modernize privacy and security certification
criteria rather than remove them. Doing so will better align the certification program with real‑world risk, protect
patients, and sustain confidence in federally recognized health IT certification.

Qualifacts respectfully urges ASTP/ONC to:

    1. Retain and modernize privacy and security certification criteria, ensuring alignment with today’s
       cybersecurity threat landscape and the HIPAA Security Rule.
    2. Preserve application-level testing requirements that directly support provider compliance and protect
       patients’ most sensitive information.
    3. Maintain QMS certification expectations that ensure rigor in the development of certified health IT.

Qualifacts shares ASTP/ONC’s vision of a modern, interoperable, and innovation-ready health ecosystem. We
believe firmly that interoperability and privacy/security must evolve together to support a trusted, resilient, and
patient-centered future.

Respectfully submitted,

Hope Winkowski, NCC, OHCC, HPOC
Vice President, Product Compliance
Qualifacts Systems LLC
