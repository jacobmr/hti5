HTI-5 Proposed Rule Comments

                                 Respectfully Submitted

The Texas Health Services Authority’s Interoperability Collaborative is a vendor agnostic
stakeholder driven collaborative that seeks to remove barriers to safe, secure transport of
clinical information with a goal of improving outcomes and decreasing costs of healthcare.

The stakeholders commend ASTP / ONC on initiatives reducing burden and encouraging
innovation and we support this vision. Comments are to ensure that there are no
unintended consequences by sharing information garnered from front-line clinicians.

Comments regarding HTI-5 changes are provided in isolation without benefit of future rule
making considerations that could negate many of these stakeholder concerns.

• Industry standards drive innovation and efficiency; federal assistance is needed to
support adoption.
• If the intent is to reduce burden, removing standards and certifications will increase
burden rather than reduce it, because proprietary ‘standards’ require multiple
mapping and maintenance overhead for each vendor connection.
• Gaps in data quality and integrity due to minimal standards guidance will undermine
trust in the data, reduce its use and impact, driving healthcare costs upward.
• The EHR is the engine of the organization. If an EHR vendor changes standards or
falls behind, the barrier to change is high for the practice/hospital. It is a major
capital expenditure where organizations cannot easily rip and replace. The
organization may be held back by a vendor who does not keep up with the industry.
Current requirements set a minimal technology base that supports interoperability.

Concerns:

1.  The ecosystem relies on adoption and enforcement of standards including
    innovative technology developers. If all the parties are not relying on the same
    standards and timeline for adoption there may be unintended consequences
    leading to issues with data comparability across systems.
2.  Without certification oversight, the users of the system will be forced to validate
    functionality and standards adoption, rather than relying on their vendor, and
    increasing their administrative costs.
     3. Updates will not be timely or synchronized leading to data incompatibility with
    vendors that have implemented standards.
3.  Data comparability across populations and time spans will be impacted.
    a. Changes in EHR certification requirements potentially impact how measures
    are calculated and could impact how cross system and cross time measures
    are trended and evaluated.
    b. Potential negative impact to facilities, HHSC, CMS and state Medicaid with
    program effectiveness measurement.
4.  Patient perspective with multiple providers with different EHRs for individual access
    should be consistent.
5.  Shifting responsibility for data integrity entirely to the provider from a shared
    responsibility with vendor thereby increasing the cost for healthcare providers.
6.  Vendors may take the path of least resistance and only support, develop, or
    implement the minimum requirements.
7.  If current technology is no longer a requirement for certified EHRs:
    a. What will support be like in the future?
    b. Will there be any development for technologies no longer required for
    certification?
    c. Will there be an increase in costs for implementation and / or support should
    an organization wish to use these technologies?
8.  Limiting the market options for organizations that desire to continue leveraging
    stable processes integrated into provider workflow. Providers could be forced to
    stay with legacy systems that have today’s functions rather than having options to
    advance to more modern technologies.
9.  Closed loop referrals are a focus for the Texas community including specialists,
    hospitals, DME companies, Home Health and Community Based Organizations.
    Not all niche systems support this core functionality, and community-based
    organizations often do not. Open, shared, standards help drive adoption, use and
    impact.

Recommendations:

1. Maintain certification requirements and restructure administrative processes to
   decrease the burden for developers and system administrators to maintain and
   expand:
   a. Clinical Decision Support
   b. Family Health History
   c. Implantable device list
    d. Clinical information reconciliation and incorporation
   e. Care plan
   f. Clinical quality measures
   g. Amendments
   h. Integrity
   i. Patient health information capture
   j. Automated numerator recording
   k. Automated measure calculation
   l. Quality management system
   m. Consolidated CDA creation performance
   n. Direct Project
   o. Direct Project, Edge Protocol and XCR / XDM

2. Revise vendor’s certification process to include status of implemented and
   deployed.
3. Include community-based organization vendors in certification.
4. Expand certification to include vendors supporting organizations receiving Medicare
   funding.
5. Provide incentives for organizations to use a certified product.

The Why -

1.  Certification Criteria: Clinical Quality Measures Report and Filter & Integrity,
    Clinical Quality Measures, Automated numerator recording, Automated
    measure calculation, and Quality management system

How will we know if our programs are successful, if the data is not available with new
technology? Currently, promoting Interoperability criteria certification standards
calculations are built into EHRs. Removing certification will put the burden on every facility
to prove how the numerator and denominator are calculated, rather than referring to the
certification of the EHR system. This is not a small undertaking as the systems are
complex.

Organizations participate in many audits and regulatory evaluations in the normal course of
business. A certified electronic health record provides each organization that uses its
software validated data submission with said audits and regulatory evaluations. If there is
no longer a requirement for a certified EHR, data validation could prove to be more
cumbersome and costly for front-line clinicians and facility administrators. The burden of
data accuracy is pushed solely to the provider. While providers have final responsibility,
the EHR vendors support these efforts through the certification process.

Comparison perspective, currently systems calculate numerators and denominators using
the same standard making comparison valid and reliable both nationally and locally.
Removing the certification standards could invalidate this comparison adversely impacting
comparability.

Examples: CMS Promoting Interoperability, Closed-loop Referrals, e-prescribing, and UDS
data reporting for HRSA funding

2.  Certification Criteria: Direct Project, Direct Project, Edge Protocol and
    XDR/SDM

Closed-loop referrals are a key initiative in Texas leveraging Direct Messaging as an
efficient, cost-effective transport mechanism. While niche systems may not have HISPs
that provide access to Direct Messaging, standards enable competitors to offer this
functionality at little cost. This allows providers to send information electronically, rather
than reverting to less efficient fax or telephone calls.

Electronic Case Reporting is also an important initiative for Texans for population health,
CMS reporting and notification to our Birth Defects Registry that provides services to this
fragile population.

Front-line care providers are working diligently to improve referrals to community-based
programs to move away from telephone tag and faxing to an integrated solution. i.e. if food
is to be part of the health domain, we need to be able to efficiently, securely communicate
needs building on what we have to control costs.

3.  Certification Criteria: Amendments, Integrity and Patient Information Capture

Clinicians are focused on increasing patient and family management of their own data and
health. Individual access with consolidation of data from varied facilities and vendor
platforms provides that foundation. However, data harmonization and standards are
crucial to making this information useful and trustworthy to the consumer who is not
educated in medicine.

Examples: Parent taking child to an emergency room, that uses a different EHR. You want
that process to be efficient, effective and accurate.
 4. Certification Criteria: Family health history, Implantable devices, Clinical
Decision Support, Transitions of Care, Clinical information reconciliation and
incorporation, Decision support interventions, and Consolidated CDA creation.

The Texas Health Services Authority’s stakeholders have worked diligently to move away
from fax communication allowing clinical information from diverse vendors and
organizations to seamlessly flow into the clinician’s workflow supporting the use of clinical
decision support tools and optimizing the EHR. A few of our efforts include discharge
summary, transfer summary, OB flowsheet and behavioral health C-CDAs. These efforts
have improved efficiencies across our state while supporting our clinicians and patients.
FHIR has promise but is not yet widely adopted or available. FHIR also depends on the
structure and data integrity efforts initiated with C-CDA. IT support is often lacking in our
rural communities, and they rely on their vendors. Consistently applied standards and
certification of the vendor products support Texans when they seek care elsewhere by
making information transferrable, speeding care and reducing duplicate, unnecessary,
services.
