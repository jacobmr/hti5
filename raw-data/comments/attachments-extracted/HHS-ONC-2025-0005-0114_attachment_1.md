The American Academy of Dermatology Association (AADA) appreciates the opportunity to
provide comments to the U.S. Department of Health and Human Services (HHS) in response to
the Health Data, Technology, and Interoperability: ASTP/ONC Deregulatory Actions to Unleash
Prosperity (HTI-5) proposed rule, which builds off of a series of HTI rules published since 2023.

The AADA represents more than 17,500 dermatologists nationwide who are committed to
excellence in the medical and surgical treatment of skin disease; advocating for high standards
in clinical practice, education, and research in dermatology and dermatopathology; and driving
continuous improvement in patient care and outcomes while reducing the burden of disease.
The AADA appreciates HHS efforts to reduce burden, provide flexibility to physicians, and
support innovation through the removal and revision of certain criteria and regulatory
provisions as well as to clarify information blocking definitions. Although the bulk of the
proposed rule focuses on information technology (IT) developers and vendors, we
identified several potential issues impacting dermatologists, as listed below.

Proposed Removal/Revision of Certification Criteria
The proposed rule outlines de-regulating the Health IT Certification Program by removing or
otherwise streamlining requirements viewed as overly burdensome on health IT developers,
misaligned with well-established health IT products on the market, as well as promote
innovation and lower costs. The rule includes eliminating or revising almost 70% of
requirements for the Health IT Certification Program (cutting 34 and revising 7 out of 60
requirements). While the proposed removal/revision of certification criteria will primarily impact
health IT developers, we urge ASTP/ONC to reflect upon how these changes could impact
patients, dermatologists and other health care providers. Factors to consider include:

 ASTP/ONC proposes to revise the Patient Demographics certification criteria so that
certified technology would no longer be required to capture sexual orientation, gender
identity, and sex parameter for clinical use. Patient demographics such as these can
directly impact the provision of appropriate and safe care. Dermatologists prescribe
treatment with reproductive safety implications (e.g., oral retinoids, spironolactone,
tetracycline) and an accurate documentation of sex assigned at birth and gender identity
is vital for risk assessment, counseling and documentation. Inconsistent capture across
electronic health records (EHRs) increases the chances of clinical miscommunication and
patient harm. ASTP/ONC is urged to retain these criteria and re-institute these
criteria in the United States Core Data for Interoperability. Should these criteria be
removed, ASTP/ONC is asked to develop guidance on capturing patient
demographic data across EHRs to alleviate risk of medical errors resulting from
incomplete collection of such data.

 Some certification criteria are being proposed for removal (e.g., criteria related to use of
consolidated clinical data architecture) because the industry is working towards Fast
Healthcare Interoperability Resources-based or more modern solutions (e.g., the Care
Plan criteria). There is concern that removal of the criteria prior to implementing new
standards creates an informational gap. ASTP/ONC is asked to develop a seamless
transition plan prior to removal of the criteria to minimize potential clinical care
disruptions.

 As recently as 2024, ONC adopted criteria that requires certified IT developers to provide
"model cards" or "nutrition labels" detailing artificial intelligence (AI) algorithm data,
biases, and performance to clinicians. However, ASTP/ONC is now proposing
to remove these specific transparency requirements for predictive decision support
interventions. For clinicians using AI to complement clinical care, “model cards” provide
essential information on training data, intended use, limitations, and performance. This
proposal would eliminate a critical layer of accountability, potentially creating risks related

 ASTP/ONC notes in some areas, specific functionality has been so widely adopted that the
criteria are no longer necessary (e.g., Patient Health Information Capture, Automated
Measure Calculation, etc.). In these cases, ASTP/ONC does not believe that removal of the
criteria will result in decreased functionality of Health IT products. While we support
reducing unnecessary criteria, we urge ASTP/ONC to establish a clear transition plan prior
to removing any criteria. To ensure interoperability across systems, ASTP/ONC is
asked to develop a clear transition plan that assesses the impact of the changes to
ensure there are no data or clinical care gaps as a result of eliminating the criteria.

 Additionally, ASTP/ONC is called upon to ensure that such deregulation does not make
reporting by dermatologists, other physicians, and health care providers more difficult or
result in key information gaps. We recommend ASTP/ONC coordinate with CMS to
ensure these criteria support CMS provider reporting requirements. We urge
ASTP/ONC to refrain from removing or revising these criteria until both initiatives
are aligned.

Updates to Information Blocking Regulations
ASTP/ONC is proposing more stringent rules related to information blocking by revising or
removing certain terms, conditions and exceptions that it believes are being misused or abused.
While the proposals are geared primarily toward health IT vendors, there are concerns whether
these proposals will inadvertently make it more challenging or confusing for physicians to
comply with information blocking regulations. We support access and exchange of electronic
health information (EHI) in a manner that protects access to EHI while not creating excessive
burdens on physicians.

In support of clarity, ASTP/ONC is urged to prioritize education for physicians related to
information blocking. This includes plain language regulatory guidance and FAQs, advanced
notification of perceived information blocking, and working with practices to facilitate corrective
action before penalizing practices. Physicians should have a meaningful opportunity to address
