Executive Summary
HTI-5 constitutes a crucial opportunity to transition the focus of national policy from compliance-
based interoperability toward performance-driven, AI-ready infrastructure. We support the
streamlining of overly prescriptive certification and reporting requirements. However, regulatory
simplification must be coupled with measurable performance standards and clear governance
articulation.
We formally recommend that the HTI-5 rule-making process: 1. Replace checklist-based certification methodologies with outcome-based interoperability
validation. 2. Explicitly recognize federated trust architectures as a preferred pathway for modernization. 3. Clarify definitively that governance-based safeguards do not constitute information blocking. 4. Integrate expectations for AI transparency and auditability in conjunction with deregulation. 5. Elevate the improvement of data quality as a national priority for interoperability reform.
The United States possesses ample data access; the current deficit lies in a durable trust infrastructure
and consistent performance accountability. HTI-5 is positioned to address both deficiencies.

I. Transition from Prescriptive Compliance to Measurable Performance
We endorse ONC’s initiatives to reduce administrative burden where existing requirements
do not substantively advance interoperability. At the same time, reducing prescriptive elements must
not result in diminished accountability for real-world performance.
Certification reform should move toward performance-based validation that reduces
administrative overhead while emphasizing demonstrated API availability, real-world FHIR
conformance, and measurable timeliness of authorized data access. Interoperability should be assessed
based on whether certified capabilities function reliably in operational environments—not solely on
whether those capabilities are documented for certification purposes.
Over the past decade, our team has encountered multiple, certified health IT systems in which
capabilities represented in certification documentation could not be implemented effectively in real-
world settings. In some cases, this created significant implementation delays and resource burdens for
providers, public health partners, and interface teams attempting to operationalize data exchange.
These experiences suggest that documentation-based compliance alone may be insufficient to ensure
meaningful interoperability outcomes.
A performance-based approach would better align certification with actual use, improve
accountability for certified functionality, and help ensure that federal investments support systems that
reliably meet the operational needs of patients, providers, and public health agencies. In this way,
effort and investment can shift from demonstrating paper compliance toward validating functionality
that materially advances interoperability.

II. AI Enablement Necessitates Governance, Not Solely Access
HTI-5 appropriately seeks to accelerate innovation, including the development and
deployment of AI-enabled health applications, however, AI-readiness requires infrastructure
capabilities extending beyond mere expansion of data access.
Interoperability infrastructure must robustly support provenance tracking, reproducibility of
results, maintenance of audit logs for AI-generated outputs, and clear delineation between clinical
decision support and autonomous decision-making. Transparency concerning intended use and model
domain is indispensable for sustaining public trust. Governance frameworks must explicitly address
the persistent risk of reidentification, particularly when AI entities merge health data with publicly
available datasets, ensuring this risk is acknowledged and mitigated within required access controls
and auditability standards.
We formally recommend that ONC integrate baseline AI governance expectations alongside
deregulatory reforms. Innovation and accountability are not mutually exclusive; within the context of
health care, they are inherently interdependent.

III. Federated Trust-Based Infrastructure and Public Health Modernization
HTI-5 should provide explicit recognition of federated architectures as a preferred model for
modernization.
Disparate data aggregation models inherently introduce elevated operational risks, political
complexities, and scalability constraints. Federated Health Data Unit models enable jurisdictions to
maintain requisite control over dataset availability, refresh frequency, and governance policies, while
simultaneously facilitating cross-state analytics through secure, distributed query mechanisms.
 Such architectures can inherently support granular, role-based permissions, explicit purpose-
of-use validation, output controls (e.g., small-cell suppression), and comprehensive auditability. These
features do not impede interoperability; rather, they enable responsible and sustainable
interoperability.
Federal policy should therefore prioritize encouraging interoperable coordination layers over
the mandated development of centralized repositories.
HTI-5 presents a vital opportunity to strengthen public health interoperability beyond
traditional episodic reporting models.
Effective public health practice requires longitudinal, reproducible analytics supported by
standardized data normalization pathways, distributed query capabilities, and measurable
improvements in data timeliness and completeness. Modernization efforts should facilitate robust
cross-jurisdiction collaboration without imposing the requirement of central custody of data.
Deregulation must therefore facilitate sustainable public health infrastructure, not merely
transactional data exchange mechanisms.

IV. Clarification of Information Blocking Safe Harbors
We support the continued rigorous enforcement of information blocking prohibitions.
Concurrently, HTI-5 must clarify that governance safeguards implemented to uphold privacy, security,
and ethical data utilization standards do not constitute unlawful interference.
Specifically, the following practices should be explicitly recognized as permissible when
appropriately structured and applied:
• Governance-based query review processes
• Formal purpose-of-use validation
• Strict role-based access restrictions
• Small cell suppression and data output thresholds
• Mandatory audit logging requirements
Absent clear regulatory safe harbors, organizations may exhibit reticence in implementing
appropriate oversight mechanisms due to potential regulatory concern. Deregulation must remove
ambiguity that discourages responsible stewardship of health data.

V. Elevation of Data Quality as a National Priority
Interoperability conducted without adequate data quality inherently produces scalable systemic
error. While the scope of data quality is broad, the need for semantic standardization is a critical
starting point, as illustrated by the following common example: Type 2 diabetes mellitus in one system,
T2DM in another, and a diagnosis code of E11 in a third are all the same condition. This variation
prevents systems from talking to each other, leading to errors in aggregating relevant records.
HTI-5 would benefit significantly from formally recognizing data quality measurement and
improvement as foundational to interoperability performance. Establishing baseline quality metrics,
implementing recurring remediation cycles, and mandating transparency regarding data completeness
and normalization status can measurably strengthen outcomes without increasing unnecessary
bureaucratic burden.
Performance-based accountability in data quality constitutes a profoundly pro-innovation
reform.

Conclusion
HTI-5 offers a meaningful and timely opportunity to modernize the United States’ health data
infrastructure by reducing unnecessary regulatory friction while concurrently strengthening
performance expectations.
We respectfully urge ONC to undertake the following actions:

1.  Replace prescriptive certification requirements with measurable interoperability outcomes.
2.  Encourage federated trust-based architectures that respect and preserve state-level control.
3.  Articulate clear governance safe harbors within information blocking regulations.
4.  Embed requirements for AI transparency and auditability alongside deregulation.
5.  Elevate data quality as central to the overall interoperability reform agenda.
    Durable innovation fundamentally requires durable trust. HTI-5 is poised to advance both
    objectives.
