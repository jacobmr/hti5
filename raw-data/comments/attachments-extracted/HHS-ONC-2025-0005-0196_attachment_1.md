Dear Dr. Keane:

On behalf of the over 93,000 members of the American College of Surgeons (ACS), we
appreciate the opportunity to submit comments to the Assistant Secretary for Technology
Policy/Office of the National Coordinator for Health Information Technology (ASTP/ONC)
Health Data, Technology, and Interoperability: ASTP/ONC Deregulatory Actions to Unleash
Prosperity (HTI-5) proposed rule on December 29, 2025.

The ACS is a scientific and educational association of surgeons founded in 1913 to improve the
quality of care for the surgical patient by setting high standards for surgical education and
practice. The ACS is dedicated to improving the care of the surgical patient and setting standards
of care in an optimal and ethical practice environment. As such, we understand the critical role
technology plays in achieving this mission. This includes the need for policy to ensure health
information technology (health IT) supports high-quality patient care, maintains the security of
patients’ electronic health information (EHI), and provides support to care teams and other
stakeholders to drive positive change. With our decades of experience aggregating and evaluating
data and leveraging these data to support implementation of quality programs, we believe we can
offer key strategic insights to ASTP/ONC.

The modern electronic health record (EHR) emerged primarily to digitize documentation of
individual medical services for the purpose of billing. That origin constrained what the EHR
became—but it also opened an extraordinary window of opportunity. ASTP/ONC’s HTI-5
proposed rule, with its emphasis on fast healthcare interoperability resources (FHIR)-forward
modernization and the dismantling of information blocking, represents a pivotal moment to move
beyond those original constraints and to realize the EHR’s unfulfilled potential as a platform for
clinical intelligence, quality improvement, and patient-centered care.

We commend the Agency for this ambition. At the same time, the ACS urges ASTP/ONC to consider the
following points to guide their deregulatory actions:

    1. Health IT must evolve from a documentation system into an open, standards-based platform that
       enables diverse expertise—clinical specialists, quality improvement teams, patient advocates, population
       health researchers, and fiscal analysts—to build upon a shared foundation of interoperable clinical data.
       For too long, proprietary barriers have confined digital innovation to the vendor’s roadmap rather than
       enabling the ingenuity of the clinical and research communities who understand the problems most
       intimately.
    2. Deregulation must be sequenced to protect the integrity of the existing quality infrastructure while
       the bridge to FHIR-native solutions is built; the surgical patient cannot afford a gap in the continuity of
       data-driven quality measurement.
    3. The ultimate measure of success for any certification change must be whether it advances the
       interest of the patient—not merely the efficiency of the vendor or the convenience of the regulator.

These are not abstract aspirations. Instead, they offer practical considerations through which the ACS evaluates
the certification criterion proposed for removal, timeline for FHIR transition, and revision to the information
blocking rules. The House of Surgery has invested heavily in data registries, episode-based quality measurement,
and decision support tools that depend on reliable, interoperable data flows. We offer the comments that follow in
that spirit—as a constructive partner to ASTP/ONC in building a patient-centered health IT ecosystem. Our
detailed comments are below.

HEALTH INFORMATION TECHNOLOGY STANDARDS, IMPLEMENTATION SPECIFICATIONS,
AND CERTIFICATION CRITERIA AND CERTIFICATION PROGRAMS FOR HEALTH
INFORMATION TECHNOLOGY

Certification Criteria for Health IT

ASTP/ONC has implemented numerous certification criteria for health IT to ensure that products meet
technological capability, functionality, and security requirements as part of the ONC Health IT Certification
Program. This program also designates criteria for Certified Electronic Health Record Technology (CEHRT)
established by the Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS). The CEHRT definitions incorporate the
Base EHR definition and may also incorporate certification criteria necessary to report applicable objectives and
measures under the Medicare Promoting Interoperability (PI) Program, the PI performance category in the Merit-
based Incentive Payment System (MIPS), certification criteria determined applicable for an Alternative Payment
Model (APM), and other certification criteria. To participate in the PI Program and the MIPS PI performance
category, eligible hospitals, critical access hospitals (CAHs), and eligible clinicians must use CEHRT as specified
by the applicable definition. In addition, CEHRT is required for the purposes of determinations regarding
Advanced APM status. ASTP/ONC tracks and publishes data on the adoption and use of health IT, including
CEHRT, by United States (US) hospitals and office-based physicians.

There are currently sixty certification criteria included in the ONC Health IT Certification Program. Within this
proposed rule, ASTP/ONC proposes the removal of thirty-four certification criteria for health IT and updates
seven of the existing criteria based on analysis of standards that are out of date, have become a market baseline,
have not been adopted over the years, or have minimal benefit for providers or patients. Overall, the ACS is
supportive of ASTP/ONC’s efforts to modernize its programs through a “FHIR-forward” strategy. The
College maintains that keeping pace with the rapidly evolving digital environment through use of
standards-based application programming interfaces (APIs) is important to continue to improve the flow

and availability of clinical data for optimal patient care. However, we have several questions and concerns
about how ASTP/ONC plans to achieve this goal without an actionable blueprint provided in this rule.
Consistent with our second point described above that deregulation must be sequenced to protect existing
quality infrastructure, the ASTP/ONC has taken steps to remove many important and useful certification

Deregulation must not dismantle the progress made with existing infrastructure before the bridge to the
future is built. We provide further details regarding our questions and concerns below:

Overarching Comments:

For numerous certification criterion, ASTP/ONC cites the reason for removal as FHIR-based solutions and/or new
functionalities that are better suited to achieve ASTP/ONCs goals of furthering industry innovation, reduced
burden, and cost. As stated, the ACS is supportive of transitioning to FHIR and ASTP/ONC’s goals to
implement modern technologies, but we are concerned with the lack of clarity around ASTP/ONC’s plans
to remove existing certification criteria without a mandated, mature FHIR replacement that is accessible to
not only the health IT system but third-party vendors that interact with health IT. For example, many
clinical data registries still rely heavily on Consolidated Clinical Document Architecture (C-CDAs) to transmit
quality data. By removing criteria around abilities to transmit C-CDAs without a mature FHIR-based solution that
can be feasibly integrated into registry systems, this could destabilize the quality measurement infrastructure that
the surgical community and others have built over decades. The risk is not theoretical: the ACS National Surgical
Quality Improvement Program (NSQIP) and other specialty registries depend on reliable data pipelines whose
disruption would directly harm patient-level quality measurement. Ultimately, we are concerned that without a
clear pathway toward these transitions there will be breakdowns in the flow of data. Likewise, ASTP/ONC
proposes to remove all Privacy and Security Certification Criteria as the Agency believes the costs and burden of
these requirements outweigh their intended purpose and benefits. The ACS supports the prioritization of privacy
and security measures that are fit for purpose, use case specific, and consistent with other market requirements,
but we are concerned that such capabilities do not yet exist. While we understand the removal of outdated
requirements is necessary to continue to advance the program, we recommend ASTP/ONC create timelines
around how they will transition to new certification criteria and functionalities for health IT that reduce
gaps between removal of existing criteria and implementation of FHIR-based solutions.

It is also important that ASTP/ONC create mechanisms to monitor whether new standards are being implemented
and used as intended without adverse impact on the clinical community. Loosening and tightening requirements
for health IT vendors will inevitably require numerous, significant updates to provider systems, and we are
concerned about unintended consequences, such as unreasonable costs being passed down to physician practices
in order to update or maintain existing systems. The vision of an open platform loses its promise if the cost of
connecting to it falls disproportionately on the clinicians and specialty organizations who stand to benefit most
from interoperable data. To this end, we urge ASTP/ONC to implement policies that ensure the costs of
building and maintaining API connectors do not disproportionately fall on end-users, including individual
surgeons' practices. The financial and technical burden of this industry-wide pivot must be shared
equitably and not become another unfunded mandate for clinicians.

In addition, multiple criteria proposed for removal are incorporated in CMS incentive programs, such as the MIPS
PI category and the Medicare PI Program. Within this proposed rule, ASTP/ONC states that it will delay the
removal of these criteria to ensure CMS has time to update program requirements. While we appreciate efforts to
give CMS time to adjust PI programs, we recommend a broader re-evaluation of the PI programs and their
efficacy. The PI programs were implemented to increase adoption of certified health IT and standardization across
3
the health care system and ensure the meaningful use of health IT in clinical practice. Just as ASTP/ONC are
proposing to modernize the Certification Program due to shifting goals and evolving standards, it is also time for
ASTP/ONC to work with CMS to modernize the PI programs so that they better reflect progress made in terms of
adoption and use of CEHRT, and better recognize the evolving health IT needs of the future. Some examples
might be to incorporate measures that acknowledge responsible use of artificial intelligence (AI)-based
applications and reward evolving use cases of open, interoperable data exchange.

In conclusion, it is important that ASTP/ONC create a two-fold strategy that can address the immediate
technological needs of the healthcare system, while also aligning with its strategy to continue to move the US
healthcare data infrastructure into the future. It is easy to identify the downfalls of maintaining a data
infrastructure that is based on C-CDAs, but ASTP/ONC must also acknowledge that there are barriers to the real-
world implementation of FHIR and make strides to create a more mature FHIR ecosystem that can be realized by
all stakeholders. ASTP/ONC must also realize that beyond accelerating the FHIR transition, the development of
advanced AI technologies for health care is moving rapidly and must remain top of mind as it makes major
updates to requirements for certified health IT.

Care Coordination Certification Criteria

Decision Support Interventions

ASTP/ONC proposes to revise various elements of the “decision support interventions” (DSI) certification
criterion. This certification criterion ensures that users can leverage health IT for clinical decision-making by
supporting their selection of both evidence-based and predictive DSIs while enabling access to transparent
information on DSI performance and quality. As more AI-based predictive and decision support tools are adopted
across clinical care, the need for transparency is paramount. If the EHR is to evolve into an open platform upon
which AI-driven clinical intelligence is built, then transparency about the algorithms running on that platform is
not optional – it is foundational. Therefore, the ACS does not support the removal of the requirements within
this criterion around providing source attribute support, access, and modification, as well as requirements
to manage risks related to the development and deployment of predictive DSIs. The implementation of these
requirements, which have been referred to as “model cards” were a major step toward providing more transparent
information around AI algorithm data, biases, and performance to clinicians. ASTP/ONC states that certified
health IT vendors have reported that during care delivery, most clinical users lack the time or technical
background to engage with source attribute information for either evidence-based DSIs or predictive DSIs
supplied by the developer of certified health IT. While this might be true to some extent, this type of information
is still relatively new and foreign to clinical users. However, given the speed at which AI is evolving and being
incorporated into the practice of medicine, this is information they will inevitably need to learn how to navigate.
If anything, they need more experience r to become comfortable with it, not less. Ultimately, while we understand
that there may be room to improve the usability of these data, the removal of this requirement without a more
usable solution seems extremely premature and is a step backward in efforts to create more transparency
around AI-based tools.

Increased AI transparency is required to gain the clinical community’s trust in AI-based tools. Removing this
requirement signals to the physician community that easing developer burden is a higher priority than building
trust and confidence among end users. It also risks shifting the burden to clinicians, who will be forced to evaluate
and understand the inner workings of DSIs without adequate support. As more AI-based tools are integrated into
clinical care, ASTP/ONC must have a strategy to incorporate evaluation and transparency requirements into its
certification criteria that can keep pace with the evolving digital landscape. To that end, the ACS recommends

that ONC/ASTP maintain these requirements. The ACS also urges the Agency to work with the clinician
community to better understand what information would be of value to clinicians. Creating a public-facing
mechanism that offers targeted information that meets the needs of numerous stakeholders with varying
expertise in AI-based tools—not just those with an understanding of the technical components of evidence-

adoption of these tools. The surgical community, with its deep tradition of morbidity and mortality review,
understands better than most that clinical accountability requires transparent information about the tools upon
which decisions are made.

Conditions and Maintenance of Certification Requirements for Health IT Developers

Insights

Within this proposed rule, ASTP/ONC makes several edits to the Conditions and Maintenance of Certification
requirements, including the Insight Condition which will now be referred to as “Insights.” Currently, the Insights
Condition includes seven measures on which health IT developers must annually report under the Certification
Program. However, ASTP/ONC proposes to remove or descope six of the existing measures within the Insights
Condition and limit reporting to the “use of FHIR in apps through certified health IT” measure. These measures
were initially implemented in compliance with section 4002(c) of the 21st Century Cures Act, which specifies
requirements to establish an EHR Reporting Program to provide reporting on certified health IT in the categories
of interoperability, usability and user-centered design, security, conformance to certification testing, and other
categories as appropriate to measure the performance of EHR technology. The EHR Reporting Program is
intended to serve the critical role of: 1) addressing information gaps in the health IT marketplace, 2) providing
insights on the use of specific certified health IT functionalities, and (3) providing information about use of
certified functionalities by end users. However, ASTP/ONC is proposing to drastically scale it back before it has
even been given the opportunity to demonstrate its value.

The ACS has concerns about the scope of these proposed reductions. The implementation of the Insights
Condition was an important step toward marketplace transparency—a principle that is reinforced by the 21st
Century Cures Act. If the Agency is committed to an open platform model for health IT, it must retain the ability
to assess whether that platform is functioning as intended but also provide end users with meaningful information
that they can use when making decisions about investing in these products. We recommend that ASTP/ONC
carefully evaluate which Insights measures remain necessary to monitor the health of the FHIR ecosystem
and the degree to which interoperability goals are being realized in practice, not merely in specification.
Vendor reporting requirements, while they may draw pushback, are an essential feedback loop for the regulatory
process itself.

Information Blocking

“Access” and “Use” Definition

ASTP/ONC proposes to revise the definitions of “access” and “use” in the federal code for information blocking
to emphasize that the definitions include automated means of access, exchange, or use of EHI— including,
without limitation, autonomous AI systems. This proposal specifies that access means the ability or means
necessary to make EHI available for exchange or use, including by automation technologies such as robotic
process automation and autonomous AI systems. It also specifies that “use” means that EHI, once accessed or

                                                                                                                     5

exchanged (through whatever technological means), can be understood and acted upon without limitation, by
automation technologies such as autonomous AI systems and robotic process automation.

The ACS thanks ASTP/ONC for this revision, which may prove to be among the most consequential
provisions in the proposed rule. It provides the necessary regulatory protection for deploying AI agents to
query EHRs for clinical data without the risk of being blocked by an EHR vendor as a potential security
threat. By clarifying that automated systems have the same right to access EHI as human users, ASTP/ONC
removes a barrier that has constrained innovation by clinical organizations and third-party developers alike. For
example, this can open pathways to leverage AI agents to construct surgical episodes or identify adverse events
within EHR systems. For organizations focused on data analytics, this validates a technical roadmap based on
scalable automation over slow, costly manual abstraction and solidifies the legal foundation for using AI to power
next-generation quality improvement.

While we are supportive of this revision, we also want to highlight that there must be continued efforts to
maintain cybersecurity protection. First, ASTP/ONC should provide clear guidance that communicates that actors
subject to HIPAA must continue to comply with HIPAA when providing automated access/exchange/use, and that
implementing automation-specific safeguards to satisfy HIPAA and security obligations is appropriate and
expected. The ACS recommends that ASTP/ONC develop guidance stating that actors may implement and
enforce reasonable, consistently applied security controls for automated access/use without such controls
being presumed to “interfere” for information blocking purposes, provided they are tailored to objective risk and
applied in a non-discriminatory manner.

Infeasibility Exception Revisions

Third Party Seeking Modification Use Condition

ASTP/ONC proposes to remove the Third-Party Seeking Modification Use condition to prevent abuse by actors,
such as EHR developers, from unnecessarily inhibiting access, exchange, and use of EHI by their competitors or
other third parties. The Agency believes the condition is unnecessary and removing it will better support the
access, exchange, and use of EHI. ASTP/ONC also cites that it has found that there are confusion and misuse of
this condition. The Agency also believes that some actors may be misapplying the condition or intentionally
abusing customers’ and third parties’ misunderstandings of the condition or, in the case of business associates, the
flexibility to confirm whether a third-party modification use request is actually from their HIPAA-regulated
health care provider customer. ASTP/ONC also cites concerns that this condition is being abused by EHR
developers to limit third parties, including other actual or potential business associates of the EHR developer’s
health care provider customer, from accessing or using a health care provider’s EHR and relevant EHI.

The ACS is supportive of this proposal, and thanks ASTP/ONC for actively monitoring and addressing
areas where actors are misusing exceptions to block the flow of EHI. ASTP/ONC has observed substantial
growth in technical approaches that enable efficient, appropriately secure modification use, as well as two-way
exchange interactions between different developers’ systems. Therefore, it is not reasonable or necessary for an
actor to dismiss these requests as “infeasible” without engaging in information gathering or analysis to work
through an alternative manner.

When actors cite this exception to refuse access and exchange of data with third-party applications, they create a
one-way data flow where information is generated outside the clinical workflow but cannot be integrated into
clinical data systems. By removing this exception, the flow of data can officially “close the loop.” Closing the

                                                                                                                     6

loop means that external platforms can write patient-generated goals, patient reported outcome measures
(PROMs), or risk assessments directly into the clinical workflow for the care team to act upon. It means the
surgical patient’s own voice—their goals, their functional outcomes, their experience—can become part of the
clinical record, not an afterthought stored in an external system. This is the kind of patient-centered innovation
that a truly open health IT ecosystem should enable, and the ACS commends ASTP/ONC for taking a
decisive step in this direction.

Manner Exception

To further explain the applicability of the Manner Exception where the actor has the technical capability and is
able to reach agreement with the requestor to fulfill access, exchange, or use of requested EHI in a manner
requested by the requestor, ASTP/ONC proposes to revise the “manner requested” condition of the Manner
Exception to provide enhanced certainty that the Manner Exception does not cover any contracts, agreements, or
licenses that: are not at market rate, are contracts of adhesion, or include unconscionable terms.

The ACS supports these revisions. The proposed changes to the Manner Exception reinforce the principle that if
an efficient, standards-based pathway to data exists, a data source should not be permitted to force a partner to use
a less efficient method. This is particularly relevant for organizations that seek to ingest core FHIR resources—
such as Problem Lists and Procedures—to longitudinally construct surgical episodes of care. The ability to access
data via FHIR Bulk Data Access APIs, without being steered toward more costly or cumbersome alternatives, is
essential to making the open platform vision a practical reality for surgical quality measurement.

Improved Educational Materials to Better Support Compliance

While the ACS is supportive of tightening information blocking requirements to address bad actors, we note that
increased regulatory complexity may create confusion for health care providers who are also subject to these
rules. We ask ASTP/ONC to develop clear, accessible guidance and resources to help healthcare providers
navigate updates to the information blocking regulations, ensuring that the compliance burden does not
inadvertently discourage the very data sharing these rules are designed to promote.

The ACS appreciates ASTP/ONC’s willingness to undertake this comprehensive modernization effort. We
recognize that the choices before the Agency are genuinely difficult: maintaining legacy infrastructure carries its
own costs, and the status quo serves no one’s long-term interests. The ACS looks forward to opportunities to
collaborate with ASTP/ONC to help ensure that the bridge to a FHIR-native, AI-ready, patient-centered health IT
ecosystem is built with clinical input, rigor, and intentionality. Please contact Jill Sage, Chief of Quality Affairs,
at jsage@facs.org, with any questions.
