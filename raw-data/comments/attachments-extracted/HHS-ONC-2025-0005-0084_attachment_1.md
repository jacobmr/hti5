Deregulatory Certification Changes and Transition to FHIR-Based APIs
AdvaMed supports efforts to streamline outdated or redundant certification criteria
and to modernize interoperability around FHIR-based APIs. Reducing unnecessary
compliance burdens can create capacity for innovation and accelerate the
deployment of advanced medical technologies.
We urge ONC to consider the downstream effects of narrowing certification scope to
primarily FHIR-based APIs and removing certain non-API certification criteria and
referenced standards. Imaging and other clinical ecosystems remain dependent on
a mix of standards and transport approaches, including HL7 v2 messaging in
multiple settings and Direct-based exchange mechanisms (including XDR/XDM in

Inclusion of AI and Automated Systems in “Access” and “Use” Definitions
HTI-5 proposes to revise the definitions of “access” and “use” to emphasize that
automated means of access, exchange, or use of EHI, including autonomous AI
systems, are included within the scope of information blocking
AdvaMed recognizes the importance of ensuring that AI-enabled systems do not
become vehicles for improper restriction of information. However, applying identical
information blocking treatment to both human-initiated application access and
backend, automated AI processing may create unintended compliance burdens.
Many AI-enabled medical technologies operate as headless backend services that
ingest EHI for model inference without direct user interaction, process data
transiently without retaining full clinical context, and interface across both certified
and non-certified systems. Treating all automated ingestion as equivalent to
patient- or provider-facing application access could create ambiguity regarding
actor status under the information blocking regulations, allocation of responsibility
for downstream data uses, and compliance obligations within complex multi-party
ecosystems.
ONC should provide additional clarification on how actor responsibilities apply in
multi-system AI environments, distinguish between automated clinical processing
and end-user application access for compliance purposes, and confirm that
developers of FDA-regulated AI-enabled medical devices are not subject to
duplicative oversight where FDA already governs safety and effectiveness.
Revisions to the Manner Exception and Emphasis on “Analogous” Access
AdvaMed supports ONC’s efforts to promote pro-competitive access to EHI. The
proposed replacement of “same” with “analogous” access in the Manner Exception
may appropriately discourage preferential treatment of embedded or proprietary
solutions when similar access is available to third parties.
Ensuring that internal applications are not favored over external developers aligns
with long-standing goals of fair and open interoperability.
However, narrowing the availability of the Manner Exception and increasing the
burden to justify differential access may introduce practical challenges for both EHR
