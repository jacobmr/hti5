Official Comment: HTI-5 Proposed Rule
The Alliance submits this official comment in response to the HTI-5 Proposed Rule. Our commentary is
derived from the aggregated experiences and preferences of our ~100 members, who are patients, caregivers, and
everyday citizens: the “end users” of American healthcare. About 5 members are citizens of other countries,
whom we asked to share their perspectives as well, as we believed they would possess valuable comparative
perspectives.

The open-ended survey questions we shared with members can be found in an appendix below. All analysis of
the survey was completed by members of the Alliance Strategic Office. All member responses were manually
reviewed.

I. Members largely prioritize safety and transparency over accelerated
innovation
In the proposed rule, the agency justifies removing transparency requirements by asserting they "may not have
the savings and benefits we anticipated" and citing developer complaints that the requirements are "burdensome
and detrimental to such innovation." Members of the Alliance largely did not want to deprioritize
patient safety and algorithmic transparency.

Over 75% of Alliance members expressed moderate or severe concern regarding medical errors caused by AI and
software. Our members repeatedly highlighted the risks of AI "hallucinations," biased training data, and the
fundamental lack of interpretability in AI models, which undermined their confidence that AI would or could
be used responsibly in a healthcare context. One member voiced concern over “biases within training sets and
historical, proven discrepancies in care afforded to different racial groups.” Another member stated, "AI is an
incomparably more complicated piece of technology... without a detailed understanding of the inner workings
of a tool we use, I believe errors are more likely."

However, roughly 15% of members reported low concern, often attributing this to a belief that AI could
ultimately outperform humans, improving overall productivity. One member explained that “in general [they]
believe that AI and software will make fewer errors than humans.” Many members stated that their AI stance
was contingent “on if they were using it as a primary diagnostic tool or as a second opinion,” indicating that if
AI responses are always verified by humans, they would be more willing to involve AI in their care.

Without substantial additional public education, it seems likely that the benefits of innovation will be perceived
by many end users to be less important than patient safety.
II. Shifting the evaluatory burden to hospitals may decrease consumer
confidence
The agency's proposal notes that "clinical users lack the time or technical background to engage with source
attribute information" and therefore proposes eliminating the requirement for developers to provide it.
However, the perspective of one expert —Dr. Nigam Shah, Professor of Medicine at Stanford University and
Chief Data Scientist for Stanford Health Care—we consulted in advance of our member survey was that
removing developer transparency simply externalizes the burden of evaluating AI models.

When surveyed, a majority of our members stated that their confidence in the accuracy and robustness
of clinical AI would decrease if the task of evaluation shifted from developers to hospitals. Alliance
members challenged the agency's logic for eliminating transparency requirements:

    ●​ Misalignment of expertise: One member pointed out that "hospital staff are medical experts, not
         software experts. An AI tool is basically a black box for non-specialists." Another member added, “Most
         hospitals do not have the same level of technical expertise, time, or resources as AI developers to
         conduct rigorous validation from scratch.” Members largely believe that providers, who are already
         experiencing historic levels of administrative burnout, do not possess the specialized software
         engineering expertise or access to proprietary information necessary to validate these algorithms
         from scratch.
    ●​ Exacerbating health inequities: Some members believe this shift will create a bifurcated, inequitable
         healthcare system. While large, well-resourced academic hospitals might afford in-house data scientists
         to test AI models, smaller, rural, and community hospitals may not be able to. As one member noted,
         "Big research hospitals might handle it fine, but smaller ones won’t, so safety ends up depending on
         where you get treated.”

On the other hand, nearly 28% of members stated that a shift in regulatory responsibilities from developers to
hospitals would make them more confident in clinical AI. These members argued that hospitals have valuable
medical knowledge that could allow for better alignment of patient outcomes than AI developers. One member
stated that a shift in regulatory responsibilities from developers to hospitals would make them “more confident
because hospitals are more qualified and more objective than AI developers.” Another believes that “hospitals
and healthcare workers will have increased or additional perspective and nuance on the responses of AI.”
However, it is unclear if removing model cards will cause hospitals to perform this kind of evaluation more
thoroughly than they would otherwise, especially because they will have less access to proprietary information.

In addition, some members advocated for collaborative evaluation. One member wrote: "AI developers AND
hospitals should jointly do AI system evaluation. More evaluation is better. AI developers know how their
models perform, the architecture, why it does what it does. Hospitals know the impacts of Type 1 vs Type 2
errors. It should be collaborative."
Given a range of member preferences that generally suggest that rollbacks in transparency disclosures
will be perceived negatively by the public, we encourage the agency to either:

    1.​ Abandon the proposed rollback of mandatory transparency disclosures.
    2.​ Invest substantially in communications about the benefits of the rollback to end users.

 Appendix A: Survey questions
Question 1: What is your current level of concern, if any, about medical errors caused by AI and software?

Question 2: Without “model cards,” the task of evaluating AI systems will shift from AI developers to
hospitals. Would this shift make you more, less, or equally confident in the accuracy and robustness of the AI
models used in clinical practices? Why?
