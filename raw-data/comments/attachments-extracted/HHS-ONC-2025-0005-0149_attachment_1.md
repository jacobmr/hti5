Section HL7 Recommendations and Perspectives
Guidance, Guardrails and Continued Support for Standards Harmonization:
HL7 is pleased that HTI-5 represents a significant policy pivot from certification-
driven interoperability assurance toward market-driven accountability. While this
transition enables innovation and lowers barriers for developers, it also transfers risk
to providers, networks and exchange participants who still depend on document-based
exchange. HL7 encourages ASTP/ONC to pair deregulation with clear transition
guidance, guardrails and continued support for standards harmonization to prevent
fragmentation during the industry’s evolution from documents to Application
Programming Interfaces (APIs).

              Managing Transition Risks: HL7 supports the strategic direction of HTI-5 while
              urging ASTP/ONC to explicitly manage the transition risks created by reduced
              certification coverage, particularly for document-based exchange that remains
              foundational to healthcare interoperability today.

              (USCDI) Version 3.1 and Removing Outdated Standards: HL7 supports the

Overarching removal of outdated standards and the adoption of United States Core Data for
Thoughts Interoperability (USCDI) Version 3.1, as steps toward modernizing the
interoperability ecosystem.

              Careful Re-examination of Proposed “Remove or Revise” Certification Criteria:
              HL7 emphasizes that a number of certification criteria proposed for removal or
              revision—particularly those related to auditability and data segmentation—serve as
              the necessary “trust rails” and foundational data provenance required to enable safe,
              transparent and accountable Artificial Intelligence (AI) integration in clinical
              workflows. We emphasize also it is not just about legacy support but enabling the
              future. HL7 recommends a careful re-examination of proposed “Remove or Revise”
              certification criteria, particularly for those at the heart of effective and innovative
              healthcare. We highlight issues related to particular certification criteria on pages 6-11
              of this letter.

              FHIR-Based Interoperability Mechanics and Alignment: HL7 supports removing
              outdated, inconsistent and no longer referenced standards as criteria for certification
              and updating base Electronic Health Record (EHR) definitions accordingly. However,
              we strongly recommend including certification criteria to enable baseline functionality
              aligned with the Centers for Medicare and Medicaid (CMS) Interoperability and Prior

          Non-FHIR Standards and C-CDA: HL7 supports and applauds ASTP/ONC’s HTI-5
          focus on FHIR-based interoperability. However, for functional areas where a FHIR
          standard is not fully mature or widely adopted yet, we believe that continuing to
          reference existing non-FHIR standards in certification criteria provides an important
          baseline signal. Regarding C-CDA use and implementation, HL7 importantly
          highlights HTI-5’s de-emphasis of document-centric certification occurs while C-
          CDA exchange remains materially significant in national networks (e.g., Carequality
          reports ~1.2B clinical documents exchanged monthly). Revising criteria to remove
          mention of any standards or removing criteria altogether due to a lack of mature FHIR
          standards, HL7 believes has the potential to destabilize programs such as current
          public health integration programs for example. We suggest that ASTP/ONC could
          retain some certification criteria and return references to non-FHIR standards while
          work continues to advance FHIR maturity and adoption for those functional
          areas. Alternatively, HL7 recommends leveraging other avenues (e.g., the
          Interoperability Standards Advisory) for formally naming standards for functional
          areas if not in health IT certification criteria. HL7 also encourages ASTP/ONC to
          help define and enable a path forward (including reasonable time frames considering
          budgetary and other constraints) toward advancing FHIR standards and adoption for
          those areas. We discuss this further in our comments below.

          Certification Criteria and Administrative Burden, Security and Audit
          Requirements: The proposed removal or consolidation of certification criteria
          reduces administrative burden but shifts greater responsibility to vendor clients to
          ensure their systems operate securely and interoperably in practice. HL7 notes this
          creates risk, if organizations rely solely on minimum compliance rather than adopting
          shared, standards-based implementation approaches.

          The proposed removal or consolidation of certification criteria would enhance the use
          of FHIR. However, the loss of generalized security and audit requirements may have
          strong repercussions for non-FHIR standard use and access of information from
          patients and other stakeholders. HL7 recognizes that consent and the ability to have
          consent records available is critical in enabling patients more ability to control their
          information. Without audit and disclosure requirements, this ability and knowledge
          of the information use is lost.

          HL7 urges a more thorough review of the (d)(1) to (d)(10) Certification Criteria
          requirements to ensure that the ability to maintain patient access and existing (non-
          FHIR) standards isn’t lost. Until and if a universal standard for security and tracking
          would be established, basic standards are needed.


          Design and Performance Certification Criteria - Consolidated Clinical
          Document Architecture (C-CDA) Creation Performance:
          As stated above, HL7 supports the transition to FHIR-based standards. However, we
          emphasize that C-CDA-based standards are still relevant for daily payer, provider
          and other activities and ASTP/ONC should monitor and evaluate the likelihood of
          degradation or elimination of these capabilities, or prohibitive costs, as a result of
          removing from certification criteria. HL7 recommends setting a future date that
          payers and providers can work to ensure FHIR is advanced efficiently and
          effectively.
          Regarding these issues, HL7 notes that:
             •   Retiring C-CDA document centric requirements without a defined timeline for
                 FHIR readiness (including small/rural and post-acute and long-term care) risks
                 disrupted continuity of care. HL7 recommends phased changes based on a
                 rational, defined timeline.
