Dear Assistant Secretary Keane:

The Pharmaceutical Care Management Association (PCMA) appreciates the opportunity to
submit comments on the Health Data, Technology, and Interoperability: ASTP/ONC
Deregulatory Actions to Unleash Prosperity (HTI-5) proposed rule (proposed rule) published by
the Department of Health and Human Services (HHS) Assistant Secretary for Technology
Policy/Office of the National Coordinator of Health Information Technology (ASTP/ONC) on
December 29, 2025. 1

PCMA is the national association representing the country’s pharmacy benefit managers (PBMs),
which administer prescription drug plans (PDPs) and operate specialty pharmacies for more than
289 million Americans with health coverage through Fortune 500 companies, health insurers,
labor unions, Medicare, Medicaid, the Federal Employees Health Benefits Program, and the
Exchanges established by the Affordable Care Act.

PCMA supports ASTP/ONC’s goal to modernize and streamline the ONC Health IT Certification
Program (Certification Program). We recognize the importance of reducing unnecessary
regulatory burden, encouraging innovation, and transitioning toward more scalable, API-driven
interoperability. At the same time, we urge ASTP/ONC to proceed cautiously to avoid further
fragmenting health care and increasing risks for some participants in the health IT ecosystem
during a period of significant transition. PCMA’s members and their health plan clients rely
extensively on interoperable health IT infrastructure to support coverage determinations,
utilization management, care coordination, and secure exchange of medication-related
information with providers and pharmacies. As service providers operating at the intersection of
payers and clinicians, PBMs depend on stable and predictable interoperability standards.
Decisions made in HTI-5 therefore directly affect the ability of PBMs and health plans to
exchange information efficiently, maintain compliance with federal interoperability requirements,
and support patient access to medications.

In summary, we recommend:
• Retaining baseline direct messaging certification capabilities, including delivery
notification functionality, during the transition to FHIR-based exchange.

I. PCMA recommends that ASTP/ONC retain baseline Direct messaging
certification requirements, including delivery notification functionality, until
FHIR-based exchange capabilities are consistently implemented nationwide.

In particular, PCMA is concerned that elements of the proposed deregulatory approach risk
removing foundational interoperability capabilities before a consistent, nationwide FHIR-based
exchange has been fully implemented. In the absence of a uniform and mature FHIR
framework, hybrid exchange models remain operationally necessary for providers, payers, and
their service providers. Overly aggressive deregulation at this stage could delay progress
toward CMS’s broader interoperability goals and weaken trust among market participants who
must make long-term technology investments.

One such concern is the proposed removal of Direct messaging certification requirements under
the ONC Health IT Certification Program. We understand that the intent is to move away from
document-based exchange and toward a more flexible FHIR-based API ecosystem. However,
as a practical matter, Direct messaging functionality supported through certified health IT
remains an essential, operationally embedded exchange mechanism for many providers and
payers. The proposed removal of direct messaging-related certification criteria risks unraveling
a widely deployed electronic communication channel for clinicians and payers to rely on today.

Direct messaging continues to support a wide range of critical workflows, including
administrative coordination, care transitions, coverage determinations, utilization management,
and payer-to-payer communication. These use cases remain particularly important where
standardized APIs are not yet universally available or consistently implemented across
providers and vendors. Until API-based exchange is uniformly operational, direct messaging
continues to serve as a dependable baseline capability that supports continuity and operational
stability.

We are also concerned that removing certification requirements related to direct delivery
notifications would degrade the reliability and safety of electronic exchange. Current certification
standards ensure that direct messages include send and receive delivery notifications, which
provide essential assurance that information has been successfully transmitted and received.
Eliminating these requirements risks increasing operational uncertainty for providers, payers,
and health information service providers, while also introducing potential patient safety risks
during care transitions and medication-related communications. Delivery confirmation is a
foundational element of trusted interoperability and should remain part of baseline certification
expectations.

Another added benefit of retaining direct messaging is that it can decrease the volume of
privacy incidents arising from sending communications containing protected health information
(PHI) to outdated provider addresses. Some organizations have used direct messaging to send
provider communications, thereby avoiding sending information to outdated addresses found in
commercial or governmental databases.

Weakening Direct messaging certification requirements could also disrupt existing industry
alignment efforts, particularly ahead of forthcoming payer-to-payer exchange requirements. In
advance of these requirements, payers and their service providers, including PBMs, have made
voluntary commitments to reduce prior authorization burden and improve coordination. Direct
messaging has served as a key mechanism supporting this alignment across payers. Changes
to direct certification at this juncture risk undermining these efforts, increasing fragmentation
across implementations, and forcing unnecessary workflow to redesign during a critical
transition period.

For all these reasons, PCMA respectfully asks that ASTP/ONC retain a baseline direct
messaging certification requirement, including delivery notification functionality, during the
transition to FHIR-based APIs. Preserving this baseline under the Certification Program will help
prevent regression in national interoperability, support operational continuity for providers and
payers, and enable a more orderly transition to API-based exchange once replacement
capabilities are consistently available.

II. PCMA recommends that ASTP/ONC maintain consistent policy direction
across HTI rulemakings and apply enforcement discretion in a predictable and
transparent manner that supports long-term interoperability investments.

More broadly, we emphasize the importance of consistency across HTI rulemaking iterations.
While HTI-5 may not have significant immediate operational impact on PCMA’s member
company PBMs, decisions made in this rule will shape future compliance obligations and
technology adoption, including those anticipated in subsequent HTI iterations, such as HTI-6,
that may have more direct implications for PBMs and their health plan clients. Regulatory
inconsistency across rulemakings increases burden for providers and payers alike and
complicates long-term planning. As such, ASTP’s deregulatory decisions in HTI-5 should not
inadvertently create downstream challenges for future requirements.

PCMA appreciates ASTP/ONC’s request for comment on the use of enforcement discretion to
support implementation flexibility during the transition to updated certification requirements.
While enforcement discretion can help avoid unnecessary disruption, it should be applied in a
predictable and transparent manner with clearly defined timelines and expectations. Absent
clear parameters, reliance on enforcement discretion may create uncertainty for providers,
payers, and vendors making long-term investment and contracting decisions. PCMA also
underscores the importance of predictable and consistent application of enforcement discretion.
While enforcement discretion can play a valuable role in supporting innovation and flexibility,
inconsistent signals create uncertainty for providers, payers, and vendors making long-term
investment and contracting decisions. Clear and stable regulatory expectations are essential to
avoiding future compliance challenges and to maintain confidence in the Certification Program.

With respect to FHIR-based interoperability, PCMA strongly supports the policy objectives
reflected in the API certification criteria. However, we remain concerned that deregulation in the
absence of a common, clearly articulated FHIR framework introduces uncertainty in vendor
partnerships and slows adoption. A cohesive framework is needed to advance the FHIR
ecosystem; bolster collaboration among providers, payers, and vendors; and ensure that
interoperability investments yield scalable, real-world results.

Finally, we are concerned about the proposed wholesale elimination of all privacy and security
certification requirements. Eliminating these requirements would effectively shift responsibility
for ensuring that health IT systems have the necessary technical and functional capacity for
HIPAA and other privacy and security compliance from health IT developers to providers,
without reducing the underlying regulatory or operational risk or meaningfully increasing options
or lowering costs for providers. For years, ONC certification requirements have functioned as a
scalable mechanism to ensure that core privacy and security capabilities are built into certified
products at the point of development. Many providers reasonably relied on this framework when
selecting and implementing health IT, with the understanding that certified products embedded
baseline safeguards consistent with federal expectations. Removing these requirements does
not eliminate the need for robust privacy and security controls; instead, it places the burden on
individual providers to assess, configure, validate, and continuously monitor capabilities that
were previously standardized and assured at the developer level.

This shift is particularly challenging for providers and other health care entities that exchange
protected health information across complex networks of trading partners, including PBMs and
health plans. PBMs and their health plan clients depend on consistent baseline security
capabilities across provider systems to support secure exchange of medication-related
information, coverage determinations, and utilization management activities. If certification
requirements are eliminated, variability in security practices may increase risk across the
broader health care ecosystem, exposing PBMs, plans, pharmacies, and providers to greater
compliance and operational risk when exchanging sensitive information.

Similarly, the removal of audit and related certification requirements could weaken program
integrity safeguards relied upon by PBMs, health plans, and providers. Audit logs, access
controls, and standardized security features are foundational tools for detecting fraud, waste,
and abuse, and are routinely relied upon by PBMs and plans in coordination with providers.
Eliminating certification requirements for these capabilities risks creating gaps in accountability
and oversight, while increasing compliance costs for providers who must develop compensating
controls on their own. A more balanced approach would preserve not only the audit-related
requirements as provided in ASTP/ONC’s alternate proposal, but also core privacy and security
expectations at the developer level, while allowing flexibility in how those requirements evolve
alongside FHIR-based interoperability frameworks.

IV. Conclusion: PCMA urges ASTP/ONC to avoid deregulation that removes
foundational interoperability, privacy, and security capabilities before
replacement frameworks are fully operational and consistently implemented
across the health IT ecosystem.

Looking ahead, PCMA encourages ASTP/ONC to align future certified health IT requirements
with capabilities that are critical to reducing administrative burden and improving access to care,
including further supporting capabilities related to improving the ease and utilization of electronic
prior authorization and real-time benefit tools. Prioritizing the maintenance of a consistent
interoperability foundation in HTI-5 will better position providers, payers, and their service
providers to adopt and operationalize these capabilities in a manner consistent with privacy and
