Dear Deputy Secretary O'Neill:

As nursing stakeholders, the Alliance for Nursing Informatics (ANI) is pleased to offer comments
on 45 CFR Parts 170 and 171, “Health Data, Technology, and Interoperability: ASTP/ONC
Deregulatory Actions To Unleash Prosperity” (HTI-5) Proposed Rule.

The Alliance for Nursing Informatics (ANI), cosponsored by the American Medical Informatics
Association (AMIA) and the Healthcare Information and Management Systems Society
(HIMSS), advances nursing informatics leadership, practice, education, policy, and research
through a unified voice of nursing informatics organizations. We transform health and
healthcare through nursing informatics and innovation. ANI is a collaboration of organizations
representing more than 25,000 nurse informaticists and bringing together 29 distinct nursing
informatics groups globally. ANI crosses academia, practice, industry, and nursing specialty
boundaries and collaborates with more than 4 million nurses in practice today.

ANI appreciates the opportunity to comment on the HTI-5 Proposed Rule. We appreciate the
intent behind reducing regulatory burden on health IT developers. However, we have
significant concerns that several provisions of HTI-5 may inadvertently compromise patient
safety, undermine interoperability, and shift risk to healthcare organizations and consumers
who are least equipped to absorb it. Here we highlight four key areas of concern. 1. Rationale for removed certification requirements: The provided rationale for
removing certification criteria is that they have been widely adopted and will be
maintained without regulatory mandates. However, this is positioned against the
stated goal of seeking to increase competition by inviting new developers into the
market. New entrants have no historical track record of voluntary compliance with
these standards, which weakens the assumption that adoption and maintenance of
standards will occur without mandates.
 2. Shifted responsibility: We are concerned that removing certification requirements,
particularly for privacy and security, clinical decision support, and patient engagement,
does not reduce compliance burden; it simply shifts it. The proposed removal of privacy
and security certification criteria is particularly concerning in light of the escalating
frequency and severity of healthcare data breaches. These removals do not eliminate
the need for such safeguards; they transfer the onus from certified health IT developers
to healthcare organizations, many of which, especially smaller practices, rural health
systems, and safety-net providers, lack the dedicated information security
infrastructure to compensate. We urge ASTP/ONC to reconsider the removal of privacy
and security criteria and instead invest in resources and support to help both
developers and providers meet these essential requirements. 3. Expanded access of agentic AI solutions to health data: ANI supports the goal of
advancing interoperability, including through FHIR-based APIs that enable data to
follow patients across care settings. However, we are concerned about the proposed
expansion of "access" and "use" definitions to explicitly encompass automation
technologies and agentic AI systems, and the classification of denying AI-initiated data
requests as information blocking. These provisions create a pathway for AI agents to
retrieve and act upon electronic health information through APIs without sufficient
clarity on the scope of permissible access, patient consent requirements, or guardrails
to prevent secondary use of data for purposes patients did not authorize. Patients may
not fully understand the implications of granting an AI agent access to their complete
health record, including sensitive diagnoses, genetic information, and behavioral health
data. ANI recommends that ASTP/ONC clarify that agentic AI should be permitted to
automate existing manual processes for data access (i.e., completing a request for
information on behalf of a patient), but should not be granted data access that exceeds
what patients themselves currently have. We also encourage robust informed consent
mechanisms and patient education resources to accompany any expansion of
automated access to health information. 4. Certifications as the foundation for safe and scalable AI: ANI is concerned that
reducing reporting requirements and relaxing adherence to standardized data
elements will undermine, rather than advance, the goal of AI-enabled healthcare
delivery. Interoperability is essential for AI-enabled care at scale. To reach scalability,
standardized common data elements will need to be the foundation upon which
algorithms are developed, validated, and safely transported across health systems. If
developers are no longer held to consistent definitions and reporting standards,
variability will inevitably increase, creating conditions in which AI models trained and
validated at one institution may perform unpredictably or unsafely at another. This is
already a well-documented challenge in clinical AI implementation. ANI encourages
ASTP/ONC to maintain standardization and reporting requirements as essential
infrastructure for the responsible development and deployment of AI across the health
system, and to consider providing targeted support to smaller and newer developers
for whom compliance may be genuinely burdensome, rather than removing the
requirements altogether.

ANI thanks ASTP/ONC for the opportunity to provide input on the HTI-5 Proposed Rule. We
remain eager to collaborate on policies that advance innovation while maintaining the safety
and trust that patients and clinicians depend on. We respectfully urge ASTP/ONC to weigh the
long-term consequences of deregulation against the immediate goal of reducing developer
burden, and to ensure that any changes to the certification program preserve the foundational
safeguards that protect patients and support responsible AI adoption in healthcare.
