Proposed Comment on HTI-5
Submitted by: MSSP Medical Director and Government Programs Director, Privia Medical
Group North Texas
General Position
I appreciate ASTP/ONC’s effort through the HTI-5 proposed rule to reduce unnecessary
regulatory burden, modernize certification requirements, and improve the practical flow of
electronic health information (EHI). The intent to promote competition, innovation, and efficiency
across the health IT ecosystem is directionally appropriate.
However, from the perspective of an MSSP Medical Director with direct responsibility for
program compliance, physician engagement, quality performance, and attribution integrity, I
urge ASTP to ensure that deregulation does not unintentionally weaken the structural guardrails
that make interoperability meaningful, enforceable, and clinically trustworthy.
I support narrowing misuse of information blocking exceptions, but I have significant concern
that substantial rollback of certification criteria, if not carefully scoped, could reverse hard-won
progress in interoperability and reinforce vendor behaviors that CMS has spent years attempting
to correct.

1. Information Blocking: Support for Narrowing Exceptions, With
   Operational Clarity
   I strongly support the proposal to tighten information blocking exceptions. In real-world
   operations, overly broad or inconsistently interpreted exceptions have repeatedly been used to
   delay or deny access to EHI in ways that undermine care coordination, attribution management,
   quality measurement, and risk adjustment.
   Clear, operational guidance remains essential. Providers and ACOs need:
   ●​ Distinction between legitimate technical or security constraints and impermissible denial
   ●​ Alignment between enforcement expectations and actual EHR workflows
   ●​ Explicit recognition of MSSP use cases, including attribution reconciliation, quality gap
   closure, and audit defense​

The newly released FAQs are a helpful step, but continued refinement is critical to avoid
replacing ambiguity with fear-driven over-compliance that disrupts care delivery.

2. Historical EHR Vendor Behavior: Interoperability as a Control Mechanism
   It is important to acknowledge a historical reality that continues to shape the health IT
   ecosystem: EHR vendors have repeatedly used lack of interoperability as a deliberate
   business strategy.
   There is a well-documented history of vendors:
   ●​ Slowing or selectively implementing interoperability​
   Using proprietary data structures to inhibit data portability​
   Creating technical and contractual barriers that make migration prohibitively expensive
   ●​ Effectively locking physicians into dependent relationships that resemble a form of digital
   indenture​
   This lack of interoperability has not been accidental. It has often been financially advantageous
   for vendors to maintain physician dependency by making it exceedingly costly - operationally,
   financially, and clinically - for providers to leave their platforms.
   Any regulatory action that reduces certification requirements without preserving enforceable
   interoperability standards risks re-empowering these behaviors.

3. CMS Progress Toward Patient-Centered Care Must Not Be Undermined
   CMS has done substantial and commendable work to create regulatory space for physicians to
   move medicine toward a more patient-centered, value-based model. Programs like MSSP
   depend on the free, timely, and reliable flow of health information across settings and systems.
   When EHR vendors work against interoperability - whether actively or passively - they are not
   merely resisting regulation. They are impeding progress toward patient-centered care.
   Further erosion of interoperability standards does not harm vendors; it harms:
   ●​ Physicians, by increasing administrative burden and loss of autonomy
   ●​ Patients, by fragmenting care and delaying critical information
   ●​ Value-based care models, by undermining attribution, quality measurement, and
   accountability​

Any action that enables vendors to impose additional friction or dependency on physicians
ultimately causes patient harm.

4. Certification Rollback: Risk to MSSP and Federally Regulated Programs
   While reducing unnecessary certification burden is a reasonable goal, certified health IT
   remains the foundation of MSSP compliance. Certification is not theoretical; it underpins:
   ●​ Reliable quality reporting
   ●​ Risk adjustment integrity
   ●​ Attribution transparency
   ●​ Audit defensibility
   ●​ Physician trust in performance data​

If certification criteria are reduced too aggressively, the compliance burden does not disappear -
it shifts downstream to ACOs and clinicians who lack the leverage to enforce interoperability on
vendors.
Deregulation must not result in:
●​ Fragmented data standards
●​ Vendor-defined interpretations of interoperability
●​ Increased manual reconciliation by clinical teams
●​ Erosion of confidence in shared savings calculations

5. Physician Efficiency Is Patient Protection
   I am in full support of any regulatory action that meaningfully improves physicians’ ability to
   access, exchange, and use information in ways that make their work more efficient.
   Efficiency is not a convenience for physicians - it is a patient safety and patient access issue.
   When physicians spend less time fighting systems and reconciling fragmented data, they spend
   more time caring for patients.
   Anything less - including policies that allow vendors to reintroduce friction under the guise of
   deregulation - will cause harm.

6. Digital Health and AI: Innovation Requires Trustworthy Data
   Digital health tools and AI increasingly rely on EHI as their foundation. Reducing certification
   requirements without maintaining baseline data integrity standards risks accelerating innovation
   on top of unreliable inputs.
   Innovation must be enabled, but not at the expense of data quality, transparency, and
   accountability.

7. Recommendations
   I respectfully recommend that ASTP:
   1.​ Finalize narrowing of information blocking exceptions with continued practical guidance
   2.​ Preserve core certification criteria essential to interoperability and MSSP compliance
   3.​ Explicitly assess HTI-5 impacts on federally regulated value-based care programs
   4.​ Avoid shifting compliance burden from vendors to providers and ACOs
   5.​ Maintain guardrails that protect physician autonomy, patient-centered care, and data
   integrity

8. Artificial Intelligence as a Force Multiplier for Interoperability
   I am strongly supportive of the use of artificial intelligence in any manner that meaningfully
   improves interoperability, reduces administrative burden, and allows clinicians to focus on
   patient care rather than system navigation.
   AI has the potential to:
   ●​ Normalize fragmented data across EHR platforms
   ●​ Translate proprietary formats into usable clinical information
   ●​ Improve longitudinal record integrity
   ●​ Reduce manual reconciliation work currently borne by physicians and population health
   teams​

However, the full benefit of AI cannot be realized without modernizing the regulatory
frameworks that govern its use. Interoperability policy and AI policy are now inseparable.
Treating them as independent domains will slow progress and perpetuate inefficiency.

9. HIPAA Modernization and the BAA Problem
   Further revisions to HIPAA rules are urgently needed to reflect the realities of modern clinical
   practice and advanced technology.
   In particular, the current Business Associate Agreement (BAA) requirement to interface
   separately with every platform has become, in many cases:
    ●​ A costly administrative barrier
   ●​ A source of inconsistent interpretation and enforcement
   ●​ A deterrent to responsible AI adoption
   ●​ A legal trap that places well-intentioned physicians and organizations at disproportionate
   risk​

When a platform is demonstrably secure and meets baseline privacy and security standards,
requiring bespoke BAAs for every interaction is no longer reasonable. The BAA requirement, as
currently implemented, often functions as bureaucratic friction rather than meaningful
patient protection.
Failure to perfectly traverse BAA requirements can expose organizations to exceedingly high
financial and reputational risk, even when there is no patient harm, no malicious intent, and
no misuse of data.
This framework must be modernized. The law must adapt more quickly to the advanced age in
which we are practicing. The current structure is inefficient, outdated, and misaligned with the
realities of secure, scalable technology.
Simply put: cut it out, fix it, and make it reasonable.

10. Stark Law and Overlegislation in a Risk-Bearing Environment
    Similarly, the Stark Law framework requires urgent reconsideration in the context of modern
    value-based care.
    The majority of physicians are not attempting to exploit patients or game financial
    arrangements. They are trying to help patients navigate increasingly complex systems while
    assuming more financial and clinical risk than ever before.
    Yet, we have over-legislated ourselves into a situation where:
    ●​ Providers are constrained from simple, compassionate acts
    ●​ Fear of technical violations overrides clinical judgment
    ●​ Assistance that would clearly benefit patients is avoided due to legal risk​

The notion that a physician office might be prohibited from occasionally paying for a patient’s
prescription to be filled - solely due to fear of violating Stark or related laws - while
simultaneously being held accountable for outcomes, utilization, and cost, is fundamentally
irrational.
As providers take on increasing downside risk, the regulatory framework must evolve to
reflect that alignment of incentives. Laws designed to curb abuse by a small number of bad
actors should not continue to restrict the vast majority of clinicians who are acting in good faith.
This imbalance is harmful to:
●​ Patients, who face unnecessary barriers to care
●​ Physicians, who are forced into moral and legal tension
●​ Value-based programs, which depend on flexibility, trust, and clinical discretion​

This situation is deplorable and must be corrected.
11. Overarching Principle: Efficiency Is Care
Interoperability, AI adoption, HIPAA modernization, and fraud-and-abuse reform are not
separate issues. They are all expressions of a single principle:
Efficiency in healthcare delivery is patient protection.
Any policy that enables physicians to work more efficiently improves patient care.​
Any policy that allows vendors or outdated regulations to impose friction harms patients.
Anything less than meaningful reform in these areas will continue to cause harm.

Closing
HTI-5 is an opportunity not only to reduce burden, but to correct long-standing structural
misalignments that hinder patient-centered care. Interoperability must be enforced. AI must be
enabled responsibly. And legacy regulatory frameworks must evolve to match the realities of
modern medicine.
Thank you for the opportunity to comment.
