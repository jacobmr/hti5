Dear Assistant Secretary Keane:

UnitedHealth Group (UHG) is submitting comments in response to a Proposed Rule on Health Data,
Technology, and Interoperability published in the Federal Register on December 29, 2025 (90 FR 60970). The
Proposed Rule addresses certification criteria and programs for health information technology (IT), as well as
information blocking restrictions applicable to developers of certified health IT.

UHG is a mission-driven organization dedicated to helping people live healthier lives and helping make the
health system work better for everyone through two distinct platforms—UnitedHealthcare, our health benefits
business, and Optum, our health services business. We work with employers, providers, and governments to
serve people and share a vision of a value-based system of care that provides compassionate and holistic
care.

UHG has provided leadership in efforts to enable fast, reliable, and secure information sharing among health
care providers, health plans, patients, and caregivers and we strongly support your efforts to streamline the
health IT certification process and reduce barriers to the development of interoperable data systems.
Interoperability of health information promotes better clinical outcomes, addresses gaps in patient care, and
empowers patients with actionable information to better manage their own health.

Our comments below are intended to help facilitate this process and ensure that health care providers, health
plans, and patients receive the full benefits of an interoperable health care ecosystem.

Revised Certification Criteria

Decision Support Intervention Criteria (45 CFR 170.315(b)(11))

The Proposed Rule revises the Decision Support Intervention (DSI) certification criteria by removing several
requirements. As noted in the preamble to the Proposed Rule, “DSI certification criterion ensures that users
can leverage health IT for clinical decision-making by supporting their selection of both evidence-based and
predictive DSIs.” (90 FR 60986). DSI provides critical support to health care providers at the point of care and
should be available as a component of health IT. We agree with the changes to the criteria outlined in the
Proposed Rule which offer a streamlined set of requirements for health IT developers.

Removed Certification Criteria

Implantable Device List (45 CFR 170.315(a)(14))

The Proposed Rule deletes the certification criteria requiring health IT modules to exchange, record, and allow
a user to access a list of Unique Device Identifiers (UDIs) associated with a patient’s implantable devices.
HHS believes that “some health IT developers may choose to voluntarily update relevant certified Health IT
Modules to utilize USCDI v6 with the modified definition of the UDI data element.” (90 FR 60983-60984). The
preamble to the Proposed Rule notes that health care providers participating in the Medicare Promoting
Interoperability Program must adopt health IT certified to the implantable device list criterion and that surveys
demonstrate adoption of health IT products meeting this requirement.

UHG continues to believe that certified health IT should be required to include a list of UDIs associated with a
patient. Access to this information supports clinical decisions regarding a patient’s care. While we recognize
that many health IT products may include this functionality, it is premature at this time to remove the ability to
exchange, record, and access UDIs from the required criteria.

Consolidated CDA Creation Performance (45 CFR 170.315(g)(6))

The Proposed Rule removes a certification criteria ensuring the proper conformance of Consolidated Clinical
Documentation Architectures (C–CDAs) for transition of care and referral summaries that are sent to and
received from external organizations. The rationale for this change is the belief that the health care community
is migrating to adoption of Fast Healthcare Information Resources (FHIR) standards to enable greater
interoperability of information. Many health care organizations, like UHG, utilize C-CDA to retrieve
supplemental data to support the closing of gaps in care for HEDIS measures, risk adjustment, and care
management programs. Until FHIR standards have the ability to replace the C-CDA, organizations are
dependent on this key data format to exchange clinical data.

We note that the Health Level 7 (HL7) Foundation responsible for developing FHIR standards has not finalized
standards comparable to C-CDA and it is premature to remove C-CDA performance criteria at this time.
Updates to this certification criteria should be paused until the standards are finalized and adopted at scale
across the health care community.

Application Access – Patient Selection (45 CFR 170.315(g)(7))

The Proposed Rule removes certification criteria to support the capability to receive a request with sufficient
information to uniquely identify a patient and return an ID or other token that can be used by an
application to subsequently execute requests for that patient’s data. In the preamble to the proposed Rule,
HHS notes that this functionality is addressed in the SMART App Launch Implementation Guide and is
associated with measures under the Provider to Patient Exchange and Health Information Exchange objectives
of the Medicare Promoting Interoperability Program and the MIPS Promoting Interoperability performance
category. Numerous clinical decision support applications across the enterprise use SMART on FHIR to
trigger context appropriate interventions. We worry that removing this requirement will make SMART app
launch more difficult in Electronic Health Records as EHR vendors may deploy non-standard approaches
without this certification. We oppose this change and ask that the criteria be retained.
Application Access – All Data Request (45 CFR 170.315(g)(9))

The Proposed Rule deletes criteria requiring the capability to respond to requests for patient data for all of the
data classes expressed in the USCDI standard. HHS believes this functionality has been widely adopted by
health IT developers and a specific requirement is no longer needed. In addition, the preamble notes that
removal “could spur further industry innovation and support the development of more creative interoperability
solutions.” (90 FR 60998). Responding to requests for patient data is a key component of care delivery and
clinical support. While we recognize that many EHRs include this functionality we believe it should be retained
as a certification criteria. We oppose this change and ask that the criteria be retained.

Direct Project (45 CFR 170.315(h)(1))
Direct Project, Edge Protocol and XDR/XDM (45 CFR 170.315(h)(2))

The Proposed Rule removes criteria supporting the ability to send and receive direct messaging. This change
is proposed because HHS feels that most EHRs currently support this functionality and removal will lessen
development burdens. UHG relies on direct messaging in a variety of contexts. We send and receive direct
messages to and from EHRs, mostly containing C-CDA data. If the criteria is changed to no longer require the
ability to send or receive direct messages, it could significantly impact critical data flows. While we agree that
most EHRs will provide the flexibility to send and receive messaging, we recommend that the certification
criteria be retained.
