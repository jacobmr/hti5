EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

eHealth Exchange submits the following comments on the Information Blocking provisions of the HTI-5
Proposed Rule. Our positions are summarized below and discussed in detail in the sections that follow.

“Access” and “Use” Definitions (§ 171.102). We support ASTP/ONC’s proposed clarifications to the
definitions of “access” and “use” to expressly encompass automated technologies, including robotic
process automation and agentic AI. We also support ASTP/ONC's alternative proposal to make a
conforming clarification to the definition of “exchange” and recommend that ASTP/ONC finalize both
proposals to avoid an unintended negative inference.

Infeasibility Exception (§ 171.204). We support removal of the Third Party Seeking Modification Use
Condition. We support the proposed changes to the Manner Exception Exhausted Condition, with one
exception: We recommend retaining the “similarly situated” language in § 171.204(a)(4)(iii) and offer a
suggested alternative formulation that we believe is consistent with ASTP/ONC’s objectives while
accounting for meaningful, non-discriminatory differences amongst requestor types.

Manner Exception (§ 171.301). We share ASTP/ONC’s concern that some Actors may exploit the Manner
Exception to impose exploitative contracting terms. However, we strongly oppose ASTP/ONC’s primary
proposal to add contractual limitations to the manner requested condition. The proposed “contract of
adhesion” definition is substantially overbroad, capturing the standardized participation agreements—
including the Common Agreement and Terms of Participation under TEFCA—upon which trusted health
information exchange depends and, if finalized, would create an immediate compliance crisis for
HINs/HIEs. The proposed “market rate” requirement, borrowed from Stark Law, would displace cost-
recovery pricing models and raise the cost floor for access to EHI. The proposed “unconscionable terms”
definition provides insufficient guidance for regulated parties to determine, ex ante, whether their
contract provisions comply with the Information Blocking regulations. The cumulative effect would
severely harm the HIN/HIE community and the under-resourced organizations they serve, while
advantaging the dominant Actors whose practices prompted ASTP/ONC’s concern.

        We support, instead, ASTP/ONC's alternative proposal to remove § 171.301(a)(2), thereby

requiring that all fees and licensing terms satisfy the Fees Exception and Licensing Exception, respectively.
If ASTP/ONC elects to finalize its primary proposal, we offer alternative definitions for “contract of
adhesion” that distinguish between agreements that restrict exchange and those that enable it.

TEFCA Manner Exception (§ 171.403). We support removal of the TEFCA Manner Exception, though on
grounds that differ from ASTP/ONC’s stated rationale. Changes to the Exchange Purposes SOP have
fundamentally altered the nature of “TEFCA Exchange” such that the exception no longer serves its
intended purpose. However, removal of the TEFCA Manner Exception is irreconcilable with ASTP/ONC’s
primary Manner Exception proposal and further supports the need to finalize the alternative Manner
Exception proposal.

I. INFORMATION BLOCKING (PART 171)

A. “Access” and “Use” Definitions (§ 171.102)

ASTP/ONC proposes to add language to the definitions of “access” and “use” at 45 C.F.R. 171.102 to
expressly state that these terms include access and use by automated technologies such as robotic
process automation (RPA) and autonomous/agentic AI systems. (90 FR 61006.) We support ASTP/ONC’s
proposed clarifications to these definitions.

In the Proposed Rule, ASTP/ONC also specifically seeks feedback on whether a similar clarification should
be made to the definition of “exchange” in § 171.102. (Id.) ASTP/ONC observes in its discussion on the
primary proposal, “that actors and those who interact with them would benefit from explicit regulatory
text specifying that the access, exchange, and use definitions are not limited to manual processes but
apply equally to access, exchange and use of EHI that occurs via automated means,” (id.). We agree and
support a similar revision to the “exchange” definition, as briefly discussed below.

Although we recognize that the current and proposed definitions of “access” and “use” encompass the
act of “exchange,” we believe that conforming changes to all three definitions would provide greater
clarity to actors and those with whom they interact. Conversely, by only including such a clarification in
the definitions of “access” and “use,” one might think it reasonable to apply the canon expressio unius est
exclusio alterius (the expression of one excludes the other).

B. Infeasibility Exception (§ 171.204)

1. Infeasibility – Third Party Seeking Modification Use Condition

ASTP/ONC proposes to remove the Third Party Seeking Modification Use Condition from the Infeasibility
Exception. In proposing this change, ASTP/ONC cites amongst its rationale the concern that the
condition is being used to limit healthcare providers’ ability to use innovative third-party software with
their EHRs. ASTP/ONC also identifies improvements in technical approaches for modification use and
that the Security and Preventing Harm Exceptions provide adequate alternative protections for Actors.
We are persuaded by ASTP/ONC’s weighing of risks/benefits and support removal of this condition.

2. Infeasibility – Manner Exception Exhausted Condition

ASTP/ONC proposes various changes to the Manner Exception Exhausted Condition of the Infeasibility
Exception. eHealth Exchange supports the proposed changes to this exception except for removal of
the “similarly situated” language from § 171.204(a)(4)(iii). We provide a comparison of the current
criterion, the proposed criterion, and eHealth Exchange’s suggested approach to this criterion below.

    Current: The actor does not provide the same access, exchange, or use of the requested electronic
    health information to a substantial number of individuals or entities that are similarly situated to the
    requester.

C. Manner Exception (§ 171.301)

ASTP/ONC’s primary proposal with regard to the Manner Exception is to add explicit
requirements/restrictions upon any contract/agreement or license for access, use, and/or exchange of
EHI in the manner requested. eHealth Exchange supports ASTP/ONC’s objective of ensuring that Actors
are not shielded under the Manner Exception for contracting practices that condition access, use, and/or
exchange of EHI upon a party’s acceptance of exploitative, one-sided terms that serve no legitimate
interoperability purpose. However, we do not support—and have critical concerns with—the approach
ASTP/ONC puts forth in its primary proposal to the Manner Exception.

ASTP/ONC proposes to revise this exception by adding a new § 171.301(a)(2)(iii), requiring any
contract/agreement related to the access, exchange, or use of EHI: (i) must be at “market rate”; (ii) must
not be a “contract of adhesion”; and (iii) must not contain “unconscionable terms.” eHealth Exchange’s
concerns are regarding how ASTP/ONC proposes these terms are defined and interpreted – particularly
the “contract of adhesion” definition.

For the reasons articulated below, eHealth Exchange strongly opposes ASTP/ONC’s primary Manner
Exception proposal. We support, instead, ASTP/ONC’s alternative proposal to remove § 171.301(a)(2),
thereby requiring that all fees must also meet the Fees Exception and that all licensing terms must also
meet the Licensing Exception.

1. Contract of Adhesion

The Proposed Rule defines “contract of adhesion” at 171.102 as:

      [A] contract provided on a standardized form, or on a “take it or leave it basis” without a
      realistic opportunity to bargain where the desired product, services, access, use, or
      exchange cannot be provided except by acquiescing to the form contract.

(Id. at 61033.) This definition contains three operative elements: (1) a standardized, “form contract”;
(2) that is non-negotiable (i.e., presented on a “take it or leave it” basis without a realistic opportunity
to bargain; (3) where the desired product, services, access, use, or exchange cannot be obtained except
by acquiescing.

In its discussion of the proposed Manner Exception revisions, ASTP/ONC unequivocally states:

        These types of contracts are not covered by the Manner Exception even if these agreements
        were styled as, or in fact were, achieving access, exchange, or use of the requested EHI in
        the requested manner.

    (Id. at 61012, emphasis added.) Thus, according to ASTP/ONC, Actors could not rely on the Manner
    Exception if their contracts are standardized/non-negotiable, even when the purpose of such
    standardization is to ensure that all parties are bound to the same terms governing the access, exchange,
    and/or use of EHI, and even if acquiescence results in achieving the requested access/exchange/use in
    the manner requested. 1

    What is of graver concern: The interpretive discourse does not stop there. ASTP/ONC’s commentary
    goes a substantial step further, stating: “These types of contracts would also likely constitute an
    interference (85 FR 25813)2 under the information blocking regulations.” (Id., emphasis added.)

    It is essential to understand the full import of ASTP/ONC’s interpretive position. The proposal does not
    merely remove Manner Exception protection for standardized agreements and leave the question of
    whether such agreements constitute information blocking to case-by-case evaluation. ASTP/ONC goes
    further: by stating that contracts meeting the proposed “contract of adhesion” definition “would also
    likely constitute an interference under the information blocking regulations,” ASTP/ONC is announcing
    a substantive interpretation of the general prohibition at § 171.103. Namely, the mere use of a
    standardized, non-negotiable agreement for the access, exchange, or use of EHI is, in itself, a practice
    that is “likely to interfere with, prevent, or materially discourage” such access, exchange, or use. Under
    this interpretation, satisfaction of any other exception (e.g., Infeasibility, Security, Privacy Exception)
    would be irrelevant to the threshold question of whether the practice constitutes an interference. The
    issue is the agreement’s form, not its effect. The consequence for an Actor whose practice is
    determined to constitute information blocking is exposure to civil monetary penalties (CMPs),
    regardless whether the agreement at issue actually facilitates, rather than restricts, the access,
    exchange, and/or use of EHI.

    and/or use of EHI, rather than on the substance and effect of such agreement. This focus and

While there are meaningful variations in the additional factors considered and specific tests
applied across jurisdictions, we are neither aware of, nor have we been able to identify, any legal
precedent or basis under which standardization and/or non-negotiability alone are sufficient for
classifying a contract as adhesory. A critical element of an adhesion analysis is whether the
standardized terms are designed to benefit the drafting party at the expense of the adhering
party versus whether the terms serve legitimate purposes that benefit both parties or the
system as a whole. However, the proposed definition of “contract of adhesion” includes no
mention of power inequity, a drafting party with superior bargaining strength, the relative
sophistication of the parties, or similar consideration as generally applied by the courts.

        The mere fact that a contract is standardized does not make it oppressive; what matters is
        whether the terms substantively impose unreasonable burdens on the weaker party for the
        benefit of the stronger. However, ASTP/ONC’s proposed definition stops at the form of the
        agreement. It asks only whether a contract is standardized and non-negotiable – not whether its
        terms are one-sided, oppressive, or primarily designed to benefit the drafter.

        Adopting a legal term of art while proposing material deviations from any established definition
        or understanding of such term—without articulating a reasoned basis for those deviations—is
        substantively and procedurally problematic.6 We are concerned by the absence of any indication
        in the Proposed Rule that ASTP/ONC considered the legal interpretation of the terminology
        ASTP/ONC seeks to borrow from the common law. Given that the common law approach speaks
        directly to the concern ASTP/ONC cites as its basis for the proposed “contract of adhesion”
        limitation—power inequity—this absence underscores a broader concern with the primary
        proposal: ASTP/ONC has identified a real problem but has proposed a definition that does not
        incorporate the analytical tools the law has already developed to address it.
        We recognize that ASTP/ONC is not required to adopt a common law definition verbatim for
        purposes of the Information Blocking regulations. However, where an agency elects to borrow a
        legal term of art that carries established meaning, and where the established meaning of that
        term already incorporates the very element (power inequity) that the agency identifies as its
        foundational concern, the decision to not engage with that body of law warrants explanation,

    2. Market Rate

    ASTP/ONC further proposes to expressly require that, to meet the Manner Exception, any
    contract/agreement for the access, exchange, or use of EHI “must be at market rate.” (90 FR 61012.)
    The proposed definition of “market rate” would borrow from Stark Law’s (42 U.S.C. § 1395nn) “fair
    market value” definition: “[T]he value in an arm’s-length transaction, consistent with the general market
    value of the subject transaction.” (Id., emphasis added.) eHealth Exchange does not support this
    approach to ensuring fees for access/exchange/use of EHI and/or licensing interoperability elements
    are not excessive. Rather, we believe ASTP/ONC should rely on the existing Fees Exception and
    Licensing Exception, as alternatively proposed.

    Stark Law (“Stark”) developed around a very different problem, and its definition of “fair market value”
    (FMV) is not well suited to the proposed application within the Manner Exception. Healthcare fraud
    and abuse laws, such as Stark, are focused on referrals for healthcare services that are ultimately self-
    enriching. Their intent is not to ensure fairness in contracting. Notably, healthcare fraud and abuse
    laws scrutinize, and potentially prohibit, arrangements that are for less than fair market value – not just
    those that exceed FMV. The rationale in the fraud and abuse context is that offering something (e.g.,
    office space) at less than FMV can act as an inducement or reward for referrals that, in the case of Stark,
    financially benefit a physician at the expense of Medicare/Medicaid.

    Not-for-profit HINs/HIEs typically charge less than the “general market value of the subject transaction”
    for access, exchange, and use of EHI as compared to for-profit Actors. By requiring that all Actors
    seeking to comply with the Manner Exception must offer products/services/licenses related to the
    access, exchange, and/or use of EHI at the “market rate,” this proposal may level the cost ceiling, but
    it will significantly raise the cost floor. When approximately two-thirds of the market share is
    controlled by two for-profit EHR developers,7 their fees ARE the “general market rate.”

    The existing Fees Exception requires that fees be reasonably related to an Actor’s costs of providing the
    access, exchange, and/or use of EHI. (45 C.F.R. 171.302(a)(1)(ii).) It also requires that fees be
    “[r]easonably allocated among all similarly situated persons or entities to whom the technology or
    service is supplied, or for whom the technology is supported,” (id. at (a)(1)(iii), emphasis added). The
    foregoing is consistent with not-for-profit HINs/HIEs generally operating on a cost-recovery model, and
    it allows them to comply with the Fees Exception while providing an affordable means to access,
    exchange, and use EHI to under-resourced groups.

    In contrast, under the proposed “market rate” condition, not-for-profit HINs/HIEs that structure their
    agreements to comply with the Manner Exception would be forced to abandon cost-based pricing

eHealth Exchange agrees that agreements related to the access, exchange, and/or use of EHI should

ASTP/ONC proposes to define “unconscionable terms” at § 171.102 as “terms that are excessive,

Manner Exception Conclusion

Taken together, the three proposed additions to the manner requested condition—the “contract of
adhesion” definition, the “market rate” requirement, and the “unconscionable terms” limitation—would
fundamentally undermine the legal and operational infrastructure upon which trusted health information
exchange in this country depends.

First, the “contract of adhesion” definition would categorically disqualify from Manner Exception
protection the standardized participation agreements that are foundational to HIN/HIE operations — not
because these agreements restrict access, exchange, or use of EHI but merely because they are
standardized. Second, the “market rate” requirement, borrowed from a statutory framework designed
to address an entirely different problem, would force not-for-profit HINs/HIEs to abandon cost-recovery
pricing models and align their fees with rates set by dominant for-profit EHR developers, which would
effectively raise the cost floor for organizations that currently provide the most affordable means of
accessing, exchanging, and using EHI. Third, the “unconscionable terms” definition—a dictionary-level
meaning backed by a single illustrative example—would leave those responsible for drafting agreements
that govern the access, exchange, and use of EHI unable to determine, ex ante, whether their contract
provisions comply with the Information Blocking regulations and a Damoclean sword of civil monetary
penalties looming for guessing wrong.

The cumulative effect of these three proposals on the HIN/HIE community would be severe. HINs and
HIEs would face a regulatory environment in which: (i) their foundational participation agreements are
categorically excluded from the Manner Exception and characterized as presumptive interferences; (ii)
their cost-based fee structures are displaced by a market rate standard that rewards the pricing power of
dominant actors; and (iii) the permissible scope of a routine contract provision, indemnification, is defined
so vaguely as to be unworkable. This would force HINs/HIEs to either: (a) expend resources they do not
have to individually negotiate participation agreements with hundreds or thousands of participants,
thereby undermining the transparency and consistency that make trusted exchange possible; or (b)
continue operating under their existing standardized participation agreements with the knowledge that
ASTP/ONC has affirmatively characterized such agreements as practices that “would also likely constitute
an interference under the information blocking regulations” and accept the corresponding exposure to
civil monetary penalties. (90 FR 61012, internal citation omitted.) Unlike the loss of Manner Exception
protection, which would merely leave the question of information blocking to case-by-case evaluation,
ASTP/ONC’s preamble commentary establishes a presumption that such agreements are themselves
interferences. An HIN/HIE choosing option (b) would not be operating in regulatory gray area; it would
be operating under an express agency interpretation that its foundational contracting practice likely
violates federal regulation. That is an untenable position. The practical result is that option (b) is no
option at all, which makes all of the costs, delays, and destructive consequences for trusted exchange
under option (a) the only realistic path.

The operational burden would be staggering, and the lack of transition period unworkable. ASTP/ONC’s
commentary indicates that contracts meeting the proposed “contract of adhesion” definition “would also
likely constitute an interference under the information blocking regulations.” (Id.) However, the proposed
changes to the Information Blocking regulations contain no transition period; they would take effect on

the effective date of a subsequent final rule. (Id. at 61005.) Existing standardized participation
agreements, many of which have been in place for years and govern active exchange relationships among
hundreds or thousands of participants, would be immediately subject to the presumption of interference.
HINs/HIEs would need to renegotiate each of these agreements on an individualized basis — a process
that could realistically take years to complete, particularly given the legal resources required and the
number of participants involved. During that period, organizations would face an untenable choice:
continue operating under agreements that ASTP/ONC has characterized as likely interferences, with
corresponding exposure to civil monetary penalties, or suspend participation agreements and disrupt
the exchange of EHI upon which clinical care, public health, and patients depend. Neither outcome
serves the objectives of the 21st Century Cures Act.

We also note that the Regulatory Impact Analysis of the Proposed Rule does not anticipate costs
associated with the proposed changes to the Information Blocking regulations. (Id. at 60974-75, 61016.)
We respectfully submit that assessment does not reflect the full operational impact of the primary

The legal and operational costs associated with renegotiating each HIN/HIE participation agreement on

approximately 55 hours. At prevailing rates for experienced health information technology counsel, the

approximately $12 million to $16 million. This figure is exclusive of the upfront cost of converting our
standardized agreement framework into a negotiable one (estimated at $140,000 to $360,000), and
exclusive of the legal costs that each of our Participants would independently incur to engage their own

The organizations most harmed by this outcome would be precisely those ASTP/ONC’s Information

We recognize and share ASTP/ONC’s concern that some Actors may exploit the Manner Exception to

identified. Its definitions are overbroad, poorly suited to the concern and/or domain, or insufficiently
defined. The combination of all three would do more harm than the practices they are intended to
address.

For the foregoing reasons, eHealth Exchange strongly opposes ASTP/ONC's primary Manner Exception
proposal. We support, instead, ASTP/ONC's alternative proposal to remove § 171.301(a)(2), thereby

permitted to cherry pick TEFCA exchange partners, free from contractual non-discrimination constraints,
and subject those selected partners to additional terms (including fees), such arrangements should not
be shielded from information blocking scrutiny merely because they are further limited to being offered
only via TEFCA.

Included in its rationale for proposing to remove the TEFCA Manner Exception, ASTP/ONC states, “The
exception is also now unnecessary because it describes activities that are… still covered by the Manner

that removal of the TEFCA Manner Exception, in conjunction with the changes to TEFCA Exchange
discussed above, warrants ASTP/ONC finalizing its alternative proposal to the Manner Exception to
remove § 171.301(a)(2).
