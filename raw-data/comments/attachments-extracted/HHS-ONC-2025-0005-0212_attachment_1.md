Ascension appreciates the opportunity to submit comments on the proposed rule recently issued by the
Office of the Deputy Secretary and Assistant Secretary for Technology Policy (ASTP) and Office of the
National Coordinator for Health Information Technology (ONC) entitled Health Data, Technology, and
Interoperability: ASTP/ONC Deregulatory Actions To Unleash Prosperity (RIN 0955-AA09) (“HTI-5”).1

Global Comments

Ascension is one of the nation’s leading non-profit and Catholic health systems, with a Mission of
delivering compassionate, personalized care to all, with special attention to those most vulnerable. In
FY2025, Ascension provided $1.7 billion in care of persons living in poverty and other community benefit
programs along with $1.8 billion of unreimbursed care for Medicare patients. Across 15 states and the
District of Columbia, Ascension’s network encompasses approximately 97,000 associates, 23,100 aligned
providers, and 91 wholly owned or consolidated hospitals. Ascension also operates 26 senior living
facilities and a variety of other care sites offering a range of healthcare services.

We appreciate ASTP/ONC’s continued leadership in advancing interoperability through HTI-5, particularly
the agency’s commitment to modernizing the Health IT Certification Program. By prioritizing Fast
Healthcare Interoperability Resources (FHIR)-based Application Programming Interfaces (APIs) and
streamlining the information blocking regulation, these proposals take a significant step toward reducing
the administrative friction that plagues the healthcare ecosystem. We specifically applaud the move to
eliminate redundant or onerous requirements. Removing these barriers allows providers to refocus their
energy where it matters most: preventing and treating chronic disease and spending meaningful time
with their patients.

Yet, as we move toward a more deregulated environment, it is critical to evaluate these changes through
the lens of their downstream impact on the delivery system. While reducing developer burden is a valid
objective, it must not come at the expense of healthcare providers. We are concerned that several

Certification Program Proposals

​ Advancing FHIR for Clinical Utility

While we strongly endorse ASTP/ONC’s focus on FHIR-based interoperability as a scalable foundation for
exchange, its real-world value is limited if it only supports data retrieval without enabling controlled data
contribution. As a health system, we find that APIs lacking "write" capabilities often force clinicians to
manually re-enter external data, which exacerbates documentation fatigue and increases the risk of
medical errors. For FHIR to truly modernize care coordination rather than simply layering a new interface
over legacy, document-centric models, the policy must evolve to support practical workflow integration
and safeguarded data contribution that preserves clinician oversight.

Furthermore, fostering a trusted, deregulated environment requires a common set of technical and
governance rules to mitigate the legal and operational risks we face. Without standardized expectations
for implementation consistency, auditability, and data provenance, the uncertainty of a distributed
ecosystem may actually discourage adoption and undermine the goals of HTI-5. We urge ASTP/ONC to
establish a universal framework even if largely voluntary to align incentives and provide the
accountability norms necessary for sustainable, high-value partnerships in a FHIR-enabled landscape.

        Preserving Privacy and Security Baselines

We appreciate ASTP/ONC’s intent to foster innovation by removing the fourteen privacy and security
certification criteria, but we are concerned that complete removal may create significant compliance
gaps for health systems. Many providers rely on these certified standards to meet HIPAA obligations and
other legal requirements; without this baseline, smaller and rural systems in particular may struggle to
source cost-effective, secure alternatives. While we agree that a less prescriptive approach can spur
competition, the absence of common expectations for authentication, encryption, and multi-factor
authentication risks increasing costs and introducing new vulnerabilities into the interoperability
ecosystem.

We support the decision to retain audit-related requirements to safeguard against fraud, but we
recommend that the same logic of maintaining system integrity should apply to other core security
functions. Rather than total removal, we encourage ASTP/ONC to offer voluntary, modular, or
standardized approaches to these capabilities. This middle ground would grant developers the flexibility
to innovate while providing health systems with the baseline technical assurances necessary to

​ Ensuring AI Transparency for Clinical Oversight

While HTI-5 maintains the requirement for Certified Health IT to support decision support interventions
(DSIs), the proposal to eliminate AI model card and transparency requirements to reduce developer
burden may create significant downstream liability for health systems. We recognize the need to avoid
stifling innovation; yet, removing these transparency artifacts deprives providers of the technical insights
essential for clinical oversight, patient safety, and procurement due diligence. Without standardized
documentation, health systems lack the necessary information to satisfy internal governance and comply
with emerging state-level AI disclosure and automated decision-making laws.

In order to balance innovation with accountability, we recommend that ASTP/ONC retain a voluntary
transparency framework rather than a total removal of these requirements. This approach would provide
the critical documentation necessary for responsible AI adoption while avoiding prescriptive mandates
on developers. By preserving a pathway for standardized artifacts, ASTP/ONC can ensure that those of us
on the front lines of care have the functional information required to meet our due diligence obligations
in an increasingly regulated AI landscape.

​ Operationalizing Enforcement Discretion

Ascension welcomes the codification of the 2025 enforcement discretion policies, such as streamlining
Insights reporting and descoping real-world testing. Formalizing these policies reduces administrative
burden and provides much-needed clarity during this technical transition. In order to maintain
stakeholder confidence, we urge ASTP/ONC to ensure these discretions are applied consistently and
transparently. We encourage the issuance of additional guidance to operationalize these policies,
reaffirming that good-faith efforts to comply with evolving standards remain a central consideration in
enforcement decisions.

Information Blocking

We commend ASTP/ONC’s efforts to simplify the Information Blocking framework and remove provisions
prone to misuse. While modernizing these rules is a vital step toward a more workable environment,
streamlining the exceptions must not inadvertently penalize health systems for good-faith decisions
rooted in legitimate technical, security, or governance constraints. Ensuring continued participation in
national interoperability requires that the agency pair these narrower exceptions with explicit
enforcement discretion and clear guidance. Without such safeguards, providers risk facing compliance
liability for legitimate actions taken to protect system integrity. Regulatory evolution will only succeed if
backed by predictable, uniform enforcement that accounts for operational realities health systems face.

​ Infeasibility Exception

By removing the manner exhausted and third party seeking modification conditions, ASTP/ONC
effectively eliminates unnecessary complexity and curbs potential misuse. We endorse this shift, as these
requirements often demand technical evaluations that are nearly impossible for health systems to
operationalize in real-world settings. Eliminating these conditions significantly sharpens regulatory clarity
and reduces administrative friction. The agency must provide explicit enforcement discretion in order to
cement the success of these reforms, which will ensure that good-faith infeasibility determinations are

​ Manner Exception

We support ASTP/ONC’s proposal to refine the “manner requested” condition, as the current broad
language creates inconsistent interpretations and compliance risks. By narrowing this scope, the
exception can better facilitate timely access to health information while allowing actors to offer feasible
alternatives when specific requests aren't technically possible. This shift toward reasonableness reduces
delays and prevents misuse. Nevertheless, we encourage ASTP/ONC to clearly define how enforcement
discretion will protect those making good-faith efforts to accommodate requests within complex
technical environments.

​ TEFCA Manner Exception

As a health system, we support ASTP/ONC’s proposal to remove the Trusted Exchange Framework and
Common Agreement (TEFCA)-specific manner exception, as a unified regulatory framework simplifies
our compliance efforts and promotes consistency across all exchange models. Yet, we are concerned that
removing this dedicated pathway may create uncertainty for systems that have made significant
technical and operational investments to meet TEFCA’s rigorous requirements. Without the specific
exception, it remains unclear how our necessary adherence to TEFCA’s unique governance and security
constraints will be weighed against information blocking obligations under the more general infeasibility
or security exceptions.

In order to ensure this transition does not discourage TEFCA participation, we urge ASTP/ONC to provide
explicit guidance and good-faith enforcement discretion for health systems navigating these complex
technical environments. These proposals are a positive step toward a more comprehensible framework;
however, they must be paired with clear examples or FAQs that illustrate how we can rely on remaining
exceptions when TEFCA mandates conflict with specific access requests. By providing this clarity,
ASTP/ONC can reduce our administrative burden and support the continued growth of a trusted, scalable
national health information exchange.

Conclusion

Ascension values ASTP/ONC’s efforts to modernize interoperability through a more flexible,
outcomes-based framework, but we believe this shift must be paired with clear guidance and codified
enforcement discretion for good-faith compliance to ensure the health information ecosystem remains
both reliable and scalable. We appreciate your consideration of our comments. If you have any
questions, or if there is any additional information we can provide, please contact me at
mark.howell1@ascension.org.
