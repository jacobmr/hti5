DETAILED COMMENTS ON INFORMATION BLOCKING

DEFINITION UPDATES: “ACCESS,” “USE,” AND “EXCHANGE”

ONC proposes to revise the definitions of “access,” “use,” and “exchange” to make explicit that these concepts
include automated and machine-enabled means, such as robotic process automation (RPA), software agents, and
autonomous or “agentic” AI systems, not just human, manual processes. ONC states that these existing definitions
have been interpreted too narrowly in practice and do not reflect how EHI moves in modern healthcare
environments.

Advocate Health supports ONC’s effort to modernize these definitions to reflect current automated and API-
driven -driven environments. We believe that these proposals appropriately clarify that these concepts include
system-to--system- and automated activities, which align regulations with how care delivery and interoperability
function in practice.

At the same time, we urge ONC to pair these definition updates with clear guidelines recognizing legitimate security,
system stability, and patient safety controls, so that automation used in good faith- is not subject to undue
compliance uncertainty or retrospective enforcement scrutiny.

INFEASIBILITY EXCEPTION: REMOVE "THIRD PARTY SEEKING MODIFICATION USE" CONDITION § 171.201(A)(3)

The 21st Century CURES Act focuses on access, exchange and use of EHI by individuals and designated actors.
Advocate Health agrees that third parties have been using it to pressure actors into providing access beyond what
the statute requires.

Advocate Health agrees that information blocking claims should not be an open-ended right for an unrelated third-
party to demand system changes or modifications. We have also experienced third parties asserting information
blocking to compel workflow changes, interfaces, or custom builds that are not clinically necessary. Providers often
bear the hidden costs when these types of requests are made, including EHR configuration work, IT security
reviews, legal and contracting overhead and clinical workflow disruption. These claims represent an operational and
financial burden.

We therefore agree with ASTP/ONC’s proposal to remove “third-party seeking modification use” condition. We
believe that this proposal will simplify compliance determinations and make information blocking enforcement
more predictive and administrable. We want clear and defensible rules that we can use consistently across our
compliance and legal departments and our IT governance committee. We interpret the CURES Act as protecting
patient access, provider-to-provider exchange and provider-authorized third parties, not giving rights to external
vendors to demand EHR modifications. We believe that this change will reduce our legal exposure and
administrative friction without restricting a provider's ability to share data for care, payment or operations.

INFEASIBILITY EXCEPTION: REVISE OR REMOVE THE "MANNER EXCEPTION EXHAUSTED" CONDITION § 171.201(A)(4)

Under the current information blocking framework, an actor may rely on the infeasibility exception if they have
already offered at least two alternative, interoperable manners of access, exchange, or use, and those alternatives
were not accepted. HTI-5 proposes to revise or remove this concept, explaining that it has been stretched beyond
its original intent and used as a de facto safe harbor for denying access.

As a large, integrated health system, Advocate Health supports ONC’s intent to refocus the infeasibility exception on
genuine technical, legal, patient safety-, or security constraints, rather than procedural thresholds. We agree that
the prior “manner exception exhausted” condition has, at times, been applied mechanically and has not always
advanced meaningful access.

However, the “exhaustion” construct has served as one of the few compliance benchmarks available to providers.
Removing or revising it without clear replacement guidelines risks increasing subjectivity, documentation burden,
and retrospective enforcement uncertainty for providers that are acting in good faith.

If ONC proceeds with this change, we urge the agency to define clearly what constitutes “reasonable efforts” when
assessing infeasibility and to recognize specifically material system degradation, cybersecurity risk, patient safety,
and clinical workflow impacts as legitimate bases for infeasibility. Providers need prospective clarity on how
infeasibility will be evaluated, not retrospective judgment after care delivery decisions have already been made.

With these guardrails in place, we believe ONC can effectively curb the misuse of the Infeasibility exception while
allowing large health systems to continue making responsible, patient-centered decisions without unnecessary
regulatory uncertainty or administrative burden.

MANNER EXCEPTION: REVISE THE “MANNER REQUESTED” CONDITION § 171.301(A)

ONC’s proposal to revise the “manner requested” is to provide certainty that the manner exception does not cover
any contracts, agreements, or licenses that: 1) are not at market rate, 2) are contracts of adhesion, or 3) include unconscionable terms.

ONC is concerned that some actors may be incorrectly citing the “manner requested” condition to persuade
requestors that the exception applies to agreements and terms to which it has never applied.

We support ONC’s goal of ensuring that the manner exception advances interoperability and does not create unfair
barriers to access. We share ONC’s concern that alternative manners of access should not discourage real use of EHI
through excessive fees, ‘take it- or- leave- it’- contracts, or other coercive terms.

At the same time, the proposed revisions introduce new terms, such as “market-based- rates,” “contracts of
adhesion,” and “unconscionable terms,” into what has typically been a more technical and operational analysis.
Providers are well positioned to assess feasibility, security, and clinical impact, but are not often the parties setting
pricing or contract terms, which may be set by vendors or networks.

Without clear guidelines, this shift risks forcing providers to judge if a contract is fair rather than providing access
and may increase compliance risk even when providers are acting in good faith to offer secure, workable
alternatives.

While we understand ONC’s rationale, we believe that providers need clear guidance to avoid confusion that can
slow down data sharing or lead to making overly cautious decisions. We recommend that ONC:
• Clearly distinguish what decisions the provider decides from the pricing and terms set by third-party
vendors,
• Provide examples showing when infeasibility applies and when alternative manners are considered
reasonable, and
• Clarify documentation expectations, so providers are not required to generate extensive analysis for routine
requests.
