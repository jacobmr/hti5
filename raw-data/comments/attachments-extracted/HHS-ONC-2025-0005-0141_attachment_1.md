RE: Health Data, Technology, and Interoperability (HTI-5) Proposed Rule (RIN 0955-AA09)

Dear Assistant Secretary Keane:

AARP, which advocates on behalf of 125 million Americans age 50 and older, thanks you for
seeking feedback on health information technology standards. We appreciate the Assistant
Secretary for Technology Policy and the Office of the National Coordinator for Health
Information Technology’s interest in harnessing technology to improve patient experience and
outcomes. Furthermore, we share your belief that – when used appropriately – technology and AI
can lower health care costs through increased efficiency and productivity. Our focus is on
ensuring older Americans are confident their privacy is protected, their data is secure, and a
human relationship with their provider is maintained. Accomplishing these goals requires
holding health technology, such as electronic health records, to high standards.

This proposed rule focuses on deregulatory actions regarding health information technology
standards, implementation specifications, certification criteria and certification programs for
health information technology, and information blocking. The stated intent is to reduce burden,
offer flexibility to both developers and providers, and support innovation. We believe the intent
of regulatory actions should also be to protect consumers and provide meaningful oversight and
accountability.

AARP applauds your effort to prevent information blocking. Consumers need to have access to
their personal health information and have the right electronic health information go to the right
people. We appreciate your responsiveness to previous comments in proposing clarifications and
reducing exceptions to information blocking rules. Furthermore, we support your continued
vigilance to minimize potential abuse of other exceptions-to-the-rule. We hope to work with you
to safely eliminate barriers to information sharing.

However, AARP is concerned that the practical impact of some of the other proposed changes
will be to lower standards for health IT to the detriment of consumers. We are especially
troubled by the proposal to remove 34 and revise 7 of the 60 certification criteria under the
Certification Program. It is our understanding that health IT developers choose to be certified in

a variety of areas, and that being certified in applicable areas allows them to be used by providers
participating in various payment programs. The proposed rule states:

       The removal or revision of the identified certification criteria would reduce
       burden and costs for health IT developers and clinicians, for reasons including
       but not limited to, eliminating the need to design and meet specific certification
       functionalities; prepare, test, and certify health IT in certain instances; and
       maintain conformance to certification requirements. We do not anticipate that
       the proposed removals or revisions would alter behavior across product
       implementations by health IT developers for the capabilities that have thus far
       been included as part of certification criteria in the Certification Program. We
       have observed over time that certification is likely no longer a primary factor
       driving improvements or compliance in particular areas, such as with respect to
       privacy and security.

ASTP/ONC states that because these criteria are widely adopted there is no need to continue
verifying compliance. We recognize that some of these criteria are technological standards meant
to ensure that different systems are compatible and can talk to each other (e.g., Fast Healthcare
Interoperability Resources or FHIR). In those cases, once everyone is speaking the same
language – and the criteria no longer steers or incentivizes a developer to adopt that language –
we would agree that certain criteria can be retired or revised. However, the proposal to remove or
revise several criteria that have consumer-friendly design and functionality, the removal of all 14
privacy and security criteria, and reductions in the transparency of artificial intelligence use in
decision support interventions do not adhere to the same spirit.

With regards to consumer-facing criteria, we disagree with ASTP/ONC’s rationale. First, this
argument focuses on existing technology and the unlikelihood that removing certain criteria from
the certification program will result in changes to that existing technology. An unanswered
question is how to ensure that future innovative systems still comply with the established
baseline, if that baseline remains the minimum requirement.

Second, certification criteria are a floor, not a ceiling. ASTP/ONC should encourage developers
to move beyond the standard. The proposal to remove criteria is just as likely to result in a
regression to the lowest common denominator as it is to spur innovation. If developers are not
held accountable for including specific design features, they could simply not offer them absent a
demonstrated market demand. This is particularly true for features that may apply , and are
extremely important, to a limited number of consumers. It is imperative that ASTP sets a
standard that maximizes access and quality for all people.

For example, ASTP/ONC proposes to revise the “patient demographics and observations”
certification criterion. This criterion requires the technology to have the capacity to record
specified individual characteristics. It does not require that this capacity be used by individuals –
only that it is available to be used. Under the proposed revision, a health IT module would
not need to demonstrate that it can record certain personal characteristics that could help inform
the delivery of quality care for those individuals. We believe that this criterion should retain the
current standards.

Removal of criteria elsewhere could also negatively impact trust in health IT systems. For
instance, ASTP/ONC justifies the removals of “privacy and security” criteria by stating the
criteria are already covered by HIPAA. But, inclusion under the certification program provides a
federal guardrail specifically aimed at ASTP/ONC, ensuring developers and their technology
must meet these standards for baseline security. In other words, even if the requirements of
HIPAA and the Certification Program are the same, how and to whom the requirements are
applied is different. HIPAA pertains to health care providers, health plans, and business
associates. It applies legal requirements to how those entities use, disclose, and protect personal
health information. The Certification Program pertains to the technologies and developers,
serving as a guardrail by requiring ASTP/ONC to certify/validate the technology meets specified
criteria. It serves as a check on making sure the technology has the capacity to achieve the
security required under HIPAA. HTI standards therefore must continue to include strong privacy
and security protections to proactively prevent technology from being a weak link in the defense
of patient information. At a minimum, the HTI standards should require retaining the auditable
events and tamper resistance, audit reports, and auditing actions on health information criteria
and associated privacy and security certification framework requirements, as proposed by ASTP
as an alternative proposal.

Likewise, with regards to transparency, dropping the AI “model card” transparency and source-
attribute requirements in “decision support interventions” criteria could erode patients’ and
health care providers’ confidence in the use of AI-enabled health IT products to support clinical
decision making. In 2023, a workgroup of the American Medical Informatics Association
recommended developing validation, verification, and certification processes for AI-CDS
systems with appropriate documentation and training. Lack of transparency may further limit
adoption of AI-enabled decision support technologies and hamper their competitiveness, despite
their potential to better aid providers in real-time care. Increasing transparency in AI source-
attributes would allow purchasing health systems (rather than point-of-care users) to discern
between different products based on their source and clinical information.

We also have concerns about the removal of the “family health history” criterion and the “patient
health information capture” criterion. “Family health history” requires that the user is enabled to
record, change, and access a patient’s family health history using standardized terminology.
Requiring electronic health records to capture this information in a reliable and standardized way
assists providers in identifying patients at higher risk for certain conditions. “Patient health
information capture” allows providers to incorporate unstructured patient generated health data
or data from a non-clinical setting into the patient record. Removal of these criteria could affect
patient engagement and care planning.

Lastly, with regards to the “design and performance” certification criteria, ASTP/ONC believes
that health IT developers have significant experience with implementing CMS requirements and
will continue to make sure that measures are recorded in a manner consistent with those
requirements even without these criteria. Therefore, ASTP/ONC is proposing removal of these
certification criteria. AARP does not share the belief that the impact of these criteria has
diminished since their adoption and that the administrative burdens to fulfill the requirements
now outweigh the benefits.

Conclusion

ASTP/ONC argues that developers will continue to include these and other data components
even if they are not necessary for certification. We believe this is a faulty assumption. First, if
developers are hindered by the certification process because it is too costly and time consuming
to demonstrate compliance, then ASTP/ONC can revise the process without removing the
requirement. ASTP/ONC already allows for attestation (rather than demonstration) elsewhere to
meet Conditions of Certification and Maintenance of Certification requirements. We believe that
attestation or revisions to align with other requirements elsewhere would alleviate any
administrative burdens without weakening standards. Second, if developers claim that creating
features to meet these criteria is too costly and burdensome, then there is no reason for them to
continue offering the feature without the certification requirement. It would be wrong to assume
they would continue to altruistically do something that they find onerous. In either case, the
certification criteria itself must remain in order for the tool to be offered. Removing or lessening
any criteria indicates that it is no longer important and should not be required.

AARP appreciates the opportunity to respond to the health data, technology and interoperability
proposed rule. We understand the challenges of balancing privacy and security with the desire to
improve care through data and technology. As you lay the groundwork for future innovation, we
urge you to ensure people can retain control of the use of their own information and that
individual and family caregiver needs will be given the utmost priority. We look forward to
working with you to make sure personal health data is used appropriately and responsibly in the
delivery of care. If you have any questions, feel free to contact me, or have your staff contact
Andrew Scholnick of our Government Affairs team at ascholnick@aarp.org.
