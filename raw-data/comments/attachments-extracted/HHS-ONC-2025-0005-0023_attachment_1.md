Dear Mr. Lipinski: I write to briefly comment on the above proposed rule. I suggest in the final
rule ASTP/ONC note the complexities of electronic health record (EHR) adoption in the
behavioral health sector and maintain the emergency access (§170.315(d)(6)) and accounting of
disclosures certification criteria (§170.315(d)(11)). Thank you for considering this input which
represents solely my personal views and not those of any other individual(s) nor an agency/
organization.

    A. For the statement reading, “ASTP/ONC tracks and publishes data on the adoption and use
       of health IT, including CEHRT, by US hospitals and office-based physicians. Our most
       recent survey data from 2021 shows that 96 percent of hospitals and 78 percent of
       physicians reported having a certified EHR.” It is important to note that EHR adoption
       continues to lag in the behavioral health (BH) space relative to other health care sectors.
       As one source bluntly puts it: “A majority of behavioral health providers are using
       outdated and disjointed electronic health record (EHR) and care coordination systems –
       and they know it.”1 The HHS Assistant Secretary for Planning and Evaluation likewise
       concluded in a recent report (December 2024) that “BH provider use of EHR lags behind
       other providers such as hospitals and physician practices (primary care, medical and
       surgical specialists)[….]”2 Additional financial and technical support as well integrating
       clinical decision tools within EHRs are among potential solutions proposed to foster EHR
       adoption in the behavioral health sector. In short, while tools may be “‘widely adopted’”
       or “‘widely implemented’” throughout the health care system as a whole this nonetheless
       may mask discrepancies in certain sectors.
    B. ONC/ASTP notes general support for Fast Healthcare Interoperability Resources (FHIR)
       regulatory policies. FHIR continues to be viewed by some as being important specifically
       in the behavioral health space.3

 C. Emergency access: I suggest that the emergency access criterion (§170.315(d)(6)) not be
removed as proposed.4 This provision enables known users to potentially have EHR
access during clinical emergencies and contingencies should standard system access be
disrupted to support patient care. Moreover, as ONC/ASTP itself has recognized, the
section is potentially broad enough that this could apply during broader public health
emergencies or even routine circumstances when standard patient care is challenged such
as a mass casualty incident.5 ONC/ASTP articulates no specific rationale for this
provision’s proposed removal from the certification criteria. Moreover, reflecting its
broader relevance, this specific section is cross-referenced twice in the most recent
HIPAA Security Rule draft6 and emergency access also is discussed in the current
Security Rule. Preserving this standard may encourage health care entities to ensure they
maintain this capability in their emergency and other plans.
D. Also, I urge that ONC for now maintain as is the accounting of disclosures criterion
(§170.315(d)(11)) as removing this requirement may have follow on implications for
other security and privacy requirements.7 Notably, this criterion cross-references the
HIPAA Privacy Rule and §170.210(d).8 This section also has a complex intersection with
HIPAA requirements.9 At this point, there are enough cross-references and
interdependencies among the various final and proposed privacy/security rules, including
even 42 CFR Part 2 (Confidentiality of Substance Use Disorder Patient Records)(§2.25),
that I would advise further evaluation before making any changes to this provision.
Speaking generally, I suggest a more measured approach, perhaps removing a few of the
criteria, re-evaluating the impact of these changes a year or two later, and then potentially
removing or revising other criteria rather than removing so many criteria at one time.
