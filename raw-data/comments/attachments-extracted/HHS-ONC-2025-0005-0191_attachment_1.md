Subject: ECRI Comments in Response to HTI‑5 Proposed Rule — Implications for Patient Safety,
Clinicians, and AI Adoption

February 26, 2026

Executive Summary
The HTI‑5 Proposed Rule represents the most significant contraction of the ONC Health IT Certification
Program since its inception. The rule proposes the removal of more than 50% of certification criteria,
including requirements related to clinical decision support, safety‑enhanced design, quality management
systems, accessibility‑centered design, and privacy and security certification criteria, while repositioning
certification around FHIR‑based APIs and future AI‑enabled interoperability. These changes eliminate
long‑standing expectations around usability, safety engineering, system integrity, auditability, and secure
access that have historically functioned as a minimum safety floor.

While ECRI supports reducing unnecessary regulatory burden and advancing interoperability, HTI‑5 raises
material clinician usability and patient‑safety concerns due to the elimination of critical guardrails without
clearly defined, enforceable replacements. ECRI recommends retaining (not removing) the safety
guiderails and supports the enhancement of the proposed API features of HTI-5.

Potential Impact of the Proposed Rule

    1. Patient Safety Implications

The principal concern is not interoperability itself, but the removal of safety‑critical guardrails without
articulated successor requirements. Key risks include diminished transparency and evidence standards for
decision support, weakened safety engineering and usability expectations, and gaps in baseline security,
integrity, and availability controls—particularly concerning given expanded AI‑enabled access to EHI. By
eliminating multiple privacy and security criteria and specific risk management requirements for AI, the
federal government is shifting the burden of safety verification entirely to healthcare providers and
purchasers. Smaller practices may lack the technical expertise to independently verify that their software
is safe and secure. The resulting risk is compounded, system redundancy is eliminated, and unfortunately
patients may be harmed as a result. With transparency requirements eliminated, we will not be able to
discern why. Removing technical security requirements could also lead to gaps in data integrity. If patient

    2. Impact on Clinicians

The proposed changes in HTI-5 extend beyond broad patient safety concerns and will have a direct,
tangible impact on the daily work of clinicians. The removal of key certification criteria will likely increase
workload, compromise usability, and introduce new risks in critical areas like medication prescribing.
Additionally, removing audit logs eliminates traceability for clinical decision making which is critical for
root cause analysis in response to an adverse event. This perpetuates risk and can cause future harm.

    •   Impact on Clinician Usability and Workload: The elimination of certification criteria for "safety-
        enhanced design," "usability," and "accessibility-centered design" removes the incentive for
        health IT developers to prioritize the user experience of clinicians. Without these standards,
        clinicians may be faced with systems that are less intuitive, more difficult to navigate, and prone
        to causing errors. This poor usability directly increases clinician workload and cognitive burden,
        forcing them to spend more time on documentation and administrative tasks and less time on
        direct patient care. The increased frustration and inefficiency associated with poorly designed
        systems are also significant contributors to clinician burnout.
    •   Impact on Medication Prescribing Safety: A critical concern is the removal of requirements for
        clinical decision support (CDS). Certified CDS has provided a foundational safety net for
        medication prescribing, including automated checks for drug-drug interactions, drug-allergy
        contraindications, and appropriate dosing. Without these certified guardrails, the reliability and
        availability of these vital safety features may diminish. Clinicians may no longer be able to trust
        that their EHR will effectively alert them to potentially life-threatening prescribing errors. This
        shifts the burden of medication safety back to the individual clinician, increasing the likelihood
        of manual errors and adverse drug events.
    •   Increased Burden of System Vetting: By removing numerous safety and security certification
        criteria, HTI-5 effectively transfers the responsibility of technology vetting from the ONC to
        individual healthcare organizations. Clinicians and their institutions will now be responsible for
        assessing the safety, security, and usability of health IT products themselves. This represents a
        significant new workload, particularly for smaller practices that lack the dedicated IT staff and
        technical expertise to conduct such rigorous evaluations. This shift not only increases the
        administrative burden on clinicians but also creates a risk of inconsistent safety standards across
        different care settings.

    3. AI‑Relevant Changes

The HTI-5 proposed rule is notable because it seeks to remove previously mandatory AI model card
requirements. An AI model card is a standardized, short document that acts as a "nutrition label" or
"instruction manual" for an artificial intelligence model. It is designed to provide transparency by detailing
a model's purpose, development process, performance, and limitations. Additionally, model cards
function as a mechanism for communicating key provenance information about an algorithm to clinicians

and purchasers. Without a uniform requirement to disclose funding sources and related conflicts of
interest for developer-supplied predictive tools, health systems may have reduced ability to identify
incentives that could shape model objectives (e.g., utilization or revenue-aligned predictions), interpret

risk of biased recommendations. This transparency gap is especially concerning as HTI-5 simultaneously
emphasizes AI-enabled data use and automation, potentially increasing the scale at which such tools
influence clinical workflow and patient outcomes.

If model cards and transparency requirements are removed, providers lose the ability to evaluate whether
an IT tool is appropriate for their patient population. For example, a model trained on data from white
males in an urban environment could be deployed in a rural environment for female patients of color with
no disclosure obligation.

Summary
As a leader in the space of health technology safety, clinical and patient safety, human factors, and
systems design, ECRI recommends a balanced approach to rulemaking that includes elements to achieve
burden reduction and FHIR‑based interoperability while including key requirements related to
decision‑support transparency, safety‑enhanced design, usability for clinicians, and core security and
audit controls.
