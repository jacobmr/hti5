Dear Dr. Keane,

The American College of Obstetricians & Gynecologists (ACOG), representing more than 62,000
physicians and partners in women’s health and the health of individuals seeking obstetric and
gynecologic services, appreciates the opportunity to provide comments on the Assistant Secretary
for Technology Policy/Office of the National Coordinator for Health Information Technology
(ASTP/ONC) proposed rule titled Health Data, Technology, and Interoperability: ASTP/ONC
Deregulatory Actions to Unleash Prosperity. We look forward to working with ASTP/ONC to
implement policies to improve the interoperability and privacy of sensitive patient health data.

As the national medical specialty society of women’s health physicians, ACOG firmly believes that
policies should be designed to protect the privacy, security, and safety of all women and individuals
who identify as women. To that end, access to medical records must be limited in certain
circumstances to protect the trust and security of the physician-patient relationship and the safety
of the patient. Unique considerations apply to medical record access in several circumstances
relevant to obstetrician-gynecologists (ob-gyns), including intimate partner violence (IPV), the
unique health needs of adolescent patients, punitive actions against pregnant and postpartum
patients with perinatal alcohol and substance use disorders, and those seeking reproductive health
care services.

IPV continues to be a significant public health problem in the US. The Centers for Disease Control
and Prevention (CDC) reports that nearly half of women in the United States (41% or 61 million)
experienced psychological aggression by an intimate partner during their lifetime, with 16 million
of those women stating that they first experienced this IPV before they reached the age of 18.1
Often, the abuser progressively isolates the woman from family and friends and may deprive her of
food, money, transportation, and access to health care, making medical record access a matter of a
patient safety and physical security.2,3

Unique health privacy considerations also apply to adolescent patients so that adolescents can
comfortably access care and discuss sensitive topics and behaviors that affect their health and well-
being. ACOG recommends that there should be a private conversation between the health care
provider and adolescent patient in order for the adolescent to be more comfortable talking with
their physician or other qualified health care professionals about issues such as sexuality and birth
control.4 With the expansion of electronic health records (EHRs) and patient portals, special privacy
considerations are needed to protect adolescents’ access to care and foster trust between the
patient and their physician.

Furthermore, health information is frequently used punitively against women who are pregnant
and parenting to address perinatal alcohol and substance use disorders despite the
recommendations of medical societies.5,6,7,8 With drug overdose and suicide as the leading causes of
maternal mortality, policymakers must ensure that medical record access policies do not
discourage pregnant and breastfeeding individuals from seeking care for addiction and mental
health disorders.9

It is with these goals in mind that we submit the following feedback and recommendations.

IV. Information Blocking (Part 171)

Section IV.A. “Access” and “Use” Definitions

ASTP/ONC is proposing to add language to their “access” and “use” definitions regarding electronic
health information (EHI). Specifically, they are proposing to explicitly codify that “access” means
the ability or means necessary to make EHI available for exchange or use, including by automation
technologies such as robotic process automation and autonomous artificial intelligence (AI)
systems. The Office is also proposing to explicitly codify that “use” means the ability for EHI, once
accessed or exchanged through whatever technological means, to be understood and acted upon,
including, without limitation, by automation technologies such as autonomous AI systems and
robotic process automation.

As an alternative to only revising the “access” and “use” definitions, ASTP/ONC is proposing to
revise the “exchange” definition in addition to the proposed revisions to the “access” and “use”
definitions. Specifically, they are proposing that the meaning of “exchange” includes, without
limitation, the ability for EHI to be transmitted, through use of automation technologies (e.g.,
robotic process automation, autonomous AI systems) or otherwise, between and among different
technologies, systems, platforms, or networks.

Electronic access to patient health information has the potential to increase standard-based
interoperability and make accessing this data easier both for providers and for patients. However,
this increased ease of access comes with an associated risk that ASTP/ONC must mitigate,
especially considering the inclusion of automated technologies in these proposed definitions. EHR
vendors do not currently have the capabilities to meet data segmentation standards for sensitive
health information and physicians facing information blocking requirements are put in a
compromising position of either not disclosing relevant health information about a patient’s
sensitive health care data or risking penalty. Further, current automation technologies are not fully
tested or understood, and so transmitting sensitive EHI could lead to its exposure to malicious
parties, causing harmful outcomes for patients. This is especially true for patients at risk of IPV or
other domestic violence circumstances. ACOG thus asserts that until confidentiality can be
completely assured when utilizing these electronic tools, the chances of risking patient safety are
too high for ASTP/ONC to codify unlimited usage of, access to, or exchange of EHI.

There has also been limited experience in how best to protect health data in the context of AI
programs. AI programs in health care inherently require large amounts of data, and there is
currently no established protocol for encryption and sharing of this data for research and clinical
purposes.10,11 While AI programs and techniques may have utility in synthesizing and sharing
health data, we have a long way to go before the imperative safety of this work can be actualized.
Accordingly, ACOG recommends against finalizing either of these proposals.

Section IV.B.2. Manner Exception Exhausted Condition

ASTP/ONC is proposing to increase the minimum number of alternative manners required to be
considered to have “exhausted” the Manner Exception from “two” to “all,” in line with the original
proposal for the manner exception exhausted condition. In referring to “all alternative manners,”
ASTP/ONC does not mean all possible manners of exchange other than the manner requested, but
rather specifically those manners that are set forth in the text of the alternative manner condition.
This addition would further reiterate what the regulation text says at present and what has been
stated in the preamble of the HTI-1 Final Rule. ASTP/ONC also welcomes comment on whether
revisions to the wording of the second subparagraph of the manner exception exhausted condition
of the Infeasibility Exception would help ensure an actor can only claim to a requestor that fulfilling
the request is “infeasible” under the manner exception exhausted condition when the actor has
exhausted the Manner Exception, as intended.

In the third subparagraph of the manner exception exhausted condition, ASTP/ONC proposes three
revisions: first, to replace “same” with “analogous” as it refers to the access, exchange, or use of EHI;
second and third, changing the “substantial number” element to “any,” by which ASTP/ONC means
at least one individual or entity; and removing consideration of whether such individual or entity is
similarly situated to the requestor. The proposed revisions would explicitly exclude from
application of the condition any situation where the actor offers at least one individual or entity the
requested access, exchange, or use. If ASTP/ONC finalizes the proposal to retain the manner
exception exhausted condition with removal of the “similarly situated” element from its third
subparagraph, instead of the alternative proposal described below to remove the condition in its
entirety, the Office also proposes to remove the fourth subparagraph completely.

In the alternative to the proposed revisions to the condition above, ASTP/ONC proposes to remove
the manner exception exhausted condition entirely if public comments received on this proposed
rule indicate that the proposed revisions would not be sufficient to eliminate or substantially
mitigate the misuse or abuse of the condition. ASTP/ONC seeks comments on this alternative
approach. If the Office were to finalize the alternative proposal to remove the manner exception
exhausted condition, subparagraph (4) within the list of Infeasibility Exception conditions (the
manner exception exhausted condition paragraph) would be reserved to preserve accuracy of
references in existing information blocking education materials to specific conditions of the
Infeasibility Exception by CFR citation. ASTP/ONC is not proposing at this time to redesignate any
of the conditions currently codified within the Infeasibility Exception that would remain in the
event they finalize the alternative proposal to remove either subparagraph (4), the proposal
discussed to remove subparagraph (3) of the Infeasibility Exception conditions, or both.

ACOG is concerned that many of the vocabulary changes suggested in ASTP/ONC’s first proposal
could create more problems than they solve. For example, increasing the minimum number of
alternative manners required from “two” to “all” runs the risk of further delaying the receipt of
health data because the party sending it must then jump through more bureaucratic hoops to meet
the manner exception exhausted condition. Providers should do their due diligence in their
handling of patient data, but if said party is unable to meet this information request, it makes
jumping through those hoops all the more frustrating for both the requestor and the party
attempting to meet the request. Conversely, increasing the minimum number of alternative
manners required can further increase the administrative and financial burden on the providers to
identify these manners and complete thorough review of their appropriateness and feasibility.
Further, making small vocabulary changes to the manner exception exhausted condition may seem
minor, but bad actors could take advantage of those changes and endanger patient safety by
exposing sensitive health data or using that data, once they receive it, to harm patients undergoing
sensitive procedures.

In a more general sense, though, ACOG’s goal is not just to safeguard sensitive health data, but also
to avoid confusion for providers and patients. We find that changes such as switching “same” to
“analogous” or “substantial number” to “any” are not important or consequential enough to force
regulatory change; indeed, they would add unnecessary confusion to provisions that are already
confusing and complicated. One such provision is that ASTP/ONC would reserve subparagraph (4)
of the Infeasibility Exception conditions (listed under § 171.204(a)) if they finalized the proposal to
remove the manner exception exhausted condition. This subparagraph, § 171.204(a)(4), is itself the
manner exception exhausted condition, and ACOG requests further clarification on this provision as
we are unsure how the manner exception exhausted condition subparagraph can be retained while
also removing the condition.

That being said, ACOG supports the existing iteration of the manner exception exhausted condition.
Many practices do not have the financial resources to safely and effectively fulfill a request in the
manner proposed, and physicians and practices should not be disincentivized to implement these
technologies or penalized unduly for requests they are unable to fulfill. Ultimately, ACOG
recommends that vocabulary revisions from the first proposal are not finalized. However,
ACOG recommends even more strongly that ASTP/ONC keep the manner exception
exhausted condition in place as it has already been finalized.
Section IV.C. Manner Exception

ASTP/ONC offers two proposals. First, they propose to revise the Manner Exception to add a third
subparagraph to the second part of its manner requested condition to incorporate in regulation text
and explicitly state that any contract or agreement related to the access, exchange, or use of EHI: (1)
must be at market rate; (2) must not be a contract of adhesion; and (3) must not contain
unconscionable terms. As stated in the Cures Act Final Rule, agreements must be negotiated at
market rate.

ASTP/ONC is proposing to add several definitions to the existing regulatory language within this
first proposal. First, the Office is proposing to define “market rate” using paragraph (1) of the
definition of “fair market value” found in the Stark Law regulations. Second, ASTP/ONC is proposing
to define “contract of adhesion” as a contract provided on a standardized form, or on a “take it or
leave it basis” without a realistic opportunity to bargain where the desired product, services,
access, use, or exchange cannot be provided except by acquiescing to the form contract. Third, they
are proposing to incorporate the Merriam-Webster Dictionary’s definition of “unconscionable” to
define “unconscionable terms” as those terms that are excessive, unreasonable, or shockingly unfair
or unjust. An actor’s practice that meets the manner requested condition of the Manner Exception
does not currently, and would not under the first proposed revision, have to also satisfy the Fees
Exception or the Licensing Exception in order for the actor to have certainty the actor’s practice
specific to charging a fee or licensing interoperability elements for fulfilling the requested EHI
access, exchange, or use in the manner requested would not be considered information blocking.
ASTP/ONC seeks comments on the proposed provision and proposed definitions, suggestions for
alternative definitions, and any missing components that would more explicitly describe excluded
contractual terms.

The changes ASTP/ONC has suggested indicate not only increased clarity, but increased protections
relating to EHI. Requiring contracts and agreements dealing with EHI exchange, access, or use to be
at “market rate,” consistent with the Stark Law regulations’ definition of “fair market value,” should
help to keep exchange, access, and use of EHI reliable and trustworthy. Prohibiting contracts of
adhesion in such a setting will help prevent power imbalances in these EHI exchanges. Indeed, this
will allow health care providers and other such parties to have more power in negotiating EHI-
related contracts, which will give them more room to protect sensitive health data as well. ACOG is
especially pleased by ASTP/ONC’s proposal to block these contracts from including
“unconscionable terms.” ACOG has long prioritized fair and equitable health care delivery that
keeps both patients and their sensitive health data safe, and this proposal will prevent any actors
from exchanging, accessing, or using EHI in an unreasonable or unjust way. ACOG also appreciates
that under these proposed revisions, actors would not also have to satisfy the Fees or Licensing
Exceptions (in addition to the manner requested condition of the Manner Exception) to ensure that
their behavior does not constitute information blocking, as we have expressed concerns about
expanding information blocking requirements in the past due to this potentially risking the privacy
of sensitive health data. As such, ACOG recommends finalizing this proposal.
As an alternative to the first proposal, ASTP/ONC proposes to remove the agreement that if an actor
fulfills a request for EHI in any manner requested, any fees charged by the actor in relation to
fulfilling the request are not required to satisfy the Fees Exception, and any license of
interoperability elements granted by the actor in relation to fulfilling the request are not required
to satisfy the Licensing Exception. The Fees Exception details the conditions an actor’s practice
must meet when charging fees for accessing, exchanging, or using EHI to not be considered
information blocking, and the Licensing Exception does the same for an actor's practice to license
interoperability elements in order for EHI to be accessed, exchanged, or used. Under this alternative
proposal, ASTP/ONC proposes that the agreement would need to satisfy the Fees Exception and the
Licensing Exception, even when fulfilling the request in any manner requested. This approach
would mean that, while the actor and requestor retain flexibility to reach mutually agreeable terms
for the actor to fulfill access, exchange, or use of the requested EHI in the manner requested, any
fees charged by the actor and any licensing terms for interoperability elements from the actor to
the requestor related to fulfilling the request must meet all of the requirements of the Fees
Exception and the Licensing Exception for the actor to have certainty that their practice of charging
fees or licensing interoperability elements would not constitute information blocking.

Enforcing the Fees Exception and the Licensing Exception protects unnecessary or unwieldy
penalties associated with activities that potentially result in information blocking. By implementing
a requirement to satisfy these exceptions in the case of fulfilling a request by any manner, the Office
is potentially subjecting actors to information blocking penalties that they otherwise would not
have accrued. ACOG is also concerned that requiring agreements and contracts to additionally
satisfy the Fees and Licensing Exceptions to avoid running afoul of information blocking
requirements will further strain provider capabilities and make it more difficult for them to protect
and care for their patients effectively. Therefore, ACOG recommends against finalizing this
alternative proposal, and instead we reiterate our recommendation of the first proposal.
