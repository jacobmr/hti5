Dear Dr. Keane,

The American Nurses Association (ANA) appreciates the opportunity to comment on the Assistant
Secretary for Technology Policy (ASTP)/Office on the National Coordinator for Health Information
Technology (ONC) proposed rule seeking feedback on deregulatory action to support adoption and
use of health information technology (HIT). As the national voice of nursing, ANA supports
modernization of HIT policies that reduce administrative burden while strengthening
interoperability, transparency, and patient safety.

More specifically, ONC should:
• support reducing outdated certification criteria,
• ensure that nursing-relevant data informs initiatives and measures,
• reinstate decision support transparency requirements, and
• protect data privacy.

ANA is the premier organization representing the interests of the nation’s over 5 million registered
nurses (RNs) through its state and constituent member associations, organizational affiliates, and
individual members. ANA advances the nursing profession by fostering high standards of nursing
practice, promoting a safe and ethical work environment, bolstering the health and wellness of
nurses, and advocating for healthcare issues that affect nurses and the public. ANA members also
include the four Advanced Practice Registered Nurse (APRN) roles: nurse practitioners (NPs),
clinical nurse specialists (CNSs), certiﬁed nurse-midwives (CNMs), and certiﬁed registered nurse
anesthetists (CRNAs). RNs serve in multiple direct care, care coordination, and administration
leadership roles, across the full spectrum of healthcare settings. RNs provide and coordinate
patient care, educate patients and the public about various health conditions including essential
self-care, and provide advice and emotional support to patients and their family members.

Many of our nurses see firsthand how the use of HIT impacts nursing practice and patient care.
ANA is encouraged by ONC’s desire to reduce regulatory barriers to bolster use of HIT but also
recognize the need to balance that with nurse practice and patient privacy. ANA appreciates
thoughtful consideration of our recommendations.
 1. ONC must support reducing redundant and outdated certification criteria

ONC proposes to remove over half of the certification criteria in the health IT certification program.
ONC asserts that these criteria are either redundant or impede innovation. Removing these criteria
would also support the Administration’s goal of removing regulatory burdens. ANA strongly
supports ONC’s efforts to streamline certification by removing obsolete or duplicative
requirements. Reducing unnecessary certification elements can lower implementation burdens on
developers and healthcare organizations, ultimately reducing workflow inefficiencies that directly
impact nursing practice.

ANA recommends that ONC proceed with removal of criteria that no longer align with modern
interoperability standards if functionalities essential to nursing practice such as, care plans,
clinical decision support, and documentation tools, are preserved and not weakened.

A) ONC must address concerns with eliminating decision support transparency
requirements.

ANA supports reducing unnecessary administrative and regulatory burden, but is concerned by
removal of transparency requirements for algorithms, AI-augmented decision support, and other
automated tools. Nurses rely on clear visibility into how technology influences clinical
recommendations. ANA also supports requiring vendors to disclose whether any clinical output
has been influenced by AI/machine learning (ML) and the extent of this influence. ANA
recommends that ONC examine retaining, revising, or replacing model card‑type transparency
requirements to ensure nurses can understand: data sources, known limitations, intended use,
and potential bias or equity considerations.

B) ONC must ensure that data privacy and security is protected as certification criteria
are removed.

As certification elements are deprecated, ANA emphasizes the need to maintain strong protection
around patient information. Nurses are frontline stewards of patient privacy. ANA recommends
conducting structured impact assessments to verify that removal of legacy criteria does not
unintentionally weaken privacy or security safeguards. ONC should also provide clear and timely
guidance to healthcare organizations about any new or revised expectations for compliance.
Furthermore, data privacy and security criteria must not be removed from the certification criteria.

C) ONC must provide clear guidance for transition periods.

Removing and updating certification requirements can create practical challenges for health IT
developers, nursing informaticists, and healthcare organizations responsible for implementation
and governance. ANA recommends that ONC provide detailed timelines, transition plans, and
technical guidance. ONC should offer supported resources such as mapping tools, crosswalks,
and educational webinars on navigating sunset criteria and adopting updated API standards. ANA
also encourages ONC to create and lead a nursing advisory workgroup to support transition
planning and evaluation.
 2. ONC must support for Fast Healthcare Interoperability Resources (FHIR)‑First, modern
Application Programming Interface (API)–driven interoperability.

ONC proposes to create a new foundation on which to build the future FHIR requirements. This
new interface will support AI enabled solutions. ANA looks forward to working with the
Administration on these AI driven solutions and believes that AI should be available for all to use in
the appropriate manner. ANA also supports ONC’s direction toward universal, modern, FHIR-
based APIs that support standardized data exchange across all clinical settings. Reliable
interoperability is essential for:

• Real-time decision-making at the point of care
• High‑quality documentation
• Nurse-led care coordination
• Informatics innovation

ANA recommends that ONC ensure nursing-relevant data elements (e.g., assessments, diagnoses,
interventions, outcomes, care plan items) are explicitly included and mapped in required FHIR
implementation guides. Furthermore, ONC should engage nursing informatics experts in shaping
these specifications.

3.  ONC must clarify information blocking definitions.

ONC proposes editing the definitions of both “access” and “use” in the Code of Federal
Regulations to ensure that the definitions include automated access to records. This new definition
specifically includes AI and other autonomous systems. ANA supports updated definitions that
reduce ambiguity and better align with modern interoperability capabilities. Nurses require timely,
reliable access to complete patient information to deliver safe care.

ANA recommends ensuring revised definitions do not create new obstacles to care team access.
ONC should monitor for unintended consequences in post‑acute and community-based settings,
where nurses often experience the greatest information gaps.

4.  ONC must ensure ongoing nursing engagement and evaluation.

Nurses are the largest segment of the health workforce and the most frequent users of electronic
health record (EHR) systems. Nursing informaticists are deeply experienced in the implementation
realities of health IT.

ANA recommends establishing mechanisms for continuous nursing involvement in ONC standards
updates, implementation monitoring, and post‑rule evaluation. Furthermore, ONC should conduct
periodic assessments of burden reduction and interoperability outcomes, including effects on
clinical workflow, safety, and data quality.

ANA supports ONC’s commitment to modernizing certification, strengthening interoperability, and
reducing burden. ANA urges ONC to ensure that these changes continue to protect essential
nursing functionality, promote transparency in AI-enabled tools, safeguard patient privacy, and
support the informatics infrastructure that underpins safe, effective nursing practice.
