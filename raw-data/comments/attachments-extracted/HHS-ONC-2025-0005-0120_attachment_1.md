At the same time, we are concerned that several of the deregulatory steps taken in HTI-5 risk
introducing architectural gaps that may slow real world interoperability and weaken the feedback
loops that ASTP/ONC (and the market) need to manage a successful transition.
For example, ASTP/ONC proposes removing specific transparency and risk management
elements for predictive Decision Support Interventions (DSIs), including provisions identified in
45 C.F.R. § 170.315(b)(11)(iv)–(vi). As noted, Dartmouth Health supports innovation and
burden reduction where appropriate, but believes ONC should retain minimum guardrails for
predictive DSIs used in clinical settings: (1) intended use and limitations, (2) monitoring and
feedback loops, (3) auditability, and (4) clear human override pathways. These guardrails remain
narrow, testable, and consistent with a risk-based approach.
Minimum guardrails for DSIs can be established by preserving a minimal, low-burden baseline
by requiring certified Health IT Modules to support an exportable, machine-readable Evidence
Artifact for each predictive DSI. This would replace prescriptive model card–style
documentation while retaining essential transparency and risk-governance hooks for
procurement, clinical trust, and audit readiness—without requiring raw training data, protected
health information, or disclosure of trade secrets or confidential commercial information.
In addition to the risks posed to individual patients in the DSI arena without maintaining
minimum guardrails, other proposals present significant risks to the speed, uniformity, and
reliability of public health data exchange and to the sustainability of public health surveillance
infrastructure in general. Reducing or eliminating common health information technology
standards and certification requirements will predictably slow data flow and increase variability
in how data are received by public health agencies. When standards are removed, health IT
developers and healthcare organizations implement data exchange using different formats,
structures, and levels of completeness. As a result, public health agencies no longer receive data
in a consistent, standardized manner and must accommodate multiple vendor-specific
implementations for the same reporting use cases – thus increasing inefficiencies.
If ASTP/ONC moves forward with decertification or the removal of functional guardrails
without first defining how data is created, collected, and preserved in a usable, searchable, and
sharable format, we risk patient harm and compromise the trustworthiness of patient medical
records and thus our healthcare system.
