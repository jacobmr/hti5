           Manifest MedEx (“MX”) appreciates the opportunity to provide comments on the proposed rule titled
           "Health Data, Technology, and Interoperability: ASTP/ONC Deregulatory Actions to Unleash
           Prosperity" (the "Proposed Rule"). MX is a not-for-profit health information exchange (“HIE”) serving
           the state of California, connecting providers, payers, and public health agencies. We commend
           ASTP/ONC's commitment to reducing regulatory burden and focusing on outcomes rather than
           prescriptive technical criteria. While MX supports several aspects of this deregulatory initiative, we write
           to express significant concern regarding certain provisions that, if finalized as proposed, would introduce
           substantial contractual, operational, and compliance risks for health information exchanges and networks
           nationwide, with the potential to unintentionally materially reduce the exchange of health information.

           I. Proposed Changes to the Manner Exception: Contract of Adhesion Definition
           MX strongly opposes the proposed definition of "contract of adhesion" set forth in § 171.102 and the
           corresponding revision to § 171.301(a)(2). ASTP proposes that, to satisfy the Manner Exception, any
           agreement governing access, exchange, or use of EHI: (1) must be at market rate; (2) must not be a
           "contract of adhesion"; and (3) must not contain "unconscionable terms."
           The proposed definition would classify as a contract of adhesion any "contract provided on a
           standardized form, or on a 'take it or leave it basis' without a realistic opportunity to bargain where the
           desired product, services, access, use, or exchange cannot be provided except by acquiescing to the form
           contract." This definition focuses entirely on the form of the contract, not its purpose or effect.
           Of gravest concern is ASTP's statement that contracts meeting this definition "would also likely
           constitute an interference under the information blocking regulations." This language suggests a
           presumption of information blocking for any standardized agreement, regardless of whether such
           agreement actually facilitates the access, exchange, or use of EHI.

                B. The Proposal Would Undermine Its Stated Purpose
           ASTP cites concern about "the inherent power inequity between EHR developers and various types of
           requestors." The proposed remedy, however, would achieve the opposite of its intended effect:
                •   It would undermine trusted exchange by discouraging the standardized, common agreement
                    terms that enable it;
                •   It would advantage large EHR developers, which can better afford the legal staff needed to
                    support individualized negotiations with hundreds or thousands of customers; and
                •   It would disadvantage small providers and nonprofit HIEs who lack the resources for bespoke
                    contracting.
                C. Recommended Approach
           We recommend that ASTP adopt its alternative proposal: retain the language at § 171.301(a)(1) as-is
           and require that, to satisfy the Manner Exception, an Actor must also meet the requirements of the
           respective Fees Exception and Licensing Exception. This approach would mitigate the concerns
           described above while honoring "the breadth of the EHI definition and flexibility in the manner an actor
           fulfills a request to access, exchange, or use EHI, while providing market participants the ability to reach
           and maintain market negotiated terms."
           If ASTP proceeds with the primary proposal, we urge adoption of the existing common law instead of
           creating a new legal doctrine that could potentially undermine ongoing information exchange. We
           recommend the following alternative definition:
           Contract of adhesion means a contract that: (i) is presented on a standardized form without realistic
           opportunity to negotiate material terms; (ii) is offered by a party with substantially superior bargaining

           II. Proposed Removal of the TEFCA Manner Exception
           MX supports the proposed removal of the TEFCA Manner Exception at § 171.403. As ASTP recognizes,
           TEFCA has continued to mature and develop since the exception was finalized in the HTI-1 Final Rule.
           The market now provides adequate alternatives that offer comparable or better connectivity, and a federal
           incentive specific to TEFCA is no longer necessary. Market forces should drive appropriate adoption
           and participation, while participants in TEFCA remain subject to the full information blocking
           regulations.
           We note, however, that if ASTP finalizes the contract of adhesion definition as proposed while removing
           the TEFCA Manner Exception, TEFCA participants would face an untenable regulatory posture: ASTP
           would mandate the use of standardized agreements as a condition of TEFCA participation while
           simultaneously classifying such agreements as presumptive information blocking.19 This inconsistency
           must be resolved.
           III. Proposed Removal of the "Third Party Seeking Modification Use" Condition
           We do not support the proposed removal of the "third party seeking modification use" condition from
           the Infeasibility Exception at § 171.204(a)(3). While we appreciate ASTP's concern that this condition
           is "susceptible to misuse by actors withholding EHI," our operational experience suggests this exception
           serves an important function in ensuring data accuracy.
           HIEs routinely receive requests from non-HIPAA-covered entities seeking revision to EHI. In many
           cases, after consultation with the originating HIPAA-covered entity, the requestor's characterization of
           information as "erroneous" proves incorrect. These situations extend to patients who may genuinely
           forget a medication or procedure, or who may seek additional medications or procedures that could cause
           them harm.
           The exception as currently codified is narrowly tailored, applying only when the third party is not a
           HIPAA-covered entity. If ASTP proceeds with this proposal, we request specific guidance clarifying
           that the Security and Preventing Harm exceptions may be used in circumstances where data modification
           requests raise accuracy or safety concerns.
           IV. Proposed Clarification Regarding Automated Access
           We support ASTP's proposal to clarify that "access," "exchange," and "use" include automated means
           such as robotic process automation and autonomous artificial intelligence systems. This clarification
           appropriately aligns the regulations with the statutory information blocking definition, which "focuses
           on practices 'likely to interfere with, prevent, or materially discourage access, exchange, or use of
           electronic health information' without any specification of, or limitation to, access, exchange, or use of
           EHI through particular mechanisms, technologies, or workflows."
           We emphasize that entities employing automated systems must continue to comply with existing security
           and privacy standards, including the HIPAA Privacy and Security Rules, and we encourage ASTP to
           continue monitoring the use of such tools to ensure alignment with the FAVES principles (fair,
           appropriate, valid, effective, and safe).

           V. Proposed Changes to the "Manner Exception Exhausted" Condition
           We support ASTP's proposed syntactical changes to the Manner Exception Exhausted condition at §
           171.204(a)(4) to ensure the exception is not abused, and we recommend against the alternative proposal
           to remove the condition entirely. This condition serves a legitimate function in protecting actors from
           requestors who impose unreasonable demands where agreement is truly unlikely. We recommend that
           ASTP retain this condition with the proposed clarifying language while focusing enforcement on those
           who abuse the exception.
           VI. Proposed Changes to Certification Requirements
           We generally support ASTP's deregulatory approach to certification requirements, but would note that
           the industry might not yet be ready and able to move entirely away from CCD-based exchange and
           removal of standardization of CCDs has the potential to result in lower quality data exchange and a
           disproportionate burden on smaller, less resourced providers and participants in exchange. We
           recommend working towards this goal, but including it in a subsequent rule change instead of adopting
           it at this time.
           Conclusion
           We appreciate ASTP's commitment to reducing regulatory burden and focusing on outcomes. However,
           the proposed contract of adhesion definition represents a significant departure from established legal
           principles and would impose substantial operational and compliance risks on the health information
           exchange infrastructure that serves millions of patients nationwide.
           We urge ASTP to:
                1. Adopt the alternative proposal to retain § 171.301(a)(1) as-is rather than adding the contractual
                   limitations at proposed § 171.301(a)(2)(iii);
                2. If the contractual limitations are finalized, adopt the common law definition of "contract of
                   adhesion";
                3. Retain the "third party seeking modification use" condition with clarifying guidance;
                4. Finalize the proposed clarification regarding automated access; and
                5. Retain the "manner exception exhausted" condition with the proposed syntactical improvements.
           We welcome the opportunity to discuss these comments with ASTP and to provide additional
           information as the agency works toward a final rule that promotes interoperability while protecting the
           trusted exchange infrastructure that healthcare organizations and patients rely upon.
