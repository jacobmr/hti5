Dear Dr. Keane,

On behalf of The MetroHealth System in Cleveland, Ohio we support ASTP/ONC’s aim to
modernize the Certification Program, streamline duplicative requirements, and advance a
durable foundation for FHIR‑based APIs and AI‑enabled interoperability. We also appreciate the
intent to clarify information sharing while reducing burden.

From an economics perspective, we acknowledge that tighter information‑blocking provisions
and clearer rights of access for third‑party apps could, over time, lower barriers to entry, spur
competition on top of EHR platforms, and stimulate innovation (including AI ‑enabled tools),
potentially yielding dynamic efficiency gains that benefit patients and providers. However, those
potential long‑run gains do not obviate the near‑ to mid‑term rise in transaction costs for
hospitals absent guardrails—e.g., loss of standardized contracts, Stark‑style fair‑market‑value
analyses for pricing, compelled replication of bespoke integrations when prior access exists, and
reduced ability to differentiate risk among requesters—which collectively increase legal,
valuation, security review, and technical integration workloads. We therefore support
pro‑competitive goals while urging ONC to preserve standards‑first pathways and reasonable
risk‑based terms so that innovation benefits can be realized without imposing disproportionate
costs on care delivery.

Several specific provisions—as drafted—would create material clinical, operational, and
compliance risks for hospitals. We respectfully request the following targeted revisions and
clarifications:

1.  Clarify “Access” and “Use” for automation; remove “without limitation.”

    HTI‑5 expands the definitions of “access” and “use” to include automated actors (RPA/agentic
    AI). We ask ONC to delete the phrase “without limitation,” and to expressly affirm that actors
    may impose reasonable, risk‑based terms—such as throttling, change‑management
    coordination, audit logging, and sandbox testing—when automation tools interact with certified
    EHR technology. This ensures safety, data‑integrity, and cybersecurity without impeding lawful
    access.

2.  Preserve a workable path in the Manner and Infeasibility exceptions.

We urge ONC to retain a clearly defined “Manner Exhausted” condition and to avoid shifting
thresholds from “substantial number” to “any,” or removing “similarly situated.” As drafted,
hospitals and developers could be compelled into bespoke, non‑standard integration paths if any
prior requester ever received an “analogous” capability, increasing cost, complexity, and risk.
Prioritizing standards‑based exchange remains essential.

3. Permit standardized (non‑unconscionable) contracts; avoid importing Stark FMV into
   information‑blocking pricing tests.

To keep transaction costs manageable for providers, ONC should permit standardized terms (so
long as not unconscionable) and avoid tethering “market rate” to Stark‑based fair‑market‑value
constructs that are ill‑suited to API and interoperability licensing.

4. Maintain the TEFCA Manner Exception and add targeted incentives for smaller providers to join
   TEFCA.

We support TEFCA’s rapid progress and want to accelerate inclusion of smaller and lesser-
resourced providers (e.g., Critical Access Hospitals, FQHCs, rural practices). Rather than
eliminating the TEFCA Manner Exception, we recommend: (a) retain the exception; (b) extend
eligibility so a provider may rely on it even if the requester is not yet a TEFCA participant; (c)
create a time‑limited safe harbor for low‑resourced providers so fulfillment via TEFCA satisfies
the Manner Exception; and (d) consider modest federal incentives (implementation grants,
waived network fees, and simplified onboarding) to defray initial costs. These adjustments would
expand nationwide exchange while reducing fragmentation risk for patients who receive care
across disparate settings. [TEFCA‑related proposals in HTI‑5]

5. Retain essential evidence transparency for Clinical Decision Support (CDS).

We support removing low‑value certification burden, but request preserving core evidence
transparency for CDS so hospitals can govern clinical content and predictive tools responsibly
(e.g., source attributes/model briefs at implementation).

6. Implementation timeline.

If ONC finalizes information‑blocking revisions, we request a minimum six‑month effective‑date
runway to update policies, contracts, and technical controls across our enterprise.

These adjustments would maintain momentum toward a modern, standards‑based ecosystem
while safeguarding patient safety, data integrity, and operational feasibility for hospitals and
community providers alike.
