The Anesthesia Patient Safety Foundation (APSF) mission is to ensure that no one is harmed by anesthesia care.
In pursuit of that mission, APSF believes that patient harm is not an acceptable cost of modern medical care.
Over the past three decades, as health IT systems have become essential to providing patient care, we have seen
how poorly designed medical technology, inadequate human factors engineering, weak quality systems, and
insufficient transparency can lead to medication errors, wrong-patient events, delayed diagnoses, and
catastrophic adverse outcomes. The original health IT certification standards were developed in direct response
to documented patient harm — including findings from the Institute of Medicine’s landmark 2011 report Health
IT and Patient Safety: Building Safer Systems for Better Care, which identified poor usability and lack of
systematic risk management as major threats to patient safety. These standards were not bureaucratic artifacts;
they were safety interventions, designed to establish a minimum floor of protection for patients across all care
settings.

This letter is complementary to and supportive of the comments set forth in HTI-5: A Patient Safety
Analysis from Jacob Reider, former ONC Director, and colleagues. APSF strongly supports retaining the patient
safety–related certification criteria currently proposed for removal under the HTI-5 rule. The following
rationale is provided.

Certification Requirements Exist Because Patients Were Harmed

The certification criteria targeted by HTI-5 — including Safety-Enhanced Design, Quality Management
Systems, audit logging, authentication and access control, and clinical decision support (CDS) source
transparency — were implemented as direct responses to real-world patient injuries and deaths. The safety-
enhanced design requirement (170.315(g)(3)) operationalized human factors engineering and usability testing
for high-risk functions such as medication ordering and drug interaction checking. The quality management
system requirement (170.315(g)(4)) embedded structured risk analysis and systematic safety processes into
health IT development. Audit log criteria and tamper-resistance provisions created accountability and enabled
investigation of adverse events. Authentication and multi-factor requirements reduced wrong-patient risks. CDS
source attribution requirements protected clinicians and patients from undisclosed commercial influence.

These protections collectively form a baseline safety infrastructure. Removing them risks eroding safeguards
that have enhanced patient safety.

“Widespread Adoption” Is Not a Safety Strategy

APSF concurs with the analysis that the rationale of “widespread adoption” is logically and historically
flawed. Safeguards have become widespread precisely because they were required. In anesthesia, we did
not abandon pulse oximetry as a recommended monitoring standard once adoption became universal; we
recognized that the requirement itself created the norm. The same principle applies here. A safety floor is
not obsolete because it is universally met — it is functioning as intended.

Absent certification requirements, there is no assurance that new entrants — including AI-enabled
systems — will maintain these protections. Market competition alone does not reliably reward safety
investments, particularly when those investments increase development cost and time to market.

Security Is Safety

APSF is particularly concerned about the proposed removal of authentication, audit, and integrity
requirements. In high-acuity environments such as the operating room, incorrect patient identification or
corrupted clinical information can have immediate and catastrophic consequences. Security controls are
not merely privacy protections; they are essential safety controls to prevent unauthorized access and
actions taken on the wrong patient. HIPAA establishes legal obligations but does not verify
implementation, test usability, or create the same enforcement pathways that certification enables.

Transparency and Enforcement Matter

The enforcement history by the Department of Justice involving vendors such as eClinicalWorks and
Practice Fusion demonstrates that certification requirements provide meaningful accountability
mechanisms. Audit log criteria enabled investigation of system failures, and CDS source attribution
requirements exposed undisclosed commercial influence in clinical decision support. Eliminating these
criteria would remove proven enforcement tools and weaken incentives for compliance.

Interoperability and Safety Are Complementary

APSF supports HTI-5’s emphasis on interoperability, FHIR-based exchange, and modernization of
standards. However, interoperability validation does not substitute for internal product safety. Data that
move seamlessly between systems remain dangerous if the originating system is poorly designed,
inadequately tested, or influenced by undisclosed conflicts of interest. Product safety and interoperability
are complementary, not interchangeable.

Protect the Safety Floor

Health IT certification has served as a national safety floor — not a ceiling — ensuring that even small
practices, rural hospitals, and resource-limited organizations can rely on baseline protections embedded in
their technology. Removing core safety criteria shifts verification burdens onto organizations least
equipped to perform independent evaluation and risks creating a two-tier market in which cost
competition outpaces safety assurance.

For these reasons, the Anesthesia Patient Safety Foundation strongly endorses the following
recommendations articulated in HTI-5: A Patient Safety Analysis:

• Retain the Safety-Enhanced Design requirement (170.315(g)(3)) in full.
• Retain the Quality Management System requirement (170.315(g)(4)).
• Retain audit log and tamper-resistance requirements (170.315(d)(2), (d)(3), (d)(10)).
• Retain authentication and multi-factor access control requirements (170.315(d)(1), (d)(13)).
• Retain CDS source attribution and funding transparency requirements (170.315(b)(11)).
• Reject the “widespread adoption” rationale as a basis for removing safety requirements.
• Distinguish clearly between interoperability validation and intrinsic product safety.
• Preserve enforcement mechanisms that have demonstrably protected patients and the public fisc.

Modernization and burden reduction are legitimate policy goals. However, patient safety protections that
were adopted to mitigate documented risks should not be dismantled absent compelling evidence that
those risks have been eliminated or are better mitigated through alternative mechanisms.

APSF stands ready to collaborate with ONC and other stakeholders to ensure that health information
technology continues to advance innovation while preserving the hard-earned safety gains of the past
three decades.
