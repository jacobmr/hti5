PUBLIC HEALTH DIVISION
Office of the State Public Health Director
Data Modernization Office

Tina Kotek, Governor

February 18, 2026

To the National Coordinator for Health Information Technology:

On behalf of the Oregon Health Authority Public Health Data Modernization Office, we appreciate the
opportunity to provide comments on the proposed rule addressing deregulatory actions within the Department
of Health and Human Services related to health information technology standards, implementation
specifications, certification criteria and certification programs, and information blocking.

The proposed deregulatory actions present significant long-term risks to the speed, uniformity, and reliability of
public health data exchange and to the sustainability of public health surveillance infrastructure nationwide.

In practical terms, reducing or eliminating common health information technology standards and certification
requirements will predictably slow data flow and increase variability in how data are received by public health
agencies. When standards are removed, health IT developers and healthcare organizations implement data
exchange using different formats, structures, and levels of completeness. As a result, public health agencies no
longer receive data in a consistent, standardized manner and must accommodate multiple vendor-specific
implementations for the same reporting use cases.

This variability requires public health agencies to perform additional data transformation, mapping, validation,
and quality assurance activities before data can be used for surveillance. These added steps introduce delays,
often days or weeks, reducing the timeliness of outbreak detection, trend monitoring, and response activities
that depend on near real-time data. These delays directly undermine the objectives of the Centers for Disease
Control and Prevention’s Data Modernization Initiative, which emphasizes timely, standardized, and
interoperable data to enable rapid decision-making and response.

Because public health reporting is mandatory, public health agencies do not have discretion over whether to
receive non-standard data. Instead, limited technical and financial resources must be redirected away from
analytics, automation, and modernization efforts and toward basic data normalization, remediation, and system
maintenance. This shift runs counter to the intent of the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention Public
Health Infrastructure Grant (PHIG), which invests federal resources to strengthen foundational public health
capabilities, modernize data systems, and reduce manual and duplicative work across jurisdictions.

The lack of uniform data also undermines national comparability. Public health surveillance relies on consistent
definitions and data structures to aggregate and analyze information across facilities, regions, states, and federal
partners. Increased variability at the point of data generation makes it more difficult to produce accurate,
comparable national surveillance metrics, weakening coordination, situational awareness, and collective
response, which are key goals of both the Data Modernization Initiative and federally supported public health
surveillance programs.

These impacts compound across the public health system. As customization, manual intervention, and rework
become more common, data pipelines become slower, more fragile, and less adaptable. This threatens the long-
term sustainability of public health surveillance infrastructure across the United States, particularly for under-
resourced jurisdictions, and reduces the return on substantial federal investments made through initiatives such
as PHIG to modernize and strengthen public health data systems.

Public health surveillance depends on the timely and uniform receipt of data from healthcare organizations.
Reducing standardization slows the flow of usable data, increases inconsistencies across reporting entities, and
degrades the ability of public health agencies to rapidly aggregate, analyze, and act on information. Innovation
in public health is best supported when data are received in a standardized, predictable, and timely manner,
enabling automation, advanced analytics, and scalable modernization in alignment with Centers for Disease
Control and Prevention Data Modernization Initiative priorities. Fragmented data ecosystems, by contrast, force
public health agencies to focus on basic data normalization rather than innovation, increasing costs and slowing
progress.

These operational impacts directly reinforce the five core points raised by the Association of Public Health
Laboratories (APHL): (1) that removing standards shifts interoperability burden and cost onto public health, (2)
that withdrawing Health IT certification for public health systems undermines infrastructure modernization, (3)
that eliminating Laboratory Order Interface and Laboratory Results Interface standards slows electronic test
ordering and results reporting, (4) that prohibiting standardized contracting language creates unnecessary
barriers for public health reporting, and (5) that public health interoperability and innovation operate under
constraints distinct from commercial healthcare information technology.

For these reasons, maintaining strong, enforceable standards and certification criteria is essential to protecting
public health surveillance infrastructure and maximizing the return on substantial federal and state investments
made through the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention Public Health Infrastructure Grant (PHIG) and
the Data Modernization Initiative. While policies that reduce standardization may offer short-term flexibility,
they carry significant long-term risks of fragmentation that undermine interoperability, equity, and public health
readiness.

Accordingly, the Oregon Health Authority urges the Assistant Secretary for Technology Policy and the Office
of the National Coordinator for Health Information Technology to reinstate withdrawn public health standards
and certification requirements and to preserve strong, enforceable interoperability expectations that ensure
timely, uniform, and reliable data exchange for public health reporting.
