Dear Representatives of ASTP/ONC:

I am a trial attorney from Ohio in my 27th year of practice. I have spent my career
representing patients whose medical records were fabricated, altered, withheld, or
accessed in secret. I write today not as a critic of modernization or a defender of
bureaucracy. I write because I have seen, in courtroom after courtroom, what happens
when the electronic health record goes unguarded—and I cannot remain silent while
this agency proposes to remove the safeguards before building anything to replace them.
Before I address the law, I want you to understand what I have witnessed:
I have held in my hands an operative report signed and digitally authenticated sixteen
minutes before the surgery it describes ever began. I have proved to a court that a
nursing home fabricated a fall risk care plan after the patient it purported to protect had
already died. I have watched a major hospital system insist for months that it had
produced a complete medical record—until a court-ordered live inspection revealed 845
pages of withheld documents. I have documented, entry by entry, a nursing home where
vital signs were copied from shift to shift by nurses who never touched the patient,
where medications were charted as given hours after they were overdue, where
repositioning was documented before it could conceivably have occurred. And I have
litigated, for the family of a deceased patient, the question of who accessed his medical
record after his death—a question the defendants fought, through two rounds of
appeals, to prevent anyone from ever answering.
Every one of those facts was discoverable for one reason: a federally certified audit trail
existed. The certification requirements that HTI-5 proposes to eliminate are the reason
justice was possible in each of these cases. Remove them, and the fraud is invisible.
I write to ask ASTP/ONC, with the full weight of five cases and two decades of practice,
to pause this rulemaking. Do the foundational work first: finalize a modern,
comprehensive, enforceable audit trail standard. Implement it. Test it. Then streamline.
To remove the guardrails before the new road is built is not reform. It is an open
invitation.

I. Five Cases. One Lesson.
The following cases are not theoretical. They are documented in Ohio court records.
They are the cases that happened to reach my desk, or the desks of my colleagues,
because the families were tenacious enough to fight and the courts were wise enough to
compel. They represent a fraction of what occurs every day in hospitals and nursing
homes whose electronic records are never challenged, never inspected, and never
audited.

Case One: The Operative Report Written Before the Surgery Began
Another client was forty-five years old, healthy, and active. She had one medical issue:
mild stress incontinence after childbirth—a condition millions of women manage with
panty liners. She sought a routine outpatient procedure. Instead, she endured two
separate puncture injuries to her urinary system, neither of which her surgeon
disclosed. The first, to her bladder, was documented but never revealed. The second, to
her urethra, was not even recognized. She left the hospital unaware that urine was
leaking through a hole in her urethra into a fresh surgical incision. For weeks she
contorted her body just to void, resorting to catheterization. She was told nothing.
She will never be dry again. Her physician told her plainly: “Dry is no longer an option.”
She has undergone eight separate procedures—open repairs, mesh excision, amnion
grafting, urethral bulking injections, a pubovaginal fascial sling requiring harvest of her
own abdominal tissue, and reconstructive plastic surgery. Her husband described the
woman sitting beside him: “She’s just not the same person anymore. Everything is
planned around whether she’s going to have an accident.”
And then there is the documentation.
Before her second surgery, her surgeon created and digitally signed a complete operative
report at 12:22 p.m.—sixteen minutes before surgery began at 12:38 p.m. The report
documented findings, diagnoses, what was operated on, how much was removed, blood
loss, and “no complications.” It was authenticated and entered into the permanent
record before the patient was ever anesthetized. Before the first incision. Before the
surgeon had seen a single thing she claimed to have documented.
This fraud was uncovered through a court-ordered forensic inspection of the hospital’s
Soarian EMR by a nationally recognized expert in clinical forensic documentation
analysis who has reviewed more than 1,000 cases and conducted more than 65 live EMR
inspections. The timestamps in the audit trail are, in her expert words, “system-
generated, tamper-proof, and federally certified to prevent alteration.” They are
incontrovertible. They exist because ONC certification requires them to exist. Remove
the certification requirement that mandates the audit log. Remove the timestamp. And
what was done to this woman in that operating room disappears entirely.

Case Two: The Nursing Home That Invented a Care Plan After the Patient Died
In a wrongful death case, I litigated in Cuyahoga County, my client’s mother died
following a fall at a nursing facility. The defense produced what appeared to be a
contemporaneous Fall Risk Care Plan—a document central to the defense theory that
the facility had properly assessed this resident’s safety. It appeared in the records as
though it had always been there.
It had not. The audit trail—which the defendants fought to withhold for months,
claiming first that it did not exist, then that it was managed by a Canadian vendor
beyond subpoena, then that it was not kept in the ordinary course of business—
ultimately proved that the Fall Risk Care Plan was created after the patient’s discharge
and death. The facility manufactured a document, stripped the “Created On” date from
the printout, and produced it in litigation as evidence of contemporaneous care.
The trial court held defendants in contempt. Sanctions escalated to $5,000 per day.
After losing their appeal to the Ohio Supreme Court, the defendants produced 1,145
pages of audit data. The care plan did not just contain inadequate interventions. It did
not exist at all. (Bolaney v. Mapleview Operating Company, LLC, Cuyahoga County
Case No. CV-20-934555.)

Case Three: The Hospital That Withheld 845 Pages — Until a Judge Ordered a
Live Inspection
I represent a patient against a major Ohio hospital system. The defendants represented,
repeatedly and under the authority of a January 2025 Stipulated Order, that they had
produced the complete electronic medical record and all audit trails.
They had not.
After months of motions, hearings, and emergency filings, the Court ordered a live
virtual inspection of the patient’s EMR. Ms. Gonsman conducted that inspection over
three days in August 2025. In 19.75 hours of forensic examination, she located 845
pages of medical records the defendants had never produced—despite their assurances.
Those records included revision histories, a canceled surgical imaging order, radiology
studies, chemotherapy administration records, and the identity of a previously
undisclosed individual who had placed the imaging order. The treating physician’s
supplemental report stated that these records “fundamentally altered the medical and
causation analysis of the case.”
The Judge’s October 7, 2025 Sanctions Order was unsparing: “The fact that Plaintiffs
were forced to proceed with a multiple day, virtual, live EMR inspection to obtain
additional documents that should have been produced in discovery demonstrates that
the Summa Defendants failed to comply with this Court’s January 29, 2025 Order and
with the general principles of discovery.” The Court ordered the hospital to pay all costs
of the inspection, including attorneys’ fees and expert fees. (Hamilton v. Summa
Health, et al., Summit County Case No. CV-2023-09-3525.)
Eight hundred and forty-five pages. Three days of inspection. A fundamentally altered
case. All hidden—until the tools that certification mandates made them findable.

Case Four: The Hospital System That Fought to Hide Who Accessed a Dead
Man’s Medical Record
A patient died of amiodarone toxicity. His family retained counsel and sought the
electronic medical record. The defendants—University Hospitals Health System and
affiliated entities—produced an audit trail. But it was redacted. Among the items
withheld was precisely the information a grieving family most needs to know: who had
accessed the patient’s medical record after his death, and what they had done there.
The defendants fought through two separate rounds of appeals to keep that information
hidden—first appealing the trial court’s June 22, 2023, order compelling production,
then amending that appeal to specifically challenge the obligation to produce
“unredacted audit trails from 2015 to date.” The Ohio Eighth District Court of Appeals
dismissed both appeals sua sponte as lacking a final appealable order. In doing so, the
Court of Appeals acknowledged the issue directly, noting that the defendants had argued
that “the audit trail showing post-death access of the appellant’s electronic medical
record would disclose attorney work product”—a claim the appellate court could not
evaluate because the redactions themselves prevented any meaningful review.
Two months after the trial court ordered production, Plaintiff had received not a single
compelled document. The defendants had filed an appeal, then a motion to stay, then an
amended appeal. Each maneuver cost the patient’s family time. The audit trail
compelled was specific and exacting: native Microsoft Excel or CSV format, from the
defendants’ SQL servers, in color, including all metadata for every access event—the
time of initiation, time of termination, identity of person, and description of actions—for
every date from the time of care through the present. Also required was the hospital’s
master IT integration map: a diagram showing every software system used during Mr.
Eden’s care. (Estate of Eden v. Goldstein, et al., Cuyahoga County Case No. CV-22-
962194.)
The integration map demand reveals a truth this rulemaking must confront directly: a
single patient’s care may be distributed across Cerner, eClinicalWorks, Soarian, and
other platforms simultaneously, each running its own audit log, each a separate and
contested production battle. When ASTP/ONC removes certification criteria, it removes
the requirement that any of these systems generate a complete, reliable audit trail in the
first place. My client’s family needed to know not just what was in the record, but who
went there after he was gone. That is a question that only the audit trail can answer. And
it is the question these defendants spent months and two appellate filings trying to
prevent anyone from asking.

Case Five: The Nursing Home Where the Audit Trail Exposed Systematic
Falsification
In a fifth case currently in litigation, forensic analysis of a nursing home resident’s
records—conducted across PointClickCare and EPIC platforms—revealed a pattern of
documentation fraud that would have been entirely invisible without the audit trail.
Staff documented on the Treatment Administration Record that Daily Skilled Charting
was complete on specific dates. The audit trail confirmed that no such charting existed.
The entries were fabricated.
Multiple assessments bore Effective Dates that preceded their actual Created Dates—
backdated documents designed to appear contemporaneous with care events they were
meant to record.
Staff were required to monitor and record vital signs every shift. The audit trail revealed
that across more than a dozen dates, identical vital sign readings appeared across day,
evening, and night shifts—entered by different nurses—with the audit trail confirming
that no new measurements were taken. The numbers were copied. Not measured.
Copied.
Repositioning—the primary clinical intervention to prevent pressure ulcers—was
documented hours before the scheduled care event it purported to describe. Whether it
ever actually occurred, no record can prove.
The resident’s warfarin management was marked by dangerously elevated PT/INR
values—readings of 49.3/5, 66/5.7, and 60.5/5.3. The Medication Administration
Record showed the same stale INR value copied forward across days of subsequent
entries, giving the false appearance that current monitoring had occurred when it had
not. A call log directed a dosage change, but no new order was entered. The prior dose
continued.
A progress note documenting the resident’s vital signs of 70/50, heart rate of 137, and
oxygen saturation of 76% at the time of her emergent transfer—a note describing a
patient in crisis—was entered after her discharge. It was hidden from initial production
through the simple mechanism of a date-range limitation on the records production
request. It was recovered only during a live inspection.
Then the hospital’s EPIC audit trail was produced in redacted PDF format—not the
original, sortable, filterable Excel format in which it exists. The forensic expert
concluded that “redactions to federally mandated audit data are unacceptable for a
forensic analysis” and that the PDF format “hides additional data that would be visible if
the Excel version was produced.” A redacted PDF is not an audit trail. It is obstruction
wearing the face of compliance.
Every one of these findings was visible only because a certified audit trail existed and
was ultimately produced. Remove the certification. Remove the trail. And not one of
these facts can be established.
II. The True Cost of Deregulation
The HTI-5 Proposed Rule projects $1.53 billion in savings for EHR developers. That
figure is real. What is not measured—what no regulatory impact analysis ever
measures—is the cost borne by patients when accountability is the price of efficiency.
The federal government estimates that healthcare fraud costs hundreds of billions of
dollars annually. A substantial portion of that fraud is enabled, concealed, and
perpetuated by electronic medical records that are manipulated after the fact: vital signs
copied rather than measured, operative reports completed before surgery begins, care
plans fabricated after death, medications charted as administered when they were not,
post-discharge entries documenting crises hidden behind date-range limitations, and
audit trails produced in redacted PDFs designed to defeat forensic analysis.
ASTM E2147-18, codified at 45 CFR §170.299, states in Section 3.1.19.1 that “audit data
is integral to self-authentication and trustworthiness of patient information including
the medical record and billing record.” Section 1.2 requires that “full transparency of
modifications or deletions or both is mandatory.” Section 3.1.2.1 requires that “all
accesses shall be recorded, and the entire access record shall be provided when an
access record is requested.” And Section 4.3 states that access to audit logs by attorneys
or risk management is “not privileged” and must be “fully transparent and disclosed”—a
principle I have now litigated in multiple cases against defendants who treat it as an
open question.
That standard was written in 2018. It does not address artificial intelligence generating
clinical notes indistinguishable from physician-authored documentation. It does not
address connected medical devices creating automated entries. It does not address copy-
forward functionality that propagates a single vital sign reading across weeks of a
patient’s record. It does not address ephemeral messaging, or synthetic data, or pre-
documentation—entries made before the care they describe has occurred. These are not
edge cases. They are standard features of modern EMR practice, and they are
unaddressed by the standard this rulemaking would remove before replacing.
The answer to an outdated standard is not the absence of a standard. It is a better one.
And the sequence matters absolutely. You cannot responsibly remove the floor before
you have built the one to replace it.

III. What Must Come First
I ask ASTP/ONC to pause the HTI-5 deregulatory changes and take the following steps
before any certification criteria are removed:

1. Finalize a Modern, Comprehensive Audit Trail Standard
   A successor to ASTM E2147-18 must be codified and enforceable before a single
   criterion is removed. That standard must require capture and flagging of AI-generated
   entries as machine-authored; identify copy-forward and copy-paste events with
   timestamps showing source and destination; record pre-documentation — entries
   created before the care event they describe; capture all post-discharge access, including
   access by any individual after a patient’s discharge or death; and require that audit data
   be produced in original, unredacted, machine-readable format. A redacted PDF is not a
   compliant audit trail. It must be expressly prohibited as a production format.

2. Require Multi-Platform Coverage and Software Identification
   My case experience demonstrates a problem that must be confronted directly: a single
   patient’s care is often distributed across multiple, disconnected EMR platforms, each
   maintaining its own audit trail. Certification must require that every software product
   used to create, modify, view, or transmit health information generate a complete audit
   log; that patients and their representatives be entitled to audit data from all platforms;
   and that any facility operating multiple systems provide a complete integration map
   upon request. TEFCA compliance must mean that audit trails follow the data, wherever
   the data lives.

3. Prohibit Ephemeral Data Destruction
   No health information—in the primary record, the audit log, any connected device, or
   any messaging platform used in connection with patient care—may be automatically or
   programmatically destroyed. Data must be retained in readable, reproducible format for
   as long as any care relationship, investigation, litigation hold, or applicable regulation
   requires. The post-discharge progress note in Case Five, documenting a patient in crisis
   and hidden by a date range, is a precise illustration of what programmatic destruction
   and selective production costs. It cost that family months and nearly cost them
   everything.

4. Codify the Non-Privileged Status of Audit Trails and Enforce Patient Access
   Section 4.3 of ASTM E2147-18 states that access to audit logs by attorneys or risk
   management “are not privileged actions and must also be fully transparent and
   disclosed.” I have litigated that exact privilege claim—twice successfully and at great cost
   to my clients and the courts’ time. HHS must codify this principle expressly in
   regulation. It must also establish enforceable patient access requirements for audit trail
   data, with meaningful penalties for non-compliance. A right that requires years of
   motion practice to exercise is not a right. It is a promise.

5. Require Pre-Documentation Detection
   The case I handled where an operative report was signed before the patient was
   anesthetized is possible because nothing in the current system prevents a physician
   from completing and authenticating a report before the procedure it describes. Any
   modernized certification standard must require that EMR systems identify and flag pre-
   documentation: entries created and authenticated before the scheduled care event they
   purport to describe. This feature alone would have made the fraud in Case One
   immediately visible to any reviewer with access to the record. It must be required.
   IV. A Request, and a Warning
   I have practiced law long enough to know that comment periods are sometimes pro
   forma—the decision largely made, the rule largely written, the public participation a
   courtesy extended before the inevitable. I hope this is not one of those times.
   I am asking ASTP/ONC to look at what I have placed before you. An operative report
   signed sixteen minutes before surgery. A care plan fabricated after a patient’s death.
   Eight hundred and forty-five pages hidden until a judge ordered a three-day live
   inspection. Two rounds of appellate filings to prevent disclosure of who accessed a dead
   man’s record. Vital signs copied shift to shift. A patient in crisis, her final emergency
   note hidden behind a date range, recovered only by inspection. These are not
   hypothetical concerns. They are documented cases, decided by Ohio courts, involving
   real patients, real families, and real records that were falsified, withheld, or accessed in
   secret.
   For every case that reaches a courtroom, there are a hundred that do not. For every
   audit trail obtained through contempt proceedings and court-ordered inspection, there
   are a hundred that are never requested, never produced, and never examined. The
   protections at issue in this rulemaking are not protections for lawyers. They are
   protections for every patient who will never know they were needed.
   The electronic health record is the most consequential document in modern medicine. It
   drives clinical decisions, supports billing claims, defends litigation, and tells the story of
   what was done to a person in their most vulnerable hours. It must be trustworthy. And it
   can only be trustworthy if the systems that generate it are certified to produce a
   complete, accurate, unredacted, machine-readable record of every entry made, every
   record accessed, every change made, and every user who was there—including after the
   patient has been discharged, or has died.
   Do the hard work first. Finish the modern standard. Implement it. Enforce it. Then
   streamline what remains. Do that, and I will be among the first to support the reforms
   that follow.
   Do not do that—and the cost will be borne, as it always is, by the patients. By the
   families who will sit across from me someday holding records that have been shaped
   and trimmed into something that no longer tells the truth. By the courts that will spend
   years litigating what should never have been hidden. By the nurses and physicians
   whose honest work will be buried beneath the misconduct of the few who understood,
   too well, that an unmonitored system is an invitation.
   I respectfully ask that you not extend that invitation.
