February 24, 2026

RE: Public Comment on Proposed Rule: RIN 0955-AA09

Dear ASTP representative:

          I write to you on behalf of the Pennsylvania Association for Justice, an organization

made up of trial attorneys that is dedicated to preserving patient safety and the public’s
meaningful access to their own protected health information. Ensuring the integrity and security
of electronic health information (EHI) is vital to our members and clients. All health-care
consumers are entitled to records secure from prying eyes while also making all of those records
available to the consumers. While we support the goal of interoperability, we are deeply
concerned that the Health Data, Technology and Interoperability (HTI-5) proposed rule risks
unintentionally impairing patient’s ability to access all of their data by modifying certification
requirements before modernizing data standards.
We routinely use audit trail data to uncover medical data that was added to, modified or
deleted from the original data. At times, these changes to the medical record are routine and
consistent with standards of care. Yet there are many times when we find that this altering of
data resulted in preventable catastrophic harm or, even worse, were attempts to conceal conduct
that must be revealed so that it can be prevented in the future.
The integrity of the medical record is only as reliable as its audit trail. An updated audit
trail standard is required for the security of EHI to verify its integrity and curtail billing fraud.
The current standard, ASTM E2147-18, is outdated. Since 2018 the use and content of medical
records have evolved dramatically. If we move forward with the decertification or removal of
functional guardrails without first defining how data is created, collected, and preserved in a
usable, searchable, and shareable format, we invite patient harm and compromise the
trustworthiness of our healthcare system.
Our advocates routinely find that these current fundamental ambiguities create a system
that obscures and conceals preventable medical error and fraud. Each one of us relies on the
healthcare industry at some point and has a stake in protecting the integrity of the EHI that our
healthcare providers utilize to diagnose, treat and monitor our loved ones when they are sick.
To ensure medical records remain a “source of truth,” Health and Human Services must
prioritize a revision of the audit trail standard that includes:
• AI, Medical Device and Messaging Integration:
o Explicitly capturing the activities of AI algorithms, connected medical
devices, and professional messaging to ensure clinical data is transparent
and traceable.
• Software Identification and Interoperability:
 o Audit trails must identify the specific software used in patient care.
Furthermore, software must be interoperable for safe functionality within
facilities and for sharing information in compliance with TEFCA.
• Granular Metadata and Fraud Prevention:
o Standards must mandate the collection of precise data elements, including
user roles and detailed activity logs (e.g., “copy-paste,” “copy forwards,”
and “view-only” access.) Audit trails that are transparent and reveal
corrections, deletions, or overwrites are essential to identify weaknesses in
safety protocols and prevent the fraudulent practices that cost billions of
dollars annually.
• Patient Access:
o We must address the reality that many patients still do not have access to
their EHI despite such transparency being required by the law.

        Accurate data retention is of paramount importance. Data retention must be clearly

defined so that information is available as long as patient care, investigations or litigation holds
are ongoing and to remain in compliance with state and federal regulations.
We strongly urge HHS to prohibit “ephemeral messaging” or any automatic destruction
of data. Data must be retained in a readable, reproducible format.
We agree that we must establish clear, shared definitions for terms like ePHI, EHI,
Generative AI and Synthetic Data. However, these standards for data preservation and the ability
to reproduce data must be updated, codified and enforced before deregulation of certification is
considered. Taking away this system without first putting critical safeguards in place ensures
continued unsafe practices and insidious billing fraud.
We are opposed to the deletions of sections 170.315(d)(10), 170.315(d)(2),
170.315(d)(3), 170.315(b)(11) and 170.315(g)(3). We also remain deeply concerned with the
safety implication of failing to define provider chat platforms as Electronic Health Information in
section 170.102.
Audit trails are essential to patient safety and accountability. Without well-defined
standards it will be impossible to ensure the integrity of electronic health information leaving
consumers open to clinical dangers while leaving taxpayers vulnerable to fraud.

Eric Wietz Karesa Rovnan
