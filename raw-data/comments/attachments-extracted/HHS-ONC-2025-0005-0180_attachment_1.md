Accessibility-Centered Design

AAP is particularly concerned about the proposed removal of accessibility-related certification requirements.
Accessibility is not a static feature; it requires continuous monitoring, updates, and testing as platforms evolve.
Eliminating certification expectations in this area risks reducing access to patient portals and electronic health
record (EHR) functionality for patients, parents, caregivers, and healthcare staff with disabilities.

In pediatrics, accessible portals are critical to ensuring that parents and caregivers can engage in their child’s
care, review health information, and communicate with clinicians. AAP strongly recommends that ASTP/ONC
retain accessibility requirements within the certification program or identify alternative mechanisms to ensure
that certified health IT continues to meet accessibility standards over time.

Care Coordination

AAP is concerned with the proposed removal of Transitions of Care, Care Plan, and Clinical Information
Reconciliation and Incorporation (CIRI) certification criteria. These functions remain foundational to safe and
effective care coordination.

While the proposed rule emphasizes a shift toward FHIR-based exchange, in practice, many healthcare
organizations still rely on Consolidated Clinical Document Architecture (C-CDA) documents to send and
receive patient information, and EHRs must be able to generate and transmit these documents for receiving
systems to incorporate data at all.

ASTP/ONC acknowledges that the health care system is not ready to fully replace C-CDA for transitions of care.
Until FHIR-based exchange is reliably available across receiving settings, C-CDA functions as a necessary
“backup” for continuity of care, and removing the ability to create and send these documents risks widening
interoperability gaps. This challenge is especially pronounced for organizations that lack robust information
exchange infrastructure or access to national networks. AAP recommends that ASTP/ONC consider pairing any
reduction in certification requirements with concrete support for participation in Qualified Health Information
Networks (QHINs) to improve real-world information exchange.

Immunization Data Exchange and Public Health Reporting

AAP strongly opposes the proposed removal of immunization-related measures from the Insights Condition,
which would eliminate transparency into how frequently certified health IT supports immunization data
exchange and access. While the underlying functionality is expected to remain available, eliminating reporting
on electronic submission of immunization administrations to immunization information systems (IIS), as well
as clinician and patient access to immunization histories and forecast recommendations, would significantly
reduce visibility into real-world use of these capabilities.

Given the central role of IIS in pediatric care, population-level surveillance, and outbreak response, AAP urges
ASTP/ONC to identify alternative mechanisms to monitor utilization of immunization data exchange and
access over time. Maintaining insight into how these functions are used is critical to ensuring they continue to
be supported, maintained, and equitably available across health systems, particularly for smaller practices and
safety net providers, even if specific Insights Condition reporting metrics are retired.

Clinical Decision Support and AI Transparency
AAP has significant concerns regarding the proposed removal of AI transparency requirements, including
model cards, within the certified health IT program. Eliminating these requirements would shift a substantial
evaluation burden onto clinicians, health systems, and patients, who are not well positioned to independently
assess the safety, validity, and appropriate use of AI-enabled tools.

In practice, eliminating standardized model card requirements will force informatics and IT professionals to
expend significant effort to locate or reconstruct basic information needed for governance and safe
implementation of AI-enabled decision support, increasing costs and variability while reducing transparency.
This change would shift responsibility for AI safety from vendors to providers and health systems, without
continued regulatory oversight of vendor AI development or non-provider use of individual patient data.
Additionally, these changes risk eroding certified clinical decision support safeguards that ensure clinicians
remain involved in determining which functions may operate autonomously and which require appropriate
provider oversight.

AAP urges ASTP/ONC to reconsider these removals and to maintain a baseline level of transparency and
governance within the certification program to protect patients and support responsible innovation.

Conclusion

AAP supports thoughtful modernization of the ONC Health IT Certification Program but cautions against
deregulation that may undermine accessibility, interoperability, transparency, and patient safety. Certification
has historically played a critical role in setting minimum expectations across the health IT ecosystem, and its
removal should not result in diminished protections for children, families, and clinicians.
