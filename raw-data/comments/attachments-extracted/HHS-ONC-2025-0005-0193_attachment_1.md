Premier appreciates the opportunity to submit comments to the Assistant Secretary for Technology Policy (ASTP)
and Office of the National Coordinator for Health Information Technology (ONC) on the proposed deregulatory
rulemaking addressing health data, technology and interoperability (HTI-5). Premier values the Administration’s
continued commitment to technology-enabled healthcare that reduces administrative burden while improving access
to high-quality care. In our detailed comments below, Premier urges ASTP/ONC to:

     •   Finalize proposed changes to information blocking exceptions to prevent misuse and promote fair,
         interoperable data exchange;
     •   Ensure the certification program deregulation preserves provider trust, transparency, safety and
         accountability;
     •   Balance innovation-enabling flexibility with baseline privacy, security and AI transparency safeguards;
     •   Advance Fast Healthcare Interoperability Resources (FHIR)-based interoperability with consistent
         implementation, governance and write-capable use cases;
     •   Coordinate across HHS to drive greater standardization of supply chain and other administrative data,
         promoting true interoperability across the clinical enterprise; and
     •   Extend burden reduction efforts across the broader health technology ecosystem to avoid shifting complexity
         and risk downstream to providers and patients.

Our recommendations are discussed in greater detail below.

II. MODERNIZING INFORMATION BLOCKING REQUIREMENTS TO PREVENT GAMING

Premier applauds ASTP/ONC’s efforts to address misaligned incentives in the current information blocking
exceptions framework. Premier strongly supports meaningful interoperability across the healthcare sector,
which must include appropriate incentives across data-sharing parties to facilitate the flow of data and
enable a higher standard of analytics-driven patient care.

In this rulemaking, ASTP/ONC proposes to tighten information blocking exceptions that have been overused or
misused, while expanding core definitions to support more automated data exchange. Specifically, the agency
proposes to eliminate the “third party seeking modification use condition” and revise or remove the “manner exception
exhausted condition” from the Infeasibility Exception, amend the Manner Exception to exclude certain contractual
and pricing arrangements and remove the Trusted Exchange Framework and Common Agreement (TEFCA) Manner
Exception.

Proposed Revisions to Infeasibility Exception

ASTP/ONC proposes to strike the “third party seeking modification use condition” due to concerns that it has been
misused to withhold electronic health information (EHI) and inhibit access and exchange by third parties engaged by
patients and providers. ASTP/ONC notes particular concern that electronic health record (EHR) developers have
relied on this condition to limit access by trusted third parties, including provider business associates.

Premier strongly supports the proposed revision and agrees that this exception has been routinely invoked
to deny legitimate access, exchange and use of EHI needed to support care coordination, quality
improvement, and value-based payment models. In practice, this condition has enabled categorical refusals of
requests involving write-back, normalization or enrichment of data by trusted third parties, even where such
functionality is technically feasible and essential to healthcare provider operations. Eliminating this condition would
better align the information blocking framework with real-world workflows and shift the focus from protecting
incumbent system designs toward enabling standards-based interoperability consistent with the goals of the 21st
Century Cures Act.

ASTP/ONC similarly raises concerns regarding misuse of the “manner exception exhausted” condition, which allows
denial of requests after offering alternative exchange methods. The agency notes that this condition may force
requestors into inferior or impractical exchange methods or be used to deny exchange altogether.

Premier supports the removal or substantial limitation of the “manner exhausted” condition. Providers and
their trusted partners have been forced into manual, delayed, or non-standardized access pathways even when
automated, application programming interface (API)-based exchange is feasible and necessary. This approach
prioritizes legacy system constraints over patient and provider needs and undermines the intent of the information
blocking framework. Narrowing or eliminating this condition would reduce strategic misuse and encourage scalable,
interoperable exchange.

Proposed Changes to Manner Exception

ASTP/ONC proposes to amend the Manner Exception so that it does not apply where access is conditioned on
non-market rates, contracts of adhesion or unconscionable terms. The agency appropriately recognizes that
contractual leverage has been used to nominally offer access while making exchange commercially unreasonable.

Premier urges ASTP/ONC to finalize this proposal. Providers have experienced commercially unreasonable
pricing and one-sided contractual provisions that impede interoperability while preserving technical compliance.
Clarifying that permissible manners of exchange must be both technically feasible and commercially reasonable will
promote fair access, strengthen competition, and prevent contractual language from being used to obstruct data
exchange.

Proposal to Remove TEFCA Manner Exception

Premier supports the removal of the TEFCA Manner Exception, which currently permits actors to deny
requests for access, exchange or use of electronic health information on the basis that TEFCA-based
exchange is available. While Premier strongly supports TEFCA as an important national interoperability framework,
this exception has been applied in ways that unnecessarily restrict lawful, non-TEFCA exchange and limit providers’
ability to access data through methods that best meet their operational, clinical and analytic needs.

Reliance on the TEFCA Manner Exception has created barriers to timely, automated access outside TEFCA
workflows, even where such exchange is technically feasible and consistent with the goals of the Cures Act. Premier
recommends that ASTP/ONC finalize the proposed removal of this exception to ensure that participation in
TEFCA enhances, rather than supplants, broader interoperability and does not become a justification for
denying alternative exchange pathways. Eliminating the TEFCA Manner Exception would reinforce that TEFCA
is only one pathway among many for data exchange and help ensure that information blocking protections continue
to prioritize access, flexibility and patient- and provider-driven use of electronic health information.

Premier also urges ASTP/ONC to examine how TEFCA’s business incentives interact with post-Cures Act
interoperability obligations. TEFCA’s profitability and access control dynamics, Premier recommends that
ASTP/ONC clarify, through regulation if necessary, the conditions under which QHINs or Participants may
refuse requests, and that ASTP/ONC, rather than QHINs, define what constitutes a reasonable request.

III. RIGHT-SIZING CERTIFICATION TO PRIORITIZE OUTCOMES

In an effort to reduce burden and costs for health IT developers, ASTP/ONC proposes to remove 34 and revise 7 of
the current 60 certification criteria under the certification program. Premier supports efforts to modernize certification
but urges careful calibration to preserve transparency and provider trust.

Real World Testing

Under current regulations, health IT developers must, as a Condition of Certification, successfully test the real-world
use of the technology for interoperability in the type of setting in which such technology would be marketed. In this
rulemaking, ASTP/ONC proposes to 1) eliminate the requirement to submit real-world testing (RWT) plans; 2) limit
full RWT results reporting and 3) allow the use of the standards version advancement process (SVAP) for the
remaining non-API RWT certification criteria with minimal reporting requirements.

Premier believes that health IT end-user input should be included in every phase of health IT solution development,
from initial design to full testing in clinical workflows. Healthcare organizations are uniquely situated to determine
whether a health IT solution can be implemented without significant effort beyond the value incurred by adoption. As
such, while Premier supports ASTP/ONC’s efforts to reduce unnecessary burden within the Certification Program,
Premier cautions that eliminating the RWT criteria should not diminish transparency into how certified health IT
performs in real-world clinical and operational settings. Providers rely on credible signals that certified products will
function reliably at scale, support API-based exchange and integrate effectively with third-party applications used for
quality improvement, population health management and value-based care. Premier recommends that ASTP/ONC
pair removal of the formal RWT certification criteria with alternative mechanisms through guidance,
information blocking enforcement or future API-focused requirements that continue to promote
accountability for real-world interoperability outcomes.

Privacy and Security

ASTP/ONC proposes to remove all 14 privacy and security criteria, including authentication, audit logs, encryption
and multi-factor authentication. ASTP/ONC explains that certification is likely no longer a primary factor driving
improvements or compliance in these areas, and that by removing these requirements, it will provide health IT
developers with the flexibility to provide more innovative solutions, spur competition and give healthcare providers

the opportunity to choose the best privacy and security technologies that fit their needs at the best price versus being
forced to accept those capabilities included in certified health IT.

While Premier appreciates ASTP/ONC’s intent to provide health IT developers with greater flexibility to innovate,
we are concerned that the removal of all privacy and security certification requirements may have unintended
negative consequences for healthcare providers that outweigh the potential benefits of the proposal. Many
providers and provider organizations rely on certified health IT to achieve HIPAA and other privacy and security law
compliance obligations and may struggle to meet these on their own or to find cost-effective alternatives. This is
especially the case for smaller or rural healthcare providers that have more limited resources. Premier urges
ASTP/ONC to preserve these criteria and instead refocus on bolstering privacy and security requirements
within the Certification Program. It is no secret that healthcare is under a constant threat of cyber intrusion.
Patient data remains one of the most valuable commodities on the dark web. As a result, providers need all the
tools available to them to protect that data. That includes a robust cybersecurity posture maintained by the
technology storing the data itself. While a CEHRT product is only one part of a provider’s overall technology stack,
ensuring the most robust portion of that stack has basic privacy and security protections will relieve significant
burden from providers that many are not adequately resourced to support.

For similar reasons, Premier supports ASTP/ONC’s proposal to retain certain audit-related requirements as
a safeguard to help identify fraud and abuse. This approach appropriately recognizes that, even in a more flexible
and competitive market, baseline technical and documentation requirements play an important role in enabling
oversight, accountability and trust in the health IT ecosystem.

Artificial Intelligence (AI) Transparency

While the HTI-5 proposed rule preserves the fundamental requirement for Certified Health IT to support evidence-
based and predictive decision support interventions (DSIs), it proposes to significantly streamline the associated
transparency and administrative obligations to align with broader deregulatory goals. Specifically, ASTP/ONC
proposes to reduce the scope of the DSI certification criterion to fully remove the AI “model card” requirements,
including source attribute information transparency and intervention risk management for predictive DSIs. ASTP/ONC
states that healthcare delivery organizations and healthcare professionals have not used this information, and that
the developers have reported that the requirements are burdensome and detrimental to innovation, which is contrary
to ASTP/ONC’s stated policy intent to optimize the ubiquitous use of high-quality AI in healthcare.

Premier appreciates ASTP/ONC’s acknowledgement that AI governance is evolving and that overly prescriptive
federal requirements may inadvertently constrain innovation. However, Premier encourages ASTP/ONC to
incorporate practical, real-world measures that meaningfully enhance the transparency of AI tools. Standardized
transparency tools such as AI model cards have provided practical insights to understand intended use, limitations
and risks, and to support internal governance and procurement decisions. Without continued attention to
strengthening these mechanisms, users of certified health IT may be left without the technical and functional detail
necessary to meet increasing due diligence and disclosure obligations related to the use of AI in the healthcare sector.
Premier urges ASTP/ONC to retain a voluntary or recommended transparency framework such as AI model
cards to support responsible AI adoption and comparability across products.

IV. ENABLING REAL-WORLD CLINICAL USE THROUGH FHIR-BASED INTEROPERABILITY

Premier supports ASTP/ONC’s emphasis on FHIR-based interoperability. FHIR represents a scalable and standards-
based foundation for nationwide interoperability. We support prioritizing FHIR-based APIs that move beyond “read-
only” data access to enabling “write” capabilities to support automated, bidirectional data exchange, clinical efficiency
and patient-centered care. FHIR APIs that primarily support data retrieval, without allowing healthcare providers to
enable controlled and appropriate data contribution, often fail to reduce clinician burden or replace legacy exchange
mechanisms. Providers must still manually re-enter externally sourced data into EHRs, increasing documentation
burden and the risk of error.

While Premier supports ASTP’s deregulatory efforts, there is also a need to engender trust in information exchange
in a deregulated environment. A common approach or set of rules is needed to advance the FHIR environment and
bolster partnerships. Without a shared framework, such as minimum expectations for FHIR implementation
consistency, write-access governance, auditability, data provenance and model or decision-support transparency,
healthcare providers face increased legal, operational and patient safety risk. This uncertainty can discourage
participation, slow adoption and undermine the very interoperability goals HTI-5 seeks to advance.

Premier encourages ASTP/ONC to continue evolving FHIR policy toward:
• Use-case specific, safeguarded data contribution;
• Preservation of clinician oversight and accountability;
• Broad and consistent data availability across certified products, particularly for use cases such as quality
improvement, supply chain integration and real-time electronic prior authorization;
• Practical workflow integration; and
• A common set of rules for consistent technical, governance and accountability norms.

V. UNLOCKING INTEROPERABILITY FOR SUPPLY CHAIN DATA

The healthcare technology ecosystem has made meaningful progress in advancing interoperability for clinical and
patient-generated data, driven by standards adoption and regulatory focus. However, interoperability for supply chain,
financial and other administrative data remains fragmented, limiting operational efficiency and end-to-end visibility
across the system. In healthcare supply chains today, non-standard identifiers, uneven barcode usage and siloed
system designs degrade data quality, inhibit linkage across organizations and create difficulties in time-sensitive
processes such as FDA product recalls.

Global, open standards provide the most practical pathway to high-quality, more usable supply chain data. Within
healthcare and adjacent sectors, GS1 standards, including GTIN (products), GLN (locations), GS1-128/2D
barcodes (lot/serial/expiry) and EPCIS (event-level data exchange), create a common language for identification,
capture, and sharing that AI models and other tools can reliably learn from and act on. These standards are proven,
technology-neutral and already widely globally deployed across supply chains.

Premier’s recommendations

Incentivizing the movement toward fully interoperable supply chain data is essential for both clinical and
administrative functions in the healthcare sector. Federal leadership can uniquely address persistent barriers to
supply chain data interoperability. Accordingly, Premier recommends that ASTP/ONC consider the following:

Embedding supply chain identifiers in ONC certification criteria and APIs. ASTP/ONC should ensure that
certified health IT (including EHR-adjacent inventory/OR modules) can capture, store and exchange UDI/GTIN and
GLN, and can ingest GS1 barcodes/2D symbols at receipt and point of care. Premier recommends requiring these
identifiers to be accessible via developer APIs so innovative new AI tools can consistently ingest them across
organizations.

Advancing UDI/GTIN alignment for AI-assisted safety and quality. Premier urges ASTP/ONC to coordinate
across HHS – particularly with the FDA – to reinforce GTIN as the standard product key in provider systems and item
masters so new technologies such as AI tools can link product identity to lot/serial/expiry, claims and clinical context.
This is critically important for improving safety analytics and automation of recalls.

Normalizing GLN for facility sub-location identity. Premier encourages ASTP/ONC to work across HHS to
promote GLN for standardized location master data used in supply chain, administrative and clinical workflows.
Normalized geographies and care settings improve AI feature engineering (e.g., demand signals by department) and
reduce mis-routing that would otherwise confound model training.

Requiring machine-readable recall/shortage communications. Premier urges ASTP/ONC to coordinate across
the various HHS operating divisions to encourage or require inclusion of GTIN, GLN and lot/serial in structured,

VI. ENSURING ADMINISTRATIVE BURDEN RELIEF ACROSS THE HEALTH TECH ECOSYSTEM

Premier recognizes and appreciates the significant financial benefits from burden reduction for health IT developers
estimated in the regulatory impact analysis accompanying the HTI-5 proposed rule. This very real regulatory relief
reflects ASTP/ONC’s meaningful effort to streamline requirements and free resources for innovation.

At the same time, Premier encourages the agency to consider how comparable burden-reducing approaches
could be extended, where appropriate, to other participants in the health technology ecosystem, including
providers, data intermediaries and trusted third-party partners. Ensuring that burden reduction is more evenly
distributed across data-sharing stakeholders would help reduce friction, promote trust among exchange partners and
avoid simply shifting complexity and risk downstream. Premier believes that continued progress toward
balanced regulatory relief will better enable provider organizations to redirect limited resources toward innovation,
care improvement, and scalable use of interoperable data to continue to keep the patient at the center of care.
