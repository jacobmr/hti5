Assistant Secretary Keane:

On behalf of Vanderbilt Health, thank you for the opportunity to comment on the Assistant Secretary for
Technology Policy’s (ASTP) Health Data, Technology, and Interoperability: ASTP/ONC Deregulatory
Actions to Unleash Prosperity (HTI-5) proposed rule. Vanderbilt Health appreciates ASTP’s efforts to
modernize our nation’s health IT infrastructure, improve experiences for patients, and decrease burdens on
healthcare providers. We support ASTP’s proposal to enhance flexibility in the design of electronic health
records (EHRs) and promote greater use of Fast Healthcare Interoperability Resources (FHIR)-based
Application Programing Interfaces (APIs). However, we are concerned about the proposal to completely
remove the information blocking exception “Third-party Seeking Modification Use” at this time, due to
far-reaching potential to harm patients and the integrity of EHR systems in the absence of a strong trust
framework and governance processes. Additionally, our comments herein ask ASTP for greater guidance
and protections for providers regarding patients’ preferences for sharing electronic health information
(EHI), especially in relation to patient preferences regarding third party applications.

I. Background on Vanderbilt Health

Vanderbilt Health is the trade name of Vanderbilt University Medical Center (VUMC), which is a
comprehensive research, teaching, and patient care system headquartered in Nashville, Tennessee.
Vanderbilt Health operates three inpatient facilities—Vanderbilt University Hospital, the Monroe
Carell Jr. Children’s Hospital at Vanderbilt, and Vanderbilt Psychiatric Hospital —as constituent parts
of its flagship campus. In addition, Vanderbilt Health operates four regional hospitals, over 180 clinics
throughout Tennessee and neighboring states, six surgery centers in Middle Tennessee, and joint
ventures that include the Vanderbilt Stallworth Rehabilitation Hospital. In the fiscal year ending June
30, 2025, Vanderbilt Health’s approximately 43,000 employees provided 3.3 million clinic visits,
213,000 emergency department visits, 280,000 telehealth appointments, and treated 83,000 inpatients.
Additionally, Vanderbilt Health provides nearly $1 billion in annual charity care and other community
benefits.
As a pioneer of EHR systems and other health IT, Vanderbilt Health has served as a collaborative partner
with the U.S. federal government to advance health data interoperability for nearly 25 years1. Vanderbilt
Health continues leadership in health IT innovation by pioneering safe and effective integration of artificial
intelligence in clinical settings2. Vanderbilt Health’s mission is to lead improvements in the health of
individuals and communities regionally, nationally, and internationally by combining transformative
learning programs and compelling discoveries to provide distinctive personalized care.

II. Vanderbilt Health Supports Proposed Changes to Standards, Implementation
Specifications, and Certification Criteria and Certification Programs for Health Information
Technology

The proposed changes to standards, implementation specifications and certification criteria for EHRs would
improve interoperability and health IT modernization efforts. We support continued efforts to move towards
FHIR APIs to improve data sharing and embrace the use of AI in clinical care.

However, as described below, we believe it is important for ASTP to provide detailed sub-regulatory
guidance that would empower patients to specify the granularity of EHR data shared with their patient
portal and API-based services. A lack of such guidance increases the likelihood of unintentional patient
privacy violations by third parties and lower-quality patient care. Patient privacy and safety are of utmost
importance at Vanderbilt Health.

III. Finalizing the HTI-2-Proposed Requester Preference Information Blocking
Exception and Issuing Sub-regulatory Guidance Would Give Patients Greater Agency and
Control Over EHI

Vanderbilt Health is grateful to ASTP for acknowledging the challenges certain circumstances present to
providers who are striving to responsibly provide data in accordance with patient preferences and safety. If
a patient does not wish specific information be shared, it is important that our providers are confident in
withholding it without fear of an information-blocking violation.

We were disappointed that ASTP did not finalize the Requester Preference information blocking exception
included in the HTI-2 proposed rule and we are concerned about the lack of technical guidance to navigate
current real-world situations without this exception. Most health systems place all EHI in the patient portal,
which in certain circumstances can be harmful to patients, including by causing unnecessary distress.
Currently, patients lack easy control over what information they can access independently and what
information they would rather receive in the context of clinical counseling. With the increased use of APIs,
we fear that a patient could unintentionally release more data than expected and that data could find its way
to unknown entities for uses beyond patient care. We urge ASTP to finalize the HTI-2-proposed
Requester Preference information blocking exception to empower patients with granular control over
when they receive EHI and whether EHI will be automatically shared with third parties beyond their
health care provider. Such an action would be deregulatory in nature by decreasing administrative
requirements for providers and offering greater choices and agency for patients.
In the HTI-3 final rule, ASTP stated it would allow granularity to limit the sharing of EHI based on patients’
preferences under the Privacy Exception. However, the final rule did not specify the method in which
patients can request such limitations. Providing patients with greater autonomy and control over EHI
through the Privacy Exception is particularly important for pediatric patients who develop from dependent
agents to autonomous individuals in the context of families with highly varied dynamics. As an illustrative
example, as adolescents age, their parents may wish to continue supporting care for serious chronic
conditions, but allow privacy for mental health care. While ASTP clarified that minors may request to limit
sharing of EHI with legal guardians, consistent with HIPAA, the HTI-3 final rule did not specify how nor
address state laws that may in some circumstances give EHI to legal guardians against the wishes of the
minor. Therefore, we request ASTP issue actionable sub-regulatory guidance of acceptable technology
supporting patient preferences to assist EHR vendors and providers in supporting patient privacy
concerns without information blocking under the Privacy Exception. ASTP’s recognition that
comprehensive or advanced technological capabilities will provide an advantage over limited or outdated
technological capabilities does not provide a safe industry standard with which to do so. Such guidance
would increase in utility should ASTP revisit and finalize the HTI-2-proposed Requester Preferences
information blocking exception fostering granular patient choice in additional situations.

IV. ASTP Should Delay Removal of the Third-party Seeking Modification Use
Information Blocking Exception Until a Strong Trust Framework is Established

Continuing Vanderbilt Health’s long history of pioneering innovative health IT, we are finding success with
integrating AI-enabled solutions in clinical settings3–7. This success is due in large part to a robust AI
governance process and performance monitoring program, called the Vanderbilt Algorithmovigilance
Monitoring and Operations System (VAMOS)2. A critical feature of VAMOS is monitoring for algorithmic
drift, because AI systems are not stagnant and their performance can change over time8–11. Absent
safeguards such as these, AI systems pose substantial patient safety risks, often in the form of hallucinations
or otherwise confidently displaying incorrect medical information to patients12–15.

We do not believe there are currently existing and widely accepted best practices and trust frameworks that
would enable the safe integration of third-party AI systems with the ability to modify EHI. Therefore, we
are concerned about the proposal to remove the Third-party Seeking Modification Use information blocking
exception. We are particularly concerned about the lack of guardrails pertaining to outside entities’ ability
to populate a patient’s official health record. We are concerned that if all information can be freely populated
in the health record, it could lead to ill-informed decisions and treatment. AI is only as effective as the
accuracy of the information used in the decision-making process. In such a case, would the health system
or provider be liable for the accuracy of the EHI and consequences of treatment, or the entity that owns the
third party system? We encourage ASTP to work with stakeholders to develop strategies that avoid the
“garbage in, garbage out” liability as well as develop a trust framework and best practices for validating
whether AI systems are performing as expected. If technical solutions cannot be provided, we request a
policy that provides indemnity for clinical decisions made due to incorrect data provided by a third-party.
For these reasons, we urge ASTP to preserve the Third-party Seeking Modification Use information
blocking exception until these critical patient safety concerns are addressed.
We acknowledge that APIs could be very helpful in generating data that a patient would like to see
automatically populated into their health record. If ASTP wishes to advance a policy that encourages greater
use of third-party APIs to populate EHI, we encourage a measured, risk-based approach that does not
penalize providers for protecting patient safety. For example, APIs that assist in continuous glucose
monitoring to manage diabetes enhance visibility of blood sugar levels for patients. The use of an API to
share longitudinal glucose levels with providers would be relatively low risk. Conversely, EHI related to
diagnoses or health status could be more sensitive, complex, or prone to AI errors, and result in significant
clinical consequences.

V. Conclusion

Vanderbilt Health appreciates ASTP’s efforts to improve the interoperability of our nation’s health IT
infrastructure, provide greater choice and convenience to patients, and address administrative burdens on
providers. We support finalizing changes to EHR certification criteria and standards to further these goals.
However, we urge ASTP to reconsider removal of the Third-Party Seeking Modification Use information
blocking exception and revisit finalizing the Requestor Preferences information blocking exception. We are
concerned that adopting novel technologies without adequate safeguards or widely-accepted best practices
could jeopardize patients and unfairly increase liability issues for providers from inaccurate third-party
data. If changes to information blocking exceptions are finalized as proposed, we are concerned patients
may not understand where their data is going, who/what is populating their trusted health record, and
become upset with their providers when there are poor results. We agree with ASTP that patients should be
empowered to control their EHI, and encourage an approach that maximizes trust and safety in health care.

Thank you for the consideration of the above comments. If Vanderbilt Health can provide any additional
information or assistance to ASTP related to this proposed rule, please do not hesitate to contact Nicholas
Warren, PhD, of the Vanderbilt Health Office of Federal Relations at nicholas.warren@vumc.org.
