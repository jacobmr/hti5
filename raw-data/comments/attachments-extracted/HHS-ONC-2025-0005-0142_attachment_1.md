Dear Assistant Secretary Keane:

On behalf of NextGen Healthcare, we appreciate the opportunity to comment on the HTI-5
Proposed Rule. NextGen is a member of the Electronic Health Record Association (EHR
Association), and we support the Association’s detailed comments. The perspectives below
reinforce those comments and highlight issues of particular importance to our organization and
the ambulatory providers we serve.

NextGen supports ASTP/ONC’s goal of reducing unnecessary regulatory burden while
maintaining progress toward nationwide interoperability, transparency, and patient access to
electronic health information (EHI). We agree that certification criteria not directly tied to
interoperability, patient safety, or the statutory definition of a Qualified EHR should be carefully
reevaluated. At the same time, deregulatory changes should be paired with regulatory clarity,
alignment with CMS programs, and realistic implementation timelines.

I. Resetting the Certification Program

We support streamlining the Certification Program to focus on FHIR-based APIs and USCDI.
Prompt finalization of HTI-5 certification provisions is important to reduce uncertainty affecting
development planning and provider investment decisions. Future updates—particularly in HTI-
6—should include sufficient lead time and close coordination with CMS so that certification
timelines align with Promoting Interoperability and Quality Payment Program requirements.

We agree that removal of mature, well-adopted criteria will not result in loss of functionality.
Many of the criteria proposed for removal are already deeply embedded in clinical workflows
and will continue to be supported based on provider and patient needs.

We also support eliminating or streamlining criteria that have proven overly prescriptive relative
to their value, including Clinical Information Reconciliation and Incorporation, Safety-Enhanced
Design, Consolidated CDA creation performance, and Security Tags—Summary of Care. For
Transitions of Care and View, Download, and Transmit, we recommend reducing duplicative
CCDA testing permutations that add complexity without corresponding benefit.

With respect to Predictive Decision Support Interventions (PDSIs), we understand ONC’s intent
to reduce burden by removing certain source attribute and Intervention Risk Management
requirements. However, predictive and AI-enabled tools raise unique transparency and
governance considerations. Rather than eliminating guardrails entirely, ONC should adopt a
simplified, principles-based standard.

For example, certified health IT could be required to make available basic information about a
predictive intervention’s intended use, general data inputs, validation context, and known
limitations. Similarly, a streamlined risk management expectation aligned with recognized
industry frameworks would preserve accountability without imposing detailed process
mandates. A balanced approach would reduce burden while maintaining trust and oversight.

We also support reevaluating USCDI requirements to allow certification based on the data a
developer manages within the USCDI scope, which would promote broader participation
without unnecessary development effort.

II. Privacy and Security Framework

We support modernizing the privacy and security framework and embedding security
expectations directly into functional certification criteria. However, if ONC retires discrete
security criteria, the program should still establish clear baseline security controls applicable to
all certified products.

These baseline controls should address authentication, authorization, encryption, audit logging,
integrity protections, and secure configuration management, aligned with recognized standards
such as the NIST Cybersecurity Framework or NIST SP 800-53. Certification testing should
require objective evidence that such controls are implemented by default.

III. Information Blocking Provisions

Although HTI-5 is framed as deregulatory, the proposed revisions to the information blocking
exceptions would introduce significant new compliance complexity. These changes are not
purely technical clarifications; they materially affect how developers evaluate and document
compliance.

We respectfully request that ONC quantify the incremental legal, engineering, documentation,
and contracting costs associated with the proposed revisions. Without clear burden estimates,
stakeholders cannot meaningfully assess the economic impact of these changes.

Revisions to the infeasibility and manner exceptions would require case-by-case technical and
legal analysis of requests, including documentation of why alternative methods were not
offered. Expanding the concept of “analogous” methods introduces interpretive ambiguity.
Developers rely on clear safe harbors; replacing “same” with “analogous” without defined
criteria may create uncertainty and inconsistent enforcement. ONC should either retain the
term “same” or provide objective factors and illustrative examples that clearly define
“analogous.”

Similarly, incorporating concepts such as “market rate,” “contract of adhesion,” and
“unconscionable terms” introduces commercial law doctrines that are inherently fact-specific
and context-driven. In many interoperability contexts—particularly emerging API services—
there is no transparent marketplace to establish benchmark pricing. Applying these standards
may increase transaction costs, delay contracting, and discourage investment in enhanced
interoperability capabilities.

Enterprise health IT agreements frequently bundle services, hosting, security updates, and
regulatory compliance activities. Isolating a “market rate” for a discrete data access component
may not reflect economic realities and could increase overall costs for providers.

We also oppose a one-size-fits-all application of the Fees and Licensing Exceptions. Advanced
interoperability services—such as scalable FHIR APIs and population-level exports—require
ongoing infrastructure investment, monitoring, and security oversight. Developers should retain
flexibility to structure fees that reflect these continuing costs.

NextGen fully supports preventing practices that interfere with lawful access, exchange, and use
of EHI. Our comments are intended to ensure that the exceptions framework remains clear,
objective, and administrable. Where revisions introduce ambiguity or incorporate commercial
doctrines not traditionally used in federal health IT regulation, ONC should provide defined
criteria and illustrative examples to preserve predictability and avoid unintended expansion of
enforcement exposure.

IV. TEFCA and Interoperability Incentives

We support TEFCA participation and encourage ONC to consider meaningful incentives or safe
harbors that recognize good-faith participation and infrastructure investment.

V. Burden Estimates and Regulatory Classification

We respectfully request that ONC provide revised burden estimates for the information blocking
provisions and clearly distinguish between deregulatory actions and new regulatory obligations
prior to finalization.

Conclusion

NextGen Healthcare supports thoughtful removal of outdated certification requirements and
improved alignment with CMS programs to reduce burden while maintaining interoperability
and patient access. We urge refinement of the information blocking proposals to preserve
clarity, predictability, and innovation.

We appreciate the opportunity to provide these comments and look forward to continued
collaboration with ASTP/ONC.
