SUBJECT: Request for Comments on Proposed Rule, “Health Data, Technology, and
Interoperability: ASTP/ONC Deregulatory Actions to Unleash Prosperity (HTI-5)”

The Washington State Health Care Authority (HCA) offers the following comments and
recommendations in response to ASTP/ONC HTI-5. HTI-5 may have mixed implications for
HCA. The proposed reduction or removal of certain ONC certification requirements could lower
compliance, burden and associated costs for vendors and providers, potentially enabling greater
focus on priority interoperability efforts aligned with CMS interoperability mandates and
TEFCA participation. On the other hand, to the extent the proposal reduces baseline certified
functionality, particularly in areas related to security controls and standardized exchange, it may
increase variability across health IT products. This could require HCA to assume a more active
role defining, enforcing, and contracting for minimum interoperability and security capabilities
to ensure statewide exchange, Medicaid operations, and future procurements to continue to meet
programmatic and federal expectations.

Part 170 — ONC Health IT Certification Program
§ 170.404 — API Conditions & Maintenance
Washington State HCA relies on interoperable, production-ready FHIR APIs to operate
Medicaid programs. Changes to the ONC Health IT Certification Program should not reduce the
availability, reliability, or consistency of APIs that we already depend on.
If certification scope criteria are removed or narrowed, those changes must not break existing
FHIR APIs. States cannot rewrite procurements and managed care contracts each time
certification scope changes.
ASTP/ONC should avoid ambiguity that leads to inconsistent implementation and disputes over
responsibility between developers, providers, and payers. Unclear accountability can lead to late,
missing, or unusable data. The rule should clearly define responsibilities when certified
exchange pathways fail.

§ 170.405 — Real World Testing
Real World Testing is essential. Vendors should continue to demonstrate that APIs perform
reliably at scale, not just in test scenarios.
We are concerned that narrowing Real World Testing requirements during the 2026–2027
implementation period could weaken this safeguard. ASTP/ONC should avoid introducing new
uncertainty during active implementation years and should provide clear expectations and
transition guidance if requirements change.

§ 170.102 — Certification Scope and Base EHR Definition
Changes to certification scope and the Base EHR definition have direct downstream impacts on
HCA/Medicaid procurements and contracts. HCA must design multi-year agreements based on
certified capabilities. Certification changes that remove or weaken those capabilities create
operational risk including delay's and added cost.
ASTP/ONC should preserve stability in the interoperable functionality we rely on.

Part 171 — Information Blocking
§ 171.102 — Definitions of “Access” and “Use”
Clarifications to information blocking should prevent misuse of exceptions to delay or deny data
required for HCA Medicaid operations. Information blocking is sometimes used as a legal shield
to avoid sharing data.
Our programs need timely data to pay claims, manage care, conduct oversight, and meet federal
deadlines. Routine data exchange is not optional. Definitions should clearly recognize automated
and API-based access as core forms of access and use.

§ 171.204 Infeasibility Exception and § 171.301 Manner Exception
Exceptions should not be applied in ways that delay API access or create lengthy negotiations.

State Authority
Washington State HCA requests the rule should either clearly delineate national interoperability
standards required across all government services/contractors receiving federal funding or ensure
it doesn’t limit state authority to require certain standards through contract.
If certification no longer guarantees certain capabilities, we must still be able to require them
contractually. Federal rules should not limit state authority to require specific FHIR endpoints,
data timeliness, conformance testing, or operational performance metrics.

Data Quality and State-Sponsored Clinical Data Aggregation
HCA aggregates clinical data for analytics, quality measurement, and oversight. Consistent and
complete EHR outputs are essential. If certification changes reduce consistency, state data
quality degrades and downstream costs increase for taxpayers.
ASTP/ONC should maintain sufficient standardization to support state-sponsored clinical data
aggregation.

Implementation Guidance
ASTP/ONC should publish implementation guidance alongside final policy changes. High-level
rules without examples cause confusion. Clear guidance, transition timelines, and explicit
statements of what changes now versus later—especially during the 2026–2027 implementation
period are needed.
