Re: Comments on the Health Data, Technology, and Interoperability: ASTP/ONC
Deregulatory Actions to Unleash Prosperity (HTI-5) Proposed Rule
Bridget Barnes
To the Assistant Secretary and National Coordinator, barnesbr@ohsu.edu

Oregon Health & Science University (OHSU) appreciates the opportunity to provide
comments on the HTI-5 Proposed Rule. We share the Administration’s passion for
innovation and a desire to foster an open ecosystem for AI and health technology so
that all Americans can benefit from modern care delivery. We strongly support the
goal of reducing regulatory friction to unleash prosperity; however, we believe that
for innovation to be sustainable, it must be built on a foundation of trust and safety.

Our privacy and legal teams, along with our clinical leadership, have reviewed the
proposed deregulatory actions. While we support the spirit of interoperability, we
are concerned that specific proposals in HTI-5—particularly regarding AI
transparency, information blocking exceptions, and the removal of certification
criteria—will inadvertently shift significant risk and burden onto Health Care
Providers (HCPs) and compromise patient safety.

Below are our specific comments and recommendations.

1. AI Transparency and Predictive Decision Support Interventions (DSI)

We strongly oppose the proposal in HTI-5 to remove the transparency requirements
for Predictive Decision Support Interventions (specifically, the "Source Attributes"
or "Model Cards").

Removing the simple language to share details about how the model was
developed, evaluated, tested, and generalized through model cards means that
health care organizations—and their patients—with less experience in AI and its
best uses will be vulnerable to companies who speak well but haven't tested their
systems appropriately.

The use of "model cards" as in the predictive decision support interventions
requirement is a very simple way to tell us how the system uses AI and whether it is
safe for human use. It provides a standardized "nutrition label" that allows
providers to make informed procurement and clinical decisions. Given the fact that
the FDA doesn't evaluate many of these uses, this basic requirement seems a low
bar with high value. Removing it does not unleash innovation; it unleashes opacity
that may harm patients and erode trust in AI tools.

2. Certification Criteria and Burden Shifting

Additionally, we caution against the HTI-5 proposal to remove a significant number
of certification criteria (the proposed removal of 34 criteria). Removing certain
certification criteria shifts the burden of defending interoperability claims to HCPs.
If a Health IT system is not interoperable when certified because the "functionality-
based" criteria have been removed, the provider (not the vendor) is often the one
investigated for Information Blocking when data fails to flow. Providers often lack
the technical power to fix these issues.

Additionally, “privacy and security” certification criteria, like authentication, access
controls, audit report and multi-factor authentication, are critical for systems that
process or store patients’ health data. Robust privacy and security criteria should
be a core requirement of Health IT systems, not a feature. They belong in the base
product, not on the invoice as an add-on or “premium upgrade.”

We ask that the ATSP/ONC keep certification requirements, so privacy, security and
interoperability are guaranteed as a baseline, not as a future improvement or
added cost. Certification is often the only leverage HCPs have to ensure vendors
deliver secure products that work as promised.

3. Information Blocking (IB) Exceptions

We are concerned that the HTI-5 proposals to remove or tighten Information
Blocking exceptions will make it more difficult for HCPs to safeguard the privacy
and integrity of patient records. For example, HCPs must be able to protect the
integrity of our patients’ health records by resisting inappropriate attempts to
modify or “write-back” data to patients’ records. The “Third-Party Seeking
Modification Use Condition” of the Infeasibility Exception gave HCPs a strong and
simple basis to resist requests that clearly put patient safety and data integrity at
risk. Removing that exception would require HCPs to put significantly more time
and resources into documenting other Information Blocking Exceptions to
demonstrate that our action did not constitute an impermissible Information
Blocking practice.

Similarly, the TEFCA exception should remain in place as an option to meet certain
data requests. OHSU and other HCPs invested time, resources and considerable
effort into implementing the TEFCA framework to support secure exchanges for
permitted purposes (like treatment) and to avoid the expense and burden of having
to maintain multiple different ways of sharing the same data for the same purpose.
Removing this exception increases our burden to review and respond to requests to
use new tools for the same purpose that TEFCA already supports.

Lastly, the HTI-5 proposal did not account for the increased burden and costs HCPs
would incur due to the removal and/or narrowing of Information Blocking
Exceptions that we rely on today to appropriately deny certain access or
modification requests.

OHSU asks that the ASTP/ONC consider the legitimate concerns we raised above
and to retain, unmodified, these important Information Blocking conditions and
exceptions.

4. Supporting Interoperability without Compromising Patient Privacy

We recognize that the ASTP/ONC is attempting to address potential Information
Blocking practices with these proposed changes. However, cumulatively, these
changes have the effect of prioritizing and rewarding fast, unimpeded data
exchanges while disincentivizing HCPs from taking important and reasonable steps
to verify and gatekeep access to our patients’ data.

HCPs already face challenges to protect patient data from breaches in automatic,
“trusted exchange” environments like TEFCA and Carequality (see Particle
Health/Integritort and the recent Gorilla Health lawsuits for examples). FHIR-based
exchanges do not operate in the same trust framework and can increase the risks
faced by HIPAA covered entities who currently retain the entire regulatory burden
for breaches of patient health data.

We urge the ASTP/ONC to close the accountability loop by updating the definition of
“Health IT Developer” to include any non-HIPAA covered entity that requests data
via a Certified API and including penalties if their actions cause a HIPAA breach.

Conclusion

We stand ready to work with the ASTP/ONC to refine these proposals. We believe
we can achieve a deregulatory environment that encourages business prosperity
without dismantling the safety rails that protect American patients and the
providers who care for them.
