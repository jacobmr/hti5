Dear Dr. Keane,

On behalf of nearly 30 member companies that develop, implement, and support certified health IT for
the vast majority of hospitals and ambulatory providers nationwide, the HIMSS Electronic Health Record
(EHR) Association appreciates the opportunity to provide input on Health Data, Technology, and
Interoperability: ASTP/ONC Deregulatory Actions to Unleash Prosperity (HTI-5) Proposed Rule.

We appreciate the efforts by ASTP/ONC to take deregulatory actions that could reduce burden for the
health IT software development industry and, in some cases, the providers using that software, all while
maintaining progress toward the nation’s goals of advancing interoperability, transparency, and access
to electronic health information. We agree with the assertion that many certification criteria not directly
associated with interoperability should be considered for removal, being retained only if they are
needed to satisfy the definition of a Qualified EHR or play some other critical role in fostering safe,
secure health IT adoption and use.

Despite this general support for the effort, we encourage ASTP/ONC to carefully consider the impact the
proposed certification changes would potentially have on the ease of health data interoperability and
patient safety within the health system as a whole. While there was largely consensus among health IT
developers that removing well-adopted criteria from certification would be unlikely to result in those
capabilities being removed from products, there were some voices who expressed a concern that losing
the “weight” of regulatory requirements might lead to a reduction in ongoing investment in that existing
functionality or associated process going forward, given the challenges of juggling resource constraints
against continually shifting Federal and State regulatory requirements. Additionally, there is some
apprehension that inefficiencies, added costs, and safety risks could actually increase if new EHR market
entrants are able to achieve certification by meeting only the remaining criteria, possibly ignoring
entirely standardized approaches to exchange and data harmonization that are foundational to
successful interoperability.
We also encourage not only the rapid finalization of the deregulatory actions proposed within the HTI-5
NPRM but also the prompt release of the updated and new certification and conditions and
maintenance of certification requirements that ASTP/ONC intends to include in the HTI-6 proposed rule.
It has proven difficult for us to comment as thoroughly as we would normally on the HTI-5 NPRM
without knowing the other imminent plans for the certification program. For example, it has been
unclear whether certification proposals from HTI-2 that were previously canceled will be reintroduced in
a different form in HTI-6. Additionally, areas identified in our comments as creating potential gaps may
be addressed through other concepts under consideration for HTI-6, which could ultimately affect our
perspective on specific proposals. It is these types of questions that make it more difficult for us to
comment on some of the deregulatory proposals in this NPRM and that cause some level of uncertainty
in terms of development efforts, certification planning, and provider adoption plans.

When updated certification requirements are added in the future, we also urge you to continue to look
even more closely at timelines to ensure they are adequate for the scope of the work to be completed
and to coordinate carefully with CMS to ensure better alignment between ASTP/ONC deadlines for
software certification and those requiring adoption of updated technologies by our provider customers.

Regarding the information blocking proposals, we are concerned that ASTP/ONC is overstating the
burden reduction outcomes of its proposed changes, which are not deregulatory in nature, and
attempting to blame regulated actors for misunderstanding or inadvertently misapplying Exceptions as
they currently exist. Because the agency also did not provide an estimate of the implementation costs
that would be borne by the industry, it suggests that ASTP/ONC is underestimating the true economic
impact of current information blocking policy, and the changes proposed in HTI-5. The significantly
heightened complexity and ambiguity inherent to ASTP/ONC’s proposals add to already complex
information blocking policies, which will add additional administrative burden to not only software
developers but other stakeholders who interact with our community in the process of determining the
best path forward for information access, exchange, and use. While we appreciate where ASTP/ONC
proposes to clarify definitions, it falls short of the simplification, guidance, and education that would
actually cut through the complexity of current policy.

Our detailed comments follow. We look forward to continued collaboration to advance interoperability
and improve patient care.

Terms and Definitions

General

Therefore, to provide sufficient time for CMS to update the CEHRT definitions to remove reference to the

We appreciate the coordination with CMS and their references to the certification program, as it will be

§ 170.315(a)(5) Patient demographics and observations

Under this enforcement discretion and certification guidance, we noted that ASTP/ONC will not take any
enforcement action under § 170.565 against an ONC-ACB based on non-compliance with § 170.550 for
certifying a Health IT Module that is presented for certification to the patient demographics and
observations certification criterion (§ 170.315(a)(5)) where the Health IT Module does not demonstrate
conformance with any or all of the following data and observations in paragraph (a)(5)(i): sex parameter
for clinical use, sexual orientation, gender identity, name to use, and pronouns; or does not demonstrate
conformance with any or all of data and observations in § 170.315(a)(5)(i)(D) through (H).

We support this proposal as a sensible approach to maintaining consistency with enforcement discretion
from 2025. We further suggest that this criterion be removed altogether and defer instead to USDCI’s
demographics data class. This would permit further deregulation while still satisfying the definition of a
Qualified EHR.

§ 170.315(a)(9) Clinical decision support

In the HTI-1 Final Rule, we finalized an expiration date of January 1, 2025, for the CDS certification
criterion in § 170.315(a)(9) and incorporate certain policies with modifications in the “decision support
intervention” certification criterion in § 170.315(b)(11) (89 FR 1237)

We generally support the establishment of an expiration date as a sensible approach to removing older
and outdated criteria. However, if all source attribute requirements are removed from the (b)(11)
criterion, we suggest reinstating the (a)(9) criterion without the formerly established Info button
standard requirements as a way to retain the source attribute requirements for evidence-based decision
support. Source attributes, such as the developer and funding source of interventions, are very helpful

§ 170.315(a)(12) Family health history

We propose to remove the “family health history” certification criterion. The functionality of this
certification criterion has been part of the Certification Program and associated with the Medicare
Promoting Interoperability Program and the MIPS Promoting Interoperability performance category
(including prior iterations such as the EHR Incentives Programs and the CEHRT definitions) for over 12
years. As such, the functionality is embedded in certified health IT and has been widely adopted by
hospitals and physicians due to participation in those programs.16 Further, similar to what we stated
when we adopted the certification criterion, the ability to code to SNOMED® CT U.S. Edition (including
the entire adopted version) is required to meet many of the remaining certification criteria, including
those referencing the USCDI v3 and those criteria that would include the proposed USCDI v3.1 standard.

We support the proposed removal of the “family health history” certification criteria and agree in
general with ASTP/ONC’s rationale for doing so. Because it is not cited in the Qualified EHR definition,
retaining it is not necessary. Further, it is a well-established EHR feature that developers likely do not
plan to remove from their systems even if it is eliminated as a certification criterion.

§ 170.315(a)(14) Implantable device list

We propose to remove and reserve the “implantable device list” certification criterion in §
170.315(a)(14).

We support removal of the “implantable device list” certification criterion based on ASTP/ONC’s
statement that its policy goal of broad adoption has been achieved. Further, it is not cited in the
Qualified EHR definition and remains part of USCDI. As such, retaining the certification criterion is
unnecessary. Finally, because it is already a well-established EHR feature, it will likely not be removed
from systems if the criterion is removed from certification.

§ 170.315(b)(1) Transitions of care

Our membership expressed mixed views on the proposal to reduce the scope of the transitions of care
criterion to focus on enabling the receipt of a C-CDA. We agree that it is highly unlikely that any
currently certified system would remove the ability to send C-CDAs, given that they are and will likely
remain widely used across the healthcare industry. Additionally, because the testing tool for this
criterion is notoriously buggy, it usually creates an additional testing burden. Therefore, the removal of
testing the sending use cases would achieve the Administration goal of reducing burden.

However, there are varying perspectives amongst EHR Association member companies as to whether
the proposed removal from certification would be advantageous or whether the requirement specific to

                                                                                                             5

C-CDA sending functionality should remain to ensure quality and consistency in how that functionality is
implemented across the industry. Additionally, the industry has found that it has been helpful to have a
single source of truth related to consistent implementation of a standard like C-CDA. We are also
concerned about the impact of the proposed change if new market entrants are certified for the first
time to only the remaining criteria.

Thus, we suggest an alternative proposal: remove sending C-CDA from (e)(1) but retain it in (b)(1). While
the opposite of what is proposed, this approach is more sensible in many respects. In particular, (e)(1)
includes >50 sending cases, so removing the sending use cases from that criterion would be even more
impactful for our companies in reducing testing burden. Patient sending and use of C-CDA is lower in
volume than provider use, making that the more sensible criterion to narrow.

In (b)(1), we suggest removing the validation functionality from certification, as well as the section
viewing and filtering, as both are rarely used by clinicians and administrators. Like with other criteria,
these features would likely not be removed from EHRs even if they are not subject to testing.

§ 170.315(b)(2) Clinical information reconciliation and incorporation

We strongly support the proposal to remove CIRI as a certification criterion. Many developers have felt
that the way this criterion is written constrains our opportunities to implement creative approaches to
CIRI to support providers.

The overly prescriptive nature of the CIRI criterion, for example requiring particular screen design,
hampers innovation. If retained, the criterion should be dramatically simplified to better accommodate
innovation with a focus on demonstration of incorporation, as well as removing all of the UI
requirements for what displays on what screen.

§ 170.315(b)(7) Security tags - summary of care - send

The EHR Association agrees that the “security tags—summary of care—send” criterion has not been
well-adopted due to challenges with the underlying standards and implementation guidance. Therefore,
we support its removal as certification criteria. We also note our belief that while there is a need for
comprehensive consent standards, consensus has not yet been reached across the industry as to the
optimal approach and requires more work.

§ 170.315(b)(8) Security tags - summary of care - receive

We agree that the “security tags—summary of care—receive” criterion has not been well-adopted due
to challenges with the underlying standards and implementation guidance and we therefore support its
removal as certification criteria. There is also a need for comprehensive consent standards; however,
there is no consensus across the healthcare industry on the optimal path forward.

§ 170.315(b)(9) Care plan

Because the care plan certification criterion is no longer required for CPC+, we support its removal.
Additionally, because the (a)(15) criterion was only briefly required for CPC+, we suggest that it, too, be
eliminated.

§ 170.315(b)(11) Decision support interventions

Regarding removing the requirement to support all USCDI data elements to trigger PDSI and narrowing
it to a limited set, we agree with ASTP/ONC’s assertion that some data elements have minimal value to
select PDSI. We also agree with ASTP/ONC’s assertion that the selection of PDSI based on many USCDI
data elements is of minimal value. Indeed, we think that the concept of selection in general, without
specified standards, has long generated confusion across the industry. It sets an inaccurate expectation
that any PDSI can be integrated — without actually having a standard that can be used for integration.
That is why our suggestion is for the selection component of the criterion to be eliminated entirely.

Regarding the proposed removal of the requirement to provide source attributes, we found in our
review of the proposed rule that Association members hold varying opinions. Members agree with
ASTP/ONC that end user clinicians almost never access source attributes, so requiring their inclusion
within the software (and particularly the clinical workflow) is burdensome and returns low value.
Additionally, because source attributes are almost never modified by customers, we agree that requiring
that we permit customers to edit and modify source attributes is also burdensome and of low value.

However, EHR Association members took different positions on whether source attributes should be
required to be provided at all, as well as whether that could still add value outside the software’s clinical
workflow and without modification capability. Proponents of removal from certification say they would
not stop providing source attributes and transparency information even if there is no certification
requirement, as they expect client demand for that information to continue. Many would consider how
they might provide access through different mechanisms. Members also noted the ambiguity regarding
the scope of this criterion (e.g., what is PDSI provided in the certified health IT module?), which would
be alleviated by its removal.

Conversely, others suggested that retaining the requirement to provide customers/users with at least
some source attributes, but not requiring that they be accessible in the software or that they be
modifiable, would still ensure that people who evaluate new AI models can assess the source attributes
through some sort of standardized method. Given the need to build trust amongst clinical users of AI
during this nascent stage of technology adoption, assuring continued transparency by still requiring that
the most informative source attributes be provided to users in a user-friendly way could prove helpful.

As an alternative to ASTP/ONC’s proposed approach, one suggestion would be to retain source attribute
information for evidence-based DSI to promote transparency. This would involve reversion to the (a)(9)
Clinical Decision Support criterion instead of (b)(11). The (a)(9) criterion is much simpler than (b)(11)
while still providing transparency on attributes like developer and funding source.

Regarding removing IRM, health IT vendors indicate they would likely continue their IRM programs in
some form regardless, based on customer demand.

§ 170.315(c)(3) Clinical quality measures — report

We support the proposal to remove “clinical quality measures—report” certification requirement in in
§ 170.315(c)(3)(ii), as removing outdated standards will have no impact.

§ 170.315(c)(4) Clinical quality measures — filter

Because the program that proposed to use the “clinical quality measures—filter” criterion was retired by

Privacy and Security Certification Criteria

We agree that the current certification privacy and security framework is highly complicated, requires
unnecessary burden to demonstrate, and leads to misunderstandings with certification stakeholders.
We further agree that there are other obligations that will require EHR and other health IT developers to
continue providing systems that support covered entities in compliance with HIPAA. Certain criteria that
have incorporated privacy and security, such as (g)(10), have been effective as a pathway where
compliance with functionality (such as authentication) are included within the certification of that
functional criterion.

However, there are differences of opinions among EHR Association member companies regarding
possible approaches to revising privacy and security criteria versus removing them in their entirety.
Some strongly support removing the current framework and replacing it with in-criterion security and
privacy expectations, as proposed by ASTP/ONC. Others have counterproposals on revising the current
framework. Some concerns of those favoring revision include new market entrants understanding the
technical needs of their healthcare clients.

§ 170.315(e)(1) View, download, and transmit to 3rd party

As noted in our comments on (b)(1), transitions of care, we recommend removing C-CDA generation
requirements from this criterion, whether in addition to or rather than removing it from (b)(1). While we
agree that C-CDA will play an important role in interoperability for many years to come, the inclusion of
more than 50 permutations of C-CDA generation testing in (e)(1) is especially duplicative and repetitive,
thereby creating a significant burden.

We further agree with WCAG tool licensing fees cited by ASTP/ONC and favor removing this
requirement and the NTP standard, which has always been challenging to demonstrate given the
reliance on the operating system.

§ 170.315(e)(3) Patient health information capture

We agree that the “patient health information capture” certification criterion does not deliver value in
its current form. It should be removed.

§ 170.315(f)(4) Transmission to cancer registries

We support removing the “transmission to cancer registries” certification criterion and encourage
ASTP/ONC to include a path and a plan for migration of these standards on the receiving side and
through HL7 CDA, provided an alternative standard has been identified or agency tasked with driving its
adoption (e.g., CDC).

§ 170.315(g)(1) Automated numerator recording

EHR Association member companies have mixed opinions regarding the proposal to remove (g)(1) and
(g)(2) and note the ongoing uncertainty we are left with, given the current approach. We agree that
these criteria are extremely burdensome to test and therefore strongly support approaches to
streamline and reduce the associated burden. One recommendation would be narrowing the required
test cases and changing ASTP/ONC’s policy on requiring retests every three inheritances.

That said, we are challenged to fully comment without seeing how CMS implements the corresponding
update. If the Promoting Interoperability (PI) program is changed to require only attestation-based
measures, for example, we feel the ASTP/ONC approach is appropriate. However, if PI were to retain
numerator and denominator measures, the certification guidance would remain very helpful for
measurement consistency. ASTP/ONC notes it will provide the test data and guidance to CMS for
ongoing reference; at a minimum, maintaining that material in some form will be important, given that
the burden of maintaining the measures would not be alleviated.

§ 170.315(g)(2) Automated measure calculation

See our comments on (g)(1).

§ 170.315(g)(3) Safety-enhanced design

The EHR Association feels strongly about the importance of prioritizing safety in the development
process, including user-centered design. However, we agree it is appropriate to remove the “safety-
enhanced design” criterion, given the significant expense of conducting the required tests, which is

                                                                                                           9

perhaps one of the most significant costs and sources of burden within the certification program.
Additionally, the summative testing required by this criterion has not proven useful in improving safety
despite several years of requirement. Developers typically find formative usability testing more useful in
product improvement. Thus, removing this criterion will alleviate burden and allow developers to invest
increased time and money in usability studies with greater impact.

It is important to note that developers are unlikely to change their approaches to formative usability
testing and other studies. In fact, many look at the removal of this criterion as an opportunity to put
more resources to testing of that type.

§ 170.315(g)(4) Quality management system

Most developers likely would not change their quality management systems regardless of what happens
with the QMS criterion, but as noted above, that risk is not zero. We are also unsure if new market
entrants would adopt those same or equivalent quality management systems if the same attestation
were not required. While it should be feasible for providers to assess the QMS of developers through
other means, such as requesting ISO certificates, less sophisticated or resource-strapped organizations
may not always know the best practices specific to this type of health IT development process work and
therefore may not ask for those certificates.

We also do not see significant burden reduction coming from removal of this criterion, as attestation is a
relatively low-burden process to complete.

§ 170.315(g)(5) Accessibility-centered design

Developers who are currently certified likely would not intend to change their accessibility approaches
regardless of what happens with the ACD criterion, although we are uncertain if new market entrants
will adopt similar accessibility practices. To the extent developers use accessibility conformance tools as
part of their programs, such tools can also be expensive, and we expect adoption going forward may be
uneven if the criterion is moved.

We also do not see significant burden reduction resulting from removal of this criterion.

§ 170.315(g)(6) Consolidated CDA creation performance

We agree the “consolidated CDA creation performance” criterion can be removed. In fact, we are
uncertain it has ever added value.

§ 170.315(g)(7) Application access — patient selection

We agree that the “application access—patient selection” criterion can be removed, as it is duplicative
of (g)(10).

§ 170.315(g)(9) Application access — all data request

USCDI data is available via other FHIR resources even if document-based resources are not available. As
such, we agree it is reasonable to remove the “application access—all data request” certification.

Other Criteria to Consider Removing

As noted in each of our Deregulatory RFI responses, in 2025, we believe (b)(10) EHI Export should be
considered for removal in favor of further investment in standards-based APIs. The current approach is
burdensome and does not promote use of standards, and as a result, it has led to some market
frustration with the use of the functionality.

We also suggest removing the (a)(15) criterion for the same reasons as the (b)(9) criterion.

USCDI, g(10)

To support deregulatory efforts, we recommend reviewing the number of criteria and the scope of data
required by the USCDI progression that certified health IT must support. Different health IT, including
specialty EHRs, need not support all data within USCDI, as it creates an unnecessary burden and
disincentivizes certification. By simplifying requirements to be more dynamic and support only the
applicable USCDI data types, it would ensure that many health IT vendors could participate in
certification, encouraging greater interoperability, higher quality, and faster connections through
reliable, certified APIs.

In that context, we continue to urge ASTP/ONC, as indicated in our feedback on prior HTI and MU rules,
to enable health IT to be certified to the data they “manage” within the scope of USCDI. Managing could
be defined as “supporting the ability for the pertinent data to be stored locally and for the system’s end-
users to view and interact with the discrete USCDI data while documenting and managing their clinical,
administrative, and/or financial workflows.”

Importantly, this should not be interpreted to mean the system must be the direct source of the data.
The data could be sourced from upstream systems and integrated for management in the local system
via HL7 v2 interfaces, RESTful API writes, or other methods, but the system would need to support
storage of and interaction with the discrete data locally. The scope of USCDI data elements that a
product is certified for supporting (i.e., “managing” and exposing via the relevant standard for the given
criterion) could be made transparent to the market via clear publication on the CHPL listing, and
potentially even a public disclosure on the developer’s website.

                                                                                                        11

This approach would enable specialty EHRs to avoid development of functionality that their user
community does not need. It would also allow health IT beyond EHRs to be certified to the relevant
capabilities when interacting with EHRs, patient apps, and other health IT solutions, while still ensuring
that when healthcare providers purchase and implement a product certified for USCDI criteria, it
provides them with the full, relevant spectrum of functionality critical to successful interoperability.

Standards Version Advancement Process (SVAP)

As mentioned in our comments on (e)(1), we think fewer test cases for VDT would remove duplicative
testing and alleviate burden, as there are 52 very repetitive test cases in the current procedure. If (g)(2)
is not removed, the number of test cases for that testing would be another method to reduce
certification testing burden.

We also suggest two changes to SVAP which could reduce burden for developers and simplify testing.
First, make the SVAP versions available with more advance notice, helping developers plan their
investments and avoid disrupting their long-standing development roadmaps. Second, persist the SVAP
versions until the certification floor is raised, which would help developers adopt new standards with
greater confidence that they will be able to continue to certify to them over time.

RWT Reporting

We appreciate ONC finalizing this deregulatory proposal consistent with their previous enforcement
discretion. In general, developers are unclear what the value of RWT is, as it requires significant effort to
gather data and publish with little evidence that the reports are providing any real benefit to the
industry.

Regarding SVAP, developers request clarification regarding what is expected by SVAP criteria with
respect to RWT.

In terms of expectations of conducting testing, while ASTP/ONC has been clear that only (g)(10), (g)(31),
(g)(32), (g)(33) results must be published, it is unclear if other testing is still expected to be conducted. If
other testing persists, burden is not actually reduced.

Insights

We appreciate ONC finalizing this deregulatory proposal consistent with their previous enforcement
discretion.

It takes time to develop new metrics and push out the new data capture to EHR users, which is why it is
imperative to ensure sufficient lead time prior to the data collection time period — not simply prior to
the data submission window — when updating Insights metrics with new specifications.

Definition Updates

The EHR Association appreciates additional clarity and explicit guidance offered in the complicated and
ambiguous landscape of information blocking expectations. To offer maximum clarity, we suggest ONC
update all three definitions (Access, Exchange, Use).

Infeasibility

RE: Removing the “third party seeking modification use” condition (§ 171.204(a)(3))

The EHR Association refutes the implication that health IT developers misuse the Infeasibility Exception
and find the implication to be unnecessarily inflammatory, particularly as this Exception has been rarely
used by health IT developers. We also request for ASTP/ONC to provide examples or more explanation
in the future as to what types of misuse ASTP/ONC has heard about, without providing any identifying
information. This information could be valuable to the industry and public in understanding how
ASTP/ONC and others view the condition/exception at hand and how they are intended by regulatory
authors to be used. We cannot overstate the benefit of being provided real-world examples of correct
and incorrect application of all of the Exceptions.

Further, ASTP/ONC indicates that other Exceptions might be applicable for some modification scenarios.
We note that other Exceptions do not have the limiting factor of a 10-business day communication,
which makes the Infeasibility Exception very impractical to use.

RE: Increasing the minimum number of alternative manners required to be considered to have
“exhausted” the Manner Exception.

The EHR Association again strongly refutes the suggestion that health IT developers are abusing the
Infeasibility Exception. We suggest instead that ASTP/ONC should view confusion amongst regulated
actors as a symptom of the complexity of their regulatory program and inadequacy of their guidance
and education.

We note that while ASTP/ONC adds additional expectations for the use of this Exception, such as custom
negotiation of agreements, they do not include in the HTI-5 NPRM any estimate of the burden or costs
expected to stem from those Expectations. ASTP/ONC should provide estimates to further illustrate
what they expect from regulated actors and to accurately reflect the burden associated with this change
in policy. For example, how many “explorations” of agreeable terms would be necessary, and how long
would they take?

As the EHR Association has explained in previous feedback regarding the Infeasibility Exception, the 10-
day window is inadequate. The Exception process definitively cannot be completed in such a short
timeframe. The 10-day window should begin when the negotiations have been completed. While still
aggressively fast, that would make it more practical to be able to meet the requirements of the Manner
Exception and the Manner Exception Exhausted.

Finally, ASTP/ONC seems to have overly prescriptive expectations of the roles of the actor and the
requester with respect to suggesting and accepting alternatives. Association members find that in most
integration discussions, there is a mix of the requester specifying their use case and goals and then
suggesting what standards they see as best meeting those, and the actor outlining what types of
integration options they offer. ASTP/ONC should not set an expectation that an actor specifies specific
alternatives in a particular order if it is more practical or expedient to offer a menu of relevant
alternatives and permit the requester to give input on which best meets their needs. In fact, this is

overly heavy-handed on ASTP/ONC’s part and counter to the deregulatory requirements outlined by the
White House.

RE: Replacing “same” with “analogous” as it refers to the access, exchange, or use of EHI.

The term “analogous” is ambiguous and difficult to operationalize. That ambiguity also makes it difficult
for OIG to arbitrate. Additionally, we have seen information blocking Exceptions brought into private
court cases, and as a result, the need for ASTP/ONC to ensure that the Exceptions utilize terms that can
be interpreted on an objective vs subjective basis is even more important.

Different approaches to system design can mean that what is analogous in one is not in another.

As an example, ASTP/ONC says, “For example, this condition would not be available to an actor when a
requestor requests API access with certain features, such as “write” capabilities or filtering functionality,
that the actor provides to other entities.” We are concerned that this example is misleading. Some write
APIs or some filters might be analogous to other write APIs or filters, but this example seems to imply
that those are categories that are always analogous, which they are not.

We suggest the previous wording of “same” be retained.

RE: Changing the “substantial number” element to “any,” and excluding from application of the
condition any situation where the actor offers at least one individual or entity the requested access,
exchange, or use.

When the Manner Exception Exhausted portion of the Infeasibility Exception was proposed, it was
associated with use cases such as piloting new technology or custom development without guardrails
necessary for widespread use. However, if completing a pilot or custom development leaves it open for
others to claim that their use case is analogous, it hinders or discourages innovation and seems to
undermine the proposal.

RE: Removing the § 171.204(a)(4) Manner Exception Exhausted condition if public comments indicate
that proposed revisions would not be sufficient to eliminate or substantially mitigate the misuse or
abuse of the condition.

We recommend retaining the Manner Exception Exhausted condition of the infeasibility exception. It
provides important regulatory certainty and ensures that actors can allocate reasonable resources
toward interoperable, standards-based manners and prevents requesters from attempting to force use
of non-standard or non-scalable solutions simply by refusing to accept access, exchange, or use of EHI in
any other manner. However, we do believe that this condition and the Infeasibility exception both can
be improved.

First, we favor more clarity. As noted above, ASTP/ONC should offer clarity on what it means to
negotiate, with examples. Additionally, and more importantly, the timing of the responding to requests
condition of the Infeasibility Exception needs to be modified. For this specific condition (Manner
Exception Exhausted) an actor should have at least 10 business days from the completion of the
negotiation, rather than from the receipt of the request. It is unlikely that most requests can be
appropriately identified, scoped to original manner requested, negotiated on original manner

                                                                                                          14

requested, and then negotiated on the three alternative manners within 10 business days. For smaller
vendors and larger vendors alike, there are too many requests received on a daily basis to have
adequate time to scope and work each with negotiation on multiple manners of provision, and
ASTP/ONC’s thinking also ignores how much of the requirement hinges on quick reasonable responses
from the requestor while the actor simply waits for response.

The timing within responding to requests condition should be updated for multiple conditions of the
Infeasibility Exception, including the Manner Exception Exhausted condition.

Manner Exception

RE: Incorporating in regulation text and explicitly state that any contract or agreement related to the
access, exchange, or use of EHI: (1) must be at market rate; (2) must not be a contract of adhesion;
and (3) must not contain unconscionable terms.

We agree with ASTP/ONC’s observation that the Manner Exception is complicated. However, we do not
agree that the areas ASTP/ONC attempts to clarify as “plain language” were clear to industry
stakeholders in the past, and we consider ASTP/ONC’s assumption that some stakeholders are “abusing”
the Exception unnecessarily contentious. We appreciate ASTP/ONC offering additional clarity here and
assert that even further clarity is needed.

In our discussion, it was difficult for EHR Association members to separate other aspects of an
agreement from the technical manner of exchange, and members providing examples of their own
experiences in fact did not always interpret what that would mean consistently within the current
construct. ASTP/ONC will need to provide further clarification on what is intended.

Even when members had consensus on an example of an aspect of an agreement that might be
separate from the technical manner of exchange, it was concerning how limiting this clarification might
be. For example, members discussed an example where an agreement contained both (1) a technical
method of exchange of a custom integration, (2) a joint marketing plan for the integration, and (3) a
non-market rate. If the two parties can come to agreement (1), (2), and (3), it seems the Manner
Exception should be available.

RE: Defining “market rate” in § 171.102 as “fair market value” per Stark Law regulations.

EHR Association members found this provision deeply concerning and believe it requires revision.

When actors need to pursue a fair market valuation per the Stark Law, they often engage a professional
healthcare valuation firm — there is an entire industry that performs Stark fair market valuations.
Pursuing such valuations for every possible product or module would be both prohibitively time-
consuming and expensive. Members expressed limited experience with this type of assessment to
provide estimates, but the sense was that it would take at least weeks and significant expense to
achieve a valuation. That is an unacceptable obstacle to progress.

Additionally, in the Manner Requested Condition, custom work may be contemplated for which it is not
possible to determine a market rate or in an arms-length transaction.

The effort and cost required to pursue numerous fair market valuations was also not estimated by ONC
in this regulation in any type of Economic Impact effort.

RE: Defining “contract of adhesion” as a contract provided on a standardized form

Using other information blocking Exceptions prioritizes consistency and objectivity with respect to terms
and fees. There is a perceived conflict here between ONC’s other expectations and this proposed
revision. In particular, other provisions, such as those regarding certified API pricing, require public
posting of consistent terms and pricing, whereas this provision appears to expect that those terms and
prices could always be considered negotiable and should not be on a standard form.

This provision also sets an expectation of a certain amount of bargaining. The precise amount of
bargaining that is expected is unclear. No costs for this new expectation are estimated in ASTP/ONC’s
burden estimates of this provision. That burden needs to be calculated, including anticipated use of the
manner exception, expected bargaining effort, and more. We believe it would be significant.

RE: Defining “general market value.”

The proposed definition of “general market value” has the same flaws as the other proposals.

While we are scrupulous as a trade association not to discuss specific pricing approaches of our member
companies, we are aware of the tremendous variety in the industry's approaches and struggle to see
how the proposed definitions would suit the current market. Using a market value framework is
preferable to a more universal application of the Fees and Licensing Exceptions, as it allows actors to
engage in custom exchanges at EHI under mutually agreeable terms. In many cases a cost-plus
arrangement may not be commercially reasonable or economically feasible and could serve as a
disincentive to innovative arrangements.

RE: Removing paragraph (a)(2) from the Manner Requested Condition of the Manner Exception and
applying the conditions of both the Fees Exception (§ 171.302) and the Licensing Exception (§171.303)

We understood that the Manner Requested Condition of the Manner Exception was intended to permit
more flexible terms and fees for potentially custom or non-standard work than the Fees and Licensing
Exceptions, as long as it was agreeable to both parties. When the parties could not reach agreement,
standards-based alternatives are included in the Manner Exception, along with adherence to the other
exceptions.

Based on these concerns, we oppose this proposal because it would, we believe, actually discourage
innovation. Requests for unique and custom exchanges of EHI will often require a custom pricing
arrangement. Flexibility in pricing and licensing is essential to allow parties to come to a mutually
agreeable arrangement as to how to facilitate that unique requested manner exchange of EHI. Forcing
actors to move away from that free market bargaining in order to comply with the Fees and Licensing
Exceptions will hamstring innovative arrangements and ultimately limit the ways in which EHI is
exchanged, which would be counter to ASTP/ONC’s and the Trump Administration’s policy goals

This approach also fails to incite the use of standards-based approaches in the way the Manner
Exception was originally intended. For example, we understood the intention of the Manner Exception

was to encourage standards as illustrated in the following example: a healthcare provider requests a
custom integration from its EHR developer. The EHR developer responds, outlining the cost and
timeframe for the custom integration. The healthcare provider feels the cost is too much or the
timeframe is too long; the parties are not coming to an agreement on the manner requested. The
alternative manners presented, which are standards-based, require offering costs that comply with the
Fees Exception, making the cost more agreeable to the healthcare provider. The developer is pleased to
satisfy the provider with a standards-based integration that is likely reusable rather than custom.

TEFCA Exception

We support TEFCA and incentives to participate. We have never seen the TEFCA Manner Exception as
offering an effective incentive to join. Rather, as HTI-6 and other rulemaking are considered, we
encourage consideration of a different TEFCA incentive that provides an effective safe harbor for good-
faith participation, and in the meantime, we support this proposal.

Burden Estimates

The information blocking section of ASTP/ONC’s ruling is not deregulatory. In fact, it proposes new
requirements for the industry that would have associated costs. However, no estimates of the additional
requirements introduced by the information blocking provisions were provided. The information
blocking provisions will also add compliance burdens not only for the covered actors but for those who
would in turn be interacting with actors and affected, inadvertently, by those compliance requirements.
The expectation of negotiation of all Manner Exception uses is an example. Small businesses are likely to
be disproportionately affected by such negotiations. We expect that an accurate estimate of the cost of
this proposal would include estimated numbers of actors, usage of this exception, time estimates for
required negotiation, and the rates of the staff or external counsel involved in such negotiation.

The expectation of using fair market values per the Stark exception could necessitate actors needing to
get fair market value opinions for every product/module offer, which would be time-consuming,
expensive, and yet was not estimated by ONC. We expect an accurate estimate of the economic impact
of this proposal would need to include an estimated numbers of actors, usage of the referenced
exception, and cost estimates for fair market valuations. For example, novel uses of AI could be slowed
by the need to complete such evaluations.

Ultimately, ASTP/ONC should correctly categorize their information blocking policy changes as not
deregulatory, and they should not finalize the information blocking proposals without an accurate
estimation of the burden on the industry of their policy changes. All of the information blocking policy
proposals need analysis and estimation (not just the two egregious examples we have highlighted) in a
revised proposal.
