On behalf of the Connecting for Better Health coalition, we write in response to the Assistant
Secretary for Technology Policy (ASTP)/Office of the National Coordinator for Health
Information Technology (ONC) proposed rule “ASTP/ONC Deregulatory Actions to Unleash
Prosperity (HTI-5)” published on December 29, 2025. We are generally supportive of the
proposals to promote interoperability and appreciate the opportunity to provide comments and
recommendations.

Connecting for Better Health (C4BH) is a coalition of providers, patient advocates, caregivers,
health plans, technology innovators, and community-based organizations dedicated to
advancing health and social data sharing to improve the health of all Californians. Our vision is
that every Californian and their care team will have the information and insights they need to
make care seamless, high-quality, and affordable. Advancing interoperability through
high-quality certified health information technology, and supported by a robust framework of
information blocking regulations, is essential to improving data accessibility and supporting care
for the whole person.

Informed by the broad-based expertise of our coalition, C4BH respectfully provides the
following comments to ASTP/ONC:

Certification Criteria for Health IT
C4BH supports ASTP/ONC’s goal of promoting simplification and efficiency within the ONC
Health IT Certification Program. We recognize the value of promoting regulatory efficiency to
modernize federal oversight and encourage innovation.

At the same time, we continue to emphasize the importance of certification as a shared
foundation relied upon by stakeholders across the health care ecosystem to enable

interoperability, trust, and consistency in real-world implementation. As certification
requirements are streamlined, it will be critical to ensure that existing capabilities within
certified health IT are maintained. Many organizations continue to rely on established standards
for current workflows, and preserving these legacy standards will help avoid disruptions. We
encourage ASTP/ONC to monitor maintenance of certified health IT during this transition to

We also note the importance of the Certification Program supporting functionality in practice to
advance interoperability. There is opportunity through simplification of overly prescriptive
technical mandates to focus on the advancement of operational performance of certified health
IT, which remains a challenge to date. For example, ASTP/ONC could explore areas of
coordination with other regulatory drivers, such as through HIPAA security measures and
information blocking regulations.

To support ASTP/ONC’s goals of reducing burden and promoting interoperability through the
Certification Program, we offer the following considerations:
●​ Shifting accountability to the private sector: While we support the revisions and
reductions in certification criteria, the need for aligned standards continues. Shifting
accountability to purchasers and users of certified health IT risks increasing
fragmentation and inconsistency across the ecosystem. Small providers and
community-based organizations often lack the resources or technical expertise to
independently validate health IT, security practices, or the behavior of AI-enabled
systems during procurement and implementation. Reduced federal standards may
increase cost and risk for these entities while not fully realizing gains in efficiency or
innovation, such as by increasing contract negotiations, custom integrations,
procurement diligence, and auditing and monitoring. States may need to play an
increased role in certification and maintenance to assist in consistency across systems
given the reduction in federal certification criteria.

●​ Emergency access functionality: We recommend ASTP/ONC carefully review the
proposal to remove the emergency access criterion (§170.315(d)(6)), which supports
continuity of care during clinical emergencies and contingencies and remains an
important safeguard for patient safety. Further examination will be critical to fully
understanding the operational, clinical, and patient safety implications of this change,
particularly for organizations that rely on certified functionality to maintain access
during system outages, public health emergencies, or other unforeseen disruptions.

●​ Transparency for decision support and artificial intelligence (AI): Consistent with C4BH’s
prior comments submitted in June 2023 supporting the updates in the HTI-1 proposed
rule to certification criteria and the information blocking rule, we emphasize the
continued need for transparency and accountability in decision support and AI-enabled
systems. Open source attributes and risk management details are critical for purchasers
to safely and effectively implement AI tools. Eliminating the transparency provisions in
the decision support interventions criterion (§ 170.315(b)(11)) may disproportionately
impact organizations without the capacity to independently evaluate these tools if
alternative AI transparency measures are not available.

FHIR-based APIs
C4BH supports the use of FHIR-based APIs in service of greater data interoperability. We agree
with ASTP/ONC’s assessment that FHIR supports easier scalability to enable real-time data
exchange, while noting that widespread adoption remains limited. Certification and
interoperability efforts should continue to focus on effective adoption of FHIR-based solutions
in practice to support reliable, secure, and timely exchange that meaningfully advances care
delivery.

To advance FHIR and API adoption, industry initiatives also play a critical role to achieve success
in health and social data sharing. C4BH is supporting the final mile of interoperability through
C4BH’s Community Sandbox®, which serves as a publicly available testing environment for
organizations to identify, test, and validate data exchange implementation strategies. The
Community Sandbox is an innovative tool used by over 350 organizations to pursue data sharing
approaches reflective of current organization capabilities and grounded in regulatory
requirements and best practices, allowing for real-world data sharing approaches with
meaningful cross-sector collaboration.

Information Blocking
C4BH believes secure and appropriate data exchange reinforced by information blocking
regulations and effective enforcement can promote timely access to health information while
respecting individual privacy. We support ASTP’s efforts to refine and strengthen information
blocking regulations to provide clear guidance to regulated entities and create a basis for
enforcement. Oversight and transparency will be critical to build a common understanding and
trust among providers, health IT developers, and additional stakeholders to make meaningful
interoperability gains for improvements in access, quality, and delivery of whole person care.

We generally support ASTP/ONC’s proposals to refine information blocking rules and offer the
following considerations to support implementation:
●​ Advancing granular data segmentation and consent to reduce information blocking:
C4BH emphasizes that advancing granular consent and data segmentation capabilities
are necessary to meaningfully address information blocking. When a patient’s record
includes information subject to heightened privacy protections or only certain details
have been consented to share, the absence of reliable data segmentation tools often
lead organizations to withhold entire records to avoid risk and respect privacy. This
cautious approach can unintentionally result in information blocking behaviors, even
when much of the requested information could be lawfully shared. The industry is
making advancements in granular data exchange capabilities, and we encourage
ASTP/ONC to support adoption of FHIR-based APIs and explore additional opportunities
to support granular data exchange as an essential strategy to combat information
blocking by addressing barriers to appropriate data access.

●​ Concern for exclusions of all contracts of adhesion: With respect to the proposal to
revise the “manner requested” condition of the Manner Exception, C4BH has concerns
about excluding all contracts of adhesion from this condition. As drafted, this approach
could inadvertently encompass standardized participation agreements commonly used
by health information exchange organizations, networks, and frameworks, which are
foundational to build trust and establish common practices. These standardized
participation agreements are not inherently indicative of inappropriate practices. We
recommend further engagement with stakeholders to clarify the scope of this condition
to ensure revisions target predatory practices without unintentionally affecting
standardized agreements that facilitate lawful and efficient data exchange.

●​ Increasing transparency for information blocking: C4BH believes that greater
transparency and accountability for information blocking would ultimately strengthen
trust and participation in data exchange, advancing interoperability and improving
patient care across our health care system. For example, one of the barriers to current
provider engagement in reporting information blocking is a lack of visibility into what
happens after a complaint is submitted, leaving providers feeling uncertain about
whether their efforts will lead to meaningful change. We are encouraged by ASTP/ONC
publishing data on the information blocking claims they have received thus far and
welcome seeing information published on actors who have been found by the Office of
Inspector General (OIG) to have committed information blocking. To increase
transparency, ASTP/ONC could consider mechanisms for providers to “opt in” to add

●​ AI transparency and risk management remains essential: C4BH supports the inclusion
of AI and automated systems in the access, exchange, and use of electronic health
information to account for health IT modernization and innovation. As automation
becomes increasingly embedded in systems and workflows, it is essential that
transparency and risk management are present to help maintain trust in data exchange,
safeguard patient safety, and support effective deployment. Adoption of industry
consensus standards can play a critical role in advancing these objectives and promoting
consistent, accountable implementation across stakeholders.

●​ Clarification of HIPAA provisions to support data access and exchange: C4BH
emphasizes that clarified guidance regarding HIPAA permitted purposes and covered
entities would lay the groundwork to enhance trusted data exchange. Variability in
HIPAA interpretations has contributed to persistent silos, barriers to exchange, and
uncertainty among data sharing partners. For example, differing views on whether
certain care coordination activities fall within the treatment permitted purpose can
result in inconsistent disclosure practices and hesitation to share information
appropriately. Greater clarity would promote confidence among stakeholders, support
lawful and timely exchange, and reduce compliance driven overcorrection that may limit
access unnecessarily. C4BH stands ready to collaborate with ASTP/ONC in advancing
these discussions and to continue efforts to build trust, drive consensus, and ensure that
appropriate information is accessible.
