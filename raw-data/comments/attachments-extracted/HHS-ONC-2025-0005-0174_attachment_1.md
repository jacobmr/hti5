USAging applauds ASTP/ONC for the proposed actions to reduce burden on health
IT developers by streamlining the voluntary Health IT Certification Program and
appreciates the opportunity to provide comments. However, USAging cautions ASTP
against removing the Certification Criteria for Direct Project § 170.315(h)(1)
and Direct Project, Edge Protocol, and XDR/XDM § 170.315(h)(2).

About USAging

USAging represents and supports the national network of 600+ Area Agencies on
Aging (AAAs) and advocates for the 280+ Title VI Native American Aging Programs,
which help older adults, people with disabilities and family caregivers live with
optimal health, well-being, independence and dignity in their homes and
communities. For 50 years, AAAs have served as the local leaders on aging by
planning, developing, funding and implementing local systems of coordinated home
and community-based services that enable older adults to age well at home and in
the community. AAAs develop area plans on aging then lead local networks of
providers to deliver these person-centered services to older adults and caregivers.

Direct Secure Messaging

USAging is concerned with ASTP’s suggestion to revise and remove Direct Project
and related criteria from Electronic Health Records (EHR) Certification. Direct is a
widely adopted, standards-based interface for secure exchange of information, but
there is no alternative to Direct available at scale today. Direct Secure Messaging
functions as a secure layer between systems, allowing electronic health information
to be shared between health and social care entities alike.

While we understand that the Direct transport standard would remain, USAging is
concerned that health IT developers would no longer be required to
demonstrate certified conformance with Direct protocols under the

USAging is also the home of the Aging and Disability Business Institute, which has
been a leader in the building of business capacity for health care contracting for
AAAs and other community-based organizations (CBOs) nationwide. Through these
various efforts, USAging regularly connects with AAAs and other CBOs that are
participating in exchanging information and referrals with health care partners.
Overall, removing certification for Direct may create new barriers for health care
providers and CBOs that exchange information regularly.

According to the most recent CBO–health care contracting survey administered by
the Aging and Disability Business Institute, it is estimated that 35 percent of CBOs
are sending and receiving data with health care partners via encrypted email
exchange.1

Many AAAs and CBOs utilize Direct to send/receive referrals and submit, report and
access patient health data. In some cases, this is the only way that these providers
can share data with each other. As business associates or covered entities of health
care partners, and with growing focus on aligning health and community-based care
nationally to address upstream drivers of health, AAAs and other CBOs need to
maintain Direct requirements as a trusted source of crucial health information.

If health IT developers are no longer required to demonstrate certified
conformance with Direct protocols, this may jeopardize AAA and CBO
access to Direct via protected email clients and/or EHR platforms. In the
absence of Direct, workflows default to manual sharing of data on referrals,
documentation and other care planning elements between providers.

AAAs and CBOs are at risk of bearing undue burden for securely sharing protected
health information if health IT partners are no longer incentivized to utilize Direct.
AAAs and CBOs have not received the federal IT investments that the health care
sector has, so losing access to this interface would be a major impediment to their
role in supporting better health outcomes for Americans.

Reverting to manual referral sharing, which would be their only immediate option,
can cause increased workloads for providers and CBOs having to track down
incomplete or inaccurate information. Many AAAs and CBOs report having to
document in multiple disparate systems to coordinate health and community care
information and disincentivizing Direct worsens this divide.

In conclusion, USAging urges ASTP/ONC not to remove Direct certification
requirements. Our members exchange crucial health information with health care
partners every day and removing this element may lead to increased workload for
