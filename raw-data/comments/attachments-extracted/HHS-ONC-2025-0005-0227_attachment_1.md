HHS’s Office of the National Coordinator for Health Information Technology (ONC); U.S. Core

Dear Secretary Kennedy and Administrator Oz:

Ibis Reproductive Health submits the following comment in response to the proposed rule “Health Data,
Technology, and Interoperability: ASTP/ONC Deregulatory Actions To Unleash Prosperity.” We
strongly oppose the proposed rule and urge its withdrawal.

Ibis Reproductive Health as a 501c3 nonprofit organization works to advance rigorous health research
that promotes reproductive and sexual safety, autonomy and choice worldwide. The rule proposed,
“Health Data, Technology, and Interoperability: ASTP/ONC Deregulatory Actions To Unleash
Prosperity,” if implemented, would have severe negative consequences for continuity of ongoing health
research led by our institution as well as many others, and would disproportionately affect and widen the
sexual, reproductive and family planning health disparity gaps of the marginalized groups to whom Ibis
Reproductive Health’s research, programming and community-partners attend.

This rule will exacerbate barriers transgender people already face to accessing care by removing over half
(34 of 60) and revising another seven fields from these standards – field that are essential to high quality
care and research for ALL people, regardless of gender. The fields that are proposed for removal or
revision are of particular importance to the reproductive and sexual safety, respect for autonomy and
choice of LGBTQIA+ individuals. This rule includes name to use, gender identity, pronouns, and sex
parameters for clinical use. All fields are vital to transgender, gender diverse, and intersex (TGDI) people
to ensure accurate, high-quality and affirming healthcare.

Although the proposed rule argues that the removal or revised fields would improve the efficiency and
quality of care patients receive, we strongly disagree. These fields are important for several reasons core
to the provision of high-quality, patient-centered care: comprehensive understanding, improved clinical
care, reduction of bias, research quality, and increased participation. First, these fields enable providers to
have a comprehensive understanding of their patients. These fields are important for clinical encounters
as they have helped develop a pragmatic frame of reference for providers to structure their clinical
questions, thus improving efficiency.
Second, these fields improve clinical care. By having access to these fields clinical care is improved by
offering more tailored recommendations based on known health challenges and disparities that have been
researched within their patient’s demographic group to support preventative care measures.

Third, the inclusion of these fields reduces bias. The inclusion of these fields helps to produce public
health education materials that are reflective of the identities found within the general population, thus
helping build prosocial behavior and awareness amongst localized communities.

Fourth, these fields enable high-quality research. The inclusion of these fields enables more diverse data
sets with the ability to refine conclusions that help to advance reproductive medicine for everyone.

And finally, inclusion of these fields increase participation in research and general wellness initiatives.
When people see their identities reflected in health research and dialogue they are more willing to trust
and follow the guidance produced from new research. When people see their identities reflected they are
also more likely to seek care earlier as they are less fearful of poor treatment, bias or harm that they may
be subject to when engaging with scientific and medical settings.

We support our clinical and scientific colleagues to commit to approaching gender and sex with accuracy
and thoughtfulness, thereby advancing our collective understanding of the multifaceted ways in which
gender and sex shape health experiences and outcomes. Doing so will enable more research opportunities,
policies, and practices that promote health outcomes for all.

For more detail on the harms of failing to comprehensively and accurately collect the data fields at issue
in this proposed rule, please see our commentary published in the leading Obstetrics and Gynecology
journal in the United States: Moseson et al, The Imperative for Transgender and Gender Nonbinary
Inclusion: Beyond Women’s Health, Obstetrics and Gynecology, May 2020.

We once again urge ONC to withdraw this proposed rule in its entirety.

For further information, please contact Ibis Reproductive Health at info@ibisreproductivehealth.org.
