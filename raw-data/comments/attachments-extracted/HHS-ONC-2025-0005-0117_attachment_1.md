                             Re: Health Data, Technology, and Interoperability:​
                          ASTP/ONC Deregulatory Actions to Unleash Prosperity​
                             Federal Register Citation: 90 Fed. Reg. 2025-23896​
                                                  [RIN-AA09]

LGBT Tech and The Cancer Network, together with the 52 additional undersigned organizations,
respectfully submit these comments in response to the Office of the Assistant Secretary for Technology
Policy / Office of the National Coordinator for Health Information Technology (ASTP/ONC) request for
input on proposed deregulatory actions related to health data technology and interoperability. Specifically,
we respond to the proposal to remove 34 of 60 structured data elements from the U.S. Core Data for
Interoperability (USCDI), including fields for chosen name, pronouns, sexual orientation, gender identity,
and sex parameters for clinical use.

For LGBTQI+ individuals, thoughtful health data governance is a matter of safety and dignity. As federal
agencies consider deregulatory approaches to health data interoperability, it is essential that efficiency
and innovation not come at the expense of privacy protections, nondiscrimination safeguards, and patient
autonomy.

We strongly oppose the proposed removal of these structured data elements.

USCDI is foundational digital health infrastructure. It sets a national floor for the health information that
certified health IT must be able to exchange, creating a framework through which information can follow
patients across time, providers, and clinical settings. Weakening the minimum dataset required for this
interoperability would undermine accuracy and continuity of care for LGBTQI+ patients, while worsening
the broader digital infrastructure that millions of patients rely on during their most complex and
vulnerable medical moments.

                                                       —

INTEROPERABILITY AS PROTECTION

The core purpose of uniform data standards is to ensure that electronic health records (EHRs) can
consistently collect, transmit, and receive critical patient information across systems. Prior to modern
interoperability, incompatible systems routinely resulted in lost or incomplete clinical information and
caused significant administrative burdens as a result, harming patients and providers. USCDI was
designed to prevent that fragmentation by establishing a standardized floor of data elements that must
be exchangeable across certified health IT.
 ​
The proposed rollback would remove more than half of the required structured elements, including
chosen name, pronouns, sexual orientation, gender identity, and sex parameters for clinical use. When
fields are removed from the baseline, the result is not a more streamlined process; rather, the result is
variability. Some systems may be required to continue collecting this information, others are likely to
collect it inconsistently, and others still to discontinue its collection altogether. This inconsistency
reintroduces the fragmentation that interoperability standards were designed to eliminate.

                                                         —

CLINICAL CONSEQUENCES FOR LGBTQI+ CARE

The proposed removal of structured sexual orientation and gender identity (SOGI) elements increases the
likelihood of incomplete records and avoidable errors during care transitions. These risks are especially
acute when patients change providers, access emergency care, cross state lines, or rely on telehealth and
referral networks–all things that LGBTQI+ people report are becoming increasingly necessary for
culturally competent care.

For example, sex parameters for clinical use are medically necessary in contexts ranging from laboratory
references, to medication dosing, to preventive care guidelines, including for intersex individuals whose
anatomy, hormone profiles, or chromosomal variations may not align neatly with binary sex
classifications. Chosen name and pronoun fields also have practical implications for care delivery and
patient identification, such as reducing misidentification in emergency settings, preventing misgendering
during moments of triage or intake, and supporting accurate matching of records across systems.
Consistent, structured documentation supports accurate matching of records and facilitates the building
of rapport when medical professionals begin work with new patients.

All of these are important considerations for meaningful patient access to healthcare–and meaningful
patient access in turn impacts many critical patient outcomes. It can inform the prognosis of many
treatable conditions; after all, it is harder to treat conditions without reliable access to care. It also
increases utilization of preventative services and reduces utilization of emergency room services. These
considerations do not just impact patient well-being; they impact treatment efficacy and health costs as
well. It is in the interest of patients, providers, and health systems to preserve this infrastructure.

While all patients benefit from accurate and standardized demographic and clinical data, LGBTQI+
patients are more likely to experience care disruption and provider switching due to discrimination,
geographic mobility, or loss of access to affirming care. For these patients, continuity and accuracy of
structured health information are especially critical.

​
HEALTH EQUITY THROUGH DATA INCLUSION

Structured data elements are foundational to accountability in modern digital systems. Standardized
collection and exchange enable the identification of disparities and the design of life-saving, targeted
interventions.

This matters in areas such as preventive care gaps, mental health disparities, substance use trends,
cancer prognosis and outcomes, HIV prevention and treatment, and other domains where improved
measurement strengthens care delivery and resource allocation. Removing SOGI and related structured
fields from the minimum interoperability standard does not eliminate LGBTQI+ patients or their healthcare
needs; it eliminates the ability to measure and address inequities using data-driven approaches.

LGBTQI+ and identity-related data have long suffered from exclusion in systems that impact the
community, including healthcare. Once inequities are embedded in technical standards, they become far
more difficult to correct.

Interoperability and equity are therefore interdependent. A shrinking baseline dataset reduces the health
system’s capacity to identify disparities and respond effectively.

                                                      —

USCDI is foundational infrastructure for the nation’s digital health ecosystem, one that protects patients in
an increasingly digital world. The undersigned organizations support thoughtful modernization of health
data systems that improves care delivery and patient empowerment. However, regulations that weaken
privacy, consent, and accountability will undermine trust, restrict access to care, and disproportionately
harm LGBTQI+ patients. ASTP/ONC has an opportunity and a responsibility to ensure interoperability and
innovation go hand in hand with access.

For these reasons, LGBT Tech, The Cancer Network, and the undersigned organizations respectfully urge
ONC to maintain structured USCDI data elements for chosen name, pronouns, sexual orientation, gender
identity, and sex parameters for clinical use; and withdraw the proposed removals that would reduce the
USCDI baseline and undermine reliable interoperability. Thank you for the opportunity to submit these
comments.
