Health Data, Technology, and Interoperability: ASTP/ONC Deregulatory Actions to Unleash Prosperity Rule (Dec 29, 2025)
RIN 0955-AA09

As a public health system serving many medically vulnerable patients, our primary concern is simple: any regulatory change
must protect patient safety and ensure reliable access to accurate health information at the point of care.

Modernization is appropriate and necessary. However, reducing requirements should not weaken the safeguards that
patients depend on, often without realizing they are there.

1.  Reduction of Certification Requirements

    We support removing outdated technical requirements, especially those tied to older document-based exchange
    methods that are being replaced by more modern standards. Simplifying these areas can reduce unnecessary
    administrative burden and allow healthcare organizations (HCO’s) to focus resources on more effective technologies.

    At the same time, some of the proposed removals relate to care coordination, privacy protections, and clinical data
    review. These functions matter because clinicians rely on consistent, predictable system behavior when reviewing
    medications, allergies, lab results, and outside records. If technical standards vary too widely between systems, it
    increases the risk of missing or incomplete information.

    For patients, that could mean delays in care, duplicated testing, or clinical errors. Any reduction in requirements should
    preserve the core functions that support safe and accurate decision-making.

2.  Shift to FHIR-Based Interoperability
    We support moving toward FHIR-based APIs as the national standard. This allows health systems to exchange data in a
    more flexible and modern way. It can improve how quickly information moves between providers, which benefits
    patients who receive care in multiple locations.

    As automation and AI tools become more common, it is important to ensure that data remains accurate, traceable,
    and understandable. Technology should assist clinicians — not replace their judgment. Clear expectations around
    tracking where data comes from and how it is used help prevent errors from spreading through automated systems.

    Modern tools are valuable, but they must operate within a framework that protects patient safety.

3.  Information Blocking Updates
    Clarifying that automated systems and AI tools fall under “access” and “use” reflects current reality and supports
    appropriate data sharing. Patients benefit when information can move efficiently and appropriately between systems.
    However, HCO’s must also maintain the ability to protect their systems from misuse or cyber threats. Excessive or
    poorly managed automated access can create security risks and/or strain systems that support direct patient care.
    Balanced guardrails are necessary so that expanded access does not compromise stability or safety.

         Protecting both access and system integrity ultimately protects patients.

    4.  Removal of Privacy and Security Certification Requirements
        The proposal would remove several privacy and security requirements from the certification program, including
        standards related to authentication, encryption, and audit logging. While HIPAA still applies, certification standards
        have historically ensured that vendors consistently build certain protections into their products.

                 From a patient perspective, these safeguards are foundational. Audit logs help detect inappropriate access. Strong
                 authentication prevents unauthorized entry. Encryption protects sensitive information. These are not technical
                 formalities — they directly support patient privacy and trust.

                 Reducing duplication is reasonable. However, maintaining a consistent baseline for security functions helps ensure
                 that all certified systems meet minimum expectations that protect patients.

4.  Updates to USCDI Data Elements

         Standardized data definitions help ensure that when information is exchanged, it means the same thing across
         systems. Stability in these definitions supports accurate reporting, care planning, and regulatory compliance.
         Changes to demographic data elements should be clear to avoid confusion or unintended gaps. Consistency
         reduces errors and supports individualized patient care.

5.  Public Health Reporting Simplification

         Reducing outdated reporting mechanisms may lower burden. However, public health reporting plays a critical role
         in detecting disease outbreaks, tracking antimicrobial resistance, and responding to emerging threats.
         If reporting requirements are simplified, it is important that reliability and completeness are preserved. Public
         health readiness is directly tied to patient safety, particularly in community-based and safety-net systems.

In conclusion, modernization and the move toward more flexible, FHIR-based interoperability is supported. At the same
time, patient safety must remain the guiding principle.

Regulatory simplification should reduce unnecessary burden without removing the technical foundations that protect
privacy, ensure accurate data exchange, and support safe clinical decision-making. Innovation and patient protection are
not opposing goals; both are essential to maintaining trust in the healthcare system.
