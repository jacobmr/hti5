Dear Dr. Keane,

On behalf of the Texas Medical Association (TMA) and our more than 60,000 physician and medical
student members, we thank the Assistant Secretary for Technology Policy and the Office of the National
Coordinator (ASTP/ONC) for the opportunity to respond to the proposed regulation, Health Data,
Technology, and Interoperability: ASTP/ONC Deregulatory Actions to Unleash Prosperity (HTI-5).

TMA is a private, voluntary, non-profit association and the largest state medical society in the nation. It
was founded in 1853 to serve the people of Texas in matters of medical care, prevention and cure of
disease, and improvement of public health. Today, its vision is “improving the health of all Texans.”

TMA supports reducing unnecessary regulatory burden and promoting innovation in health information
technology. However, the burden reduction in this proposal weakens the ability of ASTP/ONC to
enforce critical baseline criteria that physicians and other users have come to expect. We are concerned
that several of the specific certification criteria proposed for removal or significant reduction under HTI-
5 play a critical role in maintaining baseline safety, reliability, and interoperability across electronic
health records (EHRs). Once certification requirements are removed, vendors, particularly new market
entrants, are no longer obligated to implement or sustain these capabilities. Over time, this risks a
fragmented EHR market in which baseline safety, usability, and exchange capabilities vary substantially
by product.

Certification has functioned not merely as a market signal but as a durable accountability framework.
HTI-5 undermines that framework without a clear replacement mechanism. For example, within the
Promoting Interoperability specifications required for the Merit-based Incentive Payment System
(MIPS), there are mandates for health IT products to be certified to specific standards. TMA is

concerned that removal of certain certification requirements may seriously impede the ability of users to

TMA offers the following comments on proposals by ASTP/ONC within HTI-5.

Clinical Decision Support
ASTP/ONC proposes the removal of clinical decision support (CDS) criteria in §170.315(a)(9). The
proposal notes the CDS criteria for EHR certification was set to expire Jan. 1, 2025. ONC states that the
expiration was based on the belief the continued evolution of decision support software, especially as it
related to artificial intelligence (AI) or machine learning-driven predictive decision support
interventions, necessitates new requirements for the CDS criteria.

TMA Response: TMA opposes the removal of CDS criteria in §170.315(a)(9) and agrees that the criteria
should be updated to reflect technology changes as related to AI and machine learning. CDS tools
support clinical judgement using discrete data about the patient helping to improve patient safety and
care quality.

Family Health History
ASTP/ONC proposes removing family health history, §170.315(a)(5), from the certification criteria
since it is expected that many developers will conform to the SNOMED CT® U.S. Edition and that it
will remain part of certified health IT. Additionally, USCDI v6 includes Family Health History that
developers can voluntarily adopt before it is required.

TMA Response
TMA urges ASTP/ONC to retain family health history. Further, TMA cautions that ASTP/ONC’s
expectations of certified health IT developers could be incorrect. ASTP/ONC believes developers will
keep the family health history embedded in the EHR. If the family health history is lost, there could be
unintended consequences that would make these vital data elements difficult and possibly impossible to
restore. Loss of structured family history data undermines continuity of care and can have cascading
consequences over decades affecting preventive care, subspecialty referral, and interpretation of future
diagnostic findings

Care Coordination and Reconciliation Requirements
HTI-5 proposes the elimination of several care coordination criteria that directly affect the reliability of
exchanged clinical data, including:

• Clinical information reconciliation and incorporation §170.315(b)(2)
• Care plan §170.315(b)(9)
• Application access – Patient selection §170.315(g)(7)
• Application access – All data request §170.315(g)(9)

ASTP/ONC surmises that other regulations maintain these requirements and that by removing it from
certification it not only reduced health IT developer burden, but it allows developers an opportunity to
innovate beyond current care coordination requirements.

TMA Response
TMA is concerned with the elimination of these criteria. The removal of clinical information
reconciliation and incorporation is particularly alarming. This criterion establishes a standardized
expectation that receiving systems can reconcile problems, medications, and allergies from external
sources. Without it, EHRs may technically receive data but lack consistent reconciliation workflows,
increasing the likelihood of silent data loss or duplication during transitions of care. In real-world
clinical environments, especially emergency departments and inpatient settings, clinicians reasonably
assume that externally sourced data have undergone some level of reconciliation. When that assumption
is no longer valid, patient safety is compromised.

Decision Support Interventions
HTI-5 proposes revising decision support interventions §170.315(b)(11) among other things, major
changes include removing:
• Enablement of interventions when a patient’s medications, allergies and intolerance, and problems
are incorporated from a transition of care or referral summary received
• Source attribution disclosure related to transparency and risk management requirements for
predictive decision support

TMA Response
ASTP/ONC should not revise decision support interventions by removing criteria important to the
quality of care delivered. While TMA recognizes concerns about regulatory parity with non-certified AI
tools, removing these requirements from certified EHRs weakens oversight precisely where physicians
expect it. Decision-support interventions should be supported by peer-reviewed clinical guidance with
evidence-based care standards leading to the best possible outcomes for patients treated. Certification
has been one of the few mechanisms distinguishing governed and credible clinical decision support from
opaque automation. Reducing these requirements shifts responsibility for assessing algorithmic risk
entirely to clinicians and health systems without transparency on how decision support is generated.

Design and Performance Certification Criteria
HIT-5 proposes removal of several design and performance criteria that underpin measurement
reliability. Proposals for removal include:
• Clinical quality measure – Filter §170.315(c)(4)
• Automated numerator recording §170.315(g)(1)
• Automated measure calculation §170.315(g)(2)

TMA Response
TMA urges ASTP/ONC to not remove design and performance criteria. TMA recognizes that other
Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services programs may indirectly drive continued support for these
criteria, but eliminating certification requirements removes a consistent technical baseline. Variability in
how quality data are captured and calculated increases the risk of misreporting, misinterpretation, and
downstream payment or compliance errors, particularly for organizations participating in multiple
federal and state programs.

Safety Enhanced Design
HTI-5 proposes the removal of safety-enhanced design §170.315(g)(3) which has a focus on human
factors and product design and usability. ASTP/ONC anticipates “the removal of this criterion would

reduce costs and burden for health IT developers and health care providers. For example, when pursuing
certification, health IT developers must recruit a minimum of ten participants, conduct the necessary
testing and submit metrics for each certification criterion for which they seek certification.”

TMA Response
TMA requests the retention of safety enhanced design criterion. The functionality and usability of health
technology is paramount to patient safety. If health IT developers are no longer required to comply with
usability metrics, the potential for usability degradation grows. Product design metrics are also essential
to physicians evaluating EHRs during the selection process. The cost and burden reduction cited only
refers to health IT developers and not to users.

Consolidated CDA Creation Performance
HTI-5 proposes removal of consolidated CDA creation performance §170.315(g)(6) which dictates the
proper conformance of consolidated clinical document architecture (C-CDAs) for transition of care and
referral summaries that are sent to and received from external organizations. ASTP/ONC justifies the
removal indicating that C-CDA based standards are still in use and that the industry is shifting toward
adoption of Fast Healthcare Interoperability Resources- (FHIR) based standards, which are designed to
enable greater interoperability and innovation.

TMA Response
TMA urges ASTP/ONC to not remove the consolidated CDA creation performance criterion. This can
be problematic when sharing patient information between treating physicians. C-CDAs are critical for
care coordination due to the standardized and interoperable format of the documents. Even as industry
shifts towards FHIR-based standards, C-CDAs should remain as a standard until FHIR-based standards
are ubiquitous, and C-CDAs are no longer used for care coordination.

Public Health Certification Criteria
HTI-5 proposes:
• Removal of transmission to cancer registries §170.315(f)(4)
• Revision of transmission to public health agencies – Electronic case reporting §170.315(f)(5)
• Revision of transmission to public health agencies – Antimicrobial use and resistance reporting
§170.315(f)(6)
• Removal transmission to public health agencies – Health care surveys §170.315(f)(7)

ASTP/ONC indicates that all public health reporting to regional health departments and to the Centers
for Disease Control and Prevention are moving to FHIR-based reporting, thus making it burdensome for
health IT developers to maintain outdated standards.

TMA Response
TMA requests that ASTP/ONC retain and refrain from revising public health certification criteria. TMA
is concerned that there will be reporting gaps as all entities modernize their technologies to conform to
FHIR-based standards when sending and receiving public health data.

Another area of concern to TMA is patient matching. ASTP/ONC could help with standardized patient-
matching elements that may increase the patient-match rate. Many organizations tout a 95% match rate,
which is not good enough. TMA welcomes an opportunity to work on this challenge with ASTP/ONC.

ASTP/ONC’s oversight and authority of certified health IT that is implemented and used for patient care
remains vital. It gives physicians and other users confidence that the system purchased and used is
secure, reliable, and interoperable, all which directly support patient care and safety as well as health
care operations. When technology is no longer reliable, users are forced to transfer to another system.
Even though regulatory actions have been taken to ease the transition of patient data when changing
EHRs, it is still burdensome and not reliable, particularly when transitioning unstructured patient
information. Switching EHRs is not a good option. Rather, it is imperative that health IT developers are
continually nudged towards excellence.
