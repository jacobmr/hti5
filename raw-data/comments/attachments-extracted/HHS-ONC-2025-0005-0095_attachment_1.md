Dear Assistant Secretary Keane,

Oncology nurses are privileged to support people with cancer through their entire cancer experience, from
diagnosis and treatment into survivorship or as they face their end-of-life journey. Today, the Oncology Nursing
Society (ONS) writes to provide feedback on ASTP/ONC’s deregulatory actions outlined in the aforementioned
proposed rule.

Public Health Certification Criteria: Transmission to Cancer Registries
ONS generally supports ASTP/ONC’s proposal to remove the “Transmission to Cancer Registries” certification
criterion (§ 170.315(f)(4)) as part of ASTP/ONC’s efforts to reduce burden while continuing to support its broader
health information technology policy goals. We recognize that the current criterion relies on a Clinical Document
Architecture (CDA)-based implementation specification and may be duplicative as cancer registry reporting
continues to evolve toward Fast Healthcare Interoperability Resources (FHIR)-based approaches.

ONS also appreciates the significant work underway to modernize cancer registry reporting, including through the
Central Cancer Registry Reporting Implementation Guide and the United States Core Data for Interoperability
(USCDI)+ Cancer initiative, which are intended to promote more efficient, harmonized, and standardized oncology
data exchange over time. We further acknowledge that reporting of cancer cases is already required under state
public health laws, and therefore removal of this certification criterion is unlikely to affect those underlying
reporting obligations.

Nevertheless, and consistent with input from the cancer registry community 1, ONS encourages ASTP/ONC to
ensure that removal of this criterion does not occur before central cancer registries have the practical ability to
receive electronic cancer data through modernized reporting approaches. As the North American Association of
Central Cancer Registries (NAACCR) has cautioned, absent adequate capacity, removing established reporting
pathways risks shifting burden to manual processes and undermining the timeliness and completeness of cancer
surveillance data. To that end, ONS urges ASTP/ONC to delay removal of this criterion until central cancer registries
demonstrate readiness to support modernized electronic reporting without disruption to cancer surveillance.

The Oncology Nursing Society (ONS) is a professional association that represents 100,000 nurses and is the
professional home to more than 35,000 members. ONS is committed to promoting excellence in oncology nursing
and the transformation of cancer care. Since 1975, ONS has provided a professional community for oncology nurses,
developed evidence-based education programs and treatment information, and advocated for patient care, all in an
effort to improve the quality of life and outcomes for patients with cancer and their families.
