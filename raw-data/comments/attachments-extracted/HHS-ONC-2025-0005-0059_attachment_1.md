Fairview Health Services (Fairview) appreciates the opportunity to provide comments on the Assistant
Secretary for Technology Policy/Office of the National Coordinator for Health Information Technology
(ASTP/ONC) Deregulatory Actions to Unleash Prosperity (HTI-5) Proposed Rule.

Fairview is a nonprofit, integrated health system providing exceptional health care across the full
spectrum of medical services through our 10 hospitals and 60 clinics. Fairview’s broad care
continuum includes academic and community hospitals, primary and specialty care clinics, senior
and long-term care facilities, retail and specialty pharmacies, pharmacy benefit management
services, rehabilitation centers, counseling, home health care services, and an integrated provider
network. Fairview’s 34,000+ employees and 5,000+ system providers embrace innovation and new
thinking to drive a healthier future through healing, discovery, and education.

Fairview values ONC’s focus on reducing administrative burden, strengthening interoperability, and
modernizing the regulatory foundation to support Fast Healthcare Interoperability Resources (FHIR)
based Application Programming Interfaces (APIs) and AI enabled data exchange. We offer the
following feedback grounded in our operational experience, technology ecosystem, and previous
comments on ONC rulemaking. Fairview appreciates ONC’s continued efforts to reduce
administrative burden by building on its action to address multiagency reporting burden, improving
coordination across Health Information Exchanges (HIEs) and Qualified Health Information Network
(QHIN) ecosystems, and eliminating workflow burdens needed to achieve better treatment-oriented
data transfer between health systems. We request that ONC designate resources to help
organizations become part of a QHIN, provide appropriate QHIN oversite, require QHIN’s to share
data effectively in a standardized way, and require EHR vendors to take that data and show it to
physicians and care givers in a way that is consistent with their workflow. This will greatly
enhance the ability for patients to navigate health systems to get optimal care with efficiency for
providers. ONC should also structure the Trusted Exchange Framework and Common Agreement
(TEFCA) network in a way that QHIN’s can help with federal and state public reporting for regulatory
purposes and share standardized quality data as the next goals for interoperability through TEFCA.
The most dangerous elements from this proposed deregulation are:
• Loss of AI transparency artifacts (model cards) leaving a large burden on health systems
with significant inefficiency and risk to determine safety and quality of AI tools
• High risk longitudinal data like family history, problem lists, and social data not having
appropriate standards for dates and clinical context causing inaccurate AI interpretations
and clinical errors due to poor quality data and potentially issues with patient care if the
information is not standard functionality
• Erosion of certified clinical decision support (CDS) to place most of the burden on provider
organizations and not EHR-vendors. Also, there is a need to ensure that providers and other
clinicians stay in the loop for designing what things can be autonomous and what things need
clinical oversight with AI guidance instead of being fully autonomous
• Transfer of AI safety accountability to providers and health systems without continued
regulatory oversite of vendor AI development through FDA and other regulatory mechanisms
• Potential abuse of individual patient data by opening access to non-provider organization
entities without HIPAA regulation and decreasing oversight
• Erosion of cybersecurity standards including removal of encryption standards for
authentication credentials and other standards which will put health systems at risk for
cybersecurity attacks and data leakage

Certification Criteria for Health Information Technology
Fairview supports ONC’s goals to reduce burden where proposals truly remove duplicated and
outdated requirements, while modernizing standards to support AI enabled interoperability.
However, Fairview cautions that deregulation must avoid unintentionally eroding core
safeguards related to:
• Enabled interoperability for clinical treatment of patients;
• Clinical decision support reliability that could lose transparency, normalization, and lack of
provider oversite without regulation;
• Transparency and traceability of automated decision support functions to ensure provider
oversite;
• Accessibility‑centered EHR design that protect patients and providers with disabilities (current
regulations are not redundant with civil rights law and should be maintained);
• Provider and health system control of medical advice coming from AI tools;
• Family history and other contextual clinical data being protected and standardized for AI use
to decrease bias and clinical misinterpretation;
• Auditability and provenance of a data set being summarized by AI; and
• System security to avoid data leakage and security breaches such as encryption of
authentication credentials, Multi-Factor Authentication, and auditable events for data access.
Fairview encourages ONC to incorporate explicit guardrails ensuring vendors maintain essential
capabilities even if certain criteria are removed. Fairview respectfully requests that ONC also publish
a crosswalk or mapping showing which removed criteria previously supported clinical safety, quality,
auditability, provider oversight of medical advice, and user transparency (e.g. CDS and audit trail
elements), and how those signals will be preserved or reestablished through updated certification
elements or vendor attestations.

Conditions and Maintenance of Certification Requirements for Health IT Developers
ASTP/ONC proposes several modifications to the Conditions and Maintenance of Certification
requirements, including conforming edits to the assurances, APIs, and attestations; descoping the
“real world testing” condition and maintenance of certification requirements with certain
deregulatory actions; and removing and descoping measures associated with the “insights” condition
and maintenance of certification requirements.

Fairview supports the ASTP/ONC efforts to remove duplicative regulatory requirements and eliminate
rules that impede innovation, while aiming to better harness capabilities that are already well-
established in health IT products on the market. However, Fairview respectfully urges ONC to:
• Retain targeted real-world testing focused on FHIR API performance and provider usability of
interfaced data, even if other testing elements are descoped;
• Transition timelines for vendor attestations and real-world testing targeting FHIR API
performance and provider usability rather than removing validation outright; and
• Avoid elimination of AI model cards or at least maintain clinical safety and trust in healthcare
by not allowing deregulation to cause the loss of essentials like auditability, provenance, and
AI-based decision support that helps providers make good medical decisions instead of trying
to replace providers.

Vendors should commit to maintaining functionality even if ONC removes the criteria, but lack of ONC
oversight and guidance is concerning.

Addressing Information Blocking
Fairview supports ONC’s proposed updates to define “access,” “use,” and “exchange” in ways that
incorporate AI-enabled workflows and automated data exchange, which is increasingly central to
modern interoperability. This clarity is essential to ensure providers can deploy innovative tools
without creating compliance uncertainty. This clarity is essential to ensure providers have the
information necessary to provide high quality care and safely incorporate AI into workflows to help
them be more efficient and deliver higher quality care.

While Fairview strongly supports clarifying definitions to include AI/automation, we respectfully ask
ONC to:
• Publish implementation guidance that decreases the chance of expanded API or clinical data
usage by people not providing treatment for patients or doing public health reporting;
• Avoid shifting safety, governance and legal exposure to health providers by not requiring
vendors to maintain AI standards; and
• Provide examples for appropriate usage of things such as:
 o Autonomous agents that should have appropriate direct oversite by healthcare
providers related to what decisions can be autonomous and what decisions require
direct provider oversite;
o Batch API workflows that focus on needed information for treatment and appropriate
HIPAA protections to avoid non-treatment related usage of private patient data;
o API workflows for public health and reporting to decrease burden and provide
standards related to that kind of data sharing;
o Appropriate provider oversite for AI-mediated patient apps; and
o Enforceable expectations that ensure reliable patient access without imposing
unfunded mandates on providers.

Practical guidance will enable providers to deploy AI/Automated access without compliance
ambiguity.

Stronger Foundation for FHIR-based APIs and Support AI-enabled Interoperability Solutions
The proposed rule enables ASTP/ONC to reset the certification program’s regulatory scope and
establish a new foundation on which to build FHIR-based API requirements. Fairview supports the
ONC strategy to reset the regulatory foundation around FHIR-based APIs and interoperability to
position the ecosystem for future rules that maximize data usability and exchange. To avoid
fragmentation, Fairview strongly recommends ONC to:
• Align FHIR certification requirements with TEFCA and QHIN expectations;
• Develop a clear, staged roadmap with consistent versioning and migration timelines, and
clear testing windows for providers and vendors allowing coordination that would ensure
national interoperability frameworks reinforce one another rather than create duplicative or
conflicting obligations; and
• Incentivize better structured data for common workflows like nursing documentation, lab
results with reference ranges, or quality metrics that would allow better FHIR-based API’s and
interoperability to promote safe AI-ready data exchange that allows better summarization of
all health data and a more complete patient focused healthcare story.
