Dear Mr. Lipinski

I appreciate the rationale expressed by ASTP/ONC in this deregulatory proposed rule to ease
the burden of the certification program on health information technology (HIT) developers, and
in many cases, the specific arguments for the removal or revision of certain of the certification
criteria and associated Conditions and Maintenance of Certification requirements.

At a general level:

• The proposals to reset the certification program to prepare for a FHIR enabled future
recognizes and affirms the direction of interoperability for addressing future use cases
and needs based on the FHIR standard.

• The reduction in burden to HIT developers who are first time participants in the
certification program stands to benefit the most from reduction in program requirements
such as Safety Enhanced Design (SED) testing, Accessibility, and the need for a Quality
Management System (QMS). The reduction in burden for certified HIT developers who
have long been in the program is much more moderate.

• There still is no real provision for addressing the needs of specialty EHR developers (e.g.
for PT/OT/SLP, and chiropractic medicine) who are seeking to enable full participation by
their clients in CMS programs such as MACRA’s Quality Payment Program but for whom
certain of the certification criteria that would even survive a final HTI-5 rule as proposed
would still be faced with throwaway development such as for Electronic Prescribing or for
certain of the data types and classes present in the USCDI. Both ASTP/ONC’s program
and the perspective CMS has in defining Certified Electronic Health Records Technology
(CEHRT) remain monolithic with no provision for the needs of certain medical specialties.

• The proposal for two compliance dates does not add much value. Even if ASTP/ONC
should finalize the HTI-5 rule by mid-2026, it does not leave appreciable lead time of
much value to “transition” to the proposed removals or revisions of criteria that are
proposed to occur by January 1, 2027. Six months (or less) is not an appreciable amount
of time, and it would be simpler to make all proposals effective as of the calendar year
start so there is a clean definition to certification program requirements effective for a
full calendar year. As it is, developers seeking certification now are left with a confused
timing for when to pursue certification based on the removal of certain program
provisions and certification criteria that contribute to the certification effort. They are left
with a choice within the program year to delay seeking certification for the potential of a
less burdensome process but must bear these same burdens if they pursue certification
prior to a final HTI-5 rule. ASTP/ONC should either engage in enforcement discretion right
now to remove the more burdensome elements relieved by their proposals to remove
this unnecessary complexity of timing for HIT developers seeking certification or defer
the matter entirely to the start of 2027. The lead time needed for CHIT developers and
implementers to be able to develop, test, certify, and implement necessary updates
under HTI-2.

In the comments to follow, I provide specific comments on certain of ASTP/ONC’s proposals.

III.A. Certification Criteria for Health Information Technology
I am supportive of the need to remove certification criteria that have run their course of utility
and benefit to the certification program. I agree with ASTP/ONC’s overall premise that a high
degree of market penetration for the use of certified HIT certified for many of the criteria
proposed for removal is a good barometer to take measure of the current state of the adoption
of certified HIT with many of the capabilities present for which criteria are proposed for removal.
However, it is not all about FHIR interoperability and USCDI. By this, I mean there still should
be assurances that certified HIT certifying to interoperability criteria should be held to the
abilities to support data capture for data classes and elements included in the USCDI using the
required vocabulary standards and value sets. This may not necessitate individual criteria for
each data class or element, but new entrants to certification should be held to the same abilities
relative to data capture and data processing as mature certified products have been.

III.A.2.e Decision Support Interventions
I am opposed to the removal of source attribute information useful to providing “model card”
information to buyers, implementers, and users of HIT certified to 170.315(b)(11) for predictive
decision support intervention capabilities. ASTP/ONC argues that this information has not proven
useful to end users as reference information as they might have thought in adopting these
requirements to begin with. I agree with the sentiment that during day-to-day end use, this
information may not have great utility at the point of clinical decision making. However, that is
not its greatest utility. It is far more valuable to the buyer, and to the implementor as informative
about the artificial intelligence capabilities present in the certified product, and the availability
of this information is incredibly useful and significant to promote transparency by the developer
with its customers. I offer similar sentiment about the removal of risk management
requirements from 170.315(b)(11). ASTP/ONC also argues that developers of non-certified HIT
do not face such regulations and there is an unlevel playing field between them and certified
HIT developers who do face such regulations. Further, these non-certified developers may also
not be subject to regulation by the Food and Drug Administration as Software as a Medical
Device (SAMD) manufacturer. I argue that what ASTP/ONC is an attempt at doing the right
thing while what is happening with other kinds of health IT AI risks being the wrong thing. It is
fine to believe that there will emerge effective self-regulation in the industry based on principles
tied to frameworks such as ISO 42001, NIST’s AI Risk Management Framework, or other
voluntary approaches, but there is no requirement that they be followed, and the market feels
very caveat emptor right now. For all the deregulatory bent of this administration, this one feels
short sighted. Until the market demands it on a more consistent basis and self-regulatory
approaches are de rigueur, it seems premature to remove the requirements from
170.315(b)(11).

III.A.4 Privacy and Security Certification Criteria
The removal of all of the certification criteria for security under 170.315(d) sounds reasonable
for the matters that ASTP/ONC cites that there are other compelling statutory and regulatory
requirements for HIT developers to meet them such as HIPAA Security and Breach Notification
requirements, but with the refocusing of the certification program on FHIR interoperability, API
development, and possibly even consumer health apps or other kinds of health apps to meet
the requirements of CMS’s Health Tech Ecosystem, the potential is there for the introduction of
new actors as HIT developers who are not subject to HIPAA as consumer products (and face
little other requirement to set a minimum bar for cybersecurity), or that may be new entrants
seeking certification to only a subset of certification criteria, the significance of having a
minimum bar for cybersecurity capabilities that align to HIPAA Security bears relevance. If the
problem is confusion induced over the scope of CMS’s Promoting Interoperability/MACRA QPP
MIPS Promoting Interoperability domain’s inclusion of a requirement to do a security risk
assessment compared to broader process required for HIPAA Security, remove the requirement
from CMS’s program. However, the significance of assurance of a minimum level of
cybersecurity has its value. The criteria already have mostly been reduced to be attestation
based for testing so much of the burden of requiring them has already been taken out of the
certification effort. The criteria should remain in play. Alternatively, I do support retaining the
audit criteria at 170.315(d)(2), (d)(3), and (d)(10).

III.A.7 Design and Performance Certification Criteria
I understand the nature of ASTP/ONC’s proposals to remove as requirements certification
criteria 170.315(g)(3)-(5) for Safety-Enhanced Design (SED), Quality Management System
(QMS), and Accessibility-Centered Design. I do feel their removal risks an unlevel playing field
between certified HIT developers of long standing and new entrants, and argue for their
retention for the following reasons:

- Certification has been taken as an assurance to buyers and users of certified HIT that
  good development practices are in place to build confidence and trust in the developer.
  While certification has never been a way to assure satisfied users, and the quality of
  certified products varies, these requirements have provided at least a minimum to
  moderate bar of developer behavior and performance. For new entrants, my concern is
  they will not be held to the same level of expectation, and especially for smaller
  developers, speed to market will push “non-functional” requirements to the margin. I
  urge retention of the QMS and Accessibility-Centered Design requirements as they
  represent minimum bars that developers should follow that should also be asked of
  developers seeking certification as a matter of accountability and good process.
   - SED testing has been particularly burdensome to developers seeking certification. It
  almost has held up developers from introducing new functions and features that would
  be part of the normal course of development between the time the developer starts
  seeking certification and its successful conclusion with what has often been about a 10-
  week lead time needed to conduct the level of SED testing currently required. Further,
  for a new entrant, it may have been difficult to find the user base needed to adequately
  conduct testing. I recommend ASTP/ONC consider moderating the burden of the SED
  testing requirement without its removal entirely to allow for a less presumptive
  assumption that the end user experience cannot change during the time between
  initiating the certification process and its conclusion or to moderate the need to report
  changes to the user interface that may occur during that time and after that do not
  represent redesign or material change.

III.A.8.a.-b. Transport Methods and Other Protocols Certification Criteria
– Direct Project and Direct Project, Edge Protocol and XDR/XDM
I have concerns about the removal of these criteria as ASTP/ONC is making a hard shift to FHIR
API based interoperability in the certification program. I understand the policy reasons behind
the shift and recognize ASTP/ONC’s desire to move away from HL7 C-CDA based
interoperability. I urge ASTP/ONC to postpone a complete removal of these certification criteria
while they remain a significant basis for interoperability both in the use of certified HIT, and in
the health technology ecosystem for the time being. It seems hasty and premature to move to
their full removal even in the face of retaining the Direct Project transport standard at
170.202(a)(2) as a certification standard albeit without reference by a criterion.

III.D. Conditions and Maintenance of Certification Requirements for
Health IT Developers
I am concerned that both Real World Testing and the Insights conditions have been effectively
gutted except for the narrow focus on API related interoperability. While the two conditions bore
risks of redundancy between them, and variability and potential incomparability between
developers for Real World Testing approaches, they do represent accountability requirements
that certified capability is more than what can be proven in a testing lab. I urge particularly for
measures related to consumer access that requirements be restored to broaden the way that
certified HIT use is measured, and that ASTP/ONC focus their requirements on one set of
requirements to eliminate redundancy, reduce burden, and standardize across developers on
approach for a publicly reportable set of measures for whatever scope is adopted.

IV. Information Blocking (Part 171)
A. “Access” and “Use” Definitions
I agree with ASTP/ONC’s proposals regarding expanding the “Access” and “Use” definitions to
consider automated or robotic processes involving the access, exchange, or use of Electronic
Health Information (EHI). I support the alternative proposal to also expand the definition of
“Exchange” to treat automated or robotic processes on par across “Access,” “Exchange” and
“Use.”
B. Infeasibility Exception Revisions and C. Manner Exception
I support ASTP/ONC’s proposed revisions to the exception conditions for both the Infeasibility
and Manner exception. I strongly support ASTP/ONC’s proposal for the Manner Exception to
require the elements of contracting for assuring sound fair arms-length agreements for being
at the market rate, not being a contract of adhesion, and not containing unconscionable terms.
I have no objection to also requiring the Licensing and Fees Exceptions for agreements reached
under the Manner Exception to achieve the same end.

Other Matters
While not specifically requested as matters for comment in the proposed HTI-5 rule, I have
several other points to make.

- I understand that CMS will have conforming changes to make to both their definition of
  CEHRT and the criteria upon which Promoting Interoperability/MACRA QPP MIPS
  Promoting Interoperability measure objectives are based. This reinforces my argument
  that certification criteria changes ASTP/ONC might adopt in a final HTI-5 rule align to the
  start of a calendar year so that any conforming changes CMS would have to make which
  align to a calendar year rhythm anyway is supported. It does little good to change the
  scope of certification in the meantime either for the sake of lead time or for the sake of
  timing the effort as I discussed earlier. If ONC wants to reduce burden for certain
  elements of the program such as any adoptions of removal of the Design and
  Performance Certification Criteria, do it now by enforcement discretion so that it is
  maximally available and removes the trickery of timing when to seek certification during
  2026 over these elements.

- The USCDI is still a monolith, and while this administration has not advanced the USCDI
  versions required for certification, my concern remains that it is a monolith. This ties back
  to my comment on certification of specialty EHRs in the first section of this letter. As I
  stated in my comment letter on the HTI-2 proposed rule, I say again. This perpetuates
  the error in my opinion that all EHI within scope of the USCDI adopted as a certification
  specification at any point in time is required for interoperability for use of these certified
  capabilities. This also perpetuates the assumption that the HIT presented for certification
  to these criteria necessarily contains all the EHI within scope of the adopted USCDI
  version used as the basis for the certification specification. The USCDI monolith continues
  to operate for core certification requirements. I urge ASTP/ONC to consider how to adopt
  more filtered response capabilities within document-based interoperability and within
  patient portal access and, how to recognize that HIT that otherwise could certify based
  on EHI appropriate to their intended use and scope could be certified with lesser scopes
  of EHI than the full scale of the USCDI.

- I urge ASTP/ONC and CMS to make clear what bar non-certified HIT developers
  participating in CMS’s Health Tech Ecosystem initiative are held to for meeting the
  requirements and prerequisites of CMS’s program. It is not certification as ASTP/ONC
  administers it. What is it?
