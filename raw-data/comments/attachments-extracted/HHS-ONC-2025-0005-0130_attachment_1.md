II. Eliminating Regulatory Burden
Vega Health strongly supports ASTP/ONC’s efforts to simplify the duplicative and
burdensome regulatory thicket that is currently holding back innovative healthcare
solutions from scaling and delivering value. Currently, the state AI law landscape is
disjointed and daunting: As of this writing, 46 states have enacted 284 bills, and
2026 has already seen 771 bills introduced across 31 states. Despite federal efforts
to limit the scope of state AI legislation, the current landscape forces innovators to
spend time navigating complex regulations.

There are two well-established pathways for AI regulation: development-based
regulation and use-based regulation. As Andreessen Horowitz, the Consumer
Technology Association (CTA), and others have argued, layering regulations on the
manner in which AI models can be developed will only serve to increase compliance
costs, make it more expensive to develop and scale AI solutions in multiple states,
and create a digital ecosystem where only entrenched market incumbents have the
scale, budget, and legal team necessary to successfully scale AI solutions. By
eliminating regulatory burden and focusing enforcement on already-unlawful
outcomes, particularly in heavily regulated sectors like healthcare, the Administration
will further incentivize innovative solutions to broken processes that can reduce costs
and improve health outcomes for patients and providers.

As Andreessen Horowitz notes in the organization’s Roadmap for Federal AI
Legislation, state and federal regulation must focus on harmful uses of AI, not
preemptively impose barriers that limit market access. Developer-facing regulations
drastically extend the technology development phase and extend the time to market

entry, making it significantly more difficult for fast-moving innovators to bring
solutions to market and challenge incumbents. A competitive health AI market drives
overall improvements to privacy, cybersecurity, and safety by forcing companies to
compete to deliver the best product – and does not preclude current or future
regulations from heavily penalizing realized harms, failures, or misuse.

This regulatory simplification is particularly important for new market entrants and
innovators. Vega Health’s business is built on the ability to scale the most effective

already validated in clinical settings around the country – to solve complex problems
for health systems of all sizes. The Vega Health marketplace hosts models
developed by clinical experts that improve clinical and operational workflows for
patients and providers. The Vega Health marketplace already includes models to
predict patient deterioration, post-surgery complications, emergency department
triage, and in-hospital mortality.

While the HTI-5 Proposed Rule does not solely address AI, it reconfigures the
regulatory foundation for health IT. By simplifying and aligning the security,
interoperability, and privacy requirements broadly underpinning health IT,
ASTP/ONC can help to lay the groundwork for widespread AI innovation,
development, and scaled adoption in healthcare settings of all sizes.

Vega Health applauds ASTP/ONC’s deliberate efforts to reduce the regulatory,
administrative, and artificial barriers that have been erected by large incumbents to
disadvantage new market entrants. This is especially important when companies are
contracted by health systems to improve outcomes by leveraging data contained
within the electronic health record. Complex and conflicting regulations governing AI,
privacy, and interoperability have been used by some certified health IT vendors to
obstruct this data access or impose undue financial burden to utilize data which
belongs to the health system and its patients.

Today, certified health IT vendors argue for safe harbors shielding them from sharing
health data as a result of a misrepresentation while also using security and
compliance arguments to deny or delay lawful interoperability requests. As
healthcare’s database layer, EHR vendors cannot have it both ways – ASTP/ONC
must establish controls to prevent certified health IT vendors from leveraging market
share to crowd out or disadvantage competitors. The HTI-5 NPRM represents a
strong step towards reclaiming federal authority, but measures like a recent
ASTP/ONC FAQ discouraging limitations on data access for automation will continue
to be necessary.

III. Information Blocking

                                                                                     4

In the HTI-5 NPRM, ASTP/ONC rightly acknowledges the persistent reports of
leading EHR vendors leveraging scale and market dominance in the database and
data management layer to gain a competitive advantage in the application layer.
These actions amount to a weaponization of health IT certification standards and
processes to stifle innovation and competition, to the detriment of the broader
healthcare ecosystem and patients. EHR vendors have made it technically difficult
and costly for health systems to access and use health data generated by their
providers and patients. These obstacles are amplified for health systems that want to
utilize additional vendors bringing innovative technologies like AI. Nearly two
decades ago, lawmakers required and funded the adoption of EHRs across
healthcare under the expectation that an interoperable database layer would
accelerate the pace of innovation, improve the quality of care for patients, and make
healthcare data usable. HTI-5 has the potential to bring that promise closer to reality
by ending these practices.

Today, EHRs operate not only as health record databases but as healthcare
application developers. This has created a fundamentally misaligned incentive where
the technology vendors controlling health system data are granted broad leeway to
impose technical barriers, access fees, and administrative requirements on new
market entrants competing on the application layer. These practices stifle
technological innovation in healthcare, drive up the cost of new technologies for
patients and providers, and contribute to the inflationary effect of technology in
healthcare.

Vega Health is encouraged by HHS’s announced crackdown on information
blocking. The loosely defined nature of information blocking exceptions has allowed
well-financed incumbents to simply reject interoperability requests and force lengthy,
costly litigation. Information blocking regulations were intended to make
interoperability the default state, with actors required to prove a request fell under an
exception. Instead, dominant incumbents have abused exceptions to the rule to
require innovative patient- and provider-facing technologies to prove that a request
should be fulfilled.

In the absence of HHS enforcement actions to establish precedent and better define
exceptions, certified health IT vendors have shifted the burden and cost of proving
information blocking to innovators. This is inherently flawed and represents a failure
of policy implementation. Vega Health strongly supports the proposal to eliminate or
tighten frequently abused exception-based loopholes through the HTI-5 NPRM.

ASTP/ONC proposes to modify the manner exception to eliminate abuses of
market power. Vega Health strongly supports the inclusion of market rate caps on
data exchange contracts and the elimination of “take it or leave it” contracting terms.
As highlighted above, certified health IT vendors have leveraged their status as the
database layer within health systems to charge exorbitant fees for custom API

to continue identifying opportunities to narrow information blocking exceptions. Vega
Health also encourages ASTP/ONC and the HHS OIG to utilize enforcement actions
to establish precedent and eliminate ambiguity in exception definitions.

IV. Future-Ready FHIR
Over the past decade, it has become apparent that FHIR APIs are a crucial step
towards healthcare interoperability and standardized data exchange. FHIR-based
data exchange provides a roadmap for the kind of real-time data exchange
necessary to build meaningful AI/ML applications that leverage the vast quantities of
structured and unstructured data in healthcare. As ASTP/ONC acknowledges, the
future of healthcare interoperability and data usability will be built on FHIR standards.

As ASTP/ONC continues to develop both the FHIR standard for data exchange and
the US Core for Data Interoperability (USCDI) definitions of crucial data elements,
Vega Health encourages policymakers to prioritize standardizing elements essential
to the implementation of AI/ML solutions in health systems. By rapidly expanding the
set of standard data elements at the heart of FHIR-based interoperability,
ASTP/ONC can leverage the health IT certification program to accelerate innovation
and avoid the costly and burdensome processes – and potential information blocking
abuses – that must be navigated to exchange key data elements unavailable over
FHIR.

One pertinent example is Medication Administration Record (MAR) data. From
experience implementing AI solutions at multiple health systems, Vega Health has
recognized that MAR data are crucial inputs for commonly used clinical AI solutions,
such as inpatient deterioration or emergency department likelihood to admit
predictive models. Today, MAR data must be accessed from external databases,
through custom API infrastructure, or via HL7 data feeds – each approach

                                                                                       6

complicating model implementation and usability. Vega Health is encouraged by the
inclusion of MAR data in the draft USCDI v7 and encourages ASTP/ONC to continue
expanding the set of standard data elements as a key measure to accelerating the
implementation and use of clinical AI solutions.

Additionally, for FHIR APIs to sustain and scale health AI innovation, a few key
roadblocks must be resolved. In the HTI-5 NPRM, ASTP/ONC proposed a key
regulatory shift in the rulemaking authority derived from the 21 st Century Cures Act
and sets the foundation for future API-based certification criteria. Vega Health
applauds this shift and encourages ASTP/ONC to use these criteria to set baseline
technical standards for APIs that can address some of the information blocking
practices described above. Vega Health recommends that ASTP/ONC take the
following suggestions under consideration when developing API-based certification
criteria:

Vega Health urges ASTP/ONC to require reasonable, market-based pricing for
data queries. Often, certified health IT vendors restrict the use of FHIR APIs by
placing prohibitive user fees or per-query fees on developers. These prices far
exceed any API development or maintenance costs that the certified health IT
vendor may incur and instead are instituted to suppress and restrict innovative
technologies that a health system may seek to deploy. The changes to the manner
exception in the HTI-5 NPRM acknowledge this challenge broadly, but ASTP/ONC
should preemptively eliminate the same practices as they apply to API query fees
specifically.

Vega Health urges ASTP/ONC to place limits on the ability of certified health IT
vendors to cap the number of queries or flow of data through FHIR APIs. It
should not be up to data-layer technologies that health systems are required to
utilize by federal law to set limits on the ways in which health systems and their
chosen vendors can utilize or access system data. Complicating the picture, many
certified health IT vendors develop their own applications that are in direct
competition with outside vendors; the ability to place restrictions on competitors that
do not extend to their own enterprise solutions gives certified health IT an
anticompetitive advantage and creates misaligned incentives, limiting innovation and
competition. While some limits may be technically necessary, these limits should be
set via documented API specifications, health system or end user throughput
limitations and cost containment, or contractual agreement, not imposed
inconsistently by certified health IT to gain a competitive advantage.

Vega Health urges ASTP/ONC to establish and reinforce “right to connect” and
“right to build” permissions for health systems using certified health IT. As
part of API-based Health IT Certification Program criteria, ASTP/ONC should ensure
that health systems have the ability to access and utilize FHIR APIs for AI
implementations and development projects. Health systems have frequently

encountered obstacles to internal AI development or real-time data analysis when
specific data elements are unavailable through required certified health IT APIs.

Beyond common sets of data elements required by law or embedded into standards
like the US Core for Data Interoperability, certified health IT vendors place
restrictions or add multiple burdensome hurdles to prevent health systems and their
chosen vendors from building custom APIs that fully utilize health system data.
These barriers include contractual language that limits the ability of AI solution
vendors to employ or sub-contract domain experts with the relevant expertise to
effectively integrate AI solutions into legacy health IT systems. Legacy health IT
incumbents are limiting innovation and competition by preventing new entrants from
being able to bring in the relevant expertise to expand AI use in healthcare. This is
antithetical to innovation, anticompetitive, and an artificial inhibitor of AI innovation
and ROI. A universal “right to build” permission would allow health systems and
contracted vendors to take full advantage of FHIR APIs to unlock lower costs and
improved patient outcomes through digital development.

Vega Health urges ASTP/ONC to set standard FHIR API configurations that are
publicly available to innovators and entrepreneurs. By shifting the Health IT
Certification Program to API-based criteria, ASTP/ONC can eliminate the lack of
standardization and technical hurdles that slow and complicate implementation
projects, adding unnecessary cost to healthcare. Uniform, universal API
documentation will accelerate innovation, make data and analytics insights more
widely accessible for lower-resourced health systems, and create more resilient
healthcare data systems for a more connected future state of healthcare.

V. Market-Based AI Model Evaluation
Industry efforts to compile AI “model cards” containing common data elements have
not worked as intended. Vega Health’s Co-Founder and CEO worked with the Health
AI Partnership as that organization published the first Model Facts label for machine
learning models used by healthcare systems. Since that time, DIHI and HAIP have
facilitated the adoption of Model Facts labels by health systems to improve
communication with clinical front-line users. In contrast to this bottom-up approach,
other efforts to centralize “model cards” have not effectively served end users.
Rather, many such efforts became a gatekeeping exercise, with well-resourced
industry groups founded by large model developers competing to set the terms of
market entry and protect their own incumbent technologies.

Federal standards for the use of AI can protect consumers; however, predicting AI
model performance on an ambiguous and evolving set of metrics does not always
translate to real-world performance across vastly different clinical settings.
Meaningful regulatory action can best protect patients and consumers by requiring
disclosure of safety incidents and punishing actual harm. Vega Health strongly

                                                                                        8

supports ASTP/ONC’s effort to eliminate this restriction on competition by removing
model cards from the health IT certification criteria.

Health systems procure AI technologies to fulfill specific business objectives across
administrative, clinical, and quality workflows. In order to remain in compliance with
federal regulations, assess organizational success, and ensure the delivery of quality
patient care, health systems have the right to require some degree of model
transparency and performance evaluation. However, these metrics, the manner of
assessment, and the confidentiality of reporting should be contractually defined on a
case-by-case basis, not set by conflicted coalitions or used to limit access to app
markets.

This reality makes the need for transparent, objective post-deployment model
evaluation and monitoring in healthcare even more evident. Rather than adding a
federal stamp of approval to the AI model evaluation metrics proposed by special
interest groups, ASTP/ONC should encourage health systems and end users to
determine the most impactful metrics. Well-established contracting principles will
then shape the market and hold developers accountable for delivering on promised
performance, just as it has for Software-as-a-Service (SaaS), medical equipment,
and countless other markets.

Additionally, model evaluation should advance model procurement, implementation,
deployment, and maintenance or sunset decisions by the end user, not present
another barrier to market entry. As ASTP/ONC has recognized, federal standards for
evaluating and reporting on AI performance are inflexible and unnecessarily
burdensome. Not only does the performance of healthcare AI models vary across
patient populations and payer mixes, but benchmark and threshold performance will
vary by health system. It makes far more sense for health systems to determine what
the minimum acceptable model performance should be for their system and evaluate
on an ongoing basis than to allow large incumbents to set model evolution criteria to
limit market competition.

As the healthcare technology market moves away from AI gatekeepers and towards
real-world model evaluation and monitoring, Vega Health is confident that a market-
based framework driven by innovation will be able to replace the restrictive model
card requirements that ASTP/ONC proposes to remove. Effective real-world
monitoring requires moving beyond narrow technical metrics to assess AI
performance across multiple, interconnected dimensions. Based on our experience,
Vega Health is committed to a four-dimensional monitoring framework that captures
whether AI solutions deliver sustained value in practice, and we urge ASTP/ONC to
continue removing regulatory barriers across the following components of evaluation
and monitoring.

Technical Accuracy and Model Fidelity. Technical performance remains the
foundation of AI monitoring, but real-world technical assessment differs substantially
from pre-market validation. In dynamic clinical environments, Vega Health monitors
for model drift, including changes in model inputs, outputs, or the relationship
between them that signal potential degradation. Monitoring data quality of model
inputs is particularly important. Vega Health utilizes the Machine Learning-Data
Quality Assurance (ML-DQA) framework developed at DIHI to assess completeness,
conformance, and plausibility of all model inputs. Finally, Vega Health conducts
retrospective and prospective evaluation of AI solutions in a secure development
environment before going live in a clinical setting, ensuring model performance is
satisfactory and preventing safety incidents.

User Adoption and Clinical Integration. Technical accuracy means very little if
clinicians or end-users do not use AI tools appropriately, or if the tools disrupt
established clinical workflows. Vega Health measures override rates, time-to-action
on AI-generated alerts, and patterns of engagement that reveal whether tools
provide value or generate alert fatigue to reveal critical insights that technical
monitoring may miss. For example, high override rates may indicate low positive
predictive value prompting alarm fatigue, or they may reveal workflow misalignment
and/or insufficient user training. The infrastructure to support adoption monitoring
includes integration with clinical workflow systems to capture interaction data,
mechanisms for collecting structured user feedback, and analytics capabilities that
can identify patterns across different clinical contexts and user groups.

Clinical and Operational Outcomes. The third monitoring dimension assesses
whether AI systems achieve their intended clinical and operational objectives.
Technical accuracy and user adoption are necessary, but insufficient. The central
question is whether an AI solution improves patient outcomes or organizational
performance. This requires connecting AI integration to downstream outcomes.
Measuring outcome impact presents methodological challenges, and the metrics
tracked will vary significantly based on the nature of the AI solution or AI-enabled
device. By integrating AI performance data with electronic health record (EHR) data
and operational systems, Vega Health tracks patient outcomes, operational
efficiency metrics, and financial performance in relation to AI implementation and
usage patterns.

Return on Investment and Value Realization. The business case for AI monitoring
itself becomes clear: without systematic measurement, healthcare delivery
organizations cannot distinguish successful investments from expensive failures. On
the benefit side, we seek to track both tangible returns, and harder-to-quantify
improvements such as clinician satisfaction, faster decision-making, and reduced
cognitive burden. ROI monitoring must account for the full lifecycle of AI
implementation. Initial investments in integration and training may exceed short-term
benefits, making it critical to track value realization over appropriate time horizons.

We have observed that AI solutions often deliver value differently than anticipated –
a tool designed to improve diagnostic accuracy may deliver value through workflow
efficiency gains, or vice versa. Comprehensive monitoring captures these
unexpected benefits while identifying solutions that fail to deliver value.

Comprehensive Monitoring in Practice. The four dimensions outlined above operate
interdependently. Technical drift may manifest first as changing adoption patterns or
declining outcomes. Poor workflow integration may appear as low adoption rates or
negative user feedback before impacting clinical outcomes. Effective monitoring
requires tracking all dimensions, with specific metrics tuned to the specific use case,
and governance structures that can synthesize insights across them to inform
decisions about continuing, adapting, or sunsetting an AI solution.

The monitoring framework described above is not aspirational. It is derived from
practical implementation experience and refined through implementation across
diverse healthcare settings. The question is not whether comprehensive monitoring
is necessary, but how health systems will build or access the infrastructure and
expertise to do it effectively. As ASTP/ONC forges ahead with a new approach to
regulating health IT, including novel AI applications in clinical and administrative
settings, it is essential that the Assistant Secretary understand current capabilities
and best practices. Deregulatory efforts do not make patients less safe or health
systems less prepared; rather, they allow innovative AI solutions to take cost out of
care, improve broken processes, and put patient outcomes first.
