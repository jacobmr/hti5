The North American Association of Central Cancer Registries (NAACCR) supports the
goals of HTI-5 to reduce burden on IT developers, promote information blocking
regulations, and encourage modernizing standards towards FHIR-based cancer registry
reporting such as the Central Cancer Registry Reporting FHIR Implementation Guide.
However, the cancer surveillance community has concerns about the proposed HTI-5
rule that removes the §170.315(f)(4) certification criterion Transmission to cancer
registries on January 1, 2027. These include the following:

1. FHIR-Based resources are not yet mature or widely implemented by the
   cancer registry community. The proposed rule references emerging resources
   such as the Helios FHIR Accelerator and the Central Cancer Registry Reporting
   FHIR Implementation Guide. While promising, these efforts remain under active
   development and have not yet undergone testing, adoption, nor implementation
   by any central cancer registries and health IT developers.
   a. Clarification about the Helios FHIR Accelerator – one of the key use cases
   being explored for the Public Health Query & Response priority area is the
   retrieval of supplemental clinical information about cancer cases from a
   health IT module’s FHIR server by public health agencies. Helios is
   otherwise not directly involved in the effort to move the initial reporting of
   cancer cases via a FHIR-based approach.
   b. Additionally, cancer registry software vendors typically require up to two
   years to implement new interoperability standards, allowing time for
   development, testing, alignment, and risk management. The proposed
   timeline does not account for this implementation lifecycle.
2. Removal of §170.315(f)(4) certification criteria Transmission to cancer
   registries risks disrupting timely incidence cancer reporting. Eliminating the
   certification criteria before FHIR-based reporting is fully implemented would likely
   shift reporting to manual or non-standardized processes, increasing provider burden
   (particularly in ambulatory settings) and risk reductions in data completeness,
   timeliness, and quality. During transition periods, jurisdictions may be required to
   manage mixed submission formats (e.g., CDA, HL7 v2.5.1, and emerging FHIR
   approaches), increasing technical workload, validation complexity, and coordination
   demands, with potential (short-term) risks to data quality and timeliness.
   a. Comprehensive, timely, and standardized cancer case reporting to public
   health agencies is foundational to monitoring cancer incidence, evaluating
   treatment effectiveness, identifying disparities, guiding research, and
   informing prevention strategies.
   b. Based on a recent poll, at least 13 central cancer registry jurisdictions rely
   on the HL7® Implementation Guide for CDA® Release 2: Reporting to
   Public Health Cancer Registries from Ambulatory Healthcare Providers,
    Release 1, DSTU Release 1.1 (April 2015) for case reporting. This
   number may be higher.
   c. Since 2012, the cancer surveillance community has partnered with ASTP
   to operationalize structured EHR-based cancer case reporting. A major
   reason for this collaboration was to reduce the reliance on paper-based or
   ad hoc reporting mechanisms, which are less secure, burdensome, and
   prone to data quality issues.
3. Removal of public health reporting, including §170.315(f)(4) certification
   criterion Transmission to cancer registries, eliminates any incentive for
   EHRs to support interoperability. The certification criteria incentive is an
   important effort in advancing the timeliness, completeness, and efficiency of
   cancer surveillance in this country. Especially without setting expectations and
   support for the adoption of a FHIR implementation guide. In the absence of a
   certified national standard, state and local jurisdictions may need to establish and
   maintain their own reporting specifications, coordinate directly with vendors and
   providers, and support testing and validation processes—requiring additional
   staff capacity and resources.
   a. The cancer community is seeing an increase in patients that are being
   diagnosed or treated in the outpatient sector (urology, dermatology,
   medical oncology, etc.) instead of a hospital setting. It is critical for
   physician offices and healthcare professionals to have an incentive to
   report standardized cancer data.
   Recommended revision: We encourage ASTP to retain §170.315(f)(4)
   transmission to cancer registries health IT certification criteria until FHIR-based
   reporting has matured further, with a phased transition plan that includes defined
   minimum content expectations and coordinated testing to prevent reporting gaps.
   Note: While input on these comments were solicited by a cross-section of the
   cancer surveillance community, they do not necessarily represent the opinions of all
   central cancer registries and/or cancer surveillance partners.
