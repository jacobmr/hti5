Regarding Health Data, Technology, and Interoperability: ASTP/ONC Deregulatory
Actions to Unleash Prosperity (HTI-5 Draft Rule)

To the Assistant Secretary for Technology Policy:

The Association of Public Health Laboratories (APHL) appreciates the opportunity to
provide comments on the Health Data, Technology, and Interoperability: ASTP/ONC
Deregulatory Actions to Unleash Prosperity (HTI-5) Draft Rule and the withdrawal of certain
proposals from the original draft HTI-2 Rule. APHL represents state and local governmental
public health laboratories across the United States, and we are deeply concerned about
the impact these regulatory changes will have on public health reporting and the nation's
public health infrastructure.

We concentrate on ﬁve main points in our response: 1) the removal of speciﬁc standards
requirements shifts the burden and cost of interoperability onto healthcare organizations
and public health agencies; 2) the withdrawal of Health IT certiﬁcation requirements for
public health systems will leave public health infrastructure unable to keep pace with
healthcare technology advances; 3) the withdrawal of LOI and LRI standards for electronic
test orders and results (ETOR) will slow the adoption of a critical efficiency that improves
patient care; 4) to facilitate TEFCA exchange and public health reporting, ASTP should allow
standardized contracts in certain contexts, even if most contracts of adhesion are
prohibited; and 5) ASTP and other federal partners must ﬁnd efficiencies and cost-savings
and drive innovation in public health interoperability, which has priorities and
considerations that are distinct from healthcare IT.

Standards Support Interoperability and Create Efficiencies
The HTI-5 Draft Rule proposes to remove requirements that health IT software support
speciﬁc standards for cancer registry reporting, electronic case reporting (eCR) and
antimicrobial use and resistance reporting. While this change may reduce compliance
costs for health IT developers, it simply shifts that burden and cost onto healthcare
organizations and public health agencies.

Without mandated standards, public health agencies will receive data in various formats
from different vendors and healthcare organizations, each requiring custom integration
work. This fragmentation creates an unsustainable burden. Notably, ASTP’s move away
from standardization is a paradigmatic shift and one that is opposition to the rest of the
public health community. In its January 2026 report, CSTE’s eCR Consensus Criteria
Workgroup clearly indicated that the data exchange standard used by Healthcare
Organizations (HCOs) for the transmission of eICR data is the HL7 CDA® R2
Implementation Guide: Public Health Case Report. The transmission of eICR would be
considerably easier if EHRs could natively generate a message that is compliant with this
guide. Public health jurisdictions will continue to require that organizations report to public
health according to speciﬁc standards. To comply, healthcare organizations will have to
implement external solutions or pay the EHR vendors to customize their data exchange
exports. The same customization would have to occur multiple times, as each HCO
implements eICR, resulting in a substantial increase in costs, time and effort for the HCO
and the public health agency, not less, with more steps and planning required for
implementation, onboarding, and validation. This approach is far less efficient and more
costly in the long term than having native capability in each electronic health record (EHR)
or other health IT system to generate public health reporting in a standard format.

The ASTP Health IT certiﬁcation program is the best way to ensure that this native capability
exists. A FHIR API may technically be able to send data from one party to another, but that
capability is meaningless if the public health agency receives data in inconsistent formats
and cannot efficiently integrate the information into a usable format. Standards are not
bureaucratic obstacles; they are the foundation of true interoperability. Without them, we
risk creating a more expensive, more fragmented, and less effective public health reporting
system.

The Certiﬁcation of Public Health Systems Advances
Public Health Infrastructure Modernization
The original draft HTI-2 Rule included the expansion of the ASTP Health IT certiﬁcation
program to public health systems, such as disease surveillance systems and laboratory
information systems. APHL welcomed this expansion and especially the impact these
requirements would have on public health laboratories and agencies' ability to meet their
evolving data exchange needs. We are disappointed to see that ASTP has withdrawn these
proposals.

Without guarantees that the systems they use must support standardized messaging,
FHIR, and other emerging technologies, public health laboratories and agencies will
struggle to ensure that their infrastructure and systems can manage incoming and outgoing
data, process it meaningfully, and present it to public health professionals who need it to
drive decisions.

Public health is at a critical juncture. The COVID-19 pandemic exposed signiﬁcant gaps in
public health data infrastructure, and there has been a national push to modernize these
systems. Certiﬁcation requirements would have provided a clear path forward, ensuring
that public health systems could exchange data seamlessly with healthcare providers,
other public health entities, and federal agencies. Without these requirements, public
health risks becoming increasingly isolated from the broader health IT ecosystem, unable
to receive or share data efficiently at precisely the time when data-driven decision-making
is most critical.

The Adoption of LOI and LRI Standards Can Accelerate
ETOR
The original draft HTI-2 Rule would have required health IT software to be able to exchange
orders electronically according to the HL7 Laboratory Order Interface (LOI) standard and
results according to the HL7 Laboratory Results Interface (LRI) standard. This criterion
would have supported the adoption of ETOR, which saves time and money, improves data
quality, and accelerates turnaround time for laboratory testing, thereby directly beneﬁting
patient care.

APHL strongly advocates for the adoption of LOI and LRI standards for ETOR. These
standards are mature, widely recognized, and speciﬁcally designed for laboratory
workﬂows. Standardizing on LOI and LRI would create a common language between
healthcare organizations and public health laboratories, simplifying implementations and
accelerating ETOR adoption. Conversely, the lack of standardization can increase the
complexity and cost of an ETOR interface.

The adoption of LOI and LRI standards would simplify these implementations and
accelerate the adoption of ETOR across clinical and public health laboratories. For
newborn screening in particular, where time is critical for patient outcomes, standardized
ETOR can mean the difference between timely intervention and delayed treatment. Public
health laboratories have long recognized the efficiencies of ETOR but have had limited
success implementing ETOR solutions precisely because of the lack of standardization.
The withdrawal of LOI and LRI requirements represents a missed opportunity to drive
meaningful improvement in laboratory operations and patient care.

This lack of standardization will also increase the importance of intermediary solutions,
such as APHL’s Detor solution, which is hosted on the APHL Informatics Messaging (AIMS)
Platform. Detor supports healthcare organizations and public health laboratories through
the use of a single centralized platform for ETOR. It limits the need for custom mapping,
translation, and ongoing maintenance. Detor facilitates ETOR regardless of the standard
used by the healthcare organization or the public health laboratory.

Standardized Contracting Language Is Appropriate in
Certain Contexts
ASTP proposes to revise § 171.301(a) to add explicit contractual limitations. To satisfy the
Manner Exception, any agreement governing access, exchange, or use of EHI: (1) must be
at "market rate"; (2) must not be a "contract of adhesion"; and (3) must not contain
"unconscionable terms."

This provision effectively bans standardized contract language and requires that all
contracts between data exchange partners be negotiated separately. This approach is
inefficient and creates unnecessary administrative burden, particularly when all data
exchange partners receive identical services at the same rate with the same expectations.
This approach disadvantages non-proﬁt entities and smaller healthcare organizations and
creates insurmountable obstacles for public health agencies.

Public health reporting fundamentally differs from the rest of the healthcare market. The
AIMS Platform serves as the trusted public health intermediary precisely because it
provides consistent, equitable services to all jurisdictions. All public health jurisdictions
can connect to and use AIMS regardless of size, population served, capabilities, or region.
This consistency and transparency are essential to building and maintaining trust across
the public health community.

Moreover, public health reporting is not a commercial activity that can or should be
commodiﬁed. There is no business case for public health reporting and no mechanism to
generate proﬁt. Public health data exchange represents a critical public investment that
protects the public's health and safety. The value of this investment is realized through
reduced disease burden, maintained economic activity during public health emergencies,
and limited impact from health threats. Attempting to apply market-based contracting
principles to public health reporting fundamentally misunderstands its nature and
purpose.

APHL recommends that if an agreement supports public health reporting and if its terms
are developed or approved through a governance process that provides for meaningful
input from current or prospective participants, then it should be exempt from ASTP's
proposed contractual limitations. A structured governance process with community
involvement represents a form of collective bargaining that protects the interests of all
parties. Therefore, data use agreements signed with a public health agency, a Health
Information Exchange (HIE), or with the AIMS Platform should be permitted under the
Manner Exception.

We note that several other entities, including Qualiﬁed Health Information Networks
(QHINs) and HIEs, have raised similar concerns in their comments. We encourage ASTP to
carefully review these submissions, particularly the response submitted by eHealth
Exchange, which provides additional perspective on this issue.

Innovation in Public Health Is Fundamentally Different
from Healthcare IT
It is critical that ASTP and other federal partners recognize that public health data exchange
and interoperability operate under fundamentally different constraints and priorities than
traditional healthcare IT. These distinct requirements demand tailored approaches and
innovations to achieve efficiencies and cost-savings and to ultimately modernize the way
that public health data is collected, shared, and used. APHL is committed to supporting
public health-speciﬁc innovation and resource optimization through technical assistance,
member resources, and the centralized services of the AIMS Platform.

Conclusion
Thank you for the opportunity to comment on the HTI-5 Draft Rule. APHL urges ASTP to
reinstate the critical standards requirements and public health systems certiﬁcation
criteria that were withdrawn from HTI-2. Standards are the foundation of interoperability,
and removing them does not reduce costs; it merely shifts those costs to other entities
while fragmenting the health IT ecosystem.

Public health reporting is not optional. It is a legal requirement in all jurisdictions, essential
for protecting the public's health from infectious disease outbreaks, chronic disease,
bioterrorism, and other threats. An efficient, interoperable public health data exchange
system beneﬁts everyone: it enables faster diagnosis and treatment for patients, reduces
administrative burden for healthcare providers, provides public health agencies with the
timely data they need to respond to health threats, and ultimately saves lives.

We strongly encourage ASTP to:

• Require IT software to support speciﬁc standards for public health reporting,
including cancer registry reporting, eCR, antimicrobial use and resistance reporting,
and other data exchange use cases.
• Expand Health IT certiﬁcation requirements to public health systems, including
disease surveillance systems and laboratory information systems.
• Require health IT software to support LOI and LRI standards for electronic test
orders and results.
• Carve out an exception for standardized contracting language if the data exchange
supports public health reporting and is managed by a formal governance process.
