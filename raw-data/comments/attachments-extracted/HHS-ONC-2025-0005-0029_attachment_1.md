      Re: Comment regarding Health Data, Technology, and Interoperability:
      ASTP/ONC Deregulatory Actions to Unleash Prosperity

Dear Representative:

      Please consider my public comment to the proposed rule "Health Data,

Technology, and Interoperability: ASTP/ONC Deregulatory Actions To Unleash
Prosperity" (Federal Register, December 29, 2025; Docket ID HHS-ONC-2025-
0005). I am writing based on my work as a trial lawyer who has handled medical
numerous malpractice cases for over 22 years.

      While I appreciate efforts to reduce unnecessary burdens on health IT

developers, I strongly urge ASTP/ONC to delay deregulation of electronic health
record (EHR) certifications until critical standards for data integrity and security—
including audit trails—are updated and enforced. Premature deregulation risks
undermining patient safety, trust, and the financial integrity of our healthcare
system.

      The integrity and security of electronic health information are paramount to

me both personally and professionally. As a patient, I rely on accurate, unaltered
records for coordinated care across providers; any tampering or untraceable
changes could lead to misdiagnoses or harmful treatments. Quite frankly, I want to
know who is accessing my health information and when.

       Professionally, as a medical malpractice trial lawyer, I know that audit trails

are foundational to safeguarding EHRs and must not be treated as optional or
deregulated without robust updates. They keep everyone honest. These logs
provide a chronological, tamper-evident record of all access, modifications,
deletions, and interactions with patient data, ensuring accountability and
transparency. They are essential for detecting unauthorized alterations, errors, or
breaches, which helps maintain the reliability of medical records and complies
with HIPAA requirements for tracking who accessed protected health information

       Moreover, audit trails are vital for curtailing healthcare fraud, which costs

the U.S. an estimated $308.6 billion annually—a burden passed on to consumers
through higher premiums and out-of-pocket expenses. By revealing corrections,
deletions, copy-forwards, overwrites, and other manipulations, transparent, non-
proprietary audit trails prevent the concealment or amplification of fraudulent
billing practices. Deregulation, as proposed—particularly the removal of
mandatory audit log requirements under §170.315(d)(2)–(3),(10)—could
exacerbate this issue, facilitating practices that already drain hundreds of billions
from the system without addressing root causes.

       Standards for audit trails must evolve to keep pace with modern EHR usage,

including integrations with artificial intelligence (AI), medical devices, and
messaging systems. The current benchmark, ASTM E2147-18, published in 2018,
is outdated and does not adequately address emerging needs like AI-generated data
transparency or interoperability for safe data sharing under TEFCA. An updated
standard should mandate identification of all software involved in record creation,
define data retention periods aligned with ongoing care, investigations, litigation
holds, and regulations, and ensure audit trails are searchable, shareable, and
reproducible.

      Patients already face barriers to accessing their own EHI despite legal

mandates for transparency; weakening certifications without strengthening audit
protections will only compound this. I implore ASTP/ONC to prioritize updating
and publishing enhanced audit trail standards before proceeding with deregulation.
This approach will foster innovation while upholding patient trust, legal
accountability, and fiscal responsibility.

      Please consider also some examples from a current case I am handling.

Only by reviewing the audit trail did we learn that the surgeon revised his
operative note 4 times over the course of 14 days before closing the note in the
computer system. Once we were aware the operative note had been revised, we
were able to request the revisions, which had significant changes and edits that
were directly related to the claims of negligence in the case regarding a perforation
during surgery. Without the audit trail noting how many times and when the note
was revised, we would have never learned of the language the surgeon added,
including language that was coincidentally added after the surgeon and hospital

       Human nature being what it is, audit trails keep us honest. The computer

does not have feelings or a vested interest in outcomes. It simply records via an

we are looking at the records years from when the care occurred.
