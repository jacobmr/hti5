                                         General Feedback

We commend ASTP/ONC for taking a thoughtful and measured approach to reassessing the Health
IT Certification Program and identifying requirements that may no longer provide commensurate
value. By prioritizing interoperability, transparency, and patient access to electronic health
information while removing or refining overly prescriptive criteria, the proposed rule reflects a
pragmatic understanding of how innovation occurs in real-world care settings. As a network-enabled,
cloud-based EHR vendor serving diverse practice types and care settings nationwide, athenahealth
experiences firsthand how regulatory complexity can divert resources from usability improvements,
workflow optimization, and patient-centered innovation. We strongly support efforts to ensure
certification requirements remain targeted, outcomes-driven, and aligned with advancing high-
quality, connected, and efficient care delivery.

We strongly encourage ASTP/ONC and the Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS) to work

              Predictive Decision Support Interventions (PDSI) Certification Criteria

athenahealth appreciates and applauds ASTP/ONC’s efforts in the HTI-5 Proposed Rule to reassess
PDSI requirements in light of implementation experience and burden considerations. We strongly
support the proposal to substantially scale back these requirements. However, retaining a single
activation-focused requirement while eliminating all associated transparency provisions results in
a certification criterion that is internally inconsistent and of limited practical value.

As proposed, § 170.315(b)(11)(iii) would be the sole remaining Predictive DSI requirement, obligating
health IT developers to enable a limited set of users to select (e.g., activate) Predictive DSI
functionality when such interventions rely on a specified subset of data. At the same time,
ASTP/ONC proposes to remove all source attribute and transparency requirements intended to
inform users about model training, validation, performance, and risk mitigation. Without access to
this information, the limited set of users responsible for activating Predictive DSIs, such as
administrators or designated clinical leaders, lack the context necessary to make informed
decisions on behalf of their organizations.

While we agree that responsible PDSI development necessitates transparency to end users,
requiring enablement via “activation” at the individual intervention level is overly prescriptive and
does not account for the variety of ways in which users may appropriately engage with interventions
within a certified health IT system. We encourage ASTP/ONC to adopt a more flexible, outcomes
focused approach that ensures transparency and meaningful user choice without mandating a
single technical method of enablement. While we support the requirement that Health IT Modules
be able to support DSI based on a defined set of data elements, we do not believe that retaining the
requirement to enable a limited set of users activate PDSI functionality is beneficial. Activation alone
does not meaningfully advance informed adoption, trust, or safe use when users are no longer
provided insight into how a Predictive DSI was developed, tested, or evaluated.

ASTP/ONC acknowledges in the proposed rule that transparency requirements did not demonstrate
the anticipated benefits and that assumptions about their value to healthcare delivery organizations
may have been incorrect. athenahealth does not dispute this assessment. Accordingly, as
ASTP/ONC works to finalize the removal of Predictive DSI transparency requirements, we
recommend that you also remove the corresponding user activation requirement in §
170.315(b)(11)(iii). Retaining activation requirements without accompanying information would
render the criterion ineffective while continuing to impose implementation and compliance burdens
on health IT developers without clear benefit to end users or patients. We believe the industry would
benefit from context and clarity on the decision for the user activation requirement as it pertains to
features supplied by health IT vendors.

        Evidence Based Decision Support interventions (EBDSI) Certification Criteria

Consistent with our recommendation regarding PDSI, athenahealth recommends that ASTP/ONC
remove the user activation requirement in § 170.315(b)(11)(iii) for EBDSI, while maintaining the

overarching requirement that health IT modules support decision support interventions based on a
defined set of data elements. Preserving the data element standard maintains interoperability and
functional consistency without imposing workflow-based activation requirements that no longer
align with HTI-5’s deregulatory framework.

As noted above, athenahealth supports HTI-5’s direction to remove prescriptive requirements that
do not meaningfully advance interoperability or patient care. Consistent with that goal, we also
recommend eliminating the EBDSI feedback requirement. Feedback is essential to developing
accurate, clinically relevant decision support. Health IT developers already have strong incentives
to collect it in ways that reflect real-world clinical and operational needs. athenahealth supports
robust feedback mechanisms for both EBDSI and PDSI within the EHR and through established
external channels. However, while feedback is critical, ASTP/ONC should not prescribe specific
features, content, or data export requirements for how that feedback must be captured or shared
through certification criteria.

As proposed, the feedback requirement extends beyond enabling a feedback mechanism and
instead mandates that EHR users be able to download feedback data with specified required
elements. Similar to the source attribute provisions, this approach compels developers to build
functionality that may offer limited practical value while adding meaningful development and
certification burden. Eliminating this requirement would better align with ASTP/ONC’s stated
objective in HTI-5 to prioritize interoperability over the prescription of specific features or workflows.

Additionally, many decision support capabilities are implemented in interoperable ways that
support both EBDSI and PDSI. In practice, the same interface, such as a Clinical Decision Support
(CDS) card, may be generated by either source. Requiring feedback capabilities for EBDSI alone
could therefore unintentionally extend additional burden to PDSI functionality as well, undermining
ASTP/ONC’s intent to scale back requirements in that area and introducing unnecessary regulatory
complexity.

                                         Manner Exception

athenahealth appreciates ASTP/ONC’s continued attention to the Manner Requested Exception.
Revisions to this exception, particularly those affecting permissible pricing methodologies, could
require updates to existing commercial agreements. We encourage ASTP/ONC to consider the
practical realities of renegotiating and amending established agreements when determining
effective and compliance dates for any finalized changes.

Maintaining flexibility in pricing structures is critical to reflect the operational and financial realities
of evolving exchange models between healthcare technology companies. Entities should be able to
negotiate and apply discounts within market rates, and if both parties agree, the specific pricing
methodology should not dictate compliance. Presumptions about appropriate commercial terms
could unintentionally slow innovation, limit participation, or reduce the predictability needed to
operationalize and scale exchange services. For example, pricing models based solely on utilization
may not provide the predictability of set costs that some entities need to operationalize and scale
exchange services.

The services requiring the exchange of electronic health information continue to evolve rapidly. As
technology and market models develop, preserving flexibility in permissible pricing approaches is

               Automation Technologies and Revisions to “Access” and “Use”

athenahealth appreciates ONC’s proposal to clarify that the definitions of “access” and “use” under
the information blocking regulations encompass automation technologies, including robotic
process automation (RPA) and autonomous AI systems. We understand the agency’s intent to codify
prior FAQ guidance and provide greater regulatory certainty.

We note, however, that expressly incorporating automation technologies into the regulatory
definitions may necessitate updates to existing commercial agreements, governance frameworks,
and technical controls that were structured around traditional, user-mediated access models.
Because actors have reasonably relied on prior interpretations in structuring exchange pathways
and contracting practices, we respectfully request that ASTP/ONC provide reasonable transition
time before the revised definitions become enforceable under the information blocking framework.
Immediate effectiveness could create avoidable compliance risk while actors evaluate and adjust
contractual and technical safeguards.

We are also concerned that expanding the definitions at this stage may be premature absent clearer
guardrails regarding downstream uses of electronic health information (EHI) by automation
technologies. Automation systems, particularly autonomous AI, may access EHI for a discrete,
articulated purpose but subsequently process, transform, analyze, or operationalize that data at
scale in ways that extend beyond the original access context. The information blocking regulations
have historically focused on whether access was permitted rather than how automated systems
may subsequently deploy that data. Additional clarity regarding the boundaries of permissible
downstream automated use would help prevent uncertainty and reduce the risk of disputes under
the information blocking standard.

Moreover, certified health IT developers (CEHRT) must retain the ability to protect system security,
performance, and stability. Automation technologies operate at a scale, speed, and persistence
materially different from human access patterns. High-frequency querying, bulk extraction,
automated user interface interaction or modification, and persistent machine-driven workflows
introduce heightened risks of performance degradation, interference with system functionality, and
security vulnerabilities. These risks were not fully contemplated in earlier information blocking
exceptions and/or guidance. CEHRT developers must maintain visibility into who or what, whether
human or machine, is accessing EHI and preserve the ability to implement reasonable technical
controls to safeguard system integrity and ensure reliable access for clinicians and patients.

Finally, we respectfully request that ONC explicitly recognize that CEHRT developers may disable,
suspend, or otherwise block automation technologies that breach commercial agreements, exceed
authorized access parameters, compromise system security, or are deployed in a manner intended

to circumvent negotiated exchange arrangements, such as automated extraction of EHI to avoid
provider contractual terms. Clarification that such actions constitute permissible protective
measures and not information blocking would provide necessary regulatory certainty and align the
information blocking framework with longstanding principles of system integrity, contractual
enforcement, and responsible system stewardship. We also suggest that the ONC consider the
appropriateness of reasonable technical standards, like Model Context Protocol (MCP) frameworks
that can enable appropriate and meaningful integration that can support responsible access and
use while limiting known risks associated with automation technologies.

Automation technologies offer meaningful opportunities to enhance care delivery and operational
efficiency, and we understand and support the need for such technologies to increase data access
and interoperability in healthcare. At the same time, their scale and technical capabilities warrant
careful calibration within the information blocking regulatory structure. We encourage ONC to pair
definitional updates with appropriate transition time and clearer guardrails to ensure that the
framework supports innovation while preserving security, system stability, and contractual integrity.

                                  Electronic Case Reporting (eCR)

athenahealth strongly supports Electronic Case Reporting (eCR) and the vision of seamless, secure
public health data exchange. eCR has the potential to significantly enhance disease surveillance and
public health reporting. At the same time, implementation remains challenging, as state and federal
public health agencies face resource constraints, outdated technologies, and high workloads. These
challenges create long wait times, complex testing, and connectivity issues. This unfortunate reality
imposes practical burdens on health IT developers and clinicians.

Given these realities, eCR requirements should align with the capacity of public health agencies and
health IT developers. This could be achieved through adequate funding and resources, or by
exploring alternative pathways to support effective and achievable public health reporting. Aligning
requirements in this way will advance public health objectives while avoiding unintended burdens,
consistent with HTI-5 goals of reducing regulatory complexity and supporting practical, scalable
interoperability solutions.
