          Dear Assistant Secretary Keane,

          On behalf of the County of Santa Clara (“County”), I submit this comment in response to the proposed
          rule published by the U.S. Department of Health and Human Services (HHS) on December 29, 2025,
          regarding ASTP/ONC Deregulatory Actions to Unleash Prosperity (HTI-5). Specifically, the County
          opposes the proposal to remove the standards-based electronic case reporting requirement in 45 CFR §
          170.315(f)(5)(ii) and to only require functional electronic case reporting to public health agencies.

          I.       The County has a significant interest in ensuring standardized, automated data
                   exchanges with healthcare organizations.

          Santa Clara County is home to approximately 1.9 million residents, making it the most populous
          county in Northern California and more populous than twelve U.S. states. The County’s Public Health
          Department is a local public health agency (PHA) dedicated to protecting and improving the health
          and well-being of all residents. In the area of infectious disease and emergency response, the Public
          Health Department is responsible for preventing and controlling the spread of infectious diseases and
          planning for and responding to public health emergencies. The Public Health Department’s Infectious
          Disease and Response Branch receives reports on more than 80 different diseases and conditions;
          tracks overall trends in infectious diseases; investigates individual cases of concern (e.g., measles
          cases); provides long term case management for certain types of cases (e.g., active tuberculosis cases);
          identifies, investigates, and controls disease outbreaks; plans for and responds to public health
          emergencies; conducts HIV and other STD testing and education for vulnerable communities;
          connects individuals to treatment for substance use disorders, HIV, tuberculosis, and other diseases;
          and provides immunizations and preventive therapy to community members.

          Because Santa Clara County has one of the largest and most demographically diverse populations in
          the country and is home to Silicon Valley, many residents travel to every corner of the globe for
          personal or professional reasons. It is also a popular destination for international business and tourist

          manual reports of diseases and conditions. Automated, standards-based data exchange also benefits
          healthcare providers by reducing reporting burdens, streamlining workflows, and ensuring compliance
          with reporting requirements mandated by law.

          II.      Challenges with non-standardized reporting

          Despite significant investment and technical progress, the Public Health Department’s data
          modernization efforts are inhibited by variability of incoming HCO-provided data. Variability in data
          standards is not a minor technical inconvenience; it is a material barrier to reliable public health data
          exchange. Different HCOs submit different versions of Clinical Document Architecture (CDA)
          documents with substantial variability, including inconsistent formatting and frequent data quality
          issues. As a result, our department spends significant time investigating and troubleshooting format
          inconsistencies, recoding and testing data pipelines to accommodate unexpected variations, and
          ensuring that changes do not impact downstream reports or cause data quality issues. This reactive
          work consumes limited public health technical staff time and information technology resources. These
          resources would otherwise be used to build stronger and more durable public health data systems that
          support department activities.

          III.     Impact of proposed deregulatory actions

          The County supports the goal of reducing unnecessary burden and cost for health IT. However,
          replacing established CDA standards with unconstrained functional requirements may increase, not
          reduce, burden across the ecosystem and endanger public health.

          Common, enforceable data standards are foundational to interoperability. Permitting greater flexibility
          in document structure without maintaining strong standardization requirements would result in PHAs
          receiving increasingly heterogeneous and inconsistent data which will:

          In practice, deregulation would shift the interoperability burden from vendors and HCOs to PHAs,
          which operate with fewer resources and declining funding. It will require PHAs to develop and/or
          procure complex solutions to accommodate increased data complexity and variability and leave less-
          well-resourced jurisdictions reliant on legacy systems and data sources. Such a shift undermines the
          progress that PHAs have made toward automated case reporting and enables HCOs to retain manual
          reporting processes and legacy notification mechanisms that are slower, less efficient, and less
          reliable. This, in turn, will leave communities across the country more vulnerable during the next
          major outbreak or pandemic.

          IV.       Importance of strong data standards

          Public health modernization depends on consistent, enforceable data standards that ensure predictable
          structure, semantic integrity, and implementation alignment. Guardrails are not barriers to innovation;
          they are prerequisites for scalable interoperability. For the reasons stated herein, the County opposes
          this proposed rule change to remove the standards-based electronic case reporting requirement and to
          only require functional electronic case reporting to public health agencies. The County urges the U.S.
          Department of Health and Human Services to maintain and improve existing policies that advance
          national interoperability goals and promote modernization through clear, consistent standards.
