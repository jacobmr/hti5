The Blue Cross Blue Shield Association (BCBSA) appreciates the opportunity to provide
comments on Health Data, Technology, and Interoperability: ASTP/ONC Deregulatory Actions to
Unleash Prosperity (HTI-5) Proposed Rule as issued in the Federal Register on December 29,
2025 (90 FR 60970).

The Blue Cross Blue Shield Association is a national federation of independent, community-
based, and locally operated Blue Cross and Blue Shield companies (Plans) that collectively
provide health care coverage for one in three Americans. Our Plans contract with 96% of
hospitals and 95% of doctors across the country and serve those who are covered through
Medicare, Medicaid, an employer, or purchase coverage on their own.
BCBS Plans have a long-standing commitment to improving the interoperability and
transparency of health care information. The secure and seamless flow of health data among
patients, doctors, hospitals, and health insurance providers is essential to reducing costs and
driving positive health outcomes. BCBSA and Blue Plans have been leaders on these issues,
engaging in numerous initiatives to advance the access, exchange, and use of health data.
BCBSA is a founding member in the Health Level 7 (HL7) Fast Healthcare Interoperability
Resources (FHIR) Application Programming Interface (API) standards development efforts,
including the HL7® Da Vinci Project, as well as the Gravity Project which is working to advance
data standards. BCBSA has also been a longtime collaborator with the Sequoia Project. Plans
are also active members across the HL7, the DaVinci Project, the Workgroup for Electronic Data

Interchange (WEDI), and the FHIR at Scale Taskforce. Through these efforts, BCBSA and Plans
seek to align the health care ecosystem and drive collective action towards modernizing the
health care system.
BCBSA supports ASTP/ONC’s efforts to streamline requirements and improve the
usability and adoption of certified health IT articulated in HTI-5. Streamlining redundant
certification requirements and evolving interoperability standards can reduce complexity across
the health IT ecosystem, support more timely adoption of interoperable solutions and standards,
reduce variation in implementation, and allow regulators to focus on the areas of greatest
consumer and operational impact. All of these impacts will help support the ecosystem’s
movement towards real-world, bidirectional data exchange.

Within ASTP/ONC’s proposed reductions, we recommend ASTP retain the specific C-CDA
creation criteria for the time being. We can see a future state where these criteria can be
sunset, but it is important that deregulatory actions preserve the stability, quality, and
consistency of clinical information exchange during the industry’s transition to API-based
interoperability. While FHIR-based exchange represents the future state, C-CDA artifacts remain
foundational to many existing clinical and operational workflows relied upon by BCBS Plans,
providers, and other stakeholders. Premature reductions in certification requirements related to
the creation of standardized, high-quality C-CDA documents could risk fragmenting data
exchange and undermining interoperability gains achieved over the past decade.

We have included detailed comments on the rule below. We are also using this opportunity to
reinforce BCBSA’s recommendations on how the Administration can improve real-time data
sharing. Specifically, we urge ASTP/ONC to continue working with its HHS partners to address
the following policy barriers that are currently limiting real-time data sharing:

• Support adoption of API technology across the ecosystem to drive real-time data
sharing. As payers make the investments to drive interoperability, including electronic
prior authorization APIs, these tools can only drive value for patients if adopted across
the ecosystem.

       To drive universal adoption, we urge ASTP to work with CMS to 1) amend Medicare
       Conditions of Participation to require hospitals and clinicians to enable and use FHIR-
       based APIs; 2) create a "Provider Interoperability Readiness" grant for small and rural
       providers to implement APIs and electronic health record (EHR) upgrades; 3) publish
       and maintain a national endpoint directory, requiring payers, providers and EHR
       developers to register their endpoints; and 4) continue to promote interoperability
       through strengthened Promoting Interoperability (PI) provider performance
       measurement.

• Promote payer participation in data-sharing initiatives. We support CMS’ Aligned
Networks and ASTP’s TEFCA - these could be essential tools to advance interoperability
goals. However, today, there is not a centralized solution which can maximize
connectivity with providers nationally and with which payers can meaningfully engage.
To improve this engagement and drive interoperability forward, we recommend ASTP: 1)
establish a federal advisory committee - with meaningful payer representation - to

BCBSA Detailed Comments and Recommendations on the Proposed Rule

Issue #1: Revision of the transition-of-care criterion
HTI-5 proposes to revise the transitions of care certification criteria as of January 1, 2027.
Revisions include removing the requirements in “send and receive via edge protocol” and
“create” and to simplify the requirements “validate and display” to focus on the ability to receive
and validate C-CDA documents.
Recommendation: BCBSA recommends retaining C-CDA creation requirements until FHIR-
based transition-of-care exchange is mature and widely implemented, with any future reductions
phased to protect data quality.
Rationale: Although there is strong momentum toward FHIR-based APIs, C-CDA remains the
predominant and most reliable mechanism for exchanging comprehensive clinical information
across care settings today. This reflects more than a decade of coordinated investment across
the healthcare ecosystem. While FHIR-based APIs represent the future state, high-quality C-
CDA artifacts remain essential during the transition period.
In addition, structured C-CDA documents also play a critical role in seeding FHIR-based
longitudinal records, supporting mappings between USCDI elements and FHIR resources and
enabling hybrid exchange models. Reducing C-CDA creation requirements before functionally
equivalent FHIR-based transition-of-care mechanisms are widely deployed risks data
degradation and could-slow, rather than accelerate, the migration to APIs.
Finally, baseline C-CDA creation requirements have been instrumental in improving data quality
and consistency. Eliminating these requirements risks undermining clinical decision-making,
continuity of care, and interoperability gains achieved under prior certification editions. A phased
approach that preserves C-CDA robustness while FHIR- based workflows mature aligns best
with ASTP/ONCs goal of pragmatic, incremental modernization.
Issue #2: Removal of the Common Clinical Data Set (CCDS) as a Certification Concept

HTI-5 proposes to eliminate the Common Clinical Data Set (CCDS) construct, replacing it
with reliance on United States Core Data for Interoperability (USCDI) as the sole standardized
data baseline. This simplifies certification while removing a long-standing conceptual anchor
used across federal programs.
Recommendation: BCBSA supports replacing CCDS with USCDI as the foundational baseline
for certification and data exchange expectations.
Rationale: CCDS has served as a helpful conceptual anchor across federal programs for many
years. However, the industry has steadily and consistently migrated toward the USCDI as the
more comprehensive and future-oriented standard. Also, all certified products which meet the
base EHR definition support USCDI. Importantly, CCDS and USCDI substantially overlap, and
maintaining both concepts in parallel creates unnecessary complexity without providing
meaningful policy or operational benefits.
Transitioning fully to USCDI offers several advantages:

                                                 4

• Regulatory clarity and simplification. Consolidating around a single, authoritative data
standard removes ambiguity for implementers and reduces interpretive variance across
payers, providers, and vendors.
• Alignment across CMS, ASTP/ONC, and industry frameworks. USCDI already serves as
the data foundation for Patient Access APIs, Provider Access APIs, Payer-to-Payer
exchange requirements, TEFCA participation, and multiple value-based care programs.
Making USCDI the singular baseline strengthens coherence across regulatory efforts.
Stakeholders currently navigate multiple data constructs across programs; eliminating
CCDS reduces the burden of reconciling overlapping definitions and lowers the risk of
inconsistent or incomplete data mapping.
• A predictable mechanism for updates. Because USCDI is updated through a public,
structured process, it allows future data elements to be added in a predictable, orderly
way. Supports long-term interoperability goals. USCDI provides the flexibility needed to
evolve with the ecosystem—expanding to include data relevant to care coordination,
social needs, digital health, and emerging clinical innovations. Retiring CCDS
accelerates alignment with modern interoperability architectures and API-based
exchange.
Issue #3: Revision of “Access,” “Exchange,” and “Use” Definitions (Information Blocking)
HTI-5 proposes to update the information blocking definitions “access” and “use” – to clarify that
they include automated means of access, exchange, or use of electronic health information
(EHI), including autonomous AI systems.
Recommendation: BCBSA supports the updating of the information blocking definitions to
clarify that they include automated means including autonomous AI systems.
Rationale: Updating the definitions of ‘access,’ ‘exchange,’ and ‘use’ to explicitly include
automated, machine-to-machine activities — including autonomous AI systems — is an
important modernization of the information blocking framework. Today, much of the industry’s
data retrieval, routing, and analysis occurs through automated processes, and clarity that these
activities fall within the existing definitions helps ensure consistent expectations across
developers, payers, and providers without creating new obligations.
Some providers may express concern that explicitly recognizing automated access could
expand their compliance responsibilities or expose them to new liability tied to systems they do
not directly control. However, the proposed updates do not create any new requirements for
providers; rather, they simply clarify how long-standing definitions apply in modern technical
environments. This clarity benefits providers by reducing ambiguity, ensuring that their existing
automated workflows remain compliant, and preventing inconsistent interpretations across the
ecosystem.
By modernizing these definitions while maintaining the current compliance structure, the
proposal strengthens predictability, supports emerging technologies, and reduces the risk that
outdated terminology could inadvertently impede legitimate, low-burden data exchange.

BCBSA Detailed Comments and Recommendations on Improving Real-time Data Sharing
Issue #4 Optimizing Real-Time Data-Sharing
Despite significant federal investment and sustained regulatory attention, the current
interoperability landscape does not consistently support real-time, bi-directional data sharing
between payers and providers. These limitations are not driven by a lack of legal authority,
technical standards, or national frameworks—the foundational elements are largely in place.
Rather, progress is constrained by three interrelated gaps.

• Continued misunderstanding of HIPAA flexibilities. Many providers remain uncertain
about the scope of data sharing permitted under HIPAA, including disclosures for
treatment, care coordination, quality improvement, and health care operations. This
uncertainty results in unwarranted restrictions on data sharing, delayed exchange, and
inconsistent payer access to clinical information necessary for timely decision-making.

• Uneven adoption of modern interoperability technologies. While FHIR-based APIs,
event notifications, and interoperable data services are increasingly available, adoption
varies significantly across providers, EHR vendors, and networks. This uneven
readiness contributes to fragmented data flows, reliance on manual processes, and
delays in prior authorization, care management, and quality reporting.

• Supporting payer participation in developing national data-sharing frameworks.
Federal initiatives such as the Health Technology Ecosystem, CMS-Aligned Networks,
and TEFCA have the potential to support more standardized and scalable
interoperability overtime. However, current models do not consistently incorporate payer
connectivity or address structural barriers—such as fees, inconsistent access pathways,
privacy and security alignment, and constrained use cases—that limit meaningful payer
participation. In particular, the absence of a prioritized payer-to-payer use case under
TEFCA limits its ability to support continuity of coverage and care during member
transitions. Achieving truly bi-directional exchange will depend on creating conditions
that meaningfully support and incentivize payer, as well as other health ecosystem
stakeholder, participation.
Together, these gaps prevent progress towards reliable, real-time data exchange at scale,
undermining federal interoperability goals and constraining improvements in patient experience,
clinical decision support, administrative efficiency, and affordability.
Recommendation #1: BCBSA urges ASTP/ONC to encourage HHS/OCR to issue sub-
regulatory guidance with examples to clarify the permitted purposes for which Covered Entities
can share PHI data under HIPAA.
Rationale: Many providers are cautious about sharing clinical patient data with health plans for
care coordination and management purposes, concerned with running counter to Health
Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA) Minimum Necessary Standards. Despite
this sharing being allowable under HIPAA today, these reservations limit the ability for health
plans to work with providers to drive better outcomes for patients. Providing clarity that clinical
data is within the scope of permissible data sharing for care coordination, care management
and other treatment functions – and is encouraged – would promote the more meaningful and

                                                 6

efficient sharing of data across the health care ecosystem, which in turn, supports the delivery
of appropriate patient care and better outcomes.
Recommendation #2: BCBSA recommends ASTP work with CMS to create a new “Provider
Interoperability Readiness” grant under CMMI for small and rural providers to implement payer
facing APIs and EHR upgrades.
Rationale: To support equitable adoption, targeted federal grants should be made available to
help providers, particularly those with limited resources, modernize their infrastructure and meet
technical standards. While these changes are essential to modernizing health care and should
be required for all providers, as they are for payers, some providers will need support to stand
them up effectively and securely. This grant would complement the federal funds currently being
awarded to states to further support interoperability and digital infrastructure in rural areas under
the Rural Health Transformation Program (RHTP) and could be used to target support to
providers who are not already supported under the RHTP state grants.
Recommendation #3: We recommend ASTP/ONC take steps to expand access to high-quality
synthetic data to support interoperability testing, Connectathons, and responsible AI
development.
Rationale: To support full interoperability across the ecosystem, we expect and support the
development and implementation of additional data and use cases for APIs and other emerging
technologies. In addition, AI-enabled tools are being increasingly integrated into clinical and
administrative workflows to improve efficiency and drive improved consumer outcomes. All of
these modernization efforts require real-word, robust testing to protect against unintended and
unexpected complications and impact. The availability of high-quality synthetic data that can be
used to facilitate this testing is essential to support innovation without introducing privacy or
security risk.
ASTP/ONC prioritizing the development and dissemination of representative synthetic datasets
that can be used by providers, payers, EHR developers, and other ecosystem participants to
test would lower barriers to participation, improve implementation quality, and accelerate
adoption of real-time, standards-based exchange across the ecosystem.
Recommendation #4: CMS, in coordination with ASTP, should publish and maintain a national
endpoint directory (NED) with appropriate governance and security safeguards, requiring
providers, EHR developers and payers to register their endpoints.
Rationale: CMS should establish, publish, and maintain a National Endpoint Directory that
serves as the authoritative, nationwide source for FHIR and other exchange endpoints across
providers, payers, and certified health IT developers. CMS, in coordination with ASTP and
industry input, should define governance, validation, and minimum security requirements for
endpoint registration, and require or strongly encourage the following stakeholders to register
their endpoints:

• Providers and suppliers enrolled in Medicare, Medicaid, or Marketplace plans
• Certified health IT developers under 45 CFR Part 170

                                                 7

• Payers subject to the CMS Interoperability and Patient Access Rule (42 CFR §§
422.119, 431.60, 438.242(b)(5) 457.730, and 45 CFR § 156.221-223) to register and
maintain their active exchange endpoints within the NED.
Registrant should submit and quarterly attest to data elements — including FHIR-based URLs,
endpoint purposes (e.g., Patient Access API, Payer-to-Payer API, TEFCA QHIN node), security
metadata (e.g., OAuth 2.0 issuer, certificate thumbprint) and contact information — using a
standardized API aligned with the HL7 National Directory for Healthcare (NDH) Implementation
Guide. The NED should be accessible through authenticated, role-appropriate query
mechanisms, accessible to query through RESTful APIs and bulk downloadable formats to
enable automated endpoint discovery by payers, EHRs and HIEs.
CMS should integrate compliance into existing programs by adding a Condition of Participation
for providers, requiring certified developers to demonstrate endpoint registration as part of ASTP
certification, and ensuring payer participation under existing CMS interoperability rules.
Leveraging NDH architecture will avoid duplication, reduce administrative burden and create a
unified infrastructure for real-time, accurate endpoint discovery that enables secure, standards-
based connectivity nationwide. It would streamline connectivity by enabling payers to reliably
locate and communicate with provider systems, reducing friction and improving data routing
accuracy.
This National Endpoints Directory can – and should – build on the work CMS has already
started through CMS’ National Directory of Healthcare Providers and Services (NDH) pilot and
the National Plan and Provider Enumeration System (NPPES) digital contact infrastructure.
Through these projects, CMS has identified both the need for a centralized source of digital
endpoint information and the importance of aligning such infrastructure with existing federal
interoperability and security frameworks, and that it shares understanding of the challenges and
the need for a centralized source of information to fuel provider directories.
Recommendation #5: CMS and ASTP/ONC should align the National Endpoint Directory with
existing federal provider directory efforts.
Rationale: Fragmented directory requirements across programs contribute to inaccurate data,
duplicative reporting, and inconsistent endpoint discovery. A coordinated, authoritative
directory—aligned with NDH and existing CMS infrastructure—would improve data accuracy,
reduce administrative burden, and strengthen interoperability across payers, providers, and
health IT developers.
Recommendation #6: ASTP should encourage CMS to amend Medicare Conditions of
Participation (CoP) to require hospitals and clinicians to enable FAST Healthcare Interoperability
Resources (FHIR)-based application programing interfaces (APIs) for electronic prior
authorization (ePA).
Rationale: Consistent with HHS’ goals of moving the health care industry into the 21st century,
BCBSA and Blue Plans are committed to making real-time, protected data sharing across
payers, providers, and patients a reality. BCBSA has long been an advocate in driving these
improvements across the health care sector and the BCBS System has taken action over the
last several years to implement many of the technical and operational improvements to make
this vision a reality for patients. For example, health plans across the country are deploying the

                                                 8

FHIR-based APIs required under CMS-0057 for Provider Access, Payer-to-Payer, and Prior
Authorization exchange. Yet, the BCBS System has made commitments to go beyond CMS-
0057 and extend the Provider Access and Prior Authorization APIs to commercial lines of
business.
However, many providers are behind in making the same investments and are not required to
utilize the APIs plans are building. Today there is significant variability in providers’ adoption of
the data standards and infrastructure needed to support interoperable, real-time data sharing
with payers. This delays care decisions for patients, creates additional privacy and security
risks, and takes provider resources away from the delivery of care.
We recognize this would be a significant change for some smaller and less well-resourced
providers. As such, we encourage CMS to implement a policy that requires providers to use the
APIs. For providers who can demonstrate a need for a delay, CMS should provide a time-limited
extension and – in conjunction with ASTP – technical support to achieve adoption.
Requiring this change – with appropriate support – of all providers accepting Medicare,
Medicaid, and Qualified Health Plan beneficiaries/members would accelerate nationwide
adoption of standardized APIs, close persistent gaps, and support CMS value-based care
strategy by making timely, bidirectional data an expectation.
Recommendation #7: As CMS works toward Medicare Condition of Participation requirements,
we recommend ASTP encourage CMS to continue to promote interoperability through a
strengthened Promoting Interoperability provider performance metric of “percent of prior
authorizations transmitted electronically with supporting clinical documentation via FHIR-based
APIs”.
Rationale: Prior authorization (PA) is a common transaction between providers and payers,
with significant implications for patient safety and access to affordable care. Despite this, it is
also the most reliant on antiquated technologies, including paper submissions and faxes. PA is a
critical tool to maintain patient safety and affordability, but its value is undermined when the
system relies on slow, resource intensive communications streams like fax machines.
Health plans have committed to moving these transactions into real-time, standardized
pathways to reduce provider burden and ensure patients can get timely access to save,
affordable care. However, these investments only result in improved experiences for patients
when implemented – and used – by health plans and providers.
While CMS works towards making real-time data sharing a condition of participation for
providers in Medicare, we urge CMS to include a targeted performance measure to the CMS
Promoting Interoperability (PI) Program that goes beyond one transaction to measure ongoing
use of these real-time pathways. Measuring the percentage of prior authorizations transmitted
electronically with supporting documentation provides a direct, objective indicator of
interoperability progress between payers and providers.
By integrating this metric into the PI Program, CMS would:

• Encourage adoption of FHIR-based prior authorization APIs across providers and EHR
vendors.

• Create accountability for clinical attachment exchange which is an existing gap in
interoperability.
• Reinforce alignment between the CMS Promoting Interoperability Program and payer
interoperability requirements.
This measure ultimately supports the goals of the 21st Century Cures Act and HHS’ broader
Health Technology Ecosystem strategy by shifting interoperability from capability to
demonstrable, outcome-based exchange.
Recommendation #8: We recommend ASTP/ONC elevate data quality as a core
interoperability priority through collaboration with provider systems, health IT developers,
payers, and other ecosystem participants.
Rationale: Real-time, bidirectional data exchange depends not only on the availability of
standards, APIs, and supportive policy, but also on the quality of the data being exchanged.
Incomplete, inconsistent, or poorly structured data undermines the effectiveness of
interoperability investments, limits the value of real-time exchange for care coordination and
utilization management, and creates downstream challenges for quality reporting and
administrative efficiency. Even where standards such as C-CDA and FHIR are used, variation in
data entry and structure requires payers to invest in resource-intensive processes to assess
completeness, appropriateness of use, and provenance.
As health care data is increasingly used for advanced analytics and AIenabled tools, data
quality has become even more critical. AI systems are highly dependent on the reliability,
completeness, and consistency of underlying data, and deficiencies in data quality can amplify
risk, bias, and operational inefficiency.
While BCBSA is not recommending a specific data quality framework at this time, we
recommend ASTP/ONC place greater emphasis on improving data quality as part of its
interoperability strategy. This could include identifying priority data elements, promoting
consistent implementation of existing standards, and encouraging shared accountability for data
quality across the ecosystem.
