Dear ASTP/ONC Leadership:

CareQuest Institute for Oral Health appreciates the opportunity to provide comments on the HTI-5
proposed rule. We appreciate the Department’s efforts to reduce administrative burden and advance
a “FHIR-forward” interoperability strategy. However, we write to express significant concerns
regarding the proposed elimination of Consolidated Clinical Document Architecture (C-CDA)
generation requirements under § 170.315(b)(1), along with the removal of associated reconciliation,
care planning, and decision support transparency certification criteria.

CareQuest Institute for Oral Health is a national nonprofit working to improve everyone’s oral health
– because oral health is health.

As an organization committed to making oral health care more accessible and integrated, we
respectfully offer the following observations and recommendations to ensure that oral health is
more fully incorporated into the national health information technology (IT) and future policy
frameworks. Oral health is critical to better health, and inclusion of oral health data is crucial to
achieving ONC’s goals to strengthen nationwide health information interoperability and to better
support real-world data exchange while reducing unnecessary burden.

For the dental industry and for our vision of integrated and coordinated care inclusive of oral health,
the transition of care and decision support changes highlighted below risk creating an
“interoperability breakdown” that could challenge efforts to advance medical-dental integration (MDI),
threaten patient safety, increase administrative burden, and force providers back into manual, error-
prone workflows during medical-to-dental transitions of care.

1. The “FHIR Gap” and the Risk of Reinforcing Legacy Silos (§ 170.315(b)(1) – Transitions
   of Care)

While the medical sector has made substantial progress toward FHIR-based APIs, the dental sector
continues to operate in a lightly regulated environment without formal federal interoperability
mandates. As a result, many Electronic Dental Record (EDR) systems remain in early or incomplete
stages of FHIR adoption.

Today, C-CDA documents serve as the most reliable and widely implemented mechanism for
sharing structured clinical summaries from medical to dental providers. Eliminating the requirement
for certified medical EHRs to generate C-CDAs, before a viable, widely adopted FHIR-based
alternative exists for dentistry, will effectively further isolate dental providers in a “legacy silo.” If
medical systems are no longer required to generate standardized summaries, the only proven
interoperability bridge supporting MDI will be removed before the dental sector is technically
positioned to replace it.
This shift risks creating gaps in real-world care coordination, particularly for specialty and safety-net
dental providers who rely on document-based exchange to access critical medical context at the
point of care.

2. Erosion of Clinical Context and Patient Safety (§ 170.315(b)(1) – Transitions of Care; §
   170.315(b)(9) – Care Plan)

C-CDA documents provide standardized, structured clinical data, including problem lists,
medications, allergies, laboratory results, and care context. This information is essential for safe and
appropriate dental treatment. These data elements cannot be reliably reconstructed through
unstructured communications or incomplete point-in-time queries.

Eliminating the mandate to generate C-CDAs introduces material patient safety risks, including but
not limited to:

• Anticoagulant Therapy: Without structured summaries, dental providers may not be alerted
to anticoagulant use, bleeding risk, or recent INR values prior to invasive procedures.
• Chronic Disease Management: For diabetes patients, access to HbA1c results and related
care plans is critical for assessing infection risk and healing during periodontal and surgical
procedures.
• Recent Cardiac Events: C-CDAs ensure dental providers are informed of contraindications
for elective care following recent myocardial infarction, stent placement, or other cardiac
interventions.

In the absence of a mandated, standardized summary mechanism, this information is far more likely
to be incomplete, delayed, or lost entirely, increasing the risk of adverse events.

3. Impact of Removing Reconciliation and Care Plan Certification Criteria (§ 170.315(b)(2) –
   Clinical Information Reconciliation and Incorporation (CIRI) and§ 170.315(b)(9) – Care Plan)

We are also concerned about the proposed removal of Clinical Information Reconciliation and
Incorporation (CIRI) and Care Plan certification requirements.

• Increased Administrative Burden: Eliminating CIRI removes the expectation that systems
support reconciliation of medications, allergies, and problem lists from external sources,
increasing reliance on manual transcription and significantly raising the risk of errors.
• Fragmented Care Coordination: The removal of structured care plan requirements
weakens the ability to manage patients with complex medical and oral health needs, directly
undermining integrated, team-based care models.

These proposed changes would imperil federal goals of improving care coordination, patient safety,
and data quality across care settings.

4. Rollback of AI and Decision Support Transparency (§ 170.315(b)(11) – Decision Support
   Interventions (DSI))

HTI-5 also proposes to remove requirements related to source attribute transparency and
intervention risk management for predictive Decision Support Interventions.
While we recognize the intent to reduce compliance burden and encourage innovation, this rollback
raises concerns for both patient safety and clinical trust, specifically in medical-dental workflows
where dental providers may rely on decision support generated outside their primary system of
record.

• Loss of Transparency: Removing source attribution limits clinicians’ ability to understand
the provenance, intended use, and limitations of predictive models informing care decisions.
• Increased Clinical Risk: Eliminating intervention risk management requirements may allow
predictive tools to be deployed without sufficient guardrails, monitoring, or mitigation
strategies.
• Cross-Disciplinary Impact: In MDI use cases, dental providers may be asked to act on risk
scores or recommendations derived from medical data without visibility into how those
outputs were generated or validated.

Recommendation
We strongly urge ASTP/ONC to maintain the following certification requirements, at least for a
defined transition period that allows the dental industry and similar ancillary sectors to align with
FHIR-based interoperability and emerging AI governance standards:

• § 170.315(b)(1) – C-CDA generation for transitions of care
• § 170.315(b)(2) – Clinical Information Reconciliation and Incorporation
• § 170.315(b)(9) – Care Plan
• § 170.315(b)(11) – Decision Support transparency and risk management

Premature elimination of these requirements could:
• Create avoidable gaps in medical-to-dental care coordination
• Increase administrative burden on providers
• Heighten patient safety risks
• Undermine trust in AI-enabled clinical decision support

We respectfully ask ONC to consider the unique regulatory, technical, and market realities of the oral
health sector as part of its broader interoperability strategy.

Thank you for your consideration and for your continued leadership in advancing a secure, patient-
centered, and interoperable healthcare ecosystem.
