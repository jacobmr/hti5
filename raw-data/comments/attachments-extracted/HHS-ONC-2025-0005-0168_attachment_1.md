technology. As a mission-driven non-profit, HIMSS offers a unique depth and breadth
of expertise in health innovation, public policy, workforce development, research, and

To accelerate digital health transformation, we must foster seamless, secure, and
ubiquitous information access and interoperable health exchange. Health information
and technology can transform the health ecosystem, modernize care delivery, drive
health innovation, improve efficiency, and enable health research. Actionable health
information is critical to identifying the best pathway to Make America Healthy Again.

Re-Imagining Information Access and Information Blocking Proposals

For ASTP to drive seamless, ubiquitous, and patient-centric information access, the
foundation of policy frameworks must be grounded in an understanding of the
economic impact of those policies. Federal policy, through the Cures Act and HTI
rulemaking, has expanded expectations for interoperability and data
accessibility. Yet, these requirements have increasingly burdened hospitals and health
IT developers with limited measurable improvement in information availability.

The growth of health data, combined with increased regulatory requirements, is
increasingly transforming hospitals, healthcare practices, and health IT developers into
de factor cloud data management companies, a role that they are not structurally or
financially equipped to perform.
The result is a misalignment of incentives: providers bear the technical and economic
burden of enabling exchange, while the commercial value created through secondary
use of data accrues to downstream technology firms. Allowing agentic and
autonomous artificial intelligence (AI) to make information requests could exponentially
increase the costs of the infrastructure providers and hospitals need to validate and
facilitate those requests.

Fundamentally, HIMSS supports ASTP’s proposals to revise the definitions of “access”
and “use” of electronic health information (EHI) to enable AI-enabled information
requests and address loopholes being leveraged to withhold appropriate access to
patient EHI. However, the fundamental inefficiency in the current information access
framework is rooted in the imbalance between who pays for the cost of information
access and who benefits from that access, not in the manner and accompanying
standards determining how information is exchanged. Hospitals and health systems
carry ongoing costs for data access infrastructure that enable other organizations’
revenue-generating services. Under TEFCA and related frameworks, participants
cannot charge for individual access services, even as emerging rules expand who and
what (including AI agents and automated bots) may initiate such requests. The
structure is the root cause of the perverse incentives that limit information sharing and
slow the pace of digital health transformation.

HIMSS believes API-based architectures do not inherently conflict with the CURES Act’s
“without special effort” requirement while simultaneously unburdening providers from
supporting unlimited third-party workflows. HIMSS’ work to find drivers to increase
adoption of Individual Access Service through the ASTP LEAP grant program should help
identify potential solutions to these economic barriers. HIMSS looks forward to joining
and facilitating the conversation about how information exchange architectures
can speed the digital transformation of healthcare.

Reducing Regulatory Burden and Transitioning to API-Enabled Information Access

HIMSS strongly endorses the thoughtful transition to API-enabled information
access, leveraging mature standards, data classes and elements where appropriate.
HIMSS also supports Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services (CMS) efforts to
reduce the regulatory burden on developers and providers.

However, policy changes intended to drive digital health transformation must be
conducted in a thoughtful manner, with recognition of the economic drivers and digital
maturity of different sectors in the healthcare ecosystem. Addressing those market
drivers and the limitations of different sectors of the ecosystem will create a stronger
foundation to drive digital transformation without leaving systems and their patients and
communities behind.

Removal of functional criteria may place some providers in a challenging
position. Many of the functions proposed for removal support actions required by CMS
and state programs, as well as core operational and clinical functions at the point of
care. If these core functions (particularly those that ASTP expects developers will
continue to support due to market forces) are removed from certification, HIMSS
encourages ASTP to maintain regular touchpoints with end users to assess whether

removing those criteria has negatively impacted their workflows and compliance
activities.

HIMSS also recommends that all certification program requirements align with measures
required by various CMS reporting programs, such as the CMS Promoting
Interoperability Program for eligible hospitals and the Promoting Interoperability and
Quality Measurement/Reporting components for the MIPS program. Any functionality
removed from certification should require a corresponding removal of a provider-
required action from a CMS program where the provider utilizes that functionality. ASTP
and CMS need to coordinate on the dates when a certification change is required and
the corresponding compliance dates for hospitals and providers under CMS programs.

Finally, consistent timelines for regulatory changes that align with current
implementation cycles are critical for successful implementations. HIMSS strongly
recommends that any updated changes to certification requirements allow a two-
year window of voluntary compliance following the publication of a requirement in final
rulemaking. CMS has utilized similar timelines for implementing new quality measures
into the MIPS and Inpatient Quality Reporting Programs. For example, if a new
certification requirement is finalized in the HTI-5 final rule in 2026, the criteria should be
voluntary until CY2028.

HIMSS also recommends that CMS utilize the same timeline for regulatory changes to
the Promoting Interoperability Program for eligible hospitals and the MIPS program that
correspond with changes to the ASTP certification program. For example, if a functional
criterion is proposed for removal, then removed from the ASTP certification program,
CMS should coordinate a similar action to remove corresponding requirements that
leverage the removed functionality from Promoting Interoperability requirements using
the same two-year timeline.

The Transition from C-CDA Standards to FHIR

HIMSS wants to ensure that FHIR standards are adopted by the widest range of
participants in the healthcare ecosystem, while balancing the state of readiness of
available standards and technologies.

Functionality associated with Consolidated Clinical Document Architecture (C-CDA)
presents an unique challenge given the current infrastructure capabilities of the
healthcare ecosystem, particularly public health agencies. The C-CDA format is
outdated because it was conceived before modern, API-driven interoperability
frameworks existed. Unlike FHIR, which is designed for real-time, modular data
exchange, C-CDA relies on static documents that require significant parsing and
transformation to extract usable information. HIMSS supports ASTP plan to move away
from C-CDA supported certification functionality.

In recent years, ASTP and the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention have made
progress toward moving the public health community to use FHIR API solutions.
However, public health authorities have cited limited resources and limited adoption of
feasible public health use cases as reasons for delaying the move from C-CDA to FHIR-
APIs. For example, during the COVID-19 Pandemic, electronic case reporting became
a vital function for public health data collection and reporting. The eCR process is

heavily dependent on C-CDA infrastructure. Health IT developers have indicated they
will need to continue to support C-CDA functionality, particularly for electronic case
reporting, to meet the needs of their customers’ compliance requirements at the State,
Territorial, Local, and Tribal levels until enough resources are available to prompt the
conversion to FHIR-API solutions.

In the 2022 Public Health Information and Technology Infrastructure Modernization
Funding Report, HIMSS estimated that state and local public health agencies will
require a $36.7 billion dollar investment over the next decade to meet moder public
health data collection and reporting requirements and transition to a Learning Health
System. This investment is intended to fund adoption and maintenance of API-enabled
infrastructure and invest in the expertise needed to support it. HIMSS will continue to
encourage Congress to provide the funding needed for the entire public health
ecosystem, along with behavioral health, community services, and other health sectors
that did not receive ARRA-HITECH (Meaningful Use) incentives and are
not impacted by CMS payment incentives, to adopt FHIR-API enabled Health IT. Until
congressional action takes place, HIMSS encourages ASTP and the entire Department
of Health and Human Services apparatus to leverage every funding mechanism at their
disposal to fund FHIR API-enabled health IT investment in all health sectors that were left
out of HITECH funding (eg. public health, LTPAC, behavioral health, dentistry, and
community services organizations.) Without that infrastructure, significant gaps
will emerge, creating future costs for developers and providers and barriers to
information access and sharing.

Finally, for this section of the proposal rule, HIMSS does support ASTP’s proposed removal
of criteria § 170.315(f)(4) supporting cancer registry reporting effective January 1, 2027.
HIMSS membership reports that industry is shifting from C-CDA to
FHIR standards leveraging Helios and USCDI+ Cancer standards and data elements. In
addition, developers report very low use rates of the present C-CDA-supported
criteria. In addition, HIMSS supports the removal of Transmission to public health
agencies—antimicrobial use and resistance reporting § 170.315(f)(6) from the program,
as it has been poorly adopted and is limited by the C-CDA infrastructure.

USCDI v.3.1 as a baseline

Rapid changes to USCDI data element set place significant strain on developers to
support standards advancements. Every proposed change to USCDI
also imposes additional costs to providers at a point in time when margins in the
healthcare sector are razor thin. On a conceptual level, HIMSS supports the goal of
creating a strong USCDI baseline for certification, which allows for stability and market
certainty.

However, to advance digital health transformation, advancement of the USCDI
standard is critical. HIMSS encourages ASTP to closely monitor the advancement of
USCDI through participation in the Standards Version Advancement Process (SVAP.). If
ASTP determines that developers are not advancing standards to support the changing
needs of the healthcare sector, HIMSS recommends that any mandated
advancements of USCDI incorporate a two-year implementation period, as described
above. For example, if a mandated advancement to USCDI v7 is required in a 2027

Securing Privacy of Patient Information

HIMSS thanks ASTP for the thoughtful comments about the misalignment between
current privacy and security criteria for the certification program and
strong protection for patient data. HIMSS agrees with ASTP’s assessment that privacy
and security of data should happen system wide instead of at an application
level. Requirements for developers to consistently demonstrate functionality
that certified products have already incorporated create a
significant additional administrative burden and cost that could be directed towards
improving functionality and protection within their systems. Removing these criteria from
the certification program does not remove obligations under HIPAA for a covered
entity or business associate.

HIMSS strongly recommends that ASTP and other HHS leaders convene with industry to,
within the framework of existing law, develop a uniform nationwide governance
structure for health cybersecurity and information privacy through the creation and
adoption of consensus-based, and industry-led guidelines, best practices,
methodologies, procedures, and processes. This structure should include use cases and
implementation guidance that is scalable for a wide range of healthcare organizations
and inclusive of all provider levels and all entities that use and access health
information. HIMSS will continue to advocate for Congress to amend HIPAA to clarify
that all actors (people, applications, artificial intelligence) that “access” or
“use” EHI should be subject to the legal obligations presently required for HIPAA
covered entities and business associates.

If ASTP decides to retain any privacy and security certification criteria, HIMSS
encourages ASTP to revise maintenance of certification for these criteria to reduce
testing and reporting burden on developers. HIMSS welcomes the opportunity to
convene ASTP leaders with developers before publishing a final rule
to identify specific opportunities where burden can be reduced.

TEFCA Manner Exception

HIMSS agrees that the current TEFCA Manner Exception has not motivated
organizations to join or use TEFCA. However, simply deleting the exception will not, by
itself, increase participation in trusted exchange.

HIMSS members originally supported the Manner Exception because they wanted a
clear safe harbor: if an organization exchanges data through TEFCA in accordance
with the rules, it should be protected from information-blocking claims. Today’s
exception does not provide clear protection, and recent concerns about misuse of
data by some TEFCA participants have highlighted the need for stronger enforcement
and clearer protection for organizations that follow the TEFCA framework in good faith.

If ASTP keeps any privacy and security certification requirements, HIMSS urges ASTP to
work with industry to revise the TEFCA Manner Exception so that it truly functions as a
safe harbor for compliant TEFCA participants. HIMSS also recommends that ASTP and

Proposal to Remove Decision Support Interventions Certification Criteria

Regarding the removal of Decision Support Intervention (DSI) certification criteria, HIMSS
noted in public comments on the HTI-1 through 4 proposed rules that
the adoption timelines would present a significant regulatory burden on developers
and health systems. HIMSS also noted that end-users do not necessarily need or
understand all the source attribution materials required in the finalized Predictive
Decision Support Interventions certification criteria. Required source attributes were
often too technical for clinical end users and, based on data collected by developers
for certification compliance, source attributes were rarely accessed or updated by
end-users within their respective systems. Revising the criteria to provide transparency to
systems administrators during implementation and configuration of decision support,
rather than providing that level of transparency and editing capability to end-users,
would meet HHS goals for ensuring that predictive DSI is safe, reliable, and mitigates
harmful bias. This would also reduce regulatory burden and cost for providers and
developers.
