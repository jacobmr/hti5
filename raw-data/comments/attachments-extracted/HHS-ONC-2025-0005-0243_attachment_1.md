Information Blocking – General Comments

As set forth in detail below, BCBSMA supports the Proposed Rule’s efforts to address “concerns about
the exceptions” to the Information Blocking Rules’ requirements that may be “being misused in an anti-
competitive or obstructive way.”1 ASTP/ONC should additionally consider modifications to the Privacy
Exception at 45 CFR 171.202 and the Fees Exception at 45 CFR 171.302, as these exceptions likewise can
be (and are) used in ways that are intended to stifle competition, engage in rent seeking behavior, and
prevent the free exchange of information sought by the Rules.

In pursuing the goal of prohibiting actions that are “likely to interfere with access, exchange, or use of
electronic health information,” and in applying that prohibition broadly to “health IT developer[s] of
certified health IT, health information network[s], or health information exchange[s],” ASTP/ONC have
underscored that the entities that help to provide the infrastructure for interoperability are, in the
absence of regulation, potentially in a position to extract undue revenue from the broader project of
promoting interoperability. As one example, it is becoming commonplace for large vendors in the
electronic health record space to charge very high fees to access data for payment operations and other
administrative use cases on the part of payers. These fees do not reflect the cost of providing such data,
and inhibit sharing that would reduce administrative costs and benefit patients.2

The Privacy Exception

BCBSMA is aware of EHR vendors relying on privacy-based exceptions under the Information Blocking
Regulations to slow or refuse responses to requests, but failing to explain how specific privacy
requirements in the HIPAA Privacy Rule or comparable regulations are implicated. It is thus appropriate
to add language (in the form of an additional subsection in 45 CFR 171.202(b)(1)(I - ii)) to require
additional, more specific invocation of HIPAA on the part of actors refusing to respond to queries under
the privacy exception:

“Additionally, the actor must, as to each request that it asserts qualifies for the exception on the basis
that State or Federal law requires one or more preconditions for providing access, exchange, or use of
electronic health information that have not been satisfied, state in a written explanation the specific
basis for that assertion in State or Federal law, including specific citation to statutory language and/or
regulations and how they apply to the request. The actor must provide this explanation to the
requestor. For purposes of this subsection, the explanation requirement is not satisfied by generic or
similar reference to the HIPAA Privacy Rule or state analogues; the actor must state with specificity
which provisions of the HIPAA Privacy Rule (or regulations promulgated under a state analogue to
HIPAA) it asserts are implicated and how they apply to the request.”

The Fees Exception

As explained above, excessive fees are now commonplace when it comes to using information from
EHRs for payment or other administrative use cases (even when using the same information for
treatment use cases requested by providers is provided without such fees). These fees are inimical to
the aims of the Information Blocking regulations and, moreover, are inappropriate in light of the various
requirements imposed upon payers (but not providers) under CMS’s Interoperability and Prior
Authorization Final Rule. The Rule requires payers to implement four Application Programming
Interfaces (“APIs”) – the Patient Access API, the Provider Access API, the Payer-to-Payer API, and the
Prior Authorization API – by dates between 2026 and 2027. Though each API has a different purpose, all
are required to be implemented upon a common set of standards, namely the United States Core Data
for Interoperability (“USCDI”) and HL7 Fast Healthcare Interoperability Resources (“FHIR”) standards.
Through these APIs, payers are required to be able to provide real-time responses with respect to
queries on behalf of patients, a wide variety of data with respect to provider queries, requests from
other payers, and requests made in support of prior authorization.

There is currently no comparable requirement that health care providers and their vendors similarly
facilitate information flows from providers to payers to address queries for payment and operations use
cases. To ensure the symmetric flows of information that greater clinical data integration requires, ASTP
should establish requirements for providers to provide information relevant to payment and operations
uses at no charge, and amend the fees exception to prohibit the use of the fees exception where payers
seek symmetric data flows for payment and operations use cases.

In the alternative, the Information Blocking regulations should limit these fees, including in the following
ways:

    -    Amend 171.302(a)(1)(ii) to read “Limited to the actor’s costs of providing…” rather than
         “Reasonably related to…”.3 This would reduce ambiguity in the regulatory language and make
         clear that fees tangentially related to provisions of information or that seek to fund general
         overhead on the part of the entity fulfilling the request are not embraced by the exception.
    -    Amend 171.302(a)(2)(iii) to add the phrase, “provided, however, that the term ‘non-standard
         design’ shall not include any failure on the part of the actor, whether agreed-to or not by the

         National Coordinator, including but not limited to standards requiring provision of data in

Information Blocking – Revision of “Access,” “Exchange,” and “Use” Definitions

The Proposed Rule would revise the definitions of “access” and “use” promulgated at 45 CFR § 171.102
to emphasize that both definitions encompass automated “access” or “use,” including use by
autonomous artificial intelligence-driven systems. The Proposed Rule also considers revising the
definition of “exchange” along similar lines.

Comments

BCBSMA supports the proposed revisions. Payers, providers, and other health care system actors are
currently engaged in intensive efforts to determine how best to deploy artificial intelligence in order to
lower costs and administrative burdens and improve outcomes for patients. To do so, federal rules
should create latitude for operating artificial intelligence in relation to access, use, and exchange of
health information, where artificial intelligence shows promise in, inter alia, reducing administrative
burdens associated with processes like prior authorization.

Information Blocking – Removal of the Third Party Seeking Modification Use Condition

The Proposed Rule would remove the “third party seeking modification use” condition from the
Infeasibility Exception promulgated at 45 CFR § 171.204(a)(3).

Comments

BCBSMA supports the proposed removal. As the Proposed Rule notes, there has been “substantial
growth in technical approaches that enable efficient, appropriately secure modification use as well as

Information Blocking – Revisions to the Manner Exception Exhausted Condition

The Proposed Rule would require actors to explore additional alternative methods for providing
information before declaring that its options have been exhausted and invoking the Infeasibility
Exception. It would also narrow the circumstances in which an actor may be considered not to be
providing “the same access, exchange, or use of the requested health information” to exclude cases in
which the actor is providing “analogous” (rather than the “same”) access. The Proposed Rule would also
clarify that the manner exception exhausted condition cannot be satisfied “where the actor offers at
least one individual or entity the requested access, exchange, or use.”5

Comments

BCBSMA supports the proposed revisions. The Proposed Rule acknowledges the concern “that actors
may be abusing the exception in various ways” or that the “terms and criteria of the condition […] are
being misused.”6 The proposed revisions primarily target actors’ abilities to pick-and-choose which
requesters’ requests will be denied as “infeasible” by preventing invocation of the condition where the
actor chooses to provide the same or analogous information to other sets of requesters. As noted
above, the entities that are in a position to do such picking-and-choosing may leverage conditions like
this one to engage in anti-competitive or rent-seeking behavior that is antithetical to the purposes of the
Information Blocking Rules.

Information Blocking – Revisions to the Manner Exception

The Proposed Rule would prohibit use of the Manner Exception where an actor insists on contract terms
that do not represent fair market value, that operate as a contract of adhesion, or that contain
unconscionable terms.

Comments

BCBSMA supports the proposed revisions and encourages ASTP/ONC to define key terms – including
“fair market value,” contract of adhesion,” and “unconscionable” in ways that are protective of
requesters and that acknowledge the inherent power imbalances that many requesters face. BCBSMA
further notes that the Manner Exception in general should be applied less and less as time goes on as
