Public Comment for HTI-5 Proposed Rule

Docket ID: ONC-2025-0001 Proposed Rule: Health Data, Technology, and Interoperability: ASTP/ONC Deregulatory
Actions to Unleash Prosperity (HTI-5)

Dear Assistant Secretary and National Coordinator,

I am writing to provide formal comments on the HTI-5 Proposed Rule. While I support efforts to streamline the
Certification Program, I urge the ASTP/ONC to implement stricter guardrails to prevent large pharmacy chains from
utilizing information blocking exceptions as a shield for anti-competitive corporate policies that interfere with patient
care.

1. Prohibiting the Abuse of Infeasibility and Manner Exceptions by Pharmacies

Regarding the proposed revisions to 45 CFR § 171.204 (Infeasibility) and § 171.301 (Manner), I request that the final
rule explicitly prohibit healthcare providers (specifically retail pharmacies) from invoking these exceptions when the
following conditions of "Demonstrated Technical Feasibility" are met:

    •   Standardized Compatibility: The pharmacy already utilizes the NCPDP SCRIPT standard (v2017071 or
        v2023011) to exchange EHI with any other intermediary. Since the NCPDP standard is inherently interoperable,
        the technical ability to receive a compliant transaction from one source constitutes de facto technical feasibility
        for all others.
    •   Selective Interoperability: A pharmacy chain demonstrates technical capability by connecting to an intermediary
        (e.g., WENO Exchange) in one jurisdiction to comply with state mandates (e.g., California AB 2789), yet refuses
        to do so in others.

Currently, many pharmacies ignore valid ePrescriptions from smaller intermediaries without providing a formal
exception, later citing "corporate policy" or "technical difficulty" as a defense. If a pharmacy’s proprietary software can
process the NCPDP standard from one entity, it is "technically feasible" to do so for all. Corporate policy is a
discretionary business choice, not a regulatory exception.

2. Mandatory Use of Alternative Access Means

In cases where a pharmacy has not yet completed a direct software integration, the Infeasibility Exception should be
denied if the requester (intermediary) provides a legal, DEA-compliant (21 CFR § 1311.205) alternative retrieval method
at no cost to the pharmacy.

WENO Exchange currently offers a free, hosted retrieval application for every routed ePrescription, yet many pharmacy
chains refuse to utilize this alternative due to internal "corporate policy." This refusal is an unreasonable interference
that directly harms patients—particularly those with DEA Schedule II prescriptions who are forced to return to their
providers for paper orders because the pharmacy refuses the electronic route.

3. Closing the Enforcement Gap for Medicare Part D Pharmacies

The June 2024 Final Rule established disincentives for MIPS and Medicare Part B participants, but it left a massive
enforcement gap for retail pharmacies that primarily operate under Medicare Part D. This allows pharmacies to block
patient treatment with zero financial risk.

I urge HHS to establish "appropriate disincentives" specifically for pharmacies participating in Medicare Part D. When
the OIG determines a pharmacy has committed information blocking, I recommend that CMS apply the following:

    •   Reduced Dispensing Fees: A mandatory reduction in Part D dispensing fees for a set performance period.
    •   Loss of "Preferred Pharmacy" Status: Immediate disqualification from preferred networks in all Medicare Part
        D plans.
    •   Contractual Disclosure: Mandatory notification to all Part D Plan Sponsors, enabling them to terminate con-
        tracts based on a failure to meet federal interoperability standards.

Conclusion A pharmacy’s "manner" of doing business should not override a physician's legal prescription. Selective
interoperability is being used to foreclose market competition and run off prescribers who prefer alternative
intermediaries. I urge the Department to do this for the "silent patients"—the ones who have no voice when they stand at a
pharmacy counter and are denied medication simply because of the route the data took to get there.
