Dear Secretary Kennedy,

The American Pharmacists Association (APhA) appreciates the opportunity to provide
comments on the “Health Data, Technology, and Interoperability: ASTP/ONC Deregulatory
Actions To Unleash Prosperity,” proposed rule. APhA has provided multiple comments
recommending ASTP/ONC take action to remove regulatory barriers for pharmacists and
pharmacies to provide care, as Dr. Oz recently stated, “at the height of their licensure.” Dr. Oz
also asked, “Why don’t we use pharmacists better?” 1

APhA represents pharmacists, student pharmacists, and pharmacy technicians in all practice
settings, including but not limited to community pharmacies, hospitals, long-term care facilities,
specialty pharmacies, community health centers, physician offices, ambulatory clinics, managed
care organizations, hospice settings, and government facilities. Our members strive to improve
medication use, advance patient care, and enhance public health.

ASTP/ONC “is issuing this proposed rule in an effort to streamline and reduce administrative
burdens on health care providers, health information technology (IT) developers, and the health
IT community overall.” 2 It is also a goal to “improve patient and health care provider access to
electronic health information (EHI) and promote health IT and health care market competition

Certification Criteria for Health Information Technology (FR 60979)

ASTP/ONC notes that they have outlined certification criteria for removal or revision within the
proposed rule, 6 with some criteria being removed because they have been “widely adopted” or
“widely implemented.” 7 A certification criterion has been “widely adopted” or “widely
implemented” “when they are included in the Base EHR or CEHRT definitions and adopted by
96 and 78 percent of hospitals and physicians, respectively.” 8 A list of proposed removals and
revisions is provided within Table 1 of the proposed rule. 9 APhA notes pharmacists are not
recognized as Eligible Professionals (EPs) for CMS programs requiring CEHRT, which is a
longstanding barrier to incorporating the work of patient care teams. While APhA appreciates
ASTP/ONC’s efforts to remove unnecessary certification criteria to advance interoperability,
APhA also encourages ASTP/CMS to ensure that the removal or revision of any of these
certification criteria does not negatively impact patient care or patient privacy.

§ 170.315(b)(1) to simplify its requirements.” 10 The proposed rule provides that this “criterion
supports the structured transition of care summaries and referral summaries that help ensure
continuity and care coordination as patients move between providers.” 11 As part of this
criterion, ASTP/ONC in the past “stressed that an EHR technology’s ability to incorporate data
for medications, medication allergies, and problems in structured form from a C-CDA
document was the minimum necessary to meet this certification criterion and encouraged EHR
technology developers to go beyond this minimum.” 12 Pharmacists are the medication experts

Real World Testing (FR 61002)

Currently, health IT developers must test the real-world interoperability of their technology in
the setting where it will be marketed. 24 ASTP/ONC is proposing changes to make it where
“developers will only need to identify in their real-world testing results their voluntary updates
(health IT products, certification criteria, and standards/implementation specifications) to
standards and implementation specifications that the National Coordinator has approved
through [standards version advancement process] SVAP.” 25 ASTP/ONC states “[t]his approach
will allow developers to continue to voluntarily update to newer versions of standards once
approved by the National Coordinator, which supports advancing interoperability.” 26 Because
the way in which health IT developers demonstrate interoperability and functionality of their
technologies in “real world settings” would be affected by this proposed rule, APhA encourages
ASTP/ONC to continue encouraging health IT developers to take steps to minimize the
likelihood that their technologies malfunction outside controlled test environments. APhA
supports efforts that advance interoperability for pharmacies and pharmacists and the pace of
this advancement must also minimize concerns about patient safety, data integrity, and
administrative burden.

Information Blocking (Part 171) (FR 61005)

concerns by narrowing or eliminating these exceptions. 28

The “third party seeking modification use” condition “applies to a request to enable use of EHI
in order to modify EHI, provided that the request for such use is not from a health care provider
that is a HIPAA covered entity (as defined in 45 CFR 160.103) requesting such use from an actor
that is its business associate (as defined in 45 CFR 160.103).” 35 It does not apply “when a health
care provider that is also a HIPAA covered entity requests (directly, or through another
business associate of the health care provider) such use of EHI from an actor who is the health
care provider’s business associate.” 36 Because when a condition applies, and the exception
applies, it equates to the action not being considered information blocking, ASTP/ONC has
concerns “that certain actors, such as health IT developers of certified health IT, may seek to
misuse the proposal to restrict access to EHI in an overly broad manner.” 37 Specific concerns
included that “it could potentially inhibit care coordination by making it too easy for an actor
holding EHI to simply refuse modification use requests from third parties who also furnish
services to the same patient(s).” 38 The application of this exception has been narrowed
previously to adjust for changes in the definition of “actor.” 39 After review of the current health
information ecosystem and in consideration of the intent of the information blocking
regulations, ASTP/ONC “now believe[s] the condition as a whole is unnecessary and that
removing it will better support the access, exchange, and use of EHI and the goals we set out in
the Cures Act Final Rule to unleash innovation and competition for the improvement of health
outcomes and lower health care costs for all Americans.” 40 APhA commends ASTP/ONC for
taking steps to enhance the interoperability of EHI amongst health care providers. By
eliminating such exceptions, actors who have deliberately used them rather than supporting
interoperability efforts will be forced to share EHI when appropriate, or face disincentives for
information blocking. Given the current approaches to ensuring secure two-way exchange of
information and the ability of actors to utilize other exceptions (Security Exception, Preventing
Harm Exception) to protect patient data and satisfy other requirements, APhA supports the
removal of the “third party seeking modification use” condition within the Infeasibility
Exception to prevent information blocking and promote greater sharing of EHI with pharmacies
and pharmacists and encourages ASTP/ONC to move forward considering use of EHI with
pharmacies as sites of patient care.

Manner Exception (FR 61011)

ASTP/ONC also addresses the Manner Exception in the proposed rule, which was originally
created to “address[] comments received regarding the breadth of the EHI definition and
flexibility in the manner an actor fulfills a request to access, exchange, or use EHI, while
providing market participants the ability to reach and maintain market negotiated terms.” 47 The
agency has heard during previous requests for information and feedback sessions that “some
actors may be incorrectly citing the manner requested condition to persuade requestors that the
exception applies to agreements and terms to which it has never applied.” 48 Accordingly,
ASTP/ONC “propose[s] to revise the Manner Exception’s manner requested condition
(§ 171.301(a)) to ensure that it is clear that the Manner Exception cannot be met with contracts
that are not market rate, are contracts of adhesion, or are contracts containing unconscionable
terms.” 49 APhA supports revisions to the Manner Exception that prevent actors from
inappropriately using the condition to avoid requests for access, exchange, or use of the
requested EHI. As such, APhA encourages ASTP/ONC to use appropriate disincentives or civil
monetary penalties against health care providers, health IT developers, and health information
networks that engage in information blocking against pharmacists, pharmacies, and their
patients. ASTP/ONC should not allow siloed health systems to block EHI access by health care
providers, which is in conflict with ASTP/ONC’s interoperability efforts.

As a reminder, APhA commented on ONC’s proposed rule, “Health Data, Technology, and
Interoperability: Patient Engagement, Information Sharing, and Public Health Interoperability.”
Within this proposed rule, ONC moved to incorporate the Public Health Service Act (PHSA)
section 3000 definition of “pharmacist” into the definition of “health care provider” for
information-blocking purposes. APhA “commend[ed] ONC for taking steps to enhance the
interoperability of EHI amongst health care providers” and “support[ed] pharmacists being
recognized as providers in relation to information-blocking regulations discussed in the
proposed rule as long as pharmacists also have access to EHI to ensure the delivery of
pharmacist-provided patient care services [emphasis added].” APhA also requested that ONC
require other health care providers and developers of health IT certified under the final rules
to be prohibited from information blocking EHI to pharmacists and to penalize developers
for doing so. APhA reiterates these points as it is urgent that pharmacists gain real-time
access to essential electronic health information of their patients because information
blocking causes delays in patient care and prevents health care providers from having a
complete picture of the patient’s health, which can lead to inefficient and ineffective care.
APhA appreciates ASTP/ONC’s efforts to receive and investigate information blocking
complaints through the ONC Information Blocking Portal and Office of Inspector General (OIG)
hotline. Given the incentives for health care providers and monetary civil penalties for health IT
developers and health information networks, enforcement of these provisions will lead to
greater interoperability efforts and reduced attempts to restrict the sharing of EHI. As such,
APhA encourages the agency to share more information about how patients and health care
providers can report information blocking and any subsequent enforcement actions to promote
compliance with the corresponding rules and regulations.

APhA also recommends that ASTP/ONC convene a meeting of the technology industry and
pharmacist providers to fully advance bidirectional, interoperable, and portable patient care
records accessible to both pharmacists and other health care providers. APhA discussed this
idea with Dr. Oz in our meeting with the Administrator on November 18, 2025. As discussed in
our meeting with Dr. Oz and his leadership team, when pharmacists are integrated as providers
and granted access to interoperable records, the health care system moves closer to the vision of
a modern, patient-centered, and sustainable model of care that enhances outcomes while
delivering better value for every health care dollar. APhA believes we all share the goal of
promoting more coordinated care for all patients, including the involvement of pharmacists and
pharmacies through electronic health information sharing. This provides the patient and their
healthcare team, including their personal pharmacist, with real-time data, empowering the
patient to make decisions regarding the care and treatments they receive and giving their
providers the most accurate picture of the patient’s health and medication history. As also
discussed during our meeting, when other clinicians and CEHRT developers block pharmacists'
access to electronic health information under “information blocking,” it delays patient care and
prevents clinicians from having a complete picture of the patient's health, leading to inefficient
and ineffective care. APhA encourages ASTP/ONC to work with CMS to convene such a
meeting to facilitate prompt action in the rollout of this technology, which will require
collaborative efforts between the private technology sector and pharmacy partners. APhA
