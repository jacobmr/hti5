CRISP Shared Services, Inc. (“CSS”) appreciates the opportunity to provide comments on Health
Data, Technology, and Interoperability: ASTP/ONC Deregulatory Actions to Unleash Prosperity (HHS-
ONC-2025-0005) (the “Proposed Rule”). CSS is a not-for-profit organization managing a shared
infrastructure for Health Information Exchanges (“HIEs”) and Health Data Utilities (“HDUs”) across
the United States. Our core mission is to preserve the independence, governance authority, and
community-driven priorities of each participating HIE and HDU while achieving economies of scale.

Currently, the states and territories CSS supports include HIEs and HDUs serving Alaska,
Connecticut, the District of Columbia, Kentucky, Maryland, Rhode Island, Virginia, and West Virginia.
Across these jurisdictions, CSS and its partners function as trusted, neutral third parties responsible
for governance, privacy and security, and robust interoperability infrastructure, connecting providers,
payers, and public health agencies. Our shared architecture makes it possible to exchange data
across borders and ensures that information is standardized, harmonized, and ready to support
scalable services for millions of patients and thousands of healthcare organizations. CSS also
provides the opportunity for the HIEs and HDUs it supports to exchange data through the national
networks, such as the Technical Exchange Framework and Common Agreement (“TEFCA”),
Carequality, and eHealth Exchange. Based on the connectivity we support and the interfaces we
maintain with a wide array of certified health information technology (“IT”) systems, we have direct
experience with nearly every operational scenario implicated by the Proposed Rule.

While there are many areas of this rule on which CSS’ expertise is applicable, we would specifically
like to provide comment on the sections discussed below. Throughout, we highlight overarching
themes that should guide the Office of the National Coordinator for Health IT (“ONC”) and the
Assistant Secretary for Technology Policy (“ASTP”) as the agency considers revisions to the
Information Blocking framework—particularly where proposed changes would introduce significant
contractual, operational, and compliance risks, reduce clarity for market participants, or
unintentionally undermine the continued expansion of safe, transparent, and equitable health
information exchange.

1. Proposed Changes to Certification Requirements

Although CSS does not operate Certified Health IT, as noted above, our broad, nationwide
connectivity requires connection and exchange with such system. Based on this experience, we
generally support ONC / ASTP’s deregulatory policies in these sections. As stated in our response to
the Request for Information; Health Technology Ecosystem (CMS–0042–NC):

        Overregulation in the Health IT sector increases the burden on providers and fosters
        an industry focused on enforcing certification criteria rather than innovation.
        Excessive regulations can stifle the development of new tools and solutions by
        diverting resources towards compliance rather than creativity. The fundamental
        principles should be straightforward and aligned with the Information Blocking Rule.
        Simplifying regulations can encourage the development of effective Health IT
        solutions that prioritize patient care and data security without being bogged down by
        unnecessary regulatory hurdles. Put simply, CMS and ONC / ASTP should drive value
        rather than regulating standards and software and enforce its rules when those who
        should be sharing data do not. . . . We recommend that CMS prioritize the capture of
        high-quality data over the methods through which that data is exchanged.

We are grateful that ONC / ASTP has taken these recommendations to heart in this proposed rule and
is generally focusing on value and outcomes rather than technical criteria, while also emphasizing
the enforcement of the Information Blocking regulations.

2. Proposed Clarification to Access and Use

Although ONC / ASTP has previously finalized definitions of “access,” “exchange,” and “use,” based
on its market observations and industry input, it is proposing to clarify that such meanings include
when “that access means the ability or means necessary to make [electronic health information
(“EHI”)] available for exchange or use, including by automation technologies such as robotic process
automation and autonomous artificial intelligence systems.” (90 FR 61006). CSS supports this
proposal, or the alternative proposal, provided, as ONC / ASTP notes, entities are using such
systems in compliance with other existing standards of security and privacy, including the
HIPAA Privacy and Security rules. We also underscore the importance of ONC / ASTP’s guidance
on the use of artificial intelligence in its prior rules, specifically the FAVES (fair, appropriate, valid,
effective, and safe) guidance (89 FR 1233) and related regulations (45 CFR § 170.315(b)(11)). While
this guidance has primarily been focused on predictive decision support interventions, the FAVES
analysis is widely applicable and should be the backbone of any automated technology. We
encourage ONC / ASTP to continue to monitor the use of such tools to ensure they are aligned with
these principles.

3. Proposed Removal of the “Third Party Seeking Modification Use”

ONC / ASTP is also proposing to remove the “third party seeking modification use” condition. ONC /
ASTP had previously narrowly tailored this exception, such that it would only apply if the third party
were not a HIPAA-covered entity. (90 FR 61007). While CSS agrees with the intent of this proposal,
we do not support the removal of “third party seeking modification use.” Our HIEs / HDUs
routinely receive requests from non-HIPAA-covered entities seeking revision to EHI. In our

experience, after talking to the HIPAA-covered entity originating such “erroneous” information, it is
often true that the requestor was, in fact, incorrect about many such requests. These situations
extend even to patients, both those genuinely forgetting a medication or procedure and those that
are seeking additional medication or procedures that could cause them harm. Thus, we believe that
this exception is narrowly tailored as is, such that it ensure the accuracy of the information.

While we appreciate ONC / ASTP’s guidance that other exceptions could be used in such
circumstances, if ONC / ASTP ultimately does decide to finalize this proposal, we request specific,
guidance to clarify that those exceptions could be used for such circumstances. Specifically, if ONC
/ ASTP finalizes this proposal, we ask for regulatory language or sub-regulatory guidance
explicitly stating the lawful application of the Security and Preventing Harm exception for such
requests.

4. Proposed Changes to the “Manner Exception Exhausted” Condition

ONC / ASTP discusses potential syntactical changes to the Manner Exception Exhausted condition,
intended to ensure that the exception is not abused. (90 FR 61009-11). As an alternative, ONC / ASTP
proposed to remove the condition entirely. In our experience, this condition is helpful to ensure that
those requesting EHI do not similarly abuse their rights to receive data by requiring large levels of
effort from an actor where it is truly unlikely an agreement could be reached. However, we
understand that those asserting the exception may also abuse it. Therefore, to benefit “good”
actors, we recommend that ONC / ASTP keep this exception with the language changes
proposed to allow for clarification while focusing its enforcement on those abusing the
exception.

5. Proposed Addition to the “Manner Exception Exhausted” Condition

ONC / ASTP “proposes to revise § 171.301(a)(2) to add a new subparagraph (iii) to incorporate in
regulation text and explicitly state that any contract or agreement related to the access, exchange, or
use of EHI: (1) must be at market rate; (2) must not be a contract of adhesion; and (3) must not contain
unconscionable terms.” (90 FR 61012). While we generally support ensuring contracts are fair, we
are concerned with ONC / ASTP’s proposed definition of “contract of adhesion.” The proposal
would, if finalized, define a “contract of adhesion” as “a contract provided on a standardized form, or
on a ‘take it or leave it basis’ without a realistic opportunity to bargain where the desired product,
services, access, use, or exchange cannot be provided except by acquiescing to the form contract.”
(90 FR 61012).

Most interoperable networks use standards participation, data sharing, or “common” agreements.
Such contracts are not intended to be contract of adhesion or even a shortcut – it is foundational to
transparency and interoperability. When participants in a network know that every party is
operating under identical obligations, they can exchange data confidently with as little
operational and technical friction as possible. Without such contracts, each signer would likely
need to understand every time a new signer requested different terms, an operational
impossibility.

In addition, such a definition would likely upheave nearly all national networks. The following
would all likely be classified as “contracts of adhesion” under the proposed definition:

• The eHealth Exchange Data Use and Reciprocal Support Agreement
• The Carequality Connected Agreement and Carequality Connection (CC) Terms
• The Common Agreement (CA) and Participant/Subparticipant Terms of Participation (ToP)
under the Technical Exchange Framework and CA (TEFCA)1
• The majority of state and regional HIE participation agreements

Even more concerning, ASTP / ONC seems to suggest a presumption that “contracts of adhesion,” as
defined, would also “likely constitute an interference under the information blocking regulations” (90
FR 61012), suggesting a presumption of Information Blocking, even if another exception applies.

In the alternative, ONC / ASTP proposes to retain the language at § 171.301(a)(1) as-is and, instead,
require that, to satisfy the Manner Exception, an Actor must also meet the requirements of the
respective Fees Exception and Licensing Exception (i.e., remove § 171.301(a)(2)) (90 FR 61013). We
support this proposal, as it would mitigate the issue described above while retaining the stated
intent to honor “the breadth of the EHI definition and flexibility in the manner an actor fulfills a
request to access, exchange, or use EHI, while providing market participants the ability to reach and
maintain market negotiated terms” and emphasizing that this exception “only covers the technical
manner of exchange of the requested EHI.” (90 FR 61011-12).

If ONC / ASTP decides to finalize a new subparagraph (iii) as proposed, we implore it to rely on
the common law definition of a “contract of adhesion” rather than creating a new legal doctrine
that could undermine the trust and interoperability of decades of agreements. Specifically, we
recommend codifying that a “contract of adhesion” means:

        A contract that: (i) is presented on a standardized form without realistic opportunity to
        negotiate material terms; (ii) is offered by a party with substantially superior bargaining
        power to a party that has no meaningful market alternative for obtaining the desired
        access, use, or exchange; and (iii) contains terms that are primarily for the benefit the
        drafting party.

The above suggestion would ensure that ONC / ASTP is only targeting those contracts where a power
imbalance exists leading to one-sided terms favoring the drafter. Cooperative agreements, like those
cited above, are just that: cooperative, without the power imbalances and one-sided terms that ONC
/ ASTP seeks to dissuade.

Alternatively, ONC / ASTP could create a carve-out for contracts created from collaborative
governance and cooperation. Such an alternative would recognize that systemic participation in
the development of terms and conditions is a meaningful form of “bargaining.” As an example, ONC
/ ASTP could create a carve-out substantially similar to the following:

           An agreement is not a contract of adhesion, as defined in this section, solely because
           it is standardized if: (i) the agreement governs participation in a health information
           network or health information exchange; (ii) the agreement's terms are developed or
           approved through a governance process that provides for meaningful input from

In summary, we do not recommend that ONC / ASTP adopt its proposal to add a new
subparagraph (iii) to § 171.301(a)(2), but we do support the proposal to retain the language at §
171.301(a)(1) as-is and, require that, to satisfy the Manner Exception, an Actor must also meet
the requirements of the respective Fees Exception and Licensing Exception. If ONC / ASTP does
decide to adopt the new subparagraph, we recommend either changing the definition of
“contract of adhesion,” as discussed above, or creating a carve-out for agreements
collaboratively negotiated through a governance process.

6. Proposed Removal of the TEFCA Exception

The TEFCA manner exception codified “that an actor’s practice of limiting the manner in which it
fulfills a request for access, exchange, or use of EHI to providing such access, exchange, or use only
via TEFCA would not be considered information blocking when the practice follows certain
conditions.” (90 FR 61013). As stated in the Proposed Rule, this exception incentivized participation
in TEFCA, which, as ONC / ASTP states, is no longer needed. (90 FR 61013). CSS strongly supports
this proposal. As stated in our response to the Request for Information; Health Technology
Ecosystem (CMS–0042–NC), there are adequate alternatives to TEFCA that offer the same, or better
connectivity. As this stage, ONC / ASTP rightly states that a federal incentive is no longer
necessary; instead the market should drive appropriate adoption and participation, while still
requiring those participating in TEFCA to meet the full rules of Information Blocking.
