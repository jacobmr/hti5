Dear Secretary Keane:

On behalf of the Greater New York Hospital Association (GNYHA), representing more than 200 hospitals
and health systems across New York and surrounding states, I appreciate the opportunity to comment on
the proposed rule, Health Data, Technology, and Interoperability: ASTP/ONC Deregulatory Actions to
Unleash Prosperity.

The proposal substantially scales back requirements applicable to health IT developers, including electronic
health record (EHR) vendors. Its stated goal is to reduce burden and increase flexibility to foster innovation.
However, while requirements for developers are eased, hospitals—which rely on EHRs not only to deliver
care and securely exchange information, but also to meet extensive legal and regulatory requirements—
receive no comparable relief.

Lifting requirements for developers/vendors without corresponding adjustments for those who use and rely
on their products does not reduce burden overall; it simply shifts that burden to hospitals. In practice, this
dynamic may increase operational and financial pressure on hospitals, particularly those with limited
resources and limited leverage in the health information technology (HIT) marketplace.

GNYHA supports thoughtful modernization of HIT standards, particularly where changes could eventually
lower HIT-related costs for hospitals.1 At the same time, modernization should reflect how the health care
delivery system operates as a whole and ensure that reforms do not transfer operational burden, compliance
exposure, or financial impact to hospitals.

Our detailed comments outline areas where standards may be streamlined to ensure deregulation does not
shift new pressures onto hospitals; that transparency keeps pace with expanded information exchange; and

Detailed Comments

Context and Implications for Hospitals
The US Department of Health and Human Services (HHS) Assistant Secretary for Technology Policy,
Office of the National Coordinator for Health IT (ASTP/ONC) indicates that the proposed rule, Health
Data, Technology, and Interoperability: ASTP/ONC Deregulatory Actions to Unleash Prosperity (HTI-5),
is intended to allow the capabilities of certified electronic record technology (CEHRT) systems to evolve
in response to market needs. This is a worthwhile goal that GNYHA supports. However, hospitals are the
principal users of CEHRT, relying on CEHRT systems to support the delivery of safe, high-quality care,
securely exchange information, and comply with numerous Federal and state requirements. Thus, their
needs should also be treated as central to current market demand.

Federal policy has significantly accelerated information sharing across the health care ecosystem, where
hospitals and other health care providers are the main data suppliers. The information-blocking regulations
have expanded baseline access to electronic health information; the Trusted Exchange Framework and
Common Agreement (TEFCA) has established a nationwide governance structure for trusted exchange;
and the Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS)’s broader health technology initiatives have
encouraged expanded data sharing across traditional and non-traditional health care entities (e.g.,
technology companies).

As a result of all these changes, hospitals are operating in an environment where the volume and speed of
electronic data exchange is increasing rapidly and where new entities—including application developers,
technology companies, and AI-enabled tools—are gaining access to clinical information through automated
mechanisms. Apart from all of that, health care is one of the most heavily regulated sectors of the economy,
and hospitals must also manage the growing privacy and security exposure associated with this increasingly
rapid and expansive data exchange. For this reason, the changes proposed in HTI-5 to the health IT
certification framework and the information-blocking regulations have direct operational and compliance
consequences for hospitals.

In finalizing this proposal, GNYHA urges ASTP/ONC to ensure that: (1) deregulatory changes applicable
to certified health IT developers do not shift burden, risk, or cost to hospitals; (2) transparency and
accountability mechanisms evolve alongside expanded information exchange; and (3) revisions to the
information blocking rule preserve clear and workable pathways for good-faith compliance.

Deregulatory Changes Should Not Shift Burden, Risk, or Cost to Hospitals

Privacy and Security Criteria
ASTP/ONC proposes to remove all privacy and security certification criteria for CEHRT, as well as the
associated Privacy and Security Certification Framework.2 ASTP/ONC explains that: certification alone
does not ensure compliance with the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act of 1996 (HIPAA)
Security Rule; enterprise-wide cybersecurity cannot be achieved solely through CEHRT; and certain

corresponding changes to the HIPAA Security Rule applicable to HIPAA-covered entities.

GNYHA supports eliminating duplicative and unnecessarily burdensome requirements, particularly where
doing so provides meaningful downstream relief to hospitals. However, EHR certification has historically
established a consistent baseline of privacy and security capabilities, including access controls,
authentication measures, encryption, and audit logging. These capabilities directly support hospitals’
compliance obligations, most notably under the HIPAA Security Rule. Routinely, deficiencies in access
controls, auditing, encryption, and authentication safeguards are cited in Office for Civil Rights (OCR)
enforcement actions,3 which directs hospitals’ cybersecurity investments and underscores the importance
of these functions. Similarly, the CMS Promoting Interoperability (PI) Program requires hospitals to
conduct and attest to an annual security risk analysis,4 reinforcing hospitals’ accountability for safeguarding
electronic protected health information (PHI). While we agree that using CEHRT alone cannot ensure
compliance, certification signals the availability of baseline features that enable hospitals to implement and
document their compliance with required security safeguards.

The removal of privacy certification criteria would be equally significant. Certified capabilities such as
role-based access controls, audit reporting, accounting of disclosures, and related access management tools
support hospitals’ compliance with the HIPAA Privacy Rule, 42 C.F.R. Part 2, and applicable state
confidentiality laws. In New York, hospitals must comply with specific statutory protections governing
HIV-related information, mental health records, information about minors, and other sensitive data
categories. Standardized, embedded functionality within CEHRT supports hospitals’ ability to implement
appropriate access restrictions and track disclosures, which help hospitals comply with state law.

Absent corresponding changes to HIPAA or Medicare PI requirements, removal of privacy and security
certification criteria risks increasing the burden on hospitals, which remain fully accountable for standards
that certified technology would no longer be required to support.

GNYHA understands that ASTP/ONC anticipates that market forces will incentivize EHR developers to
maintain core privacy and security capabilities even without certification requirements. However, reliance
on the market alone may not be sufficient to retain the CEHRT features that hospitals depend on to deliver
safe, high-quality care and, importantly, meet extensive compliance requirements. In the environment that
ASTP/ONC envisions, hospitals would need to negotiate individually with CEHRT vendors to preserve
capabilities that are currently standardized through certification. Low-resourced hospitals are particularly

Alignment of Certification Changes with PI Program Requirements
HTI-5 also proposes to remove or revise certain certification criteria that support hospitals’ compliance
with the Medicare PI Program. The PI Program requires hospitals to report on specified EHR measures and
attest to their accuracy to avoid Medicare payment reductions. HTI-5 would eliminate the latter (i.e., the
certification requirements that allow CEHRT to demonstrate the accuracy of the output, including the
criteria related to automated numerator recording and automated measure calculation).6

Although removal of these certification requirements would not automatically render hospitals
noncompliant with PI requirements, these criteria have historically provided hospitals with assurance that
reported measures are calculated correctly. In the absence of these criteria, hospitals will have to rely more
heavily on vendor assurances or verify output themselves. Hospitals, particularly those without robust IT
departments or the financial ability to outsource this work, may face additional operational burden to
confirm that their CEHRT vendor’s output conforms to CMS requirements. Notably, hospitals remain fully
accountable for the accuracy of their attestations and are subject to Medicare payment adjustments for
noncompliance.

While GNYHA recognizes that these certification requirements may be unnecessarily prescriptive,
ASTP/ONC should consider what additional support or guidance is needed for hospitals that must
assume responsibility for verifying the accuracy of PI measure calculations. GNYHA urges
ASTP/ONC and CMS to coordinate closely and issue clear guidance on how hospitals can validate
the accuracy of PI measure calculations independently, particularly to reduce the burden on hospitals
with limited IT capacity.

GNYHA supports the continued advancement of FHIR-based exchange and recognizes its potential to
improve usability, promote automation, and reduce administrative burden, including related to required
reporting, over time. However, the transition from document-based exchange (e.g., structured summaries)
to API-driven, real-time data access presents a significant operational shift for hospitals. This shift affects
not only technical structure, but also a different way to view privacy and security. In this regard, under
long-standing document and transport frameworks, data exchange of structured summaries generally occurs
between defined participants using established standards. By contrast, FHIR-based APIs enable more on-
demand, automated queries by a broader range of external applications, and the privacy and security
standards for the APIs used by other data exchange participants varies and, notably, is not clear.

As the industry transitions to FHIR-based APIs, hospitals must evaluate, among other things, how third-
party APIs authenticate themselves, what data elements they access, how frequently queries occur, and how
they detect and address security incidents. These responsibilities may require new internal controls and
coordination across clinical, privacy, cybersecurity, compliance, and IT functions. Many hospitals,
particularly safety net providers, face workforce and financial constraints that make this rapid transition
challenging.

Notably, some payers, public health agencies, patient apps, and other third parties currently use FHIR-based
APIs to exchange data with hospitals. This will only increase because of HHS requirements or initiatives
or in response to market need. ASTP/ONC must ensure that the proposed CEHRT standards for API
exchange align with the technical standards of other APIs in a manner that creates transparency, particularly
around privacy and security features. Without this, there will not be trust, and expanded API use may
ultimately increase fragmentation rather than reduce burden.

As a FHIR-forward approach is advanced, we urge ASTP/ONC to tie implementation timelines for
CEHRT functionality to the demonstrated readiness of core API capabilities available in the
marketplace, including, for example, widely adopted authentication and audit methods. We also ask
ASTP/ONC to establish a consistent validation framework for FHIR-based APIs, whether through
certification criteria, implementation specifications, or subregulatory guidance.

As GNYHA noted in our response to HHS’s request for information on Accelerating the Adoption and Use

recognize that burdensome and unnecessary requirements may hinder the rapid evolution of AI-enabled

sources, and update practices becomes more—not less—important as AI functionality is embedded within
certified health IT, which is used to deliver care. Hospitals remain responsible for clinical care and for
compliance with Federal and state requirements related to quality, nondiscrimination, and patient safety.
Structured vendor transparency requirements were intended to provide a standardized baseline disclosure
across vendors, supporting hospital governance.

Removal of structured vendor transparency requirements without an alternative could shift the burden of
due diligence onto hospitals, many of which lack internal resources. While larger systems may have
capacity for independent evaluation, many safety net hospitals do not. AI systems often operate as “black
boxes,” leaving hospital and clinician users with little information as to how outputs are generated. Without
clear information from vendors about how outputs are generated, clinicians face increased burden in risk
management and oversight and potentially increased liability exposure.

We also note that emerging state requirements highlight the increasing importance of algorithmic
transparency from developers and vendors to state policy makers. For example, New York’s Responsible
AI Safety and Education Act imposes safety and transparency obligations on developers of frontier AI
models, including disclosure of safety protocols and evaluation procedures.10 These developments reinforce
the concept that states have an interest in ensuring that vendor disclosures provide consistent information
necessary for evaluating tools against expectations. As state requirements evolve--and assuming the Federal
government does not preempt such requirements--hospitals may eventually be expected to demonstrate that
AI-enabled clinical tools used in care delivery meet comparable transparency and safety standards and will
need vendor disclosure to do so.

We therefore urge ASTP/ONC to not depart from the underlying purpose of the DSI transparency
framework. ASTP/ONC should consider establishing a streamlined, standardized disclosure
framework that preserves the core objective of vendor transparency. Such a framework could focus
on providing the essential elements necessary for hospitals to evaluate a tool’s intended use,
performance characteristics, limitations, and update practices.

Information-Blocking Revisions Should Preserve Good-Faith Compliance Pathways
HTI-5 proposes to remove the TEFCA exception and to revise aspects of the Infeasibility and Manner
exceptions, all of which are found within the information-blocking rule.11 Under the current information-
blocking framework, if an actor meets the conditions of an exception, actions to restrict sharing or provide
information by different means are not considered information blocking and are not subject to associated
penalties. These exceptions therefore serve as critical tools.

Hospitals have made substantial financial and operational investments in certified health IT systems and
exchange capabilities to comply with the information-blocking rule, relying on a finalized regulatory
framework that included clearly defined exceptions. Given the significant consequences associated with
information-blocking violations, including significant fines and impacts on participation in Medicare
Shared Savings programs, hospitals require predictable standards that recognize good-faith compliance
efforts.12

As to the TEFCA exception, many hospitals are actively onboarding to TEFCA’s Qualified Health
Information Networks and integrating TEFCA participation into their exchange strategies. These efforts
have been undertaken, in part, in reliance on the existing TEFCA Manner Exception, which permits
hospitals to fulfill requests for electronic health information through participation in TEFCA rather than
through alternative exchange pathways.

Similarly, the Infeasibility and Manner exceptions recognize that there are circumstances in which fulfilling
a request in the exact manner requested may not be technically feasible or operationally appropriate and
permit hospitals to provide access through reasonable alternative approaches without risking
noncompliance with the information blocking rule. HTI-5 proposes to narrow these exceptions, but the
operational impact of that narrowing is not fully clear, particularly considering the proposal to eliminate
privacy and security certification standards. Taken together, these changes could create situations in which
a hospital lacks the functionality within its CEHRT to effectively avail itself of the Infeasibility or Manner
exceptions.

Moreover, ASTP/ONC should be concerned that narrowing the information-blocking exceptions while
simultaneously removing certification baselines may increase variation in how hospitals respond to requests
for electronic health information, including patient requests facilitated through third-party apps. If privacy
and security certification criteria are eliminated and exceptions narrowed, hospitals’ ability to evaluate,
monitor, and securely facilitate app-based access may vary significantly depending on the features available
in their CEHRT. Patients may encounter materially different responses to similar requests depending on

Conclusion
GNYHA appreciates ASTP/ONC’s commitment to advancing health care data interoperability and
modernization through this proposed rule. To ensure the proposal has a meaningful impact, innovation and
modernization should be balanced with operational feasibility and should avoid shifting undue burden to
hospitals.
