On behalf of the more than 10,000 physiatrists of the American Academy of Physical
Medicine and Rehabilitation (AAPM&R), we appreciate the opportunity to submit
comments in response to the proposed rule on deregulatory actions regarding health data,
technology, and interoperability requirements issued by the Assistant Secretary for
Technology Policy/Office of the National Coordination for Health Information Technology
(ASTP/ONC) and referenced above (also referred to as the HTI -5 proposed rule).
AAPM&R is the national medical specialty organization representing physicians who are
specialists in physical medicine and rehabilitation (PM&R). PM&R physicians, also known
as physiatrists, treat a wide variety of medical conditions affecting the brain, spinal cord,
nerves, bones, joints, ligaments, muscles, and tendons. PM&R physicians evaluate and treat
injuries, illnesses, and disability and are experts in designing comprehensive, patient -
centered treatment plans. Physiatrists utilize cutting-edge as well as time-tested treatments
to maximize function and quality of life.

Proposed Removal and Revision of Health Information Technology Certification
Criteria

ASTP/ONC is proposing to cut or revise almost 70 percent of requirements for the Health
Information Technology (IT) Certification Program that it views as either duplicative or
overly burdensome; not leading to positive impacts on patient care not resulting in expected
savings; not well established in health IT products in the market; or impeding
innovation. ASTP/ONC estimates that this rule would result in cost savings of $1.53
billion, including $650 million over the next 5 years for health IT developers, providers, and
other stakeholders.

AAPM&R appreciates ASTP/ONC’s effort to reduce administrative burdens and costs
associated with the Health IT Certification Program and to update the program to reflect
more modern trends in data exchange. At the same time, we are concerned that over-
deregulation of this program could result in unintended consequences, such as shifting
costs and burden to physicians and other end-users and eroding recent progress related to
interoperability in health care. In light of these concerns, we offer the following
recommendations:

    •   Adopt a more gradual timeline for the removal of criteria that ensures
        replacement criteria and standards are first widely tested and implementable by
        all stakeholders. This is particularly important in post-acute care (PAC) settings,
        where electronic health record (EHR) adoption has been slow, the high cost of
        upgrading technology is often difficult to justify, and interoperable, bidirectional
        data exchange remains limited. For example, AAPM&R agrees with ASTP/ONC
        that Fast Healthcare Interoperability Resources (FHIR) are a more modern and
        effective successor to Consolidated Clinical Document Architecture (C-CDA),
        offering more granular, real-time time data access. However, multiple challenges
        stand in the way of widespread FHIR implementation, including technical barriers
        and compliance concerns, and in the interim, C-CDA remains prevalent in legacy
        systems for document exchange. AAPM&R urges ASTP/ONC to not abandon
        current, long-standing criteria that clinicians continue to rely on until an
        adequate replacement infrastructure with more modern interoperability criteria
        and standards is securely in place and accessible to all clinicians regardless of
        practice setting or resource capacity. If ASTP/ONC prematurely removes
        required functionalities of certified IT prior to ensuring that end users have the
        seamless capacity to adopt new functionalities, this will put clinicians with fewer
        resources and less experience adopting interoperable technologies at a major
        disadvantage.

    •   Regarding the transition from C-CDA to FHIR, AAPM&R also urges
        ASTP/ONC to outline a clear, phased roadmap specifically tailored to post -acute
        care (PAC) settings. While we agree that FHIR represents a more modern,
        granular, and flexible interoperability standard, many PAC providers continue to
        rely heavily on legacy systems built around C-CDA for core document exchange.
        Without a structured transition plan, there is significant risk that legacy
        standards will be sunset before PAC settings have the infrastructure, resources,
        or vendor support necessary to adopt and operationalize FHIR. Establishing a
        predictable, multi-stage transition timeline—paired with technical guidance, pilot
        opportunities, and adequate implementation supports—would help ensure that
        modernization efforts do not exacerbate existing disparities or place PAC clinicia ns
        at a further disadvantage.



    •   Maintain criteria and other policies that promote transparency. AAPM&R is
        concerned about ASTP/ONC’s proposal to reduce the scope of the relatively new
        decision support interventions (DSI) certification criteria to fully remove the
        artificial intelligence (AI) “model card” requirements, which require IT developers
        to provide to clinicians what are also referred to as “nutritional labels,”
        documenting how AI algorithms are developed and tested, and their potential risk.
        At the time of adoption, ASTP/ONC stated that “these source attributes will

provide the transparency necessary for health care organizations and clinical users
to better determine whether…DSIs are fair, appropriate, valid, effective, and safe.”
However, ASTP/ONC now believes there is a lack of evidence that this
requirement currently improves patient care. Since the DSI criteria were only first
established in 2025, we are concerned that ASTP/ONC has drawn a conclusion
about the value of these criteria too quickly and without sufficient time to
accurately assess its impact. At a time when AI is becoming an increasingly
integral part of health care and clinicians are increasingly relying on AI-driven
decision support tools, it is critical that ASTP/ONC maintain criteria that support
transparency, fairness, and safety in clinical decision support and AI-driven
algorithms.

        AAPM&R is also concerned about ASTP/ONC’s proposal to remove most
        measures associated with the “Insights” Condition and Maintenance of
        Certification. Under the 21st Century Cures Act, developers of certified health IT
        are required to report on specific metrics centered around interoperability, security,
        and user experience. The goal of this policy, also referred to as the “EHR
        Reporting Program,” is to enhance transparency and provide actionable,
        standardized data to the public to address gaps in the health IT marketplace and
        assist end-users with selecting products. ASTP/ONC’s proposal to drastically scale
        back this program aligns with its earlier “Insights Condition and Maintenance of
        Certification Enforcement Discretion Notice,” which sought to “prevent and
        mitigate potential sunk costs necessary to” comply with these reporting
        requirements. AAPM&R opposes this attempt to narrow this program prior to
        implementation and prior to collecting any real-world results. ASTP/ONC
        intentionally designed this program so that metrics are phased in over three years,
        starting with calendar year 2026, and structured it “so as not to unduly
        disadvantage small and startup developers of certified health IT.” 1 Given
        prevalent misuse or abuse by IT vendors of requirements that promote
        interoperability, as identified by ASTP/ONC, we believe it is inappropriate to
        scale back this important program and the transparent practices it supports.

    •   Delay removal of criteria tied to quality reporting mandates that apply under
        programs operated by the Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services (CMS)
        and work closely with CMS to ensure that evolving certified IT criteria continue
        to support clinician compliance with such mandates, as well as ongoing high
        quality patient care. Numerous criteria that are proposed for removal are tied to
        CMS quality programs, such as the Merit-Based Incentive Payment System (MIPS)
        and Promoting Interoperability (PI). Although ASTP/ONC is proposing to delay
        the removal of some of these criteria until 2027, we believe that a longer delay is
        necessary to ensure CMS has sufficient time to harmonize its program
        requirements with updated ASTP/ONC criteria in a manner that does no t impose

• Continue to require certified technology to capture data elements that help
clinicians provide patient-centered, high-quality care. ASTP/ONC proposes to
revise the Patient Demographics certification criteria so that certified
technology would no longer be required to capture sexual orientation and gender
identity, sex parameter for clinical use, name to use and pronouns. AAPM&R
requests that ASTP/ONC strongly reconsider the implications of this policy
change, which will result in clinicians not having access to a complete portfolio
of data points that are necessary for clinical decision-making, and could lead to
substandard care.

Updates to Information Blocking Regulations

AAPM&R supports the HHS goal of advancing appropriate sharing of electronic health
information (EHI) by health care providers to support safer, more coordinated care for all
patients. As part of that effort, AAPM&R appreciates ASTP/ONC’s careful consideration
of policies over the last few years to limit information blocking and to deter “actors” from
engaging in such practices. We also appreciate that CMS has been carefully monitoring the
implementation of these new regulations in the real world and proposing to tighten existing
information blocking regulations—including certain terms, conditions and exceptions— that
it has identified as being obstructed or misused by certain actors (namely, EHR developers)
in anti-competitive ways. Overall, AAPM&R supports efforts to strengthen enforcement
and prevent ongoing barriers to patient access to EHI. However, we request that
ASTP/ONC continue to closely monitor the impact of any strengthened regulatory
framework to ensure it does not inadvertently make it more challenging or confusing for
clinicians, who are largely not the intended target of these efforts, to comply with
information blocking regulations.

We also urge ASTP/ONC to work with stakeholders to better educate actors about
information blocking regulations and compliance strategies. ASTP/ONC should provide
plain language guidance to physicians and other actors about what constitutes information
blocking. It also should continue to urge Congress to provide it with the authority to issue
binding advisory opinions regarding specific practices— including whether an exception
would or would not be met given a set of facts and circumstances— in order to provide
actors with assurances that if they receive a favorable advisory opinion, they would be
protected from penalties and disincentives for information blocking. Current regulations
center around reasonable and necessary activities that do NOT constitute information
blocking (i.e., “exceptions”). However, guidance surrounding what actually does constitute
information blocking has been less clear, making it extremely challenging for physicians to
understand whether they are properly complying with this abstruse set of regulations.

AAPM&R reminds ASTP/ONC that interoperability in the post-acute care (PAC) setting
continues to lag. While adoption of certified EHR technology (CEHRT) in the acute and
ambulatory care space is now almost universal, it is substantially lower within the PAC
settings since these providers were never eligible for federal incentives to adopt CEHRT
offered under the Medicare and Medicaid EHR Incentive Programs. Although EHR
adoption is growing in the PAC setting, it is uneven. Interoperable exchange of health
information is not routine or widely used,2 and providers use a variety of often inadequate
and non-standardized systems, often resorting to self-developed templates to make their
EHRs more user-friendly. As a result, most providers in this setting are not fully satisfied
with their EHRs when it comes to supporting their most important interoperability needs. 3

AAPM&R remains concerned that these disparities could put PAC providers at a major
disadvantage in terms of information blocking compliance and impose other challenges that
providers in other settings may not face. We request that ASTP/ONC work with CMS to
thoughtfully consider approaches to incentivize the adoption of CEHRT across PAC
settings, and work with Congress, as necessary, to effectuate such interventions. This
could include financial incentives like those offered in the acute and ambulatory settings,
as well as bonus points and reporting flexibilities for those in PAC settings who
voluntarily use CEHRT to collect and submit quality data. We also have heard from our
members that grants to fund pilot programs that use HIT to improve continuity of care
across the rehabilitation spectrum and solve other safety/quality issues— all of which rely
heavily on greater and more immediate access to EHI— would be helpful. In general,
more widespread adoption of CEHRT within the PAC setting will not only help these
providers comply with information blocking regulations, but also enhance care coordination
and communications between ambulatory, acute, and post-acute care settings, and raise the
bar for quality, overall.
