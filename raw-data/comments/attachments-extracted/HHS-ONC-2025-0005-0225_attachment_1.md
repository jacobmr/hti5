Dear Dr. Keane,

The Fenway Institute at Fenway Health welcomes the opportunity to comment on the proposed
actions. The Fenway Institute is the research, education and training, and policy arm of Fenway
Health, a federally qualified health center and Ryan White Part C HIV clinic in Boston.

As medical and public health researchers and professionals, we write in strong opposition to the
proposal to remove structured data elements from the U.S. Core Data for Interoperability
(USCDI), including fields for sexual orientation, gender identity, chosen name, pronouns, and
sex parameters for clinical use. The collection and use of these data in clinical settings is
critically important and foundational in the provision of culturally responsive and clinically
competent care for LGBTQI+ patients. According to Gallup, 9% of U.S. adults are LGBTQ+.2 It
is important for providers to know about the sexual orientation and gender identity (SOGI) of
patients, as this can inform clinical decision support and preventive screenings. SOGI data can
also be combined with race and ethnicity and other demographic data for purposes of population
health management.3 Collecting and using chosen name and pronouns is essential to providing
affirming care to transgender and nonbinary patients, as well as other patients who may not use
their legal or given name. Collecting data on sex parameters for clinical use is essential to
provide culturally responsive and clinically competent care. We believe that assigned sex at birth
is medically and clinically relevant data that should be collected and used in Electronic Health
Records, along with SOGI data and anatomical inventory data, to inform clinical decision
support, preventive screenings, and population health management.

The removal of these elements from USCDI will result in disruptions in continuity of care, and
care that is not based on a full knowledge of a patient’s demographics. It will undermine
accuracy and clinical competency: these structured data elements assist with patient matching,

We share ONC’s vision about the promise of leveraging health IT to build a nationwide,
interoperable, value-based, person-centered health system. The Fenway Institute has engaged
with ONC since 2012 on issues related to the adoption and implementation of national sexual
orientation and gender identity (SOGI) health IT standards. We appreciate the agency’s ongoing
responsiveness to our priorities across multiple administrations. In 2015, ONC adopted SOGI
standards as required fields in the “demographics” section of the 2015 Edition Base Electronic
Health Record (EHR) Definition certification criteria, making SOGI part of all Certified
Electronic Health Record Technology (CEHRT) products.5 Further, in addition to being required
fields for EHR certification, SOGI has also been included in the Interoperability Standards
Advisory (ISA) since the ISA was first published in 2015.6 SOGI standards have achieved
steadily increasing and high levels of maturity and adoption since 2015, as reflected in the 2021
reference edition of the ISA (pages 43-45) and as evidenced by SOGI’s inclusion in USCDI.7,8,9

Knowing a patient’s current sexual orientation, gender identity, sex assigned at birth, name used,
and pronouns, as well as their anatomical inventory, is important for informing clinical decision
support, preventive screenings, and for managing health disparities at the population level.10
LGBTQI+ people experience health disparities related to discrimination and stigma,11 disparities
in accessing care,12 and disparities in insurance coverage related to higher rates of poverty.13

causes LGBTQI+ people to delay or avoid medical care.15 Anti-LGBTQI+ discrimination
negatively affects the health and well-being of LGBTQI+ people.16 It also correlates with
disparities in risk factors. For example, a study of LGBT veterans found that those who
experienced discrimination in health care reported higher rates of tobacco use and lower rates of
disclosure of their LGBT identity to health care providers.17

This is why it is important to collect and use SOGI data in clinical settings. Collecting and using
these data in health care enables the provision of high quality care to LGBTQI+ patients to
address and reduce these disparities. We urge you as a medical professional to “do no harm” and
reject the proposed removal of SOGI and related fields in USCDI.

Thank you for the opportunity to comment on the proposed changes to USCDI, which would roll
back 15 years of systemic changes that have improved LGBTQI+ health equity in our country.
We urge you to reject these proposed changes that would literally erase LGBTQI+ patients from
health IT systems and undermine the health and wellbeing of millions of Americans. Should you
have any questions please email us at information@fenwayhealth.org
