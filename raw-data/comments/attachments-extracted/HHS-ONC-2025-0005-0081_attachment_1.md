On behalf of the physician and medical student members of the American Medical Association (AMA), I
appreciate the opportunity to offer our comments on the proposed rule titled, “Health Data, Technology,
and Interoperability: ASTP/ONC Deregulatory Actions To Unleash Prosperity” (Proposed Rule). The
AMA supports ASTP/ONC’s efforts to reduce unnecessary regulatory burden and incentivize new
innovations within the certified health information technology (health IT) ecosystem. Our shared goals of
patient empowerment and improved care coordination go hand in hand with appropriate regulatory
oversight. Our comments support the Trump Administration’s efforts while providing key
recommendations.

Augmented Intelligence (AI) Transparency

The AMA appreciates ASTP/ONC’s continued attention to reducing unnecessary certification burden
while positioning the Certification Program for an optimal Fast Healthcare Interoperability Resources
(FHIR)-enabled application programming interface (API) future. Artificial Intelligence (AI) has
tremendous potential and will benefit from API data streams. AI development, deployment, and use merit
a holistic approach to ensure trust, and transparency is key. A recent study by the AMA revealed that 65
percent of physicians view increased oversight as one of their top two AI adoption prerequisites, and 80
percent see transparency as essential. 1 ASTP/ONC is proposing to modify its Decision-Support
Interventions (DSI) policies. Current DSI requirements are the only enforceable transparency
baseline for AI that are embedded in physician workflows. Eliminating those requirements would
weaken trust in clinical AI at the very moment its use is accelerating.

In its Proposed Rule, ASTP/ONC explains that: (1) clinical users reportedly lack time or technical
background to engage with DSI source attribute information, and ASTP/ONC has seen no evidence that
clinicians have used source attribute functions in early deployments; (2) ASTP/ONC has encountered no
evidence that the DSI transparency requirements have improved evaluation or effective use of AI; and (3)

However, the AMA respectfully disagrees with each premise: (1) the fact that busy clinicians do not

lack value. It is evidence the information has been designed and surfaced for the wrong user at the wrong

requirements, not eliminating them. Removing the only enforceable transparency baseline in certified
health IT would not address the issue of uneven oversight, rather, it could exacerbate the issue of
inconsistent oversight, shift responsibility for evaluation and liability to physicians, and risk accelerating
adoption of opaque predictive tools in clinical workflows that lack the minimum guardrails that patients
and physicians deserve and expect.

The AMA views the key policy question as not whether transparency should be required, but what
level of transparency and risk management is appropriate, according to risk level, without
imposing unusable, or purely technical, documentation.

Where Problems May Arise

We share ASTP/ONC’s goal of reducing burden and promoting high quality AI in health care. However,
a complete removal of predictive DSI transparency and intervention risk management requirements may
risk creating predictable failure points in real world clinical use. AI can shape diagnosis, triage, treatment
planning, and resource prioritization. Without a basic transparency and governance floor, physicians and
health care organizations may lack reliable, comparable information to assess whether a tool is
appropriate for a given patient, which is precisely where the consequences of error are most severe. The
fact that early users may not have accessed or modified source attributable information should not be read
as proof that transparency is unnecessary. It more likely signals that disclosures were not presented in a
workflow-relevant, usable form, or that the intended users are governance teams rather than frontline
clinicians. At the same time, removing baseline risk management expectations eliminates a practical
backstop just as AI use is accelerating, making it harder to detect drift, bias, or unsafe performance
changes before patients are harmed. In the absence of standardized artifacts in certified health IT, the
burden of evaluation, monitoring, and accountability will inevitably shift to physicians and care
teams who often lack the time, technical resources, and market leverage to fill those gaps,
increasing both patient risk and physician exposure.

Recommendations to Accelerate AI Trust and Safety

AMA recommends that ASTP/ONC retain useful elements of predictive DSI transparency and risk
management expectations, while right-sizing them based on risk. A simple, two-tier framework would
preserve essential safeguards where predictive tools can meaningfully affect clinical decisions and reduce
burden for lower impact use cases.

    •   High risk predictive DSIs merit more safeguards where stakes are highest. ASTP/ONC
        should define “high risk” using clear, objective triggers tied to real world clinical impact, such as
        tools that materially influence diagnosis, triage, treatment choices, urgency, or patient
        prioritization (examples only). For these tools, ASTP/ONC should retain a minimum safety and
        trust package that is practical and usable: concise, plain language transparency about what the

DSI Data Scope

The AMA appreciates ASTP/ONC’s effort to reduce unnecessary certification burden while preserving
decision-support functionality that physicians rely on in practice. ASTP/ONC proposes to narrow the
scope of predictive DSI enablement, limiting it from any data element expressed in the U.S. Core Data for
Interoperability (USCDI) to a more restricted subset of data. ASTP/ONC explains this proposal is driven
by developer feedback that supporting predictive DSIs using any USCDI element has proven burdensome
and costly, with “questionable value,” and that some USCDI elements are unlikely to be meaningful
predictive inputs. ASTP/ONC further states that focusing on fewer data elements would significantly
reduce burden without diminishing users’ ability to activate a wide variety of predictive DSIs that rely on
the remaining inputs.

Where Problems May Arise

AMA can support the goal of eliminating checkbox requirements that do not advance clinical care.
However, we are concerned that narrowing predictive DSI input scope as a blanket policy could
unintentionally constrain clinically important predictive DSIs, particularly those intended for higher
stakes use cases, by discouraging or deprioritizing the availability and usability of broader, clinically
relevant medical information. Many predictive DSIs that can materially affect patient outcomes may
depend on longitudinal history and multi-condition context, including laboratory trends, imaging report
history, and other structured elements captured across the USCDI. Similarly, some use cases may benefit
from incorporating social/behavioral context and other determinants of health when available. Narrowing
the certification floor without a clear risk-based framework, risks creating variability in what physicians
can expect across products, and it could increase the risk of patient harm if AI tools operate with
incomplete context or if health care organizations cannot reliably enable the predictive DSIs they need for
complex patients.

Recommendations to Accelerate AI Trust and Safety

AMA recommends the following simple, tiered approach that reduces burden where the stakes are lower,
while preserving full clinical context where the risk of patient harm is highest.

    •   High risk predictive DSIs: Preserve access to the full range of clinically relevant data by
        keeping predictive DSI enablement tied to any data in the USCDI. High risk tools often require
        richer context to perform safely and consistently across complex patients and care settings.

Information Blocking AI Modifications

Revised “Access” and “Use” Definitions

ASTP/ONC proposes to revise the definitions of “access” and “use” to make explicit that these concepts
apply when electronic health information (EHI) is made available or acted upon via automated means,
including robotic process automation (bot) and autonomous/agentic AI systems. ASTP/ONC further
explains that the definitions are not limited to manual processes, apply equally to automated access,
exchange, and use (including autonomous AI), and that an actor interfering with such automated access,
exchange, and use may implicate the information blocking definition under the Information Blocking
Rule (IBR).

The AMA recognizes the value of this clarification. Automated access and use can improve efficiency
and scale routine workflows, and clarity that the IBR applies regardless of the technical route taken, can
help deter anti-competitive tactics that impede patient- and physician-directed data access. At the same
time, the AMA is concerned that, without explicit guardrails, the proposal could be misread as
discouraging reasonable, risk-based cybersecurity protections that are essential when automation expands
the attack surface (e.g., high frequency querying, bulk pulls, persistent tokens/credentials). This is
especially important because ASTP/ONC anticipates significantly increased use of automated means, and
because automated tools can magnify the impact of system compromise and inappropriate downstream
use.

Accordingly, the AMA recommends that ASTP/ONC clarify guidance stating that actors may
implement and enforce reasonable, consistently applied security controls for automated access/use,
such as rate limiting, anomaly/bot detection, and temporary blocking of demonstrably
compromised automation, without such controls being presumed to interfere for IBR purposes,
provided that they are tailored to objective risk and applied in a nondiscriminatory manner.
Because “use” is proposed to include EHI being understood and acted upon by automation, ASTP/ONC
should clarify delegation/authorization expectations for automated agents (e.g., documented
authorization, scoped permissions/least privilege, auditable logs) so that physician practices are not placed
in a “damned if you do, damned if you don’t” posture when protecting patients and striving to avoid
allegations of violating the IBR.

Vital Electronic Heath Record (EHR) System Functions and Costs to Physicians

EHRs have become core components in physicians’ medical practices, embedded into workflows, and are
vital to the practice of medicine. All clinicians rely on a wide array of EHR functionality and expect those
capabilities to be there when they are needed. Any disruption to an EHR’s “look and feel” may negatively
impact patient care, introduce harm, and impact physician practice.

The AMA is concerned that ASTP/ONC’s proposed removal of core EHR developer certification
requirements would move physician practices backward by stripping away a baseline floor of
functionality that physicians rely on every day to deliver care and run their practices. When foundational
capabilities are no longer required, they do not disappear, they reappear as optional modules,
implementation fees, or premium features. That will force physician practices to pay again for basic
tools that have historically been included by default, compounding EHR costs, and diverting limited
resources from patient care.

This approach also poses risks to patient safety and continuity of care. Certified health IT has long served
as a predictable foundation upon which physicians can rely, regardless of vendor or setting. Removing
core requirements increases the likelihood of missing or inconsistent capabilities that directly support
clinical workflow, documentation, ordering, results management, and care coordination. In an
environment where current, core requirements have been removed, certified API access alone is not a
substitute for baseline workflow functionality. A policy framework that prioritizes FHIR/API availability
while allowing core functions to erode, will not meaningfully advance physicians toward next generation
care, especially if physicians must purchase addons to restore the essentials.

Finally, removing the core requirements risks creating regulatory gaps that invite a fragmented, multi-
vendor patchwork: third party service companies will step in to sell replacement capabilities, pushing
physicians into a subscription model where practices must contract with multiple vendors to assemble
what is now delivered as a coherent, certified baseline. The AMA urges ASTP/ONC to right-size, rather
than eliminate, core certification by maintaining a clear minimum set of required capabilities, phasing in
any changes to avoid disruption, and requiring transparent disclosure of what functionality is included,
versus sold as an addon, so that physician practices are not forced to repurchase the basics in order to
keep caring for patients.

FHIR/API Strategy

The AMA supports ASTP/ONC’s goal to modernize the Certification Program for a FHIR-enabled future,
and we recognize the opportunity for FHIR-based APIs to advance interoperability, enable a more
competitive app ecosystem, and reduce avoidable administrative friction over time. At the same time, we
urge ASTP/ONC to ensure that it will not aggressively reduce and remove long-standing,
functionality-oriented and non-FHIR-based certification criteria, the absence of which will create a
transition valley that shifts cost, operational burden, and patient safety risk to physicians, especially
small practices, and to patients in rural and under-resourced communities.

Modernizing certification more aligned with FHIR/APIs may be the right path forward; however,
ASTP/ONC should preserve a non-negotiable clinical safety and usability floor while the ecosystem
matures. ASTP/ONC should not weaken baseline expectations for safe, usable transitions of care
summaries and reliable reconciliation and incorporation of medications, allergies/intolerances,
procedures, and problems, which are capabilities that physicians depend on every day to prevent
avoidable harm. These are precisely the workflows that fail most visibly when exchange is incomplete,
fragmented, or inconsistent across settings and stakeholders in health care delivery.

Accordingly, we recommend that ASTP/ONC pair any retirement of the consolidated clinical data
architecture (C-CDA) functionality-oriented criteria with a phased implementation approach grounded in
demonstrable readiness. Before sunsetting or materially descoping document-centric certification
requirements, ASTP/ONC should require evidence, through measurable readiness criteria, that FHIR-
based workflows are available and used at scale across inpatient, ambulatory, and post-acute settings,
including among small and rural practices. This readiness approach should include developer attestations
regarding real world capability, support, and cost impact, and independent testing to validate that API-

FHIR/API Automation and Write Capability

We support ASTP/ONC’s strategic direction to prioritize FHIR-based APIs that enhance automation and
API performance, move beyond read-only interactions, and expand the scope of data available to support
public health, quality, and other programmatic needs. These aims hold promises as more capable APIs
can enable clinically useful workflows, reduce avoidable manual effort, and modernize how data moves
across the ecosystem.

Expanding API capability is an opportunity, and it must be paired with enforceable safeguards to prevent
new categories of physician burden, patient harm, and compliance exposure, as write-capable workflows
scale. The AMA urges ASTP/ONC to recognize that beyond read-only use cases, especially patients
generated data ingestion and AI enabled write back into the EHR, create a foreseeable risk of
failure when data quality is variable, provenance is unclear, or security controls are uneven. These
risks are amplified when unverified or AI-generated content flows into structured fields that can trigger
clinical decision-support (CDS), populate the medical record, or influence quality, public health, or other
reporting programs.

ASTP/ONC should require hard controls that prevent unverified or AI-generated data from feeding CDS,
quality reporting, or public health reporting, unless and until the data are validated. This physician
review/accept/reject functionality should not be optional: physicians are the clinical experts responsible
for patient care, and they cannot be placed in a posture where opaque automation silently changes the
record or contaminates downstream reporting inputs. Without such controls, unmanageable liability
and audit exposure will likely ensue for physicians and physician organizations, particularly in
resource-limited settings, when programmatic measures and external reporting are driven by data
that were never vetted.

ASTP/ONC should require standardized provenance tagging and verification status for any patient
generated or AI-derived data that is ingested or written back into certified health IT. At minimum,
systems should clearly label the data source, how it was generated, and whether it is being used for
decision-support or reporting. Provenance and verification are essential to safe clinical use, defensible
documentation, and accurate reporting; they are also a prerequisite for meaningful interoperability when
data are shared across organizations.

ASTP/ONC should pair the transition beyond read-only interactions with a clear security baseline for
write-capable APIs, including strong authentication and authorization models, least privilege scopes,
robust auditing and logging, and protections appropriate for automation at scale (e.g., rate limiting and
anomaly detection). Expanding API scope without explicit security expectations increases the attack
surface and heightens the likelihood of credential compromise, bulk exfiltration, and downstream misuse,
risks that frequently land on physicians even when failures originate in vendor systems or third-party
tools.

Retain C-CDA Conformity and Ensure FHIR/API Robustness

We appreciate ASTP/ONC’s recognition that C-CDA remains widely adopted and will continue to be
used in the near term for transitions of care across settings, including inpatient to ambulatory, ambulatory-
to-ambulatory, and inpatient to skilled nursing facility (SNF) and long-term care workflows. That
acknowledgement reflects operational reality: physicians and care teams still depend on document-based
exchange to maintain continuity of care, particularly when trading partners have mismatched technical
capabilities and inconsistent FHIR/API conformance.

We support ASTP/ONC’s transition toward FHIR-based exchange, and that transition must be executed
in a way that does not increase physician burden or widen inequities across care settings. The risk is a
two-tier interoperability ecosystem: well-resourced systems advance to robust FHIR-enabled workflows,
while small practices, rural communities, and SNF settings are left with degraded handoffs, fragmented
data, and increasing manual work. When transition information is incomplete or unreliable, physicians
must spend time chasing missing records and performing manual reconciliation, work that is
uncompensated, operationally infeasible for resource limited practices, and directly associated with
avoidable patient harm risk.

The AMA urges ASTP/ONC to make a clear commitment: ASTP/ONC should not descope C-CDA
linked certification requirements until minimum FHIR conformance and usability expectations are
demonstrably met for real world transitions of care. At minimum, ASTP/ONC should require that
FHIR-based transition workflows demonstrate: (1) reliability and performance in practice; (2) consistent
conformance across vendors and trading partners; and (3) clinical completeness for transition content
(including medications, allergies/intolerances, problems, procedures, and key clinical context). Absent
those minimum conditions, removing or weakening C-CDA linked requirements would predictably create
a transition valley in which physicians shoulder more manual work while patients experience more gaps
in care.

In parallel, ASTP/ONC should maintain clear C-CDA quality and performance expectations during the
transition period to prevent document exchange from degrading precisely when it remains essential.
ASTP/ONC’s policy posture should be “yes, and”: modernize to FHIR while protecting current transition
workflows until the replacement is demonstrably as good or better—clinically, operationally, and
economically.

Finally, the AMA recommends that ASTP/ONC adopt a cost and contracting guardrail to prevent pay-to-
play interoperability from undermining equitable transition readiness. Any FHIR-based transition

Quality Measurement

ASTP/ONC’s effort to right-size certification and shift market attention toward FHIR/API-based
modernization is a promising path forward, given the real costs of certification and the need to avoid
locking in dated implementation approaches. We also appreciate that ASTP/ONC proposes a future
effective date (January 1, 2027) for removing the “clinical quality measures—filter” criterion, rather than
an immediate sunset, and that the proposed revision to “clinical quality measures—report” is narrowly
targeted to remove an expired transition clause.

Nonetheless, ASTP’s proposals regarding these criteria raise substantial risks of unintended
consequences for physician practices that rely on basic quality measurement workflow functionality
inside their certified health IT. The CQM filter capability was originally conceived to support electronic
clinical quality measure (eCQM) reporting at the group practice and accountable care organization (ACO)
level and to enable filtering by patient characteristics to identify disparities and gaps in care. In practice,
these workflow functions remain essential for many practices, particularly those with limited analytic
infrastructure, because they enable day-to-day operational use of quality data: stratifying populations,
validating measure denominators/numerators, generating clinician-facing lists for gap closure, and
reconciling measure discrepancies across systems. If certification no longer anchors a baseline capability,
practices will predictably face an inability to stratify/filter/measure populations without workarounds, a
return to manual chart abstraction and spreadsheet-driven reconciliation, increased measure discrepancies
and multiple sources of truth across EHR, registry, and payer systems, and additional costs as vendors
repackage previously included functions as premium analytics addons.

ASTP/ONC further states it believes certified eCQM filter functionality is low use and that broader
reporting programs require supplemental capabilities beyond certification; it also suggests developer time
is better spent on modernization efforts that leverage FHIR-based APIs for quality data exchange. We
agree that API-based exchange can be transformative over time, and API availability is no substitute for
baseline workflow functionality in the near term, especially for independent physician practices. Until
there is a low cost, consistent, and proven method to exchange quality measurement information
via APIs across the full ecosystem (EHRs, registries, ACOs, payers, and CMS aligned
intermediaries), removing the certified floor risks a net increase in burden and cost which will be
felt most by the practices least able to absorb it.

We therefore recommend a “right-size, don’t eliminate” approach with a clear transition plan.
Specifically: (1) maintain a core certification floor for quality measurement workflow functionality
through the transition, even as ASTP/ONC continues to pivot certification toward FHIR/API-oriented
criteria; (2) phase the sunset of any criterion to occur after concrete evidence that API-based quality
exchange is working at scale, at low cost, and with consistent implementation; (3) require a transparent
implementation and uptake plan for modern eCQM/digital quality measure execution capabilities
(including measure logic and consistent measure calculation behavior across products), rather than
assuming the market has already converged; and (4) coordinate with CMS and other federal program
timelines so that physician practices are not forced into manual workarounds or vendor priced bolt-ons to
meet Medicare quality obligations.

User-Centered Design

ASTP/ONC proposes to remove the Safety Enhanced Design certification criterion as of the effective date
of the Final Rule resulting from this rulemaking. The AMA supports the Agency’s broader goal of
reducing unnecessary certification burden, particularly where program requirements have drifted into
“check-the-box” compliance that does not reliably translate into better design or safer performance in the
field. We also recognize ASTP/ONC’s concern that the current user centered design (UCD) submission
mechanics—including recruiting participants and producing standardized artifacts for Certified Health IT
Product List (CHPL) posting—can impose non-trivial administrative friction. However, fully eliminating
certification-linked UCD expectations at the same time as the program shifts toward API-oriented
certification would be a strategic mistake. This approach risks undoing hard-won progress in reducing
physician burden and mitigating preventable patient harm caused by usability failures, while leaving a
growing ecosystem of API-enabled clinical workflow tools without even a lightweight, safety-oriented
usability baseline.

From the physician practice perspective, usability is not cosmetic; it is operational safety infrastructure.
Poor workflow fit increases cognitive load and “click tax,” drives workarounds, and pushes
documentation and reconciliation into after-hours time, burden that cannot be solved through payment
policy or clinical standards alone. Usability defects also create foreseeable patient safety risks: unclear
displays, problematic defaults, brittle workflow handoffs, and alert fatigue contribute to errors of
omission and commission in time-sensitive settings. These risks do not disappear in an API world; they
migrate. Clinician facing applications (apps) built on EHR APIs (including FHIR-style tools) can
introduce new failure modes that are uniquely relevant to patient safety—partial or filtered data retrieval,
latency, mis-scoped permissions, inconsistent mappings, and ambiguous error handling. In the emerging
AI environment, these issues can be amplified: an AI summary tool that pulls incomplete data but
presents the output as complete, can directly mislead physicians and harm patients. Similarly,
administrative automation (including prior authorization workflows) that fails silently, can create hidden
rework, missed deadlines, and downstream delays in care—burden that lands on physicians and staff even
when the failure originates in vendor-controlled API behavior.

Accordingly, the AMA recommends that ASTP/ONC right-size rather than eliminate UCD by replacing
the criterion with a narrower, API-focused safety/usability by design analog that is explicitly tailored to
reduce developer burden while protecting physician workflow integrity and patient safety. At minimum,
ASTP/ONC should establish an API-focused pathway that prioritizes clinician-facing workflow apps
(while also applying to patient access apps and payer/admin automation) and requires three lightweight,
high value artifacts: (1) scenario and task-oriented usability testing with representative end users, scaled
to the risk and complexity of the API-enabled workflow; (2) standardized API implementation and
workflow notes that clearly disclose intended use, known limitations, data completeness assumptions,
provenance/uncertainty cues (especially for AI-mediated views), latency expectations, error handling
behaviors, versioning/deprecation timelines, and recommended fallback workflows; and (3) a lightweight
safety-focused risk analysis that identifies foreseeable hazards from incorrect, missing, delayed, or mis-
scoped data and describes mitigations (including user-facing cues and operational guardrails).

Privacy and Security Certification Criteria

Physicians strive to appropriately secure patient and practice data and want to do their part to ensure that
their technology systems deliver effective protections. ASTP/ONC’s proposal to remove all the privacy
and security certification criteria and the associated Privacy and Security Certification Framework would
increase complexity and burden for physicians and other providers. In addition, ASTP/ONC’s stated
intention to change the current, blanket privacy and security requirements for all certification criteria, to a
system in which each criterion will have its own, embedded privacy and security requirements, will only
add to the technological intricacies that a physician practice will have to manage. This change would also
be a departure from the Trump Administration’s Executive Order 14192, a de-regulatory initiative that
seeks to lessen complexity and burden.

The AMA urges ASTP/ONC to reconsider these changes to the certification program and to retain
at least the more crucial privacy and security certification criteria. At a minimum, ASTP/ONC
should guarantee that current criteria remain in place until FHIR standards fully mature and provide an
adequate implementation timeline and glide path to reduce complexity in the transition to more API-
focused certification criteria.

It is important to note that physician practices are often rural and small- to medium-sized entities
operating with narrow financial margins. Many physicians are small business owners who cannot afford
the expense of a full-time IT professional and often must contract for IT, privacy, and cybersecurity
services. Removing the assurances that providers receive under the ASTP/ONC Certification Program’s
Privacy and Security Criteria would impose undue administrative and financial burdens on physicians as
well as diverting time and resources from direct patient care.

Finalizing these proposals would require physicians and their staff to spend considerable time
selecting appropriate technology for their IT infrastructure and ensuring compliance with federal
and state regulations or expend substantial resources to engage a health IT vendor or consultant.
Financial resource constraints mean that practices will be expected to choose between helping patients
and adopting innovative value-based care models, or making substantial investments in additional IT
infrastructure and consultants to replace the robust privacy and security certification criteria that these
proposals would remove. This will lead to inefficiencies and waste while exacerbating the trend of
shuttering small physician practices.

Physician practices understand and accept that the capabilities provided by Certified EHR Technology
(CEHRT) only assist an eligible professional in improving their technical safeguards, and the use of
CEHRT alone does not guarantee compliance with the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability
Act (HIPAA) Privacy or Security Rules. These physician practices currently enter into contracts with
attorneys and other outside consultants to meet the requirements of HIPAA compliance. By removing the
privacy and security certification criteria, a practice will have to engage an additional, costly technology
consultants to ensure that their technology infrastructure will provide the necessary privacy and security
capabilities to achieve HIPAA compliance. Again, physicians and their staff often do not have full-time,
trained IT staff that could fulfill purchasing and implementation determinations without the guidance

Care Coordination Certification Criteria

Transitions of Care

The AMA recognizes the importance of transitions of care and its critical role in health care delivery as
pivotal moments that directly impact patient safety. A key component of effective transitions of care is
the ability of physicians and other providers to accurately match patients to their medical records, where
and when needed. Demographic health data elements are a powerful tool to improve the accurate
identification of patients to match with the correct records.

ASTP/ONC proposes several changes to the transitions of care certification criterion to simplify and
refocus its requirements on enabling receipt of C-CDA documents, positioning the criterion for future
evolution to support receipt of FHIR-formatted data. One of the proposal’s provisions specifically calls
for removing the requirements in the “create” section that allow a user to create a transition of
care/referral summary using a continuity of care document (CCD) that includes patient matching data.
Certification requirements that include patient matching criteria in the CCD improve patient
safety and security by reducing patient misidentification. Moreover, it is important to note that this is
the only section within a certification criterion that references patient matching.

Although USCDI has been integral to the standardization of available data elements within certified
health IT products, there are many elements within the current version of USCDI (e.g., first name, last
name, and date of birth) that do not have standards that dictate how these data should be entered, resulting
in variation across systems and challenges in data usability and care coordination. Until USCDI is
updated with specific standards to improve patient matching, or an alternative criterion is included
for patient matching, the AMA urges ASTP/ONC to retain the sole certification criterion that
specifically references patient matching.

Security Tags—Summary of Care

The AMA supports the advancement of safe and patient-empowered sharing of health information for all.
ASTP/ONC’s proposal to remove the “security tags—summary of care—send” and “security tags—
summary of care—receive” certification criteria would represent a retreat for our community after many
years of progress. We recommend that ASTP/ONC keep these criteria as optional and, wherever these
standards are used, implement them to support security tagging of C-CDA documents at the document,
section, and entry levels.

The future of data segmentation and computable consent lies in FHIR-based standards, and a substantial
share of data exchange still occurs using C-CDA, and there remain significant utility and value in these
criteria as currently defined. The optional criteria should remain in place as we prepare to fully transition
to FHIR in the near-term. The ability to meet patients’ data sharing preferences has been largely managed
by “humans in the middle.” That is, records requests have often been reviewed and considered by medical
office staff. Sharing information with an outside entity would require information management
professionals to manually collate and redact information, based on patient preference, law, or office
policy.

The Security Tags—Summary of Care Certification Criteria, support patient data sharing preferences.
The AMA co-founded and serves on the Governing Board of the Shift Collaborative to help address
this gap. Shift is an independent health care task force of over 300 expert stakeholders that seeks to
advance a health care ecosystem in which better technology for clinical data sharing increases trust
among patients, providers, and caregivers, improving health quality and outcomes.

Today’s health data systems often force patients to choose between care and privacy. Patients should be
able to share the information they want safely, securely, and on their own terms. Ensuring that
individuals’ data sharing preferences are respected is a critical component of advancing safe, effective,
and patient-centered value-based care and chronic disease management. Retaining these criteria as
optional will support these efforts.

The feedback Shift has received is that patients want, and physicians and other providers need,
more features that support granular segmentation. In the absence of reliable, standards-based
segmentation capabilities and with the complexities of IBR practices still unresolved, physicians and
other providers may over-share to avoid information-blocking liability, under-share to mitigate privacy
risk, or under-document information in patients’ charts, thereby making the chart less reliable.

Shift takes the view that data segmentation and computable consent can only be achieved at scale through
FHIR, which is why Shift is focusing its efforts on updating FHIR standards and collaborating with key
partners across the community. In recognition of the need for granular segmentation, Shift created an
open, standards-based sandbox tool that includes a redacted record viewer that simulates what a patient
record with redacted information might look like, based on dynamic patient consent preferences. It also
supports workflow demonstrations using synthetic data in a mock EHR environment and will soon allow
vendors to connect their products via FHIR APIs to test and validate these workflows within their systems
using Shift’s synthetic data, use cases, and implementation guidance. To this end, Shift is drafting an
implementation guide to assist technology partners with implementation.

With the creation of its sandbox, Shift’s work is supporting the community’s transition to FHIR. As this
work continues, the AMA recommends that ASTP/ONC keep these criteria optional, collaborate
with Shift to use its sandbox for testing, and identify opportunities to encourage other community
stakeholders to leverage the sandbox. The future of data segmentation and computable consent is in
FHIR, and Shift’s sandbox is our community’s best shot at quickly scaling these functionalities.

requirements will not support the needs of physicians and other providers. We urge ASTP/ONC to

Real-world testing reports are a fundamental tool for providers when making technology purchasing

As we have noted, FHIR is the future. However, a fully mature and widely adopted FHIR standard is still
years away. As the transition progresses, we support ASTP/ONC in adding more FHIR API-focused
certification criteria, and we recommend that the current real world testing requirements remain in
place until FHIR’s maturation is widely accepted across the community.

Insights Condition and Maintenance of Certification Data

Since its enactment in the 21st Century Cures Act, the AMA has supported the importance of Insights
Condition and Maintenance of Certification data, and the value of requiring certified health IT developers
to report on real-world physician use of CEHRT in actual clinical settings is in reducing burden for
physicians. In addition, over the past several years, we have provided comment to ASTP/ONC and CMS
on how Insights data can replace or augment attestations from Medicare Shared Savings Program
participating physicians, regarding the use of CEHRT as part of CMS Merit-based Incentive Payment
System Promoting Interoperability performance category requirements, further reducing physician
administrative and reporting burden.

For these reasons, we recommend that ASTP/ONC withdraw its proposal to remove and descope
the measures associated with the Insights Condition and Maintenance of Certification
requirements. The reporting generated from the Insights Conditions should play a major role in helping
Department of Health and Human Services (HHS) evaluate broader CEHRT and certified API adoption
and use. Using Insights Condition data is the most effective path forward for assessing CEHRT and API
adoption and interoperability progress, without requiring individual physicians to take time away from
delivering patient care to report duplicative interoperability information to CMS.

With work underway across HHS to facilitate broader-based data exchange through the Trusted Exchange
Framework and Common Agreement (TEFCA) and the CMS Interoperability Framework,
interoperability is a central pillar of the Trump Administration’s health care delivery strategy. Insights
data can provide the metrics HHS needs to measure progress toward and the success of its initiatives.
ASTP/ONC’s proposal to reduce the Insights reporting requirements to only the “use of FHIR in apps
through certified health IT” measure will provide only a compartmentalized view of the broader
interoperability picture. We encourage ASTP/ONC to retain the full suite of seven Insights measures and
leverage them to help demonstrate progress toward the Administration’s goals while reducing the
reporting burden on physicians and other providers.

Public Health Certification Criteria

The AMA prioritizes timely, integrated, interoperable data sharing among the Centers for Disease Control
and Prevention (CDC), public health agencies (PHAs), physicians, and other health care providers. The

public health-related certification criteria are integral to this exchange, ensuring that public health data
can flow with minimal burden and bolster work across communities.

Unfortunately, PHA data and systems often remain siloed. They operate independently and often lack
straightforward ways to share information across state lines or even within agencies within a given state,
hindering their ability to support one another efficiently. At the front lines of patient care, physicians play
an essential role in public health surveillance by reporting diseases and conditions to the CDC and PHAs.
Physicians similarly expect that PHAs will communicate with health professionals in their jurisdiction
about their community’s population health status and needs, based on these data.

The AMA recommends that ASTP/ONC maintain the Public Health Certification Criteria until the FHIR
Standard fully matures and there is sufficient implementation time to ensure that CDC, PHAs, and
physicians have the technology and standards in place to transition from Clinical Document Architecture
(CDA) reporting to FHIR. We agree that the ongoing FHIR-based modernization efforts are promising.
However, until the issues with readiness to adopt FHIR standards, as well as concerns regarding the
performance, scalability, or maintenance of these standards are resolved, ASTP/ONC must leave the
transmission to cancer registries, as well as the transmission to PHAs for electronic case reporting
(eCR), antimicrobial use and resistance reporting, and health care surveys certification criteria in
place. We support an effective date no earlier than January 1, 2029, for the removal of the CDA-based
public health-related certification criteria and the adoption of FHIR-based approaches to reporting.

The AMA has long advocated an HHS-wide approach to strengthen public health infrastructure by
leveraging more advanced health IT and data exchange standards. An important component of this
approach is to support positive financial incentives for physician practices to adopt technology for public
health reporting and help ensure bidirectional information sharing. We advocate for positive incentives
for physicians to upgrade their EHR and other health IT systems to support eCR as well as positive
incentives to submit case reports that are timely and complete. Financial incentives are most effective
when framed as a positive incentive rather than a penalty. These incentives are important for often
under-resourced physician practices to invest in technology and fully transition to FHIR.

In addition, aligning data standards through the certification program and adhering to them reduces
burden for physicians and other providers by minimizing the work required for two systems to
communicate and share data effectively. Many physician practices lack the expertise and resources to
meet sophisticated IT requirements without guidance from the certification program and substantial
support from health IT vendors or consultants. Finalizing the proposed changes to the public health
certification requirements will require practices to divert resources from patient care, including
patients with complex and chronic conditions, to support the time-consuming and burdensome
effort to migrate to FHIR.

In the HTI-1 Final Regulation, ASTP/ONC supported the important connection between eCR certification
and adoption and use of public health technology. That regulation included a new certification criterion
for Transmission to public health agencies – electronic case reporting, which required health IT modules
to adopt consensus-based, industry-developed standards for eCR. Modules were also required to create
case reports for electronic transmission, consume and process case report responses, and consume and
process eCR trigger codes using Health Level 7 standards, either with the FHIR or CDA Implementation
Guides. This standardization of eCR was intended to improve consistency and increase real-time
communication between health care and public health while imposing minimal burden on physicians or
other providers.

program requirements had not been standards-based, and numerous examples revealed deficiencies in

IBR Exceptions

Removal of Subpart D Exception and Other Provisions Specific to TEFCA

The AMA and ASTP/ONC share the objective of advancing data exchange to ensure that physicians have
secure access to the right information about the right patient at the right time in their care journey. We see
TEFCA as a means to enable enhanced information exchange that should remain an option on the data
exchange landscape. The proposal to remove the TEFCA Manner Exception comes at a critical time
in network development, and we recommend that ASTP/ONC retain this exception.

As ASTP/ONC states in the Proposed Rule, TEFCA is ramping up data exchange nationwide. There is
robust participation from 12,130 Qualified Health Information Networks (QHINs), participants, and sub-
participants, with more than 474 million documents shared on the network since its go-live in December 2023. The eleven federally designated QHINs are among the most recognized names in health IT.

In addition, work at CMS to establish the CMS Interoperability Framework and CMS Aligned Networks
is promising and aligned with TEFCA. A recent ASTP/ONC blog post, The Tide and the Speedboats:
TEFCA and CMS-Aligned Networks, describes how these initiatives share the same objective, and that
their combination is powerful. We agree that both TEFCA and CMS Aligned Networks are promising and
should remain as options among the many data exchange opportunities currently available.

The removal of the TEFCA Manner Exception adds confusion to the evolving technology landscape and
sends the wrong message to prospective TEFCA enrollees about the network’s future viability.
ASTP/ONC should retain the TEFCA Manner Exception and identify additional key drivers to encourage
future QHINs, participants, and sub-participants to join the network. ASTP/ONC should only take steps
that reinforce TEFCA participation as the network continues to grow and strengthen around TEFCA as a
viable means of trusted data exchange.

Modification of Additional Exceptions

ASTP/ONC is proposing to modify or remove two additional IBR exceptions, i.e., Infeasibility and
Manner. The AMA appreciates concerns that some actors are abusing the exceptions as a cover to limit
EHI access, exchange, or use. Willful and egregious acts of information blocking should not be tolerated.
However, modifying or removing IBR exceptions impacts all actors, not just those whose conduct may
have prompted the proposed changes. Physician actors, for example, encounter many instances where it is
infeasible to share EHI, e.g., when medical records are comingled with highly sensitive information that
cannot be redacted, or when the manner requested is outside their control. To prevent some actors from
being stuck between compliance, penalties, and their good faith efforts to share information, ASTP/ONC

should make no changes to these IBR exceptions and instead, double down on enforcement and civil
monetary penalties for bad actors misusing the IBR exceptions.

Patient Health Information Capture

The AMA supports the use of patient-generated health data (PGHD) and the incorporation of other
unstructured data into a patient’s record to help improve outcomes. This information is critical for
physician-patient communication and shared decision-making, and it supports disease prevention and
management. ASTP/ONC’s proposal to remove the patient health information capture certification
criterion may instead be reformulated in ASTP/ONC’s future rulemaking in HTI-6 to safely advance the
use of PGHD and other wearables data, and to provide guidelines to ensure that the technology and data
contribute to optimal health outcomes.

For example, policies should clearly define a low-burden pathway for incorporating PGHD, including
wearable device data, into the longitudinal record in a way that is clinically meaningful, appropriately
validated, and seamlessly integrated into physician workflow—without creating new inbox volume,
reconciliation tasks, or documentation liability for practices. As FHIR standards continue to mature, the
physician community is prepared to assist the Department of Health and Human Services in developing
tools to improve the interpretation of wearables data and its impact on patient care plans and health goals.
