# Comment: HHS-ONC-2025-0005-0056

**Commenter:** Jennifer Matyac

**Posted:** 2026-02-20T05:00:00Z

**Source:** https://www.regulations.gov/comment/HHS-ONC-2025-0005-0056

I strongly oppose the HTI-5 proposed rule&rsquo;s approach of weakening certification requirements before modernizing the foundational data standards that ensure the safety, integrity, and reliability of electronic health information. The integrity of a medical record is only as reliable as its audit trail. Before deregulation of certification is considered, standards for data integrity must be strengthened and modernized including updating the audit trail standard. The current standard, ASTM E2147-18, is outdated and insufficient for the modern clinical environment. Electronic health records must be capable of being reliably reproduced in a usable, searchable, and interoperable format, with preserved context, metadata, and provenance.

The integrity, reliability, and auditability of electronic health records is foundational to patient safety, quality of care, interoperability, and accountability across the healthcare system. The accuracy, completeness, and functional integrity of records used in patient care, clinical decision-making, billing, compliance, and litigation must be prioritized. I am deeply concerned that HTI-5 risks undermining these core principles by relaxing certification requirements before modernizing the foundational data standards on which safe and reliable health information exchange depends.

Health information technology standards and certification requirements exist to ensure interoperability, safeguard patient access to data, and prevent fragmentation across systems. Eliminating or weakening these requirements does not reduce burden&mdash;it removes safeguards in a domain where errors jeopardize patient safety and carry irreversible consequences.

For these reasons, I urge HHS to reconsider deregulatory actions that remove or dilute certification requirements. Any modernization of standards should strengthen&mdash;not diminish&mdash;assurances of safety, integrity, transparency, and accountability in electronic health information systems.

Thank you.

Sincerely,
