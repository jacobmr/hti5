To the Representatives of ASTP/ONC:

I am a plaintiff medical malpractice trial attorney. For over fifteen years, I have represented catastrophically injured patients and families in cases where the truth depended entirely on the integrity of the electronic medical record (EMR). I submit this comment in strong opposition to the deregulatory proposals in HTI-5 (90 Fed. Reg. 60970 (Dec. 29, 2025))

From a courtroom perspective, what is being described as &ldquo;burden reduction&rdquo; is the removal of the only reliable mechanisms that allow courts and patients to detect fabrication, backdating, selective deletion, and undisclosed post-event alteration of medical records.

In case after case, federally required audit trails have revealed:

- Operative reports signed before surgery began.

- Care plans created after a patient&rsquo;s death but printed as if contemporaneous.

- Vital signs copied shift-to-shift without new measurement.

- Medication records charted as given after the required time window.

- Progress notes entered after discharge to retroactively justify care.

- Post-death access to medical records that defendants fought to conceal.

These facts were discoverable only because certified systems generated tamper-evident audit logs. Without mandatory audit functionality, these events would have remained invisible.

HTI-5 projects approximately $1.53 billion in cost savings to developers. That figure does not account for the cost to patients when falsified documentation goes undetected, or to courts when multi-day forensic inspections are required to uncover records that should have been produced.

Modern EMR systems now include AI-generated notes, copy-forward automation, predictive documentation tools, multi-platform data integration, and API-driven access. Interoperability without traceability creates risk. If certification requirements are removed before a modern, enforceable audit standard is adopted, accountability will weaken at the very moment documentation systems are becoming more automated and more complex.

Before removing certification safeguards, ASTP/ONC should:

Finalize and codify a modern audit trail standard that:

Flags AI-generated entries;

Identifies copy-forward source and propagation;

Captures pre-documentation and post-discharge/post-death access;

Requires retention and production in native, machine-readable format;

Prohibits automatic destruction of care-related data.

Require audit continuity across all platforms involved in a patient&rsquo;s care.

Codify that audit trails are non-privileged and subject to full patient access.

Efficiency cannot come at the expense of integrity. In litigation, audit logs are often the only mechanism preventing falsified documentation from becoming the accepted record of care.

I respectfully urge ASTP/ONC to pause the proposed deregulatory removals and implement a comprehensive, enforceable audit framework before eliminating existing safeguards.

Respectfully submitted,
Allen Tittle
Tittle & Perlmuter
Cleveland, Ohio
