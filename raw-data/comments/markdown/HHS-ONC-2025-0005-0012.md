# Comment: HHS-ONC-2025-0005-0012

**Title:** Comment from Domnick, Sean, HHS-ONC-2025-0005, HHS-ONC-2025-0005-0001, 2025-23896

**Commenter:** Sean Domnick

**Posted:** 2026-01-20T05:00:00Z

**Source:** https://www.regulations.gov/comment/HHS-ONC-2025-0005-0012

---

The integrity and security of electronic health information matters to me both personally and professionally. As a patient, I rely on medical records to be accurate, complete, and trustworthy. Clinical decisions are only as sound as the information on which they are based. If electronic health data can be altered or obscured without transparency, patient safety is put at risk.

Professionally, I see firsthand the consequences when electronic health information lacks integrity. Inaccurate or incomplete records undermine patient care, frustrate investigations, impair accountability, and erode public trust. Electronic medical records are no longer static charts. They are dynamic systems that drive clinical decisions, billing, quality reporting, and legal accountability.

Before any deregulation of certification is considered, standards for data creation, collection, preservation, and reproducibility must be updated. Electronic health data must be capable of being reliably reproduced in a usable, searchable, and shareable format. Without enforceable standards, deregulation risks weakening interoperability, transparency, and patient protection.

An updated audit trail standard is essential. Audit trails are critical to the security of electronic health information, to verifying data integrity, and to curtailing billing fraud. The current audit trail framework does not adequately reflect how modern electronic medical records function in real clinical environments.

Medical records have changed significantly since the last audit trail standard was published in 2018 (ASTM E2147-18). Today&rsquo;s records incorporate copy-forward functionality, automated documentation, decision support tools, device-generated data, messaging platforms, and artificial intelligence. Standards must keep pace with these changes.

Any updated standard must address transparency in data creation and use by artificial intelligence, medical devices, and messaging systems. When data is generated or modified by software or AI, that fact must be visible and traceable.

Despite statutory requirements for access and transparency, patients still do not have meaningful access to their complete electronic health information. Records are often incomplete or stripped of metadata necessary to understand how the information was created or changed.

Software used in patient care and in the creation of the electronic medical record must be clearly identified in audit trails. Clinicians, patients, regulators, and investigators need to know not just what the record says, but how it was produced and by what systems.

Interoperability is essential for patient safety and lawful information sharing. Software must function safely within healthcare facilities and support compliant sharing of patient health information across systems, including compliance with the Trusted Exchange Framework and Common Agreement (TEFCA).

Data retention standards must be clearly defined. Electronic health data must be preserved for as long as patient care is ongoing, investigations are underway, or litigation holds are in place, and in compliance with applicable laws and regulations.

Audit trails are foundational&mdash;not optional&mdash;to billing integrity, patient trust, and legal accountability. Audit trails, including those applicable to artificial intelligence, must be transparent and non-proprietary. They must reveal corrections, deletions, copy-forwards, and overwrites. Without this visibility, electronic medical records can enable or conceal billing fraud.

Deregulation will not solve these problems. It risks facilitating practices that already allow hundreds of billions of dollars to be lost through the improper use of electronic medical records. Strong, modern, and enforceable standards are necessary to protect patients, safeguard public funds, and maintain trust in the healthcare system.
