# Comment: HHS-ONC-2025-0005-0185

I appreciate ONC/ASTP&rsquo;s ongoing leadership in driving interoperability, strengthening data fluidity, and updating the Health IT Certification Program through the HTI‑5 proposed rule. The continued focus on standards-based exchange, burden reduction where appropriate, and readiness for emerging technologies reflects meaningful forward movement toward a more connected, efficient, and innovative digital health ecosystem. These efforts remain essential for enhancing care coordination and enabling the next generation of health IT capabilities.
At the same time, health IT is deeply embedded in the delivery of care, and its design, functionality, and safeguards directly shape patient safety outcomes. Evidence from research, real-world safety incidents, and federal enforcement actions has consistently shown that gaps in usability, auditability, authentication, clinical decision support transparency, and quality management can contribute to issues such as medication errors, wrong‑patient events, breakdowns during care transitions, and limited ability to investigate or remediate system-related harm. The Health IT Certification Program has historically served as an important national baseline for safety, ensuring foundational protections are consistently present across vendors and products. Requirements around usability evaluation, audit logging, CDS transparency, authentication, and structured quality management were established specifically to address well‑documented risks. These criteria collectively reinforce accountability, visibility, and systematic risk management in systems clinicians and patients rely on every day.
After reviewing the proposed HTI‑5 removals, the following criteria reflect significant patient safety protections:
Tier 1: Critical Safety Risk Criteria

170.315(b)(11) &ndash; Decision Support Interventions (source attribution and transparency)
170.315(g)(3) &ndash; Safety-Enhanced Design (usability testing and human factors processes)
170.315(b)(2) &ndash; Clinical Information Reconciliation and Incorporation
170.315(d)(2) &ndash; Auditable Events and Tamper-Resistance

Tier 2: High Safety Risk Criteria

170.315(d)(1) &ndash; Authentication, Access Control, and Authorization
170.315(g)(4) &ndash; Quality Management System
170.315(d)(10) &ndash; Auditing Actions on Health Information
170.315(b)(1) &ndash; Transitions of Care
170.315(d)(13) &ndash; Multi-Factor Authentication

Tier 3: Moderate Safety Concern Criteria

170.315(a)(9) &ndash; Clinical Decision Support
170.315(d)(3) &ndash; Audit Reports
170.315(d)(5) &ndash; Automatic Access Time-Out
170.315(d)(8) &ndash; Integrity

Taken together, these criteria address essential safety domains such as usability and workflow reliability, auditability and traceability, authentication and access control, clinical decision support transparency, and structured quality management. Removing them would diminish the certification program&rsquo;s role as a safety assurance mechanism and reduce the industry-wide accountability needed to ensure that baseline safeguards remain consistently implemented.
Instead of eliminating these protections, I respectfully recommend that ONC/ASTP retain them&mdash;either in their existing form or with updates that modernize and streamline them to reflect new technologies and care models. Improvements could include aligning requirements with established standards, simplifying testing procedures, or clarifying expectations. However, removing these criteria outright would eliminate foundational protections without clear evidence that the underlying safety risks have been resolved or replaced by stronger alternatives.
The Health IT Certification Program has long supported both interoperability and patient safety. As ONC/ASTP continues its modernization efforts, preserving these safety‑focused criteria will help ensure that progress in interoperability and innovation continues in parallel with a steadfast commitment to protecting patients. I appreciate the opportunity to offer feedback and thank ONC/ASTP for its continued dedication to improving the safety, reliability, and overall performance of health IT.
