Dear Dr. Kean,

The Massachusetts Health Data Consortium appreciates the opportunity to comment on the Health Data, Technology, and Interoperability ASTP-ONC Deregulatory Actions to Unleash Prosperity (HTI‑5) Proposed Rule. We hope our insights and perspectives are useful as ASTP-ONC advances its commitment to modernizing the Health Information Technology Certification Program to reduce low-value regulatory burden and advance API-forward interoperability.

MHDC strongly supports ASTP/ONC's vision of this modernization. Moreover, we aim to help ASTP-ONC realize its goals of clarifying and strengthening information-blocking policy, so that access to electronic health data works not just in principle, but in real-life practice.

Indeed, as interoperability continues to evolve from document-centric exchange toward API-forward workflows, the central challenge is no longer technical feasibility, but operational reliability at scale. So, MHDC believes HTI-5's success will hinge on whether the final rule will provide enough clarity and continuity to enable predictable, secure, reusable integration across the ecosystem as certification requirements are modernized.

For over four decades, MHDC has served as a trusted, neutral convener that advances health data exchange, interoperability, and administrative simplification across Massachusetts and nationally. As a result of our merger with the New England Healthcare Exchange Network, MHDC now supports secure data exchange and automation for over 4,000 health professionals across more than 200 organizations.

Our recommendations draw on MHDC's long history of successfully engaging payers, providers, vendors, and exchanges in real-world environments. Most importantly, all our comments aim to support ASTP-ONC's objectives by highlighting practical considerations to strengthen the operational reality of interoperability. Our general comments and recommendations are below; more-extensive details are in the attached PDF.

Based on MHDC&rsquo;s extensive experience, collaborations, and a careful review of implementations and outcomes, we conclude that burden reduction will succeed only if it decreases total friction across the ecosystem - not if it shifts cost, risk, or bespoke integration work onto providers, payers, exchanges, and implementers.

To ensure that well-intentioned deregulation succeeds in strengthening the operational reality of interoperability, MHDC therefore recommends that the HTI-5 final rule should
-Prevent burden shifting by pairing removed or revised regulations with clear crosswalks, responsibility assignment, and post-final-rule evaluation
-Preserve an interoperability floor during the transition to APIs, so that baseline exchange remains predictable and reusable
-Discourage network-by-network API arrangements and require predictable, reusable integration patterns so API exchange can scale without bespoke builds
-Maintain trust through minimum, testable security expectations to avoid gaps as legacy criteria are removed
-Define minimum, testable API security baselines and avoid security gaps during the transition from legacy criteria
-Provide an executable implementation roadmap with timelines and "removed to replaced by" crosswalks to reduce uncertainty, renegotiation, and rework
-Assign clear responsibility to developers for any displaced capability or safeguard, and commit to post-final-rule evaluation to verify burden is reduced, not shifted.
-Include worked examples for high-impact use cases to make the final rule executable in practice
-Retain a small set of meaningful interoperability performance signals so reduced reporting does not reduce accountability

MHDC also urges ASTP-ONC to address operational barriers that most-directly impede scalable exchange in practice, particularly for payer-provider workflows like prior authorization and quality exchange. MHDC therefore recommends that the final rule should:
-Prevent "pay-to-play" access to electronic health information by establishing enforceable guardrails on fees and licensing terms, with clear examples of prohibited practices and clarified enforcement expectations
-Make information-blocking updates administrable with practical examples, checklists, and workable documentation expectations that discourage strategic delay and procedural blocking
-Preserve multi-path exchange and choice by ensuring that TEFCA is effective where it fits, without becoming a ceiling that slows adoption of technically superior API-based exchange for high-volume workflows

We urge ASTP-ONC to ensure HTI-5 strengthens the operational reality of interoperability by preserving trust, preventing burden shifting, discouraging pay-to-play EHI access, and offering a clear, executable transition roadmap and input for HTI-6, as appropriate. MHDC is grateful for ASTP-ONC's leadership and for this opportunity to provide comments.
