As a team that participated in the pilot testing, we respectfully submit that the current FHIR resources for cancer reporting are not yet ready for broad operational deployment. While the potential of FHIR‑based reporting is clear, these resources require substantially more validation, real‑world testing, and implementation experience across diverse clinical environments to ensure accuracy, reliability, and consistency.

Proposed HTI-5 rule "&sect;170.315(f)(4) certification criterion transmission to cancer registries" removing certification criteria for the current, established reporting formats before widespread readiness of FHIR-based approaches would significantly disrupt cancer surveillance workflows. Such disruption would:

- Impair the continuity and completeness of cancer case reporting
- Delay the transmission of case data to public health agencies
- Negatively affect reporting timeliness, which is foundational for maintaining accurate, up‑to‑date cancer incidence rates
- Undermine the ability to monitor cancer trends, evaluate disparities, support research participation, and inform evidence‑based policy decisions

Timely case reporting is essential for the accuracy of national cancer incidence rates. Any slowdown in reporting caused by immature standards, fragmented adoption, or gaps in certified technology will directly affect the quality and timeliness of cancer registry data.

For these reasons, we strongly recommend maintaining the existing certification criteria until the FHIR resources have undergone broader testing, demonstrated operational stability, and achieved sufficient adoption across vendors and healthcare systems.

Dr. Vijay Medithi
