Introduction and Integrator Perspective

I appreciate the opportunity to comment on the HTI-5 Proposed Rule. I submit this comment from the perspective of a systems integrator that routinely translates regulatory requirements into production systems and therefore strongly support HTI-5&rsquo;s shift toward outcome-driven, standards-based interoperability.

Support for Deregulatory Reset of Health IT Certification

HTI-5 appropriately recognizes that the current certification framework contains requirements that are costly to implement but low in operational value. From an integrator standpoint, we consistently observe that:
&bull;Certification artifacts lag real-world technical practice
&bull;Compliance effort displaces investment in reliability and scalability
&bull;Redundant criteria do not materially improve interoperability

In production environments, interoperability success depends on API availability, data normalization, security, and operational governance&mdash;not static documentation. Removing low-value certification criteria allows vendors and integrators to focus on interoperability that works at scale, particularly for government programs operating under constrained budgets and timelines.

API-First Interoperability and Integration Layers

HTI-5&rsquo;s emphasis on FHIR-based APIs aligns with modern integration practice. Real-world government health environments depend on integration layers, not single systems, to connect:
&bull;HL7 v2 clinical feeds
&bull;X12 eligibility and claims transactions
&bull;Legacy batch and flat-file systems
&bull;FHIR APIs for modern access

Integration platforms translate legacy formats into FHIR resources (e.g., Patient, Encounter, Coverage, Claim, Observation) while enforcing consistent security, auditing, and governance. Prescriptive system-level certification requirements often conflict with these architectures, increasing fragility and cost.

HTI-5&rsquo;s outcome-oriented approach enables modular, resilient architectures that evolve alongside standards and technologies rather than being locked to static compliance models.

Information Blocking and Automated Access

HTI-5&rsquo;s clarification that &ldquo;access&rdquo; and &ldquo;use&rdquo; include automated and system-to-system processes is critically important.

Many essential workflows now depend on non-human access, including:
&bull;Eligibility and enrollment verification
&bull;Prior authorization workflows
&bull;Population health and quality reporting
&bull;Public health surveillance
&bull;AI-driven analytics

These workflows rely on APIs, event notifications, and batch or streaming pipelines&mdash;not manual user actions. Without clear regulatory alignment, contractual or technical constraints can unintentionally function as de facto information blocking.

By explicitly recognizing automated access, HTI-5 aligns policy with modern interoperability architectures and reduces ambiguity for implementers.

Enabling AI Through Data Accessibility

From a systems integrator perspective, most AI solutions operate as downstream consumers of interoperable data, not embedded components of certified EHRs. Common patterns include:
&bull;Predictive models consuming normalized FHIR datasets
&bull;Decision support services invoked via APIs
&bull;Fraud and population health analytics operating in separate platforms

Reducing low-value certification requirements enables greater focus on data quality, model monitoring, and operational safety&mdash;areas that directly affect outcomes. HTI-5 appropriately prioritizes data accessibility over documentation overhead, better aligning regulation with real-world risk and benefit.

Implications for Government Health Programs

HTI-5 materially improves the feasibility of modernization initiatives across:
&bull;Medicaid and CHIP programs
&bull;Medicare interoperability and analytics
&bull;Public health data modernization
&bull;Cross-agency eligibility and benefits determination

By reducing compliance burden and clarifying automation expectations, the rule supports architectures that are more scalable, cost-effective, and compatible with cloud and hybrid deployment models.

Recommendations

We respectfully recommend that ASTP/ONC:
1.Finalize the proposed certification removals with minimal re-expansion
2.Reinforce API-first interoperability as the primary policy objective
3.Provide clear guidance affirming automated system-to-system access under information blocking rules
4.Continue engaging systems integrators during finalization and implementation

Conclusion

HTI-5 represents a necessary evolution of federal health IT policy. From the perspective of a systems integrator delivering interoperability in production environments, the rule better aligns regulation with how healthcare data is actually exchanged, secured, and used at scale.

Thank you for the opportunity to comment.
