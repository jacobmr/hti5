Dear ASTP Representatives:
This matter is significant to me, professionally and personally, as well as to others for whom I bear responsibility. As the healthcare proxy and primary caregiver for both of my parents during their battles with cancer and heart disease, I relied heavily on their medical records to make informed decisions on their behalf. That reliance underscored for me the essential role that trustworthy medical documentation plays in patient care and decision-making. The same reliance exists in my role as a parent.
Professionally, I have spent years working as part of a team responsible for making decisions that can significantly affect the course of individuals&rsquo; lives&mdash;decisions grounded in and guided by the medical record. Through this work, I witness daily the critical importance of maintaining electronic medical records (EMRs) that are accurate, complete, and dependable.

I also have years of first-hand professional experience with the essential and interdependent role of the audit trail in the EMR environment. I have observed how the absence of a modernized and consistently applied audit trail standard limits the EMR&rsquo;s ability to fulfill its purpose&mdash;namely a verifiable record. While EMR systems have evolved significantly in real-world healthcare practice, including the implementation of AI integration, audit trail standards have not progressed at the same pace, creating gaps that undermine transparency, accountability, and trust.

I support the goal of enhancing interoperability for information sharing and patient safety specifically. However, I share the concerns of others who have deep concerns that the proposed Health Data, Technology, and Interoperability (HTI-5) rule, puts the goal of patient safety and trust in our healthcare system at risk by modifying certification requirements before modernizing the foundation-how data is created, collected, and preserved in a usable, and shareable format. To borrow a phrase-the house won&rsquo;t fall if the bones are good.

There exists a concurrent need for revision of the audit trail standard. ASTM E2147-18, is outdated and insufficient for today&rsquo;s clinical and technological environment. Since 2018, the use, structure, and content of medical records have evolved dramatically. To support and not thwart an accurate, complete, reliable, and verifiable medical record, a new audit trail standard should include: AI, Medical Device, and Messaging Integration; Software Identification and Interoperability; Granular Metadata and Fraud Prevention; Standards must mandate the collection of precise data elements, including user roles and detailed activity logs (e.g., "copy-paste," "copy forwards," and "view-only" access). Audit trails that are transparent and reveal corrections, deletions, or overwrites, including the specific data that was changed, are essential to prevent the fraudulent practices that cost billions of dollars annually.

Patient Access: We must address the reality that many patients still do not have access to their
complete EMR, despite such transparency being required by law.

Preservation Over Ephemerality- The move toward interoperability requires that data be not only shareable but also durable. Data retention must be clearly defined so that information is available as long as patient care, investigations, or litigation holds are ongoing, and to remain in compliance with state and federal regulations. I strongly urge HHS to prohibit "ephemeral messaging" or any automatic destruction of data. information regarding the decisions underlying their care and treatment.

Audit trails are foundational&mdash;not optional&mdash;to billing integrity, patient trust, and legal accountability. We must define what a "trustworthy record" is before we change the rules on how those records are certified. I ask that HHS pause the proposed certification changes until a modern, robust audit and preservation standard is finalized.

Thank you for your time, attention, and your commitment to the health and safety of the American public.

Respectfully,
Sabrina Cunningham
