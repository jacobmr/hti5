# Comment: HHS-ONC-2025-0005-0067

**Title:** Comment from Watts, Chloe , HHS-ONC-2025-0005, HHS-ONC-2025-0005-0001, 2025-23896

**Commenter:** Chloe Watts

**Posted:** 2026-02-24T05:00:00Z

**Source:** https://www.regulations.gov/comment/HHS-ONC-2025-0005-0067

---

I support ONC&rsquo;s goals of reducing unnecessary regulatory burden, fostering innovation, and advancing interoperable access to electronic health information, including the long‑term vision of a FHIR‑enabled, API‑driven ecosystem. However, I am concerned that this proposed rule removes critical certification, reporting, and public health infrastructure safeguards before replacement capabilities are operationally ready at scale.
FHIR‑based access alone does not equate to readiness for downstream use cases such as public health surveillance, reporting, and emergency response. The success of a FHIR‑forward strategy depends not only on data availability, but also on the readiness of receiving systems, governance, workforce capacity, and operational workflows. Many state, local, territorial, and tribal public health agencies are not yet positioned to reliably receive and operationalize FHIR‑based reporting at scale, and existing CDA‑based or hybrid pipelines remain essential for continuity.
The proposed removal or weakening of public health certification criteria and reporting transparency risks data gaps, reduced situational awareness, and uneven impacts across jurisdictions with differing levels of technical capacity. Modernization should not come at the expense of continuity, particularly during public health emergencies.
I am also concerned about the implicit retirement or de‑emphasis of the CDC&rsquo;s Vocabulary Access and Distribution System (PHIN/VADS). PHIN/VADS remains a critical operational dependency for jurisdictions and immunization information systems, and is relied upon regularly&mdash;often daily&mdash;to guide implementation decisions, resolve vocabulary questions, align reporting expectations, and support active technical discussions with providers and vendors. Retiring or allowing PHIN/VADS‑dependent workflows to atrophy before a fully operational, authoritative, and jurisdiction‑ready substitute is available risks fragmentation, inconsistent interpretation of standards, and regression in public health reporting quality. PHIN/VADS or an equivalent service should remain available until states and public health agencies confirm that replacement solutions fully meet their technical and governance needs.
The proposed rule emphasizes access to electronic health information while reducing requirements that historically supported reliability, usability, safety, and accountability once data is exchanged. This shift from outcome assurance to access assurance introduces risk for both clinical care and public health, particularly for under‑resourced providers and jurisdictions that rely on certification as a baseline safeguard.
I urge ONC to adopt a phased, readiness‑based approach to deregulation. Removal of public health and reporting‑related certification requirements should be conditioned on demonstrated readiness and sustained operational capability. A minimal, enforceable public health reporting baseline should be maintained during the transition, along with limited transparency measures that assess whether public health data exchange is actually occurring, not merely whether APIs exist.
I support ONC&rsquo;s modernization goals, but modernization must proceed in a way that preserves patient safety, public health continuity, and equity while acknowledging current operational realities.
