RE: Public Comment on Proposed Rule RIN 0955-AA09 (HTI-5)

Dear ASTP Representative:

I submit this comment as an attorney who has spent many years reviewing electronic medical records in matters involving patient safety. I am reviewing electronic medical records and audit trails at least once a week, or more. My perspective is shaped not by theory, but by real-world experience with how electronic health information (EHI) functions in practice and the relationship between records, bills, and audit trails.

At its core, this rule should be evaluated through one question: Does it strengthen or weaken a patient&rsquo;s ability to access, review, challenge, understand, and trust their own medical record?

I support interoperability and modernization. Patients deserve seamless, secure access to their health information. However, I am concerned that modifying or removing certification criteria before modernizing audit trail and data preservation standards risks undermining the accuracy and reliability of the medical record itself.

The medical record serves as the foundation for diagnosis, treatment, billing, insurance determinations, disability evaluations, and care coordination. Its integrity depends on transparent audit functionality. It is important to know what is in the medical record, but also when the information was entered, modified, or deleted. Without clear and updated standards governing how data is created, modified, stored, and exported, patients cannot be assured that their records are complete, accurate, or traceable.

In my experience, audit trails are essential not only for accountability, but for patient safety. They reveal when entries are corrected, modified, overwritten, or copied forward. Often, these changes are appropriate and clinically justified. But without transparent and modern audit standards, patients and providers alike lack confidence in the integrity of the record.

Before certification guardrails are reduced, I respectfully urge HHS to prioritize modernization of audit trail standards to ensure:

1. Meaningful Traceability
   Audit logs should capture clinically significant actions, including edits, overwrites, copy-forward functions, user roles, and access activity.

2. Inclusion of Emerging Technologies
   If AI systems, connected medical devices, or provider messaging platforms influence patient care, those activities should be logged and preserved as electronic health information.

3. Durable Preservation
   Clinically relevant communications and EHI should be retained in a readable, reproducible format consistent with federal and state retention requirements. Systems used in patient care should not allow automatic destruction of relevant clinical data.

4. Enforceable Patient Access
   Despite existing federal law, many patients still face practical barriers in obtaining complete records. Regulatory reform should reinforce&mdash;not dilute&mdash;the enforceability of patient access rights under the 21st Century Cures Act.

I am particularly concerned about the proposed removal of certain certification criteria, including 45 C.F.R. &sect;&sect; 170.315(d)(2), (d)(3), (d)(10), (b)(11), and (g)(3), without first establishing updated standards that ensure authentication integrity, audit transparency, and reliable EHI export.

Interoperability and regulatory streamlining are worthy goals. But deregulation should not precede the establishment of modern safeguards that protect the accuracy and completeness of the medical record.

Patients rely on their records as a source of truth. Federal health IT policy should ensure that those records remain transparent, durable, and trustworthy.

Therefore, I respectfully oppose the proposed changes and deletion of the identified certification criteria, as doing so at this stage would weaken essential protections that ensure patients can access complete, accurate, and trustworthy medical records. It would also create unnecessary patient safety risks by diminishing the safeguards that ensure the integrity, traceability, and reliability of electronic health information.

Respectfully submitted,
Thomas P. Ryan
Cleveland, Ohio
