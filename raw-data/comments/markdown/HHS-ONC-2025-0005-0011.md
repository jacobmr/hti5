I support disciplined, targeted deregulation that reduces administrative burden and accelerates responsible innovation in health data interoperability. However, deregulatory actions must remain consistent with the Administrative Procedure Act, the 21st Century Cures Act, and HIPAA&rsquo;s privacy and security framework. Deregulation should strengthen&mdash;not weaken&mdash;competition, patient trust, equity, and accountability, particularly as health data increasingly fuels AI-driven technologies and market consolidation pressures intensify.

⸻

To Whom It May Concern,

I submit this public comment in response to the Department of Health and Human Services&rsquo; proposed rule entitled Health Data, Technology, and Interoperability: ASTP/ONC Deregulatory Actions to Unleash Prosperity.

I comment from the perspective of an AI consultant, nonprofit executive, author, and public-interest advocate with formal training in psychology, business administration, and applied technology. My work focuses on the operational and legal consequences of health data policy across public institutions, technology developers, and community-based systems&mdash;particularly where innovation, equity, and governance intersect.

Regulatory and Market Context

Under the Administrative Procedure Act (APA), agency action must be reasoned, non-arbitrary, and supported by evidence. Under the 21st Century Cures Act, Congress made clear that interoperability and information access are core federal priorities, not optional market features. Any deregulatory action by ASTP/ONC should therefore preserve enforceable standards that prevent information blocking, protect patient agency, and promote fair competition.

Deregulation that weakens clarity or oversight risks substituting regulatory friction with market failure&mdash;particularly in a sector already prone to vendor lock-in, asymmetrical bargaining power, and opaque data practices.

Key Observations and Recommendations
1.Interoperability Must Remain a Public Good Under the Cures Act
While streamlining certification and compliance requirements may reduce costs for developers, interoperability standards must remain enforceable and technology-neutral, consistent with Congressional intent under the 21st Century Cures Act. Reduced oversight without substitute safeguards risks entrenching dominant EHR and data platform vendors, raising barriers to entry for startups, safety-net providers, and nonprofit innovators.
2.Equity Impacts Require Statutory Awareness and Measurable Safeguards
Deregulatory actions have foreseeable disparate impacts on populations already facing structural barriers to care and digital access. Consistent with civil rights obligations and federal equity directives, HHS should pair deregulation with explicit equity impact assessments, defined performance metrics, and post-implementation monitoring. Absent such measures, deregulation risks shifting costs and compliance burdens onto providers and communities least equipped to absorb them.
3.AI-Driven Health Innovation Requires Accountability to Sustain Market Confidence
Health data is now a core input for artificial intelligence, predictive analytics, and automated decision systems. Reducing transparency, auditability, or data provenance requirements in this context introduces systemic risk. Market growth depends on trust. Clear governance expectations around AI-enabled health technologies&mdash;particularly in clinical decision support, utilization management, and population health&mdash;are essential to preventing bias, preserving competition, and avoiding downstream legal and reputational harm.
4.Patient Agency and Consent Remain Central to a Functional Data Market
Efficient data exchange should not dilute patient rights established under HIPAA and reinforced through interoperability policy. Plain-language disclosures, meaningful consent, and enforceable remedies are not regulatory friction; they are trust infrastructure. Markets scale when participants&mdash;especially patients&mdash;understand and trust the rules of engagement.
5.Regulatory Certainty Is a Competitive Advantage
Predictable, clearly scoped rules lower transaction costs and attract responsible innovation. Overly rapid or ambiguous deregulation increases legal uncertainty, favors large incumbents with compliance leverage, and discourages smaller entrants who lack regulatory risk tolerance.

Conclusion

Prosperity in the health data ecosystem is not measured solely by reduced compliance lines or accelerated product cycles. It is measured by durable innovation, fair competition, patient trust, and equitable access. I urge HHS and ASTP/ONC to proceed with precision, retain core statutory protections, and commit to rigorous evaluation of market and equity impacts as these deregulatory actions are implemented.
