I appreciate ONC/ASTP&rsquo;s continued leadership in advancing interoperability, improving data liquidity, and modernizing the Health IT Certification Program through the HTI-5 proposed rule. The emphasis on standards-based exchange, reduced burden where appropriate, and preparation for emerging technologies represents meaningful progress toward a more connected and innovative digital health ecosystem. These efforts are essential to improving care coordination and enabling the next generation of health IT capabilities.
At the same time, health IT plays a central role in care delivery, and its design, functionality, and safeguards have direct implications for patient safety. Research, real-world safety events, and federal enforcement actions have demonstrated that deficiencies in usability, auditability, authentication, clinical decision support transparency, and quality management can contribute to medication errors, wrong-patient events, failures in care transitions, and an inability to investigate and correct system-related harm. The Health IT Certification Program has served as a critical national safety floor, ensuring that baseline protections are present across products and vendors. Certification criteria addressing usability testing, audit logging, clinical decision support transparency, authentication, and systematic quality management were adopted specifically to mitigate known and documented patient safety risks. These criteria help ensure accountability, transparency, and systematic risk mitigation in systems that clinicians and patients rely upon every day.
Based on analysis of the HTI-5 proposed removals, the following criteria represent important patient safety protections:
Tier 1: Critical Safety Risk Criteria
170.315(b)(11) &ndash; Decision Support Interventions (source attribution and transparency)
170.315(g)(3) &ndash; Safety-Enhanced Design (usability testing and human factors processes)
170.315(b)(2) &ndash; Clinical Information Reconciliation and Incorporation
170.315(d)(2) &ndash; Auditable Events and Tamper-Resistance
Tier 2: High Safety Risk Criteria
170.315(d)(1) &ndash; Authentication, Access Control, and Authorization
170.315(g)(4) &ndash; Quality Management System
170.315(d)(10) &ndash; Auditing Actions on Health Information
170.315(b)(1) &ndash; Transitions of Care
170.315(d)(13) &ndash; Multi-Factor Authentication
Tier 3: Moderate Safety Concern Criteria
170.315(a)(9) &ndash; Clinical Decision Support
170.315(d)(3) &ndash; Audit Reports
170.315(d)(5) &ndash; Automatic Access Time-Out
170.315(d)(8) &ndash; Integrity
These criteria collectively address core safety domains, including usability, clinical workflow reliability, auditability and traceability, authentication and access control, clinical decision support transparency, and systematic quality management. Removing these protections would weaken the certification program&rsquo;s role as a safety assurance mechanism and reduce accountability for ensuring that health IT systems incorporate baseline safety safeguards.
Rather than removing these criteria, I respectfully recommend that ONC/ASTP maintain them in their current form or optimize and modernize them as needed to align with evolving technologies and care delivery models. Optimization could include streamlining testing processes, harmonizing with existing standards, or clarifying requirements&mdash;but eliminating these criteria entirely risks removing foundational safeguards without evidence that the underlying safety risks have been eliminated or mitigated through superior alternative mechanisms.
The Health IT Certification Program has played a critical role in advancing both interoperability and patient safety. As ONC/ASTP continues to modernize the program, preserving core safety-focused criteria will help ensure that innovation and interoperability advance alongside, not at the expense of, patient safety. I appreciate the opportunity to provide input and thank ONC/ASTP for its continued commitment to improving the safety and effectiveness of health IT.
