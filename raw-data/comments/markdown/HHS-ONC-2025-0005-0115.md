# Comment: HHS-ONC-2025-0005-0115

**Title:** Comment on HHS-ONC-2025-0005, HHS-ONC-2025-0005-0001, 2025-23896

**Commenter:** Anonymous Anonymous

**Posted:** 2026-02-26T05:00:00Z

**Source:** https://www.regulations.gov/comment/HHS-ONC-2025-0005-0115

---

We support the direction of the Health Data, Technology, and Interoperability (HTI-5) proposed rule and its focus on modernizing and streamlining the federal health IT regulatory framework. In particular, we appreciate the intent to reset the scope of the ONC Health IT Certification Program by removing duplicative or outdated requirements, while establishing a stronger and more practical foundation for FHIR-based API capabilities going forward.

HTI-5 represents an important opportunity to reduce unnecessary compliance burden while reinforcing the core policy objectives of interoperability and patient access. We appreciate ASTP/ONC&rsquo;s approach to advancing a more durable and future-ready regulatory foundation that supports innovation, competition, and responsible next-generation interoperability solutions.

However, we are concerned that maintaining the requirement in 45 CFR 170.404(b)(2) to publicly publish a list of Service Base URLs - associated with the certification criteria in &sect; 170.315(g)(10)(viii)(B) - inadvertently functions as a proxy for competitively sensitive commercial data, creating unintended market externalities that undermine the competitive dynamics ASTP/ONC intends to promote. This requirement has the practical effect of disclosing both a developer&rsquo;s customer footprint and the identity of those specific customers. For small and startup developers of certified health IT, this creates meaningful commercial risk and provides a mechanism for market distortion.

When transparency requirements effectively signal customer footprint and market position, they can inadvertently shape competitive behavior in ways that favor incumbents and undermine new entrants. In other regulated industries, disclosure regimes intended to promote fairness and consumer benefit have sometimes been leveraged by dominant incumbents to monitor small rivals and disadvantage new entrants - such as the airline industry&rsquo;s Computer Reservations Systems (CRS), where real-time pricing visibility enabled market consolidation rather than healthy competition. In a market where the same dominant providers have maintained entrenched positions for decades, the continued public disclosure of Service Base URLs provides incumbents with a strategic roadmap to identify and target emerging competitors.

For small businesses and new entrants attempting to scale, this transparency creates an asymmetric risk: it allows dominant entities to deploy defensive, loss-leader tactics - such as loss-leader pricing - that can neutralize a rival before it reaches critical mass. Eliminating this requirement is essential to ensure that mandated public disclosures do not serve as a low-cost market surveillance tool for incumbents. Protecting the strategic growth of new entrants is vital to reducing the barriers to entry in the market and fostering the genuine competition this legislation seeks to unlock.
From a practical standpoint, third-party developers and integration partners rarely build viable businesses on top of platforms with small customer footprints. If public Service Base URL publication becomes a proxy for customer scale, it may reinforce a 'rich get richer' dynamic, steering ecosystem development toward incumbents and away from innovators - counter to ASTP/ONC&rsquo;s goals of competition, interoperability, and long-term market dynamism.

We recommend ASTP/ONC modify &sect; 170.315(g)(10)(viii)(B) to establish minimum qualifications (e.g., based on customer count and/or customer size) that a developer of certified health IT must meet prior to being required to publish Service Base URL documentation publicly, in a similar vein to ONC's implementation of the Insights Conditions requirement. This approach would preserve transparency objectives while protecting the growth and competitive viability of new entrants during their most vulnerable scaling phases and avoiding a compliance mechanism that inadvertently functions as a market-disadvantaging disclosure mandate.
