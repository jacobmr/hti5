ASTP representatives:

I write to you asking that the ASTP/ONC proposed rule on Health Data, Technology, and Interoperability be paused until certain underlying audit trail standards, some of which have been in place since 2018 but are now being modernized, are finalized.

I am a board certified surgeon as well as licensed healthcare attorney. In both roles, I have dedicated my career to advocating for safe patient care. My 30+ year career in healthcare has seen the demise of traditional &ldquo;paper&rdquo; records and rise of electronic health information systems/electronic health record systems (EHS and EHR). While EHS and EHR supposedly increase efficiency and portability of patient records, EHS and EHR pose a risk of allowing post-care entries in the medical record to masquerade as contemporaneously entered entries. Therefore, ensuring the integrity, credibility, accuracy, and security of electronic health information is vital to me professionally to ensure patient safety and personally as a consumer of the American healthcare system. While I support the goal of enhancing interoperability, I am deeply concerned that the Health Data, Technology, and Interoperability (HTI-5) proposed rule risks undermining these values by modifying certification requirements before modernizing foundational data standards.

Audit Trails Standards &ndash; The Critical Need for Updated Audit Trail Standards
The integrity, credibility, accuracy, and security of a medical record is only as reliable as its audit trail. An updated audit trail standard is required for the security of EHR, to verify its integrity, and to curtail medical record and medical billing fraud. The current standard, ASTM E2147-18, while robust for its timeframe of 2018, is now insufficient for the modern clinical environment. Since 2018, the use and content of medical records have evolved dramatically. If we move forward with decertification or the removal of functional guardrails without first defining how data is created, collected, and preserved in a usable, searchable, and sharable format, we invite patient harm and compromise the trustworthiness of patient medical records and thus our healthcare system.

To ensure medical records remain a transparent source of credible and truthful record entries, including the current ASTM standard who, what, where, and when such entries were made, but now requiring far more, HHS must prioritize a revision of the audit trail standard that includes:

AI, Medical Device, and Messaging Integration: Explicitly capturing the activities of AI algorithms, connected medical devices, and professional messaging to ensure clinical recommendations and data creation are transparent and traceable.

Software Identification and Interoperability: Audit trails must identify the specific software used in patient care. Furthermore, software must be interoperable for safe functionality within facilities and for sharing information in compliance with TEFCA.

Granular Metadata and Fraud Prevention: Standards must mandate the collection of precise data elements, including user roles and detailed activity logs (e.g., "copy-paste," "copy forwards," and "view-only" access). Audit trails that are transparent and reveal corrections, deletions, or overwrites are essential to prevent the fraudulent practices that cost billions of dollars annually.

Patient Access: We must address the reality that many patients still do not have access to their EHI, despite such transparency being required by law.

Preservation Over Ephemerality
The move toward interoperability requires that data be not only shareable but also durable. Data retention must be clearly defined so that information is available as long as patient care, investigations, or litigation holds are ongoing, and to remain in compliance with state and federal regulations. I strongly urge HHS to prohibit "ephemeral messaging" or any automatic destruction of data. Data must be retained in a readable, reproducible format for as long as it remains in use.

Sequential Implementation
I agree that we must establish clear, shared definitions for terms like ePHI, EHI, Generative AI, and Synthetic Data. However, these standards for data preservation and the ability to reproduce data must be updated, codified, and enforced before deregulation of certification is considered. Removing existing guardrails without a superior framework in place may actually facilitate fraudulent use of electronic health records.

Audit trails are fundamental to medical record and medical billing integrity, patient trust, and legal accountability. We must define what a &ldquo;trustworthy&rdquo; record is before we change the rules on how those records are certified.

I request that HHS pause the proposed certification changes until a modern, robust audit and preservation standard is finalized.

Thank you for your time in reviewing my comment.
