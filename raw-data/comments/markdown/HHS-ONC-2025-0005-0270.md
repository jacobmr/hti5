# Comment: HHS-ONC-2025-0005-0270

**Title:** Comment from Ullem, Beth Daley, HHS-ONC-2025-0005, HHS-ONC-2025-0005-0001, 2025-23896

**Commenter:** Beth Daley Ullem

**Posted:** 2026-03-02T05:00:00Z

We commend ONC/ASTP for its leadership in advancing interoperability and improving the usability and accessibility of health information through the HTI-5 proposed rule. Efforts to streamline certification requirements, promote standards-based exchange, and reduce unnecessary regulatory burden are important to supporting innovation and improving the efficiency of care delivery. Continued modernization of the certification program is essential as health IT evolves and becomes more deeply integrated into clinical decision-making.

At the same time, health IT is not simply an administrative tool&mdash;it is an integral component of clinical care, influencing diagnostic decisions, medication ordering, care coordination, and clinical documentation. When health IT systems lack appropriate safeguards, design rigor, or transparency, they can contribute to preventable harm. National reports, patient safety investigations, and peer-reviewed research have identified usability limitations, incomplete auditability, insufficient access controls, and opaque clinical decision support as contributing factors in medication errors, wrong-patient orders, delays in care, and barriers to identifying and correcting safety issues. The Health IT Certification Program has played a vital role in addressing these risks by establishing clear, enforceable expectations that vendors incorporate safety-focused design, accountability mechanisms, and systematic quality processes into their products.

Several of the certification criteria proposed for removal serve essential patient safety functions. These include requirements related to clinical decision support transparency, usability testing and safety-enhanced design, audit logging and audit report capabilities, authentication and access controls, multi-factor authentication, clinical information reconciliation, transitions of care, quality management systems, and protections to ensure data integrity. Specifically, the criteria identified in the HTI-5 analysis include:

Tier 1: Critical Safety Risk Criteria

170.315(b)(11) &ndash; Decision Support Interventions

170.315(g)(3) &ndash; Safety-Enhanced Design

170.315(b)(2) &ndash; Clinical Information Reconciliation and Incorporation

170.315(d)(2) &ndash; Auditable Events and Tamper-Resistance

Tier 2: High Safety Risk Criteria

170.315(d)(1) &ndash; Authentication, Access Control, and Authorization

170.315(g)(4) &ndash; Quality Management System

170.315(d)(10) &ndash; Auditing Actions on Health Information

170.315(b)(1) &ndash; Transitions of Care

170.315(d)(13) &ndash; Multi-Factor Authentication

Tier 3: Moderate Safety Concern Criteria

170.315(a)(9) &ndash; Clinical Decision Support

170.315(d)(3) &ndash; Audit Reports

170.315(d)(5) &ndash; Automatic Access Time-Out

170.315(d)(8) &ndash; Integrity

These requirements help ensure that health IT systems are developed and implemented with appropriate attention to usability, traceability, transparency, and risk management. They support clinicians&rsquo; ability to deliver safe care and enable healthcare organizations to investigate and address potential safety events when they occur. Importantly, certification provides a consistent, nationwide baseline that benefits healthcare organizations of all sizes, including those with limited technical resources to independently evaluate vendor systems.

We respectfully recommend that ONC/ASTP retain these safety-related criteria within the certification program or revise and strengthen them as appropriate to reflect current technology and best practices. While refinement and modernization may be warranted, removing these protections entirely would create unnecessary risk and reduce assurance that certified systems meet fundamental safety expectations.

The certification program has been instrumental in improving the reliability and safety of health IT nationwide. Preserving core safety-focused requirements will help ensure that progress in interoperability and innovation continues alongside a sustained commitment to protecting patients and supporting clinicians.

Thank you for your continued leadership and for the opportunity to comment on this important proposed rule.
